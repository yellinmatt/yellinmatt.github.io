Special Relativity

How a junior patent clerk in Bern rewrote space and time in his spare hours, and what E=mc² actually says.

In 1905 Albert Einstein was a technical expert, third class, at the Swiss patent office in Bern. No university post, no laboratory, no eminent mentor: he assessed applications for electrical gadgets eight hours a day and did physics in the margins. Over a few months that year he mailed four papers to the journal Annalen der Physik. The first explained the photoelectric effect and would win him the Nobel Prize. The second proved that atoms are real. The third and fourth took apart the picture of space and time that had stood since Newton. Physicists call 1905 his annus mirabilis, the miracle year, borrowing a phrase once used for Newton's own.

The trouble was light. Maxwell's equations of the 1860s assigned light a fixed speed, about three hundred thousand kilometers a second, but measured against what? Sound travels through air and ocean waves through water, so light was assumed to travel through an invisible medium filling all of space, the luminiferous aether. In 1887 Albert Michelson and Edward Morley built an instrument precise enough to catch the Earth drifting through that aether. They found nothing. The Earth plainly moves, yet light arrived at the identical speed in every direction. Einstein took the null result at its word: light has the same speed for every observer, and the rest of physics would have to accommodate it.

His relativity paper, On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies, runs on two assumptions. It cites no other papers and names only five physicists. The second assumption is the one that sounds impossible.

ReadEinstein, 1905

First postulate — the principle of relativityThe laws by which the states of physical systems undergo change are not affected, whether these changes of state be referred to the one or the other of two systems of co-ordinates in uniform translatory motion.

Second postulate — the constancy of lightAny ray of light moves in the “stationary” system of co-ordinates with the determined velocity c, whether the ray be emitted by a stationary or by a moving body.

On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies, Annalen der Physik, 1905. Trans. Jeffery and Perrett.

The first postulate is old. Galileo knew you cannot feel uniform motion: drop a ball below deck on a smoothly sailing ship and it falls exactly as it would on the dock. The second postulate is the one that breaks the old physics. Whether you sprint toward a beam of light or flee from it, you clock it at the same c. Speed is distance divided by time. If observers in different states of motion all measure light at the same speed, then distance and time cannot be the fixed backdrop Newton assumed. They have to give. Einstein followed that logic without blinking, and let space and time deform to keep the speed of light constant.

Simultaneity falls first. Two events that happen at the same instant for me need not be simultaneous for you if you are moving relative to me. Out of this comes time dilation: a moving clock, watched from rest, ticks slow. Imagine a clock built from two mirrors with a single photon bouncing between them, one tick per round trip. Set the whole clock moving sideways and the photon now has to travel a slanted, longer path between the mirrors. It cannot go faster than c, so each tick takes longer. Lengths contract along the line of motion as well, and a moving body grows more massive. At the speeds of cars and planets the change is far too small to notice; near the speed of light it dominates.

The fourth paper, barely three pages, drew the last consequence. If a body emits energy, Einstein showed, its mass falls by exactly that energy divided by c squared. Mass and energy are one thing in two costumes, traded at the rate c². Because c² is enormous, roughly a 9 followed by sixteen zeros in metric units, a pinch of mass holds an extravagant amount of energy. That is the line everyone knows, E = mc², and it is the arithmetic behind the Sun's furnace, the hydrogen bomb, and the long burn of every star in the sky. Einstein gave the paper a question for a title: Does the Inertia of a Body Depend Upon Its Energy Content? The answer was yes.

· · ·

This is not blackboard fantasy. Muons, particles born when cosmic rays strike the upper atmosphere, live about two millionths of a second and ought to decay long before reaching the ground; they arrive in abundance because, moving near light speed, their internal clocks run slow, a result Bruno Rossi and David Hall measured in 1941. The GPS in a phone applies relativistic corrections continuously; switch them off and your fix drifts by kilometers within a day. Special relativity governs only uniform motion. It took Einstein another decade to bring in gravity and acceleration, yielding general relativity in 1915. When a solar eclipse in 1919 caught starlight bending around the Sun by the amount general relativity predicted, Einstein woke up the most famous scientist on Earth.
