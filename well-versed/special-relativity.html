Special Relativity

How a junior patent clerk in Bern rewrote space and time in his spare hours, and what E=mc² actually says.

In 1905 a junior clerk at the Swiss patent office in Bern spent his evenings taking Newton apart. Albert Einstein was twenty-six, with no university post and no laboratory, and he assessed patents for electrical machines by day. In his own hours he wrote four papers and sent them to the journal Annalen der Physik, and each one reshaped physics. The first explained the photoelectric effect and would win him the Nobel Prize; the second proved that atoms are real; the last two replaced the picture of space and time that had held since the seventeenth century. Physicists have called that year his annus mirabilis ever since, the miracle year, after the phrase once used for Newton's own.

The puzzle that set it off was light. Maxwell's equations, written in the 1860s, gave light a single definite speed, but measured against what? Sound travels through air and waves through water, so physicists supposed that light moved through an invisible medium filling all of space, which they called the aether. In 1887 Albert Michelson and Edward Morley built an instrument fine enough to catch the Earth drifting through it, and found nothing at all. The Earth was plainly in motion, yet light arrived at the same speed in every direction, and Einstein chose to take that result at its word rather than explain it away.

His relativity paper, On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies, leans on just two assumptions. It cites no other paper and names only five physicists in its pages. The first is mild; the second sounds impossible.

ReadEinstein, 1905

First postulate — the principle of relativityThe laws by which the states of physical systems undergo change are not affected, whether these changes of state be referred to the one or the other of two systems of co-ordinates in uniform translatory motion.

Second postulate — the constancy of lightAny ray of light moves in the “stationary” system of co-ordinates with the determined velocity c, whether the ray be emitted by a stationary or by a moving body.

On the Electrodynamics of Moving Bodies, Annalen der Physik, 1905. Trans. Jeffery and Perrett.

The first postulate was already old when Einstein took it up. Galileo had shown back in 1632 that steady motion cannot be felt from the inside: drop a ball in the cabin of a smoothly sailing ship and it lands at your feet exactly as it would on dry land, with nothing in the fall to betray that the ship is moving. Einstein extended that familiar principle to the whole of physics, light included. The second postulate is the radical one. Run toward a beam of light or flee from it, and either way you measure the same speed c, and from that one stubborn fact the strangeness follows. Speed is only distance divided by time, so if light holds the same speed for everyone, distance and time cannot be the fixed stage Newton assumed; they bend instead.

Begin with time. Two events that look simultaneous to me need not be simultaneous to you if you are moving, and out of that comes the headline effect: a moving clock runs slow. Picture the simplest clock imaginable, two mirrors with a single pulse of light bouncing between them, one tick per round trip. Now set the whole clock gliding sideways. While the light crosses from one mirror to the other the mirrors themselves shift along, so from where you stand the pulse traces a long diagonal rather than a straight line up and down. Light cannot speed up to cover the extra ground, since its speed is the one thing that never changes, so each tick takes a little longer. The faster the clock travels, the slower it runs.

Space gives way too. A moving object contracts a little along its direction of travel, and it grows more massive as it speeds up. At the pace of cars and planets these shifts are far too small to feel; they take over only as you near the speed of light.

The fourth paper, barely three pages long, drew the final consequence. If a body gives off energy, Einstein showed, it loses a trace of mass, precisely the energy divided by c squared. Mass and energy turned out to be the same thing in different units, convertible at the rate c², and because c² is a huge number, a sliver of mass holds an enormous store of energy. That is the meaning of E = mc², the line behind the Sun's heat, the hydrogen bomb, and the slow burning of every star. Einstein gave the paper a question for a title, Does the Inertia of a Body Depend Upon Its Energy Content?, and answered it yes.

· · ·

None of this stays on paper. Muons, the particles made when cosmic rays strike the upper air, last about two millionths of a second and ought to expire long before they reach the ground, yet they arrive in great numbers, because at nearly the speed of light their clocks run slow exactly as the theory says; Bruno Rossi and David Hall measured it in 1941. The GPS in a phone makes the same correction every day, and without it your position would wander off by kilometers within hours. Special relativity governs only steady motion, and folding in gravity and acceleration took Einstein another decade, the work he finished as general relativity in 1915. When a solar eclipse in 1919 caught starlight bending around the Sun by the amount that larger theory predicted, Einstein woke the next morning the most famous scientist in the world.
