The Sermon on the Mount

On a hillside above the Sea of Galilee, Jesus blessed the poor, the meek, and the merciful, and turned the world’s order of value upside down in the three chapters that became Christianity’s moral charter.

A crowd has climbed a hill above the Sea of Galilee, and a young rabbi sits down to teach them. What he says over the next three chapters of Matthew’s Gospel would be quoted, argued over, and lived by for two thousand years. He does not open with a command or a threat. He opens with a strange list of blessings, and every one of them lands on the wrong people: the poor, the grieving, the meek, the persecuted. The Sermon on the Mount begins by turning the world’s scoreboard over.

The sermon runs from the fifth chapter of Matthew through the seventh, the longest unbroken stretch of Jesus’s teaching in any Gospel, and it takes its name from the setting, since Matthew says he “went up into a mountain” to speak. Luke carries a shorter version delivered on level ground, the Sermon on the Plain. Augustine fixed the title we still use when he wrote his own commentary around the year 393, De sermone Domini in monte, On the Lord’s Sermon on the Mount, and called the passage a perfect standard of the Christian life. Pilgrims have honored a low hill near Capernaum as the place since at least the fourth century, and the octagonal church that crowns it now, one wall for each blessing, was raised in the 1930s.

Those opening blessings are the Beatitudes, from the Latin beatus, blessed or happy. Jesus pronounces the happy ones eight times over, and the pattern never once bends toward the powerful. Read them and watch who it is that inherits the earth.

Blessed are they which are persecuted for righteousness’ sake: for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.

The Gospel of Matthew 5:3–10, King James Version (1611).

From the blessings the sermon widens into images that lodged permanently in English. The disciples are “the salt of the earth” and “the light of the world,” and “a city that is set on an hill cannot be hid.” That last line sailed to New England in 1630 in John Winthrop’s shipboard sermon aboard the Arbella, where the Puritans’ errand became a city upon a hill, and it carried down three more centuries into Ronald Reagan’s farewell image of a shining city. A single verse can seed a nation’s picture of itself.

The body of the sermon is a series of reversals of the old law. “Ye have heard that it was said… but I say unto you,” Jesus repeats, and each time he drives the commandment inward. The law forbade murder, and he reaches back to the anger behind it; it forbade adultery, and he reaches to the lustful look; it allowed an eye for an eye, and he answers with the command to turn the other cheek and love your enemies. At the centre he gives the words that became the most spoken prayer on earth, “Our Father which art in heaven.” Near the close comes the rule that nearly every ethical tradition recognizes in some form: all things whatsoever ye would that men should do to you, do ye even so to them. He ends on a builder’s picture, the wise man who set his house on rock and the fool who built on sand, and leaves the listener to decide which one to be.

· · ·

No passage in the Gospels has traveled further outside the church that keeps it. Tolstoy read the command against resisting evil as the literal heart of Christianity and built a whole nonviolent creed on it. Gandhi, who first met the sermon while working in South Africa, wrote that it “went straight to my heart,” and carried turning the other cheek into the campaign that freed India; Martin Luther King Jr. preached from it through the Montgomery boycott. Whatever a reader finally makes of its theology, the Sermon on the Mount remains the fullest answer the tradition offers to a plain and permanent question: how should a person live.
