The Goldberg Variations

A 1741 keyboard exercise built on a single bass line, and the 1955 recording by a twenty-two-year-old Canadian that gave it a second life.

Bach was fifty-six and at Leipzig when Balthasar Schmid published the work in Nuremberg in 1741. Schmid was a friend. He engraved the score onto copper plates rather than setting it in movable type, which is why the notation in the first edition is in Schmid's own handwriting. Bach called it Clavier-Übung, the fourth in a series of keyboard exercises, and gave it the working subtitle that has held for three centuries: An Aria with diverse variations for the harpsichord with two manuals. Composed for connoisseurs, for the refreshment of their spirits. Connoisseurs and the refreshment of spirits. That is the whole proposition.

The work is an Aria followed by thirty variations and then the Aria again, da capo, returning unchanged after the journey. The Aria is a slow sarabande in G major in two halves of sixteen bars each. Here is the move that took everyone three centuries to fully metabolize: the thirty variations are not on the Aria's melody. They are on its bass line and the harmonic progression that bass implies. The tune comes back only at the end. Every third variation is a canon at a successively wider interval, unison through the ninth, until the thirtieth, where instead of the expected canon at the tenth, Bach drops a Quodlibet, an old family parlor game in which two folk tunes are stitched together over the same bass. The whole structure is ten groups of three: a free variation, a virtuoso variation, then a canon. A repeating pattern that hides its own machinery inside what sounds like ornament.

The most famous origin story is almost certainly false. Bach's first biographer, Johann Nikolaus Forkel, writing in 1802, sixty years after the fact, reported that the work was commissioned by Count Hermann Karl von Keyserlingk, the Russian ambassador to the Saxon court, who suffered from insomnia and kept a teenage harpsichordist named Johann Gottlieb Goldberg in an antechamber to play through the sleepless nights. Bach was rewarded, Forkel says, with a golden goblet full of one hundred louis d'or. The variations took Goldberg's name. The story has problems. There is no dedication on Schmid's title page, which broke the publishing convention of the day. Goldberg in 1741 was fourteen. The variations are not soothing. No goblet appears in the inventory of Bach's estate. Peter Williams, in the standard scholarly edition, calls the Forkel account spurious. The name stuck anyway.

The other thing scholars discovered late was the appendix. In 1974 a French musicologist found Bach's personal copy of the printed score in Strasbourg, now in the Bibliothèque nationale de France. Bound into the back, in Bach's own hand, were fourteen further canons on the first eight notes of the Goldberg bass, catalogued BWV 1087. Fourteen is the ordinal sum of the letters BACH (2+1+3+8). Bach kept the structural game going past the printed work, for himself.

The work belonged to harpsichordists. In June 1955, a twenty-two-year-old Canadian named Glenn Gould walked into Columbia Records' 30th Street Studio in Manhattan, sat on a folding chair his father had cut down so that his eye line was almost level with the keys, and recorded the Goldberg Variations on a piano in four days. It was his first major-label session. The album came out in 1956. It sold forty thousand copies by 1960 and crossed a hundred thousand by his death, numbers that the classical recording industry did not produce in those years. Gould played the variations with a hard, detached articulation, almost no pedal, breakneck where most pianists were stately, and he skipped most of the indicated repeats. The whole thing runs thirty-eight minutes and thirty-four seconds. He hummed throughout, which the microphones picked up. The performance argued, by its existence, that Bach was modern. The recording transferred ownership of the variations from harpsichordists to the rest of the listening world inside one calendar year.

He came back to it. In 1981, after a quarter century of recording for Columbia, Gould returned to the 30th Street Studio and re-recorded the Goldberg Variations digitally. It was the last classical record made in that studio before it closed. The 1981 reading is fifty-one minutes and eighteen seconds, slower by almost a third, calmer, more architecturally calculated, with proportional tempo relationships across the variations he had not worried about in his twenties. Columbia released it in September 1982. Gould died of a stroke that October at fifty. The two recordings, twenty-six years apart in the same room, end his discography where they began it. Picking a side, 1955 or 1981, is the standard opening move in any conversation among people who actually listen to him.

What to listen for. The Aria is quieter than you expect, almost private. The early variations announce themselves; the canons are buried structural events that you hear as music, not as canons, which is the joke. Variation 25, an aching G minor adagio, was Wanda Landowska's "black pearl"; it is the work's emotional floor. The Quodlibet at thirty stitches together two German folk tunes, Ich bin so lang nicht bei dir gwest and Kraut und Rüben haben mich vertrieben, over the same old bass, a domestic joke at the end of a cathedral. Then the Aria returns. Same notes. Different thing.

ListenGlenn Gould, 1955

Start with the Aria. The sarabande lilt in three, the right hand decorated to the point of jewelry, the left hand walking a bass that you should commit to memory because every variation is built on it. Then variation one breaks the spell with two-part counterpoint at speed, and you are inside the machine.

Bach, Goldberg Variations BWV 988 (Glenn Gould, 1955) — Sony Classical official channel →

The work is one of the few in the Baroque keyboard literature that holds up under any reasonable performance choice. Hear it on harpsichord (Trevor Pinnock, Pierre Hantai), on piano (Gould, Murray Perahia, András Schiff, Igor Levit), even on string trio (Dmitry Sitkovetsky's arrangement). It survives because the underlying structure is so strong that the surface can be anything. That is, in the end, the lesson of the variations. The bass holds. Everything else is what you do with it.
