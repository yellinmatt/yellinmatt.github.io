The Points of Sail

A sailboat cannot sail into the wind, but it can sail almost everywhere else. The points of sail are the map of where the wind will let you go, and the first thing any sailor learns to read.

Stand on a dock and watch a boat tack upwind, and the first thing that looks like magic is that it moves toward the very wind that should be pushing it back. It cannot do this directly. Point the bow straight at the wind and the sails flap like laundry, the boat slows, and you are stuck in what sailors call being in irons. But swing the bow off to one side by forty-five degrees or so and the sails fill, the boat leans and accelerates, and you can travel almost into the eye of the wind by zig-zagging across it. The whole art begins with one fact: a boat sails by the wind crossing its sails, not by the wind pushing them, and the angle to the wind decides everything about how it goes.

That angle has a name at every step around the compass, and the names are the points of sail. They run in a fixed order as the boat turns away from the wind. Closest to the wind a boat can hold is close-hauled, sails pulled in tight to the centerline, the boat heeled hard and clawing upwind at roughly forty-five degrees off the breeze; this is also called beating, and it is wet, tilted, lively work. Turn farther from the wind and you are reaching, the broad middle of the range, divided into three. A close reach sits between close-hauled and ninety degrees. A beam reach takes the wind square on the side, at ninety degrees, and is usually the fastest and most comfortable point of sail, the boat upright and quick. A broad reach carries the wind over the back corner, around a hundred and thirty-five degrees. Turn all the way until the wind is dead behind you and the boat is running, sails let all the way out, riding downwind. Every direction a boat can travel is one of these, and a competent sailor names the point of sail without thinking, the way a driver knows what gear they are in.

The thirty or so degrees on either side of straight upwind is the one place a boat genuinely cannot go: the no-go zone, the no-sail zone, in irons. To get somewhere inside that forbidden wedge, you sail close-hauled on one side, then turn the bow through the wind and sail close-hauled on the other, stitching upwind in a series of Vs. That turn through the wind is a tack. Its mirror image, turning the stern through the wind while running downwind, is a jibe (also spelled gybe). Both swap which side the sails are on, but they feel different on deck: a tack is gentle, the boat slowing as it noses through the wind; a jibe can be violent, the boom slamming across with the full force of the breeze behind it, which is why a careless jibe in strong wind is the classic way to knock a crewmate overboard or break a mast.

Because the maneuvers are quick and a wandering boom is dangerous, sailors run them on a script of calls, and learning the calls is part of learning the sport. To tack, the helmsman warns the crew with “Ready about,” waits for “Ready,” and then turns the boat with “Helm's a-lee” as the bow swings through the wind and the crew trims the sails on the new side. To jibe, the sequence is “Prepare to jibe,” then “Ready,” then “Jibe-ho” as the stern crosses the wind and the boom comes over. The words sound quaint, but they are how a crew that cannot all see the same thing at once stays coordinated and unhurt, and they are the same on a club dinghy and a hundred-foot yacht.

WatchSailaBoat.TV

Tim Hore's clip walks a small boat through each point of sail with the wind drawn on the water. Watch how the sails are let out steadily as the boat turns from close-hauled toward a run, and how the helm finds the no-go zone by losing the wind.

One more thing worth carrying off the water: a surprising amount of everyday English was minted on these angles. To sail by the wind is to sail close to it, close-hauled; to sail large is to run with the wind well behind. A ship that handled well on both was good by and large, and the phrase came ashore to mean good in general, on the whole. When a helmsman steered too close and the wind caught the sails flat against the mast, stopping the boat dead, she was taken aback, which is exactly what the words still mean when news stops you cold. Knowing the points of sail is the price of admission to a sailboat; knowing where half your idioms came from is the bonus.
