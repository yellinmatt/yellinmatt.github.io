The Calling Card

For a century and a half a stiff rectangle of pasteboard opened doors, closed them, and kept a running ledger of who owed whom a visit.

The caller’s carriage did not stop for long. A footman climbed the steps, handed a small stiff card to the servant at the door, and was back down before anyone opened it wider than a crack. No one expected to be let in. Leaving the card was the whole of the errand, and it was a move in a game whose rules every family of a certain rank was expected to know by heart. That rectangle of pasteboard, no larger than the business card in a wallet today, was the smallest working unit of nineteenth-century social life, and how you handled it could seat you at a table or quietly cross you off it.

The card in the sense we mean came out of France. Before the eighteenth century a visitor who found the house empty simply left a note in his own hand; by the 1760s the fashionable households of France and Italy were leaving printed cards instead, engraved on one face with a bare space kept free on the other for a line of ink. The French called it a carte d’adresse, and later a carte de visite, and the habit crossed the Channel and the Atlantic with the rest of French manners. By the height of the Victorian era a man or woman who meant to hold a place in society, or gain one, could no more go without cards than without shoes.

The grammar of the thing was strict and unspoken. You did not call at a stranger’s house uninvited; you left your card and waited. The answer came to your own door: a card sent back meant a visit would be welcome, while silence, or a card returned sealed in an envelope, was a polite closing of the door. The whole apparatus leaned on there being servants to carry the cards in and out on a small silver tray, which is why it belonged so exactly to the classes that kept them.

What the card said, and how, was read as closely as a face. The British standard was severe: the bearer’s name, perhaps a club, and nothing else, for as Emily Post wrote, “the personal card is in a measure an index of one’s character,” and a gaudy one gave its owner away. A gentleman’s card was cut long and narrow to ride in a breast pocket, a lady’s larger and more nearly square. The blank back had its uses too, as a place for a line of ink.

Because a lady might carry a dozen cards through an afternoon of calls, she carried them in a case, and the case became a small piece of jewelry. Makers turned them out in tortoiseshell, ivory, mother-of-pearl, and engraved silver; the most coveted silver examples were the “castle-tops,” worked in relief with a view of Windsor Castle or the Houses of Parliament by the Birmingham workshops of Nathaniel Mills and his rivals.

The part everyone half-remembers is the code of the corners. Folklore turns it into an elaborate silent language: a corner folded to carry the visit to the whole household, French initials pencilled in one corner, p.f. to offer congratulations, p.c. for condolence, p.p.c., for pour prendre congé, to take one’s leave of the town for the season. Some of that was real. But the practice was looser than the legend, and the surest guide is Emily Post, writing in 1922 as the whole system was already going out of fashion.

ReadEmily Post, Etiquette, 1922

Turning down a corner of a visiting card is by many intended to convey that the visit is meant for all the ladies in the family. Other people mean merely to show that the card was left at the door in person and not sent in an envelope. Other people turn them down from force of habit and mean nothing whatever. But whichever the reason, more cards are bent or dog-eared than are left flat.

Emily Post, Etiquette, Chapter X, “Cards and Visits” (1922).

None of this was gentle. When a servant said the lady was “not at home,” she was rarely out; the phrase meant only that she was not receiving, and everyone understood the difference. The old hostesses kept their visiting lists with a banker’s care, so that, in Post’s words, “everyone’s credit was entered or cancelled according to the presence of her or his cards in the card receiver.” A young person who wanted to keep his invitations left an extra card now and then to be safe, since the formidable old ladies were quick to strike a name off and slow ever to write one back.

· · ·

By the time Post set all this down she was describing a world already slipping. A cutting machine patented in 1889 could stamp a hundred thousand cards a day just as the demand for them began to fall, and the automobile, the telephone, and a general appetite for informality finished the ritual off. Its descendants are still in our hands, though the pedigree is forgotten: the business card pressed across a table, the plain “with sympathy” tucked among funeral flowers, the reply card in a wedding invitation that keeps, however faintly, a tally of who has answered.
