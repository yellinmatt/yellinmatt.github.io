The Cigar

Wrapper, binder, filler, and a number in sixty-fourths of an inch. Learn to read a cigar, and the band stops being decoration.

Heartland

Cuba, Dominican, Nicaragua

Hand someone a cigar and most of what they need to judge it is printed on the thing itself, or rolled inside it. The leaf you see, the size on the box, the country on the band: each tells you how the smoke will taste and how long it will last. A little fluency turns the whole object legible, and a cigar lounge stops being a place where you nod along.

01

A premium cigar is three tobaccos, each doing a different job.

  • WrapperThe outermost leaf, chosen for looks and flavor. It carries the largest share of the taste and signals strength by color, from pale Claro to near-black Maduro.
  • BinderThe tougher leaf wound just beneath the wrapper. It holds the bunch together and governs how evenly the cigar burns.
  • FillerThe core, and the bulk of the cigar. In a hand-rolled smoke these are whole long-filler leaves, the engine of body and strength.

The rounded end is the cap, which you cut before smoking; the open end is the foot, which you light. Machine-made cigars use shredded short filler, which is why a hand-rolled long-filler cigar burns slower and tastes cleaner.

02

A cigar's name is really its shape. Two numbers fix it: length in inches, and ring gauge, the diameter measured in sixty-fourths of an inch. A 42 ring gauge is 42/64 of an inch across.

Petit Corona
Around 5 inches by 42 ring, near thirty minutes. The Montecristo No. 4 is the most famous example.
Corona
The historic standard, about 5½ inches by 42, close to forty-five minutes. The yardstick the other shapes are measured against.
Robusto
Short and fat, roughly 5 inches by 50 ring. The most popular size in the world for decades, and a full smoke in a compact stick.
Toro
A stretched robusto, about 6 inches by 50. The size that has crept up in girth as tastes ran to thicker cigars.
Churchill
A long 7 inches by 47 or 48, near an hour of smoking, named for Sir Winston, who is said to have gone through eight or ten a day.

Geography sets the character before the roller touches a leaf. Cuba is the old country, prized for a sweet, earthy spice that other soils chase and rarely match. The Dominican Republic, the largest premium producer, leans smoother and milder; Nicaragua runs richer and more peppery; Honduras sits earthy in between. One wrinkle of history confuses newcomers: when the United States embargoed Cuba in 1962, exiled makers rebuilt their old brands abroad, so a name like Montecristo or H. Upmann now exists in both a Cuban version and a separate non-Cuban one sold across America.

The ritual rewards patience. Cut just above the shoulder of the cap, where the rounded head meets the body, so the wrapper does not unravel, then draw cold to test the air; a good cigar pulls like a thick milkshake. Toast the foot at an angle an inch above the flame until it glows, then draw gently and never inhale, since the pleasure is flavor on the palate rather than smoke in the lungs. Let it rest between puffs, roughly one a minute, and a long ash is a sign of good construction, so knock it off only when it asks to fall.

Havana, 1935

03

When in doubt, reach for one cigar above all others.

  • CigarMontecristo No. 4 (Cuban), vitola name Mareva
  • FormatPetit corona, 5⅛ inches by 42 ring
  • StrengthMedium, creamy with coffee, cocoa, and cedar
  • SmokeAbout thirty minutes

For years the best-selling Cuban cigar in the world, now nudged for the top spot by the Partagás Serie D No. 4 but still the reference petit corona: cheap enough to be everywhere, good enough that aficionados keep boxes of it.

The brand carries one of the better origin stories in tobacco. In 1935 Alonso Menéndez bought a Havana factory and named his new cigar after The Count of Monte Cristo, the Dumas novel the lector read aloud to the rollers as they worked, and he stamped the box with a triangle of six rapiers around a fleur-de-lis, a nod to the swordplay in the novel. Montecristo now accounts for roughly a quarter of all the cigars sold by Habanos S.A., the Cuban state company that markets the island's twenty-seven protected brands, which makes it the most popular Cuban cigar on earth. Get fluent in one of these, and the rest of the humidor falls into place.
