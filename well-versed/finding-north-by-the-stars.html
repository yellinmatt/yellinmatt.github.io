Finding North by the Stars

Without a compass, you can find true north on any clear night: one faint star sits almost over the pole, and a familiar constellation points straight at it.

Stand in an open field on a clear night with no phone and no compass, and wait. Over an hour the whole sky turns. Stars climb in the east, wheel overhead, and sink in the west, the same slow rotation the Sun makes by day. One point alone holds steady. A single star hangs there hour after hour while everything else circles it, and that star stands almost exactly over the Earth’s north pole. Find it and you have found true north, fixed and free, the oldest navigational aid there is.

The star is Polaris, and the first surprise is how dim it is. People expect the North Star to blaze, yet it sits at second magnitude, somewhere around the forty-eighth brightest star in the sky, washed out for most city dwellers and easy to mistake for any of its neighbours. Sailors and scouts never hunt for it head-on. They follow a signpost. The Big Dipper, the seven-star pattern most people can already pick out, carries two stars on the outer lip of its bowl named Dubhe and Merak. Run a line up through that pair and carry it about five times the gap between them; it lands on Polaris, the last star in the handle of the fainter Little Dipper.

ReadU.S. Army Field Manual 21-76

The two stars forming the outer lip of this dipper are the “pointer stars” because they point to the North Star. Mentally draw a line from the outer bottom star to the outer top star of the Big Dipper’s bucket. Extend this line about five times the distance between the pointer stars. You will find the North Star along this line. You may also note that the North Star can always be found at the same approximate vertical angle above the horizon as the northern line of latitude you are located on.

U.S. Army, Field Manual 21-76 (Survival), §18-11, “The Northern Sky.”

The Big Dipper does not stay put. It swings low and even below the horizon through part of the year and part of the night, and when it does you reach for Cassiopeia instead. This is the lazy W, five bright stars in a flattened zigzag, riding the far side of the pole from the Dipper. The two patterns sit opposite each other and turn counterclockwise around Polaris through the night, so when one is down the other is up. Carry both in your head and you are never without a pointer to the pole.

What makes the trick work is an accident of alignment. The Earth’s axis happens to aim within about three-quarters of a degree of Polaris, so as the planet spins, that star barely moves while the rest of the sky pivots around it. A long exposure turns the effect into concentric rings of light with Polaris at the still centre, as in the photograph above. Two payoffs follow. Drop a straight line from Polaris to the horizon, and that spot is true north, the geographic pole a magnetic compass can only approximate. And the star’s height above the horizon equals your latitude: a hand’s span up at thirty degrees north, near the zenith in the high Arctic, down on the horizon at the equator. For centuries that single angle, sighted off Polaris with a sextant, told a ship’s master how far north he had sailed.

Polaris holds the job only for now. The Earth’s axis wobbles like a slowing top, tracing a full circle about once every twenty-six thousand years, and the point it aims at drifts among the stars. When the Egyptians raised the pyramids around 3000 BCE, the pole star was Thuban in the constellation Draco, and the builders sighted on that quarter of the sky down a passage cut into the stone. In roughly twelve thousand years the brilliant star Vega will inherit the post. Polaris is a tenant of the pole, never its owner, and a good one for the moment: it slides closest, within half a degree, around the year 2100, then begins to pull away.

South of the equator the sky offers no such gift. No bright star marks the south celestial pole, so southern navigators read an empty patch by triangulation. The Southern Cross, four stars in a compact kite, does the work: extend its long axis about four and a half times its own length, then drop from that imagined point to the horizon to find due south. Two brilliant stars nearby, the Pointers, both help locate the Cross and keep you from confusing it with the larger, dimmer False Cross. Beside them lies the Coalsack, a blot of dark nebula so starless it reads as a hole punched in the Milky Way. Cross the line into the southern hemisphere and you simply trade one guide for the other; the method is older than the compass, and asks for nothing but a clear night and a few minutes’ patience.
