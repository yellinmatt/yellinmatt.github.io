The Allegory of the Cave

The oldest argument that what you can see may not be what is real, told as a story about prisoners and a fire.

Picture people who have been chained in a cave since childhood, their heads fixed so they can look only straight ahead at a blank wall. Behind them a fire burns. Between the fire and their backs runs a low parapet, and along it unseen handlers carry statues and carved shapes, the way puppeteers work a screen, so that the wall in front of the prisoners fills with moving shadows. Those shadows, and the echoes that come off the stone, are the only world these people have ever had. They give the shapes names, argue about which one comes next, and award honors to whoever guesses best. They would tell you, without hesitation, that they understand reality.

This is the opening of the most quoted passage in Western philosophy. Socrates lays it out for Plato’s brother Glaucon near the start of Book VII of the Republic, written in Athens around 375 BCE, and he frames it as a picture of one thing: “the effect of education and the lack of it on our nature.” The allegory is the third in a linked sequence of images. First comes the Sun, then a divided line that sorts the visible from the intelligible, and then the cave, which sets that whole abstract scheme in motion as a story with a body in it.

“Behold! human beings living in an underground den… here they have been from their childhood, and have their legs and necks chained so that they cannot move, and can only see before them, being prevented by the chains from turning round their heads. Above and behind them a fire is blazing at a distance, and between the fire and the prisoners there is a raised way; and you will see, if you look, a low wall built along the way, like the screen which marionette players have in front of them, over which they show the puppets.”

“I see.”

“And do you see men passing along the wall carrying all sorts of vessels, and statues and figures of animals made of wood and stone…? To them the truth would be literally nothing but the shadows of the images.”

Republic VII, 514a–515c, tr. Benjamin Jowett, 1871.

Now free one of them. Plato walks the escape in stages, and each stage hurts. Unchained and forced to stand, the prisoner aches and is dazzled even by the firelight; told that the shadows he knew were less real than the carved things that cast them, he disbelieves it. Dragged up the steep passage into the open air, he is blinded by the sun and wants only to go back. His eyes adjust by degrees: first shadows again, then reflections in water, then the things themselves, then the stars and the moon, and at last the sun, which he comes to understand gives the seasons, governs the visible world, and is in some way the cause of everything he and his fellows ever saw. The sun is Plato’s image for the Form of the Good, the highest of the eternal realities that the changing world of the senses only copies.

The lesson Plato draws is sharper than it first looks. Education, on this picture, is not a matter of pouring facts into an empty mind, the way you might put sight into blind eyes. The capacity to know is already there in every soul; what it lacks is direction, and it has been pointed the wrong way, down at the shadows. The work of education is to turn the whole soul around until it faces the light. The Greek word for that turning is periagoge, a wheeling-about. Knowledge, for Plato, is less an acquisition than a reorientation.

The image has been drawn almost as often as it has been argued. In 1604 the Dutch engraver Jan Saenredam cut a famous version after a design by Cornelis van Haarlem: a vaulted cavern, a crowd of figures gesturing at a sunlit doorway, the whole scene captioned Antrum Platonicum, the cave of Plato. It is the picture most people carry in their heads when they think of the allegory, four centuries after Plato wrote it and two thousand years after he died.

Then comes the turn that gives the allegory its weight. The freed prisoner goes back down. His eyes, used now to the sun, can no longer make out the shadows the others still take for truth, and he fumbles in the dark and seems ridiculous. He has gone up, they say, and come back with his sight ruined; better not to climb at all. Plato ends the scene with a knife: if they could lay hands on the man who tried to loose their chains and lead them up, “and if they could kill him, will they not actually kill him?” He is writing about Socrates, his own teacher, condemned by an Athenian jury and made to drink hemlock in 399 BCE, a quarter-century before the ink of the Republic. The whole serene geometry of sun and shadow rests on a remembered execution.

· · ·

That is why the cave has outlasted nearly everything else Plato built. The Theory of Forms has its critics; the ideal city of the Republic, with its philosopher-kings and its censored poets, repels most readers who meet it. But the cave survives on its own, because the experience it describes keeps happening. Anyone who has changed their mind and found the old crowd unwilling to follow has felt the climb and the cost of the return. It is the reflex behind every later claim that appearances deceive, from Descartes’ deceiving demon to the simulation in The Matrix. Two and a half thousand years on, the first thing to know about the cave is that we are still, most of us, watching the wall.
