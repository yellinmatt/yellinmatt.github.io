The only man to win three World Cups

Pelé

The son of a poor Brazilian town who became the only footballer to win three World Cups, and turned the sport into the thing the world now calls o jogo bonito.

On 29 June 1958, in a stadium outside Stockholm, a Brazilian boy of seventeen took a lofted pass on his chest, flicked the ball over a Swedish defender’s head, and volleyed it home before it touched the grass. Brazil won that final 5–2 for their first World Cup, and the boy, Edson Arantes do Nascimento, wept on a teammate’s shoulder when the whistle blew. The world already knew him by a nickname whose origin he never liked and never explained. It knew him as Pelé.

He had come up from almost nothing. Born in 1940 in Três Corações, a poor town in the Brazilian interior, he was the son of a journeyman footballer and learned the game barefoot in the street, kicking a sock stuffed with rags. By fifteen he had signed for Santos, the club he would carry for close to twenty years. At sixteen he was scoring for the national side. The talent arrived whole and never thinned.

That first title in 1958 opened a run no one has matched. Pelé is the only footballer to have won the World Cup three times, in 1958, 1962, and 1970, and the middle years were unkind. A thigh injury in the second match of the 1962 tournament shut him out of the rest of it, and Brazil went on to win without him. In 1966 in England, defenders hacked him out of the competition with tackles the referees let stand, Brazil fell in the group stage, and for a while he swore he was done with the World Cup.

He came back for 1970, and the Brazil side he led in Mexico is the one most often called the greatest team ever assembled. They won all six of their matches and beat Italy 4–1 in the final at the Estadio Azteca, where Pelé headed the opening goal and then, in the closing minutes, rolled the ball sideways without looking for his captain Carlos Alberto to thunder home the fourth. That month held his boldest moments even in failure: the dummy that sent a Uruguayan goalkeeper one way while the ball ran past on the other, the shot attempted from the halfway line, and the downward header that England’s Gordon Banks somehow clawed over the bar in a stop still called the save of the century.

WatchFIFA · 1970 World Cup final

Brazil’s fourth goal against Italy, often called the finest team move in the game’s history. Watch the last touch: Pelé receives the ball at the edge of the box, waits, and rolls it flat into the path of the overlapping Carlos Alberto without so much as a glance.

The numbers around him blur into legend. Guinness credits him with 1,279 goals in 1,363 matches, a figure that counts the friendlies and exhibitions Santos played around the world to cash in on his fame. His thousandth, in November 1969, came from a penalty at the Maracanã and stopped the country for a day. The tally was never the whole of it. Pelé played as though the game were made to be watched and winning were only the half of it, and the phrase that trailed him, o jogo bonito, the beautiful game, became the name the whole sport now gives itself.

When his playing days in Brazil ended he carried the game to a country that had ignored it, signing for the New York Cosmos in 1975 and drawing American crowds to a sport they had spent a century overlooking. He retired in 1977 and spent the next four and a half decades as football’s ambassador to the world, O Rei, the King. He died in São Paulo in December 2022, at eighty-two. Diego Maradona and Lionel Messi have their partisans, and the argument over the greatest will never close, but Pelé is the one who made the case that the sport could be an art.
