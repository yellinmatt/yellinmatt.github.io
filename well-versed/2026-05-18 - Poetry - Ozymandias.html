Ozymandias

Fourteen lines Shelley wrote on a Christmas dare in 1817, before the king had reached London.

Shelley wrote it as a wager. He was wintering in Marlow with Mary during Christmas of 1817 when the banker, parodist, and family friend Horace Smith joined them. The Romantics liked these competitions. Shelley, Keats, and Leigh Hunt had recently raced one another to sonnets on the Nile. This time the prompt was a passage in the first-century-BCE Greek historian Diodorus Siculus, who described a colossal Egyptian statue and quoted the inscription cut into its pedestal: King of Kings am I, Ozymandias. If anyone would know how great I am and where I lie, let him surpass me in any of my works. Ozymandias is the Greek transliteration of User-maat-Ra, the throne name of Ramesses II. The two poets sat down to it on or around Christmas Day. Shelley finished first.

His sonnet ran in The Examiner on 11 January 1818, signed Glirastes, the “lover of dormice,” his pet name for Mary. Smith's came out three weeks later, retitled and burdened with explanation: On a Stupendous Leg of Granite, Discovered Standing by Itself in the Deserts of Egypt, with the Inscription Inserted Below. Smith was a generous man and a good banker. He was no Shelley. His sonnet opens, with no music at all, “In Egypt's sandy silence, all alone, / Stands a gigantic Leg.” Shelley's opens with a stranger walking out of a horizon. The competition was over by the third line.

ReadShelley, 1818

I met a traveller from an antique land
Who said: “Two vast and trunkless legs of stone
Stand in the desert. . . . Near them, on the sand,
Half sunk, a shattered visage lies, whose frown,
And wrinkled lip, and sneer of cold command,
Tell that its sculptor well those passions read
Which yet survive, stamped on these lifeless things,
The hand that mocked them, and the heart that fed:
And on the pedestal these words appear:
‘My name is Ozymandias, King of Kings:
Look on my works, ye Mighty, and despair!’
Nothing beside remains. Round the decay
Of that colossal wreck, boundless and bare
The lone and level sands stretch far away.”

Sonnet, loose iambic pentameter. The Examiner, 11 January 1818.

Read the architecture, not just the lines. A sonnet has fourteen lines; Shelley delivers fourteen lines. A Petrarchan sonnet has the rhyme scheme ABBAABBA CDECDE, an octave that poses and a sestet that answers; a Shakespearean has three blocks of cross-rhyme ABAB CDCD EFEF and a closing couplet GG that snaps the argument shut. Shelley does neither. He invents a hybrid: ABABACDCEDEFEF. The B-rhyme of stone and command reaches into the C-block and pulls read with it. The D-rhyme braids through the E. The E and F interlock instead of resolving. There is no clean turn, no closing couplet, no rest. The form is a wreck. The poem is a ruin too.

The frame is a Russian doll. The reader hears Shelley. Shelley hears a traveller. The traveller has stood at the broken statue. The statue's pedestal speaks for the king. The king commands a Mighty that no longer exists. By the time you reach the inscription, you have passed through four narrators and are listening to a dead man brag about a kingdom that has eaten itself. The boast survives the empire. Nothing else does. The whole poem is the gap between the size of his claim and the silence around it.

Note the date. Shelley wrote in late 1817 in anticipation of a specific object: the Younger Memnon, a seven-and-a-quarter-ton granite head-and-torso fragment of Ramesses II that the Italian strongman and engineer Giovanni Belzoni had wrestled out of the Ramesseum at Thebes on commission for the British consul-general Henry Salt. Napoleon's expedition had tried to take it in 1798 and failed. Belzoni rigged wooden rollers and ropes, dragged it to the Nile, and shipped it to Cairo by December 1816, then Alexandria, then London. The bust reached Deptford in 1818 and entered the British Museum's collection in 1821. Shelley never saw it. The poem is older than the arrival of its subject in England; the imagined ruin precedes the actual ruin. That is part of the meaning.

The statue Shelley imagined is not, strictly, this one. The actual Ozymandias colossus of Diodorus's account is a different, larger toppled red-granite statue still at the Ramesseum, originally seated, originally something like sixty feet high and a thousand tons, still in the desert and still on its back. But the Younger Memnon carries the same throne name on its shoulder, and the British anticipation of its arrival put Ramesses II in every newspaper in London in 1817. The whole sequence, of Diodorus reading the inscription in the first century BCE, Napoleon trying to crate up a king, Belzoni hauling a piece of him to Deptford, and Shelley writing it all into fourteen lines before any of it touched English soil, is the kind of historical chain the poem is actually about.

· · ·

Read it aloud once. The closing line slows on its three monosyllabic adjectives, lone, level, far, and the sands keep going past the period. The poem has been quoted at every empire's funeral since: the Soviet collapse, the British retreat from India, Bryan Cranston reciting it over a desert in a trailer for Breaking Bad. It is the most quoted thing Shelley wrote and one of the most quoted sonnets in the English language. The first thing to know about it is that he wrote it as a parlor game in three or four days, and Smith won the parlor game and lost everything else.
