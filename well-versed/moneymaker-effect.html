The Moneymaker Effect

An accountant with no live-tournament experience turned an $86 online satellite into $2.5 million and the biggest boom poker has ever seen.

Chris Moneymaker sat down at Binion’s Horseshoe in May 2003 having never played a hand of tournament poker for money in his life. He was twenty-seven, a comptroller from Spring Hill, Tennessee, and his surname was not a stage joke: his ancestors anglicized a German name, Nurmacher, on the way to America, and it happened to describe exactly what he was about to do. Eight hundred and thirty-nine players had paid the World Series of Poker’s ten-thousand-dollar entry fee that year, most of them professionals who had earned their seats the traditional way, across years of cash games and satellites in smoky rooms. Moneymaker had earned his differently.

He had qualified online. PokerStars, then a small internet card room competing against larger established sites, ran satellite tournaments that fed winners straight into live events, and Moneymaker won an eighty-six-dollar single-table satellite that carried a Main Event seat as its prize. His own later account of the story got the number wrong: his 2005 autobiography is titled after the forty dollars he believed he had spent, a misremembering thorough enough to survive on the book’s cover. The real figure mattered more to poker’s future than either number suggests, because it proved a stranger with a home internet connection could sit down at a felt table in Las Vegas and belong there.

Getting there meant beating players who had made poker their profession. Moneymaker eliminated Phil Ivey, already the most feared young pro in the game, in tenth place, then knocked out former champion Dan Harrington on the way to a final two-man match against Sam Farha, a Beirut-born pro who played the table in a half-open silk shirt with a cigarette rarely leaving his hand. The two went to their decisive hand with the blinds at 20,000 and 40,000, a 5,000 ante, and Moneymaker’s stack, 4.6 million in tournament chips, a shade ahead of Farha’s 3.7 million.

Moneymaker raised from the button with king-seven, and Farha called from the big blind with queen-nine. Both players checked a dry flop of nine, six, and two. The turn brought an eight of spades, giving Farha top pair and a backup flush draw; he led out for 300,000, a heavy overbet, and Moneymaker, holding nothing but a flush draw and a straight draw over king-high, raised him to 800,000. The river fell a blank three of hearts. Farha checked, and Moneymaker pushed every chip he had into a pot already worth 1.8 million, representing a flush he did not hold. ESPN’s Norman Chad called it live as “the bluff of the century.” Farha stared across the table for what felt, on the broadcast, like several minutes, holding nothing stronger than a pair of nines with a backup spade of his own. Then he folded, and what had been a close two-way match tipped hard toward Moneymaker.

The next big pot finished the job. Moneymaker got the rest of his chips in with five-four on a flop of jack, five, and four against Farha’s jack-ten, and his two pair held. He walked away with $2.5 million and a gold bracelet, in his first tournament, three weeks after learning he had won a seat he thought cost forty dollars.

· · ·

Poker had never had a champion like this. ESPN spent that summer broadcasting the tournament with the hole-card camera, a device that let a home viewer see both players’ cards while the pros at the table saw only one, and millions of people who had never sat at a felt table watched an accountant out-nerve a professional for the biggest prize the game offered. The following year 2,576 players entered the Main Event, more than triple the 2003 field, and the winner, Greg Raymer, had also qualified online. By 2006 the field reached 8,773, a record that stood for seventeen years. The poker press settled on a name for what had happened: the Moneymaker effect.

Moneymaker has made a career of poker ever since. The tournament that started it, entered as a rank beginner on a satellite ticket, remains his only WSOP bracelet.
