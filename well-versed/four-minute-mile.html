The Four-Minute Mile

On a windy Oxford evening in 1954, a medical student ran a distance the experts had ruled beyond the body, and his record stood for all of six weeks.

The wind very nearly cancelled it. On the afternoon of 6 May 1954 a gale was crossing the Iffley Road track in Oxford, and Roger Bannister, riding the train up from his hospital shift in London, spent the morning resolved that he would not run. He changed his mind only when he saw the flag on a nearby church stop snapping and hang slack. The annual match between Oxford University and the Amateur Athletic Association was a modest fixture, a few hundred spectators on wooden stands, yet every serious miler alive knew what Bannister meant to attempt. No one had ever run a mile in under four minutes, and physiologists had begun to describe the figure as a wall the body could not pass.

He did not run it alone. Two friends and fellow Olympians, Chris Brasher and Chris Chataway, set the pace so that Bannister could spend his strength on the finish rather than on the clock. Brasher led the first two laps, holding his line even when Bannister shouted at him to go faster, and brought the field through the half mile in 1:58. Chataway took the third lap and carried the pace to three-quarters in 3:00.7. Three hundred yards out, on the back straight, Bannister surged past him into open track, into ground no runner had reached. He flung himself at the tape and collapsed near unconscious, and the crowd's roar swallowed the time before it could be read out. The man with the microphone was Norris McWhirter, who would later found the Guinness Book of Records, and he had the showman's instinct to draw the moment out: a new track, British, European and world record, “in a time of three minutes.” The rest was lost in noise. It was 3:59.4.

Bannister was twenty-five, a medical student at St Mary's Hospital in London who trained on his lunch breaks because he had no time for more. Two years earlier the British press had branded him a failure for finishing fourth in the 1500 metres at the Helsinki Olympics. He answered by treating the mile as a problem to be solved. With Brasher, Chataway, and the Austrian coach Franz Stampfl, he took up interval training, running quarter-miles in repetition until his body learned the rhythm of the pace, and he came to believe that the four minutes were as much a failure of nerve as of legs. The number was round and forbidding, and the world's milers had circled just outside it for nine years, the Swede Gunder Hägg holding the record at 4:01.4 since 1945.

What happened next is the part worth carrying to coffee. The barrier, once crossed, turned out to be made of paper. Forty-six days after Oxford the Australian John Landy ran 3:58 in Finland and took the record away, and by the end of the decade a sub-four mile was a mark a strong club runner could aim at. Today it is the ordinary furniture of any professional 1500-metre field, cleared by high-schoolers. The lesson the milers draw from it is half physical and half mental. The body could always do it; what changed on that Oxford evening was the belief that it could be done.

The two men met only once, that August, at the Empire Games in Vancouver, in a race the press christened the Miracle Mile because both were expected to break four minutes in the same field, and both did. Landy led for most of it, and in the final bend he glanced over his left shoulder to find his rival. Bannister swept past on his right at that exact instant and won. A bronze of the two men frozen at that moment stands in Vancouver, and Landy liked to point out that it is the only statue of an athlete looking the wrong way.

· · ·

He ran competitively only a little longer before leaving the track for medicine, becoming a distinguished neurologist who always said he was prouder of his research on the nervous system than of any race he won. The mile record has since fallen by some sixteen seconds, down to the 3:43.13 the Moroccan Hicham El Guerrouj ran in Rome in 1999. None of that has dimmed the first one. The four-minute mile endures as the model of every limit that looked absolute until a single person stepped through it, and it remains the most famous four minutes in the history of sport.
