The fastest animal on Earth

The Peregrine Falcon

It folds its wings, drops from the sky at better than two hundred miles an hour, and strikes its prey in midair. A bird that once vanished from half a continent now hunts from the ledges of our cities.

A hunting peregrine begins as a speck. It climbs until it is almost lost to the eye, tips over, and falls, folding its wings tight against its body in the near-vertical plunge that falconers call the stoop. For those few seconds it is the fastest animal on the planet. Cornell's ornithologists put the speed of a full stoop near 238 miles an hour, and a falcon released from a light aircraft for one experiment was timed past 240. The whole bird is built around that fall.

The kill comes at the bottom of the dive. The falcon throws its feet forward and lands a single open-footed blow, balled like a fist, that can stun or kill a flying bird outright while the falcon itself rides through untouched, then it wheels back to catch the tumbling body and bites through the neck. The prey is almost always another bird taken on the wing, pigeons and ducks and shorebirds for the most part, though the record runs from a hummingbird to a sandhill crane, some 450 species in North America and perhaps two thousand worldwide. In ordinary flight the peregrine is far less dramatic, cruising near thirty miles an hour. The speed is a weapon it draws only in the dive.

On a ledge the peregrine is about the size of a crow, slate-gray across the back and finely barred below, with pointed wings and a dark hood that drops over the cheeks in two broad wedges, the heavy moustache mark that sets it apart from nearly every other falcon. The female is the larger bird, by as much as a third, and in falconry she alone is the "falcon"; the smaller male is the "tiercel," from an old word for a third. The name carries its own clue. "Peregrine" means pilgrim, or wanderer, and the species is one of the most widely spread birds on Earth, at home on every continent but Antarctica.

All of which made the bird's near-disappearance so frightening. After the Second World War the pesticide DDT spread across the countryside, soaked into the insects that small birds ate, and climbed the food chain into the falcons that ate those birds. It grew more concentrated at every step, and in the peregrine it thinned the shells of the eggs until the parents crushed their own clutches simply by brooding them. By the 1960s the bird was gone from the entire eastern United States, extinct as a breeding species east of the Mississippi, and its collapse was among the cases Rachel Carson set at the center of Silent Spring in 1962.

The comeback is the part worth carrying. In 1970 a Cornell ornithologist named Tom Cade began breeding peregrines in captivity and founded what became the Peregrine Fund; over the next quarter-century his program and others raised and released more than four thousand young birds, often hacking them into the wild from boxes set on towers and city rooftops. The United States banned DDT in 1972, and the falcons returned. They took, of all places, to cities, where a skyscraper ledge stands in for a cliff and the streets below run thick with pigeons, so a bird that had vanished from half a continent now nests on cathedrals and bridges in full public view. In 1999 it came off the endangered-species list.
