Marcus Aurelius

A Roman emperor's private notebook, written in Greek by lamplight at the edge of the empire.

Marcus Aurelius ruled the Roman Empire from 161 to 180 CE, the last of the so-called Five Good Emperors. He inherited a state at its territorial peak and spent most of his reign at war on the German and Sarmatian frontiers, fighting the Marcomanni along the Danube while a plague tore through his armies. He was not by temperament a soldier. He was a student of Stoic philosophy, trained from boyhood by tutors his adoptive father Antoninus Pius had hand-picked, and he disliked the throne. He took it anyway, because his teachers had told him that duty does not consult preference.

The book we read him for, the Meditations, was never meant for us. It carries no title from his hand. The Greek manuscript tradition labels it Τὰ εἰς ἑαυτόν (Ta eis heauton, "Things to himself"), and that is what it is: twelve short books of private exhortations he wrote in army camps, mostly in Greek, the language of philosophy. He addresses no audience. He repeats himself. He scolds himself by name. He reminds himself that he is going to die, that nothing he is anxious about will matter, that the only thing entirely his own is the disposition of his will.

"Begin the morning by saying to thyself, I shall meet with the busybody, the ungrateful, arrogant, deceitful, envious, unsocial. All these things happen to them by reason of their ignorance of what is good and evil." Meditations II.1, tr. George Long

The philosophy underneath is Stoicism in the Roman key: the cosmos is a single rational order, the human mind is a fragment of that order, virtue is the only good, and everything outside the will, including pain, fame, and the length of one's life, is morally indifferent. Marcus is not the first Stoic. He stands at the end of a 500-year tradition that began with Zeno of Citium teaching in the Athenian Stoa around 300 BCE and ran through Cleanthes, Chrysippus, the Roman slave Epictetus whose lectures Marcus had read closely, and Seneca who served and was killed by Nero. What is new in Marcus is the voice. He is not lecturing. He is talking to himself.

The recurring move in the Meditations is to widen the frame. He pulls back from his own anxieties to the city, from the city to the species, from the species to the cosmos, from the cosmos to the indifferent dust the cosmos will eventually grind everything to. Hierocles, an earlier Stoic, had called this oikeiōsis, the doctrine that the self extends outward through concentric circles of concern, family, city, humanity, finally the cosmos itself. Marcus lives inside that diagram. The empire he rules is not, for him, his property. It is a province of a larger thing.

· · ·

He died in 180 CE on campaign, almost certainly at Vindobona, present-day Vienna, probably of the plague that had been thinning his army for a decade. The dying instruction reported by his biographer Cassius Dio was simple. He told the tribune of the watch to go to his son. Then he covered his head and stopped breathing. The succession he had hoped to perfect by raising Commodus carefully went badly. Edward Gibbon would later mark the death of Marcus as the moment Rome began its long decline.

The bronze equestrian statue of him on the Capitoline Hill survived only because medieval Romans mistook it for Constantine and so refused to melt it down. It is the only intact bronze equestrian statue of any Roman emperor, and Michelangelo placed it at the center of his redesign of the Piazza del Campidoglio in the 1530s. The original now lives indoors in the Capitoline Museums; a replica holds the square.

What survived him in print is more important. The Meditations were unknown for centuries, mentioned almost nowhere by ancient writers, rediscovered by a Byzantine scholar around 900 CE, first printed in Zurich in 1559, translated into English by Meric Casaubon in 1634, and have never gone out of print since. They are the founding document of what people now reach for under the banner of "Stoicism": the dichotomy of control, the morning premeditation, the meditation on impermanence, the disposition that what cannot be changed must be borne, and that what can be changed begins inside the mind of the person doing the bearing.
