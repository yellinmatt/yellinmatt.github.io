The Circulation of the Blood

How a court physician overturned fifteen centuries of medicine with arithmetic, finding that the heart moves more blood in an hour than the whole body could make.

Count the blood. That was the move no one had made in fifteen hundred years, and it is the move that settles everything. William Harvey held a beating heart, watched it work, and did arithmetic. The left ventricle holds roughly two ounces. The heart beats around seventy times a minute. Multiply across an hour and the heart pushes out something on the order of five hundred pounds of blood, more than three times the weight of the man it sits inside. No body could make that much blood from food, nor burn it that fast. The same blood had to be going out and coming back, around and around in a circuit.

To see why this was a scandal, you have to know what it replaced. The second-century Greek physician Galen had taught, and every medical school had repeated for fifteen centuries, that blood worked as a one-way tide. The liver cooked food into dark venous blood, which seeped outward through the veins to feed the flesh and was used up where it arrived, like oil burned in a lamp. A separate, brighter arterial blood carried what the ancients called vital spirit out from the heart. The two streams mixed only a little, through tiny pores that Galen said ran across the thick muscular wall between the heart's two sides. Nobody could ever find those pores. Everyone assumed they were simply too small to see.

Harvey was born in Folkestone in 1578 and took his arts degree at Cambridge, and at twenty-one he went to Padua, then the finest medical school in Europe. His teacher there was Hieronymus Fabricius, an anatomist who had noticed something strange in the veins: little flaps, valves, spaced along their length. Fabricius thought they slowed the blood so the lower body would not flood. Harvey kept the observation and threw out the explanation. The valves all faced one way, toward the heart, and a brake has no reason to choose a direction. These were one-way doors.

He carried the puzzle home to London, built a thriving practice, became physician to James I and then to Charles I, and lectured on anatomy for years at the College of Physicians before he set the argument down in print. The book came out in 1628, published in Frankfurt to reach the anatomists of the continent, seventy-two pages on poor paper and thick with printer's errors. Its title runs Exercitatio Anatomica de Motu Cordis et Sanguinis in Animalibus, an anatomical exercise on the motion of the heart and the blood in animals. Everyone shortens it to De Motu Cordis.

The arithmetic told him the blood must circulate. To show how, he reached for the arm. Tie a band tight around the upper arm and the deep arteries keep pumping blood in while the surface veins, pinched shut, cannot drain it; the forearm swells and the veins stand up in knots, each knot a valve. Loosen the band a little, then press a fingertip along a swollen vein toward the hand: the vein stays empty below your finger, because the valve will not let blood run backward. Press the other way, toward the heart, and the blood gives at once. Harvey ran this on his own forearm and on anyone who would sit for it, and the plates he had cut for the book show exactly that, a bound arm, the knotted veins lettered for reference, a finger milking the blood and finding it will travel only one way.

One link was still missing, and Harvey knew it. If blood leaves by the arteries and comes back by the veins, something must join the two far out in the flesh, where the smallest vessels run. He could not see that join; it lay below the reach of the naked eye. So he asserted that it had to be there and left the gap open, trusting the argument to hold the place. The proof arrived in 1661, four years after his death, when the Italian Marcello Malpighi turned one of the new microscopes on a living frog's lung and watched the blood thread through a mesh of vessels too fine to see unaided. He had found the capillaries. The circle Harvey reasoned into existence could at last be traced the whole way round.

“Since all things, both argument and , show that the blood passes through the lungs and heart by the action of the ventricles, and is sent for distribution to all parts of the body … and is by them finally discharged into the and right of the heart, and this in such a … as cannot possibly be supplied by the , and is much greater than can be required for mere purposes of nutrition; it is absolutely necessary to conclude that the blood in the animal body is impelled in a circle, and is in a state of ceaseless motion; that this is the act or function which the heart performs by means of its pulse.”

William Harvey, De Motu Cordis, ch. 14, 1628. Robert Willis translation, 1847.

Harvey is the hinge between old medicine and new for how he got there, even more than for being right. He did not reason from Galen's authority or from what the body ought to do; he measured, tied off, pressed, and counted, and he followed the figures past every textbook on the shelf. The heart became a pump, the body a closed circuit of plumbing, and physiology turned into a science you could do with your own hands. The two ounces and the seventy beats were never a curiosity at the edge of the story. They were the argument, and everyone who had studied a heart for fifteen centuries had simply never thought to multiply.
