The Tobacco Pipe

A briar root, a carved bowl, and four leaves: how to know a tobacco pipe, and how to pack, light, and smoke one well.

The wood

Briar root burl

Carved in

Saint-Claude, France

The draw

Sip, never inhale

A pipe is the slowest way to smoke, and that is rather the point of it. A cigarette is gone in minutes and a cigar fills a room, yet a pipe sits in the hand for the better part of an hour, drawn in patient sips and relit without apology. The whole kit fits in a coat pocket: the pipe itself, a pinch of tobacco, a tamper to press it down, and a match or a soft flame. Almost every pipe worth owning is cut from one wood, and the way that wood won its place is where the subject begins.

That wood is briar, and it is a root rather than a trunk. The burl of Erica arborea, the tree heath, is a swollen woody knot that grows where the root meets the stem of a scrubby Mediterranean shrub, dense and finely grained and full of tannin from the work of holding itself together in thin, rocky ground. Those qualities make it close to ideal for a bowl: it shrugs off heat, it draws moisture out of the smoke, and instead of catching fire it builds a protective crust of carbon that sweetens each bowlful. Briar reached the trade almost by chance. The story told in Saint-Claude, the French alpine town that became the capital of pipe-making, holds that around 1854 a carver came back from Corsica with roots of the shrub and found they made a finer bowl than anything before. Within a generation briar had pushed aside the fragile clay pipe that working men had smoked for centuries.

For all its romance, a pipe is a simple machine with four working parts. The bowl is the cup that holds the fire, and the hollow inside it is the chamber. At the base of the chamber a narrow draught hole opens into the shank, the thick neck of wood running back from the bowl. Into the shank fits the stem, the slim mouthpiece, ending in the flattened bit a smoker holds in his teeth. Air pulled at the bit travels the length of that airway and down through the draught hole to the burning leaf, which is why a bowl packed too tightly will not draw at all, and one packed too loosely burns hot and bites.

Briar rules the trade, but a few other materials each keep a following. Meerschaum is a soft white mineral, sepiolite, mined almost entirely around Eskišehir in Turkey and carved while damp into bowls of remarkable detail, from turbaned sultans to the open jaws of a lion. It smokes cool and dry and does something no wood can: with every bowl the white takes a stain, warming through straw and honey to a deep amber that records years of use in the colour of the pipe. Clay, the oldest material of all, is cheap and gives the purest taste of the leaf, though it cracks easily and grows too hot to hold bare.

Humblest of all is the corncob, long written off as a country pipe and in truth one of the best values in smoking. The Missouri Meerschaum company has pressed them from specially bred cobs since 1869, and General Douglas MacArthur clenched one so often that the homely cob became part of his image.

The one shape everyone pictures, the great curved pipe of Sherlock Holmes, is the rarest of all and very nearly a fiction. It is a calabash, a bowl of meerschaum set into the neck of a curved gourd, and Conan Doyle never put one in Holmes’s mouth; in the stories the detective smokes a clay, an old briar, and a cherrywood. The calabash came later, from the stage. The American actor William Gillette wanted a pipe large enough to be seen from the back rows and balanced enough to stay clenched while he spoke his lines, and the curved gourd answered both needs. Basil Rathbone carried the look into film, and a stage prop became the most famous pipe in the world.

The pipe is only half the craft; the rest is what goes in it. Almost every blend is built from four leaves. Virginia is bright and naturally sweet, the backbone of most tobaccos. Burley is nutty and low in sugar, burns cool, and soaks up added flavour, which makes it the base of the sweet aromatic blends. Latakia, an Oriental leaf cured in smoke over smouldering wood, brings a dark campfire note and is the soul of the English blends. Perique, fermented under pressure in oak barrels in a single parish of Louisiana, runs so strong it is used by the pinch, a peppery seasoning and never the main leaf. The great divide on a tobacconist’s shelf falls between English blends, led by smoky Latakia and sold without added flavour, and aromatics, cased with flavours like vanilla or cherry that scent the room more than the palate.

Smoking it well comes down to patience. Pack the bowl in three goes, an old rule remembered as child, woman, man: first trickle the tobacco in loosely until it overflows and press with a child’s gentleness, then refill and tamp with a firmer hand, then top it off and press once more with real weight, until the leaf sits just below the rim. A test draw on the unlit pipe should pull like sipping water through a straw; if it pulls harder than that, empty it and pack again. Light it twice. The first pass, the charring light, wets and swells the leaf and is gently tamped flat; the second is the light that lasts. Then sip, slowly, an inch of breath at a time, since a pipe smoked hard turns harsh and stings the tongue while a pipe sipped stays cool and sweet down to the heel. The smoke is never drawn into the lungs. The whole pleasure is in taking your time.

How to do it

From bare pipe to first cool sip, in five steps.

01

Pick the pipe

A briar is the forgiving place to start: cool, durable, cheap enough to learn on.

  • ShapeA straight billiard sits easy in the hand and the teeth
  • SourceA clean used pipe or an honest maker like Savinelli or Peterson
  • SkipThe novelty calabash; buy that once the habit sticks

02

Choose the tobacco

Pick one blend and learn it before chasing the next.

  • EasyA mild aromatic for a sweet, room-friendly first bowl
  • ClassicA light English blend to meet smoky Latakia
  • BuyAn ounce loose from a tobacconist, not a wall of tins

03

Pack it

Child, woman, man: three fills, each pressed a little firmer.

  • FillTrickle in loose to overflowing, press with a child’s touch
  • RefillTop up and tamp firmer, then once more with real weight
  • TestDraw cold; it should pull like a straw, no harder

04

Light it

Two lights, not one: the char wakes the leaf, the second one holds.

  • CharPass the flame over the top while drawing, then tamp the rise
  • LightLight again and settle it into an even burn
  • HeatA soft flame or a wooden match, kept off the rim

05

Smoke it

Slow and cool is the whole game; let it go out before you force it.

  • SipShort, gentle draws; never pull the smoke into the lungs
  • TendTamp the ash now and then to keep the burn even
  • RestIf it heats up, set it down; a relit pipe beats a scorched tongue

Give the first few bowls the patience to learn the pack and the angle of the flame, and the pipe rewards it. An hour opens up in the day that belongs to nothing else: a small ritual of wood and leaf and fire that a hundred and fifty years of smokers have found worth the trouble of doing slowly.
