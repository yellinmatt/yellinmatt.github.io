Neoclassicism

Europe rediscovers the Greeks and Romans, 1750 to 1830.

Neoclassicism is the European art and architecture of roughly 1750 to 1830 that reached back past the Renaissance to the Greeks and Romans themselves. It is the disciplined counterweight to the powdered-wig Rococo it replaced. Where Boucher and Fragonard painted shepherdesses on pink satin swings, Neoclassicism wanted civic virtue, line over color, and what Johann Joachim Winckelmann famously called "noble simplicity and sedate grandeur."

The movement had two triggers. The first was archaeological. Excavations at Herculaneum began in 1738 and at Pompeii in 1748, and for the first time Europeans could read the actual textures, frescoes, and dinner plates of Roman daily life rather than the idealized Roman myth handed down by the Renaissance. The second was theoretical. Winckelmann, a German archaeologist working in Rome, published Reflections on the Painting and Sculpture of the Greeks in 1755 and, nine years later, the first true history of ancient art. He argued that Greek sculpture had captured "ideal beauties, brain-born images" superior to nature itself, and he separated Greek from Roman art for the first time. Painters and architects across Europe read him and reoriented.

· · ·

The touchstone painting is Jacques-Louis David's Oath of the Horatii, painted in Rome in 1784 and exhibited at the Paris Salon the following year. Three brothers stand stretched under a Roman arch, arms raised in a rigid salute, swearing to their father that they will fight and die for Rome against the rival city of Alba Longa. Their sisters, one of whom is engaged to a man on the other side, collapse in grief on the right. Three arches frame three groups. The men are all sharp angles and parallel verticals. The women are curves. Stoic duty on one side of the canvas, private feeling on the other, and David lets the viewer know which side wins.

Commissioned by a royal official to celebrate loyalty to the state, the painting landed in a Paris four years from revolution. The public read it as a call to republican sacrifice. David himself was apolitical in 1784, then radicalized fast. By 1793 he had voted to execute Louis XVI, painted the dead Marat half-slumped in his bath, and become the official artist of the new regime. Under Napoleon he kept the post and painted the coronation. The Neoclassical style was never just a style. It carried an argument about how a citizen should behave.

· · ·

David is the headline, but the movement ran Europe-wide. Antonio Canova in Rome cut marbles, including the impossibly tender Psyche Revived by Cupid's Kiss finished in 1793, that aimed for the precise sculptural ideal Winckelmann had described. Bertel Thorvaldsen, a Dane in Rome, did the same with a colder, more austere hand. Jean-Antoine Houdon modeled portrait busts of Voltaire and Benjamin Franklin and a full-length marble George Washington that still stands in the Virginia State Capitol. In architecture, Jacques-Germain Soufflot raised the Panthéon in Paris, Karl Friedrich Schinkel built the Altes Museum in Berlin, and Thomas Jefferson designed Monticello and the University of Virginia rotunda by sitting with Palladio's Four Books and a measured drawing of the Maison Carrée at Nîmes. The new American republic chose its visual grammar deliberately: marble columns and pediments, not gilt and curlicues, to signal that it intended to be Rome and Athens rather than Versailles.

By 1830 Romanticism had broken Neoclassicism's hold on serious painting. The Romantic generation wanted color, motion, feeling, the wreck of the Medusa rather than the oath of the Horatii. But the architectural language never left. Almost every state capitol, every great museum portico, every bank facade with a pediment and columns is still speaking the grammar Winckelmann taught Europe to read.
