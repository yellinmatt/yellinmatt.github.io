Physical & Self-Defense · The Gentle Way

Judo

How a slight schoolteacher turned the samurai art of jujutsu into judo, a way of using a bigger man's force against him, and carried it from a Tokyo temple to the Olympic Games.

In 1882 a slight, bookish young man of twenty-one opened a school on a dozen borrowed mats in a Tokyo temple. He stood five foot two and weighed under a hundred pounds, and he had taken up jujitsu, the unarmed fighting of the samurai, not out of any hunger for glory but because he was tired of being handled by bigger boys. Jigorō Kanō had studied it earnestly enough to see a pattern in it that his teachers had felt but never named. He called his school the Kōdōkan, the place for teaching the way, and the art he was drawing out of the old jujitsu he called judo, from jū, gentle or yielding, and dō, the way.

Gentle here names a piece of mechanics. Kanō had trained in two jujitsu schools, Tenjin-shin'yō-ryū and Kitō-ryū, and in sorting their throws he found the thing that let a small man put a large one on the floor. You do not shove a heavier body over; you first take its balance. Break a standing man's posture, draw him up onto his toes or tip him back onto his heels, and for one moment his weight rests on nothing and can be steered. Kanō made this the opening move of every throw and gave it a name, kuzushi, the breaking of balance, and he taught the throw as three beats: kuzushi to unbalance, tsukuri to fit your own body under his, kake to finish. One principle governed the whole art, seiryoku zen'yō, the best use of energy. Spend the least force that will do the job and borrow the rest from the other man.

Where the older jujitsu schools kept scrolls of techniques meant to maim, Kanō sifted. He held on to the throws, pins, chokes, and joint locks that a student could practice at full effort on a friend without breaking him, and he set aside the eye-gouges and finger-snaps that could only be rehearsed at half speed. That one decision remade the training, because it let his students spar all-out every day against a resisting partner, a practice he called randori and the fastest way anyone has found to grow skilled at a physical art. He graded the throws he kept into a teaching syllabus, the gokyō, and ranked his pupils by a ladder he seems to have devised himself, a coloured sash climbing from white to black with numbered dan degrees above it, the belt system that boxing gyms and every later martial art would borrow from him.

The proof came in a contest. Judo was one small school among dozens of established jujitsu dōjō, and the doubters were many. Around 1886 the Tokyo police, wanting to settle which art to teach their officers, held a tournament, and Kanō's Kōdōkan team met the leading jujitsu school of the day and beat it decisively. After that the argument was over. The police took up judo, the schools followed, and within a generation it was taught in Japanese classrooms as physical education, which is what Kanō, a career educator who rose to run Tokyo's college for training teachers, had wanted from the start. The throwing was a means to him. The end was seiryoku zen'yō carried off the mat into a whole life, alongside the art's second motto, jita kyōei, mutual welfare and benefit, on the conviction that no one truly improves at another man's expense.

Watch Shintaro Higashi, judo coach

Watch how the throw is set up before it is thrown: the hands and the step draw the partner off his base, so that the finish takes almost no strength. That first pull is kuzushi, and it is the whole secret of the gentle way.

Kanō spent his last decades sending it out into the world. In 1909 he became the first Asian member of the International Olympic Committee, led Japan's first Olympic team to Stockholm in 1912, and pressed for years to bring the Games to Tokyo, a wish granted in 1938, only days before he died at seventy-seven of pneumonia aboard a ship crossing the Pacific toward home. He did not live to see judo become an Olympic sport, which happened at the Tokyo Games finally held in 1964. Nor did he see the oddest branch of his tree. One of his strongest fighters, Mitsuyo Maeda, carried judo around the globe as a travelling prizefighter and settled in Belém, in northern Brazil, where the art he taught there was reworked by the Gracie family into Brazilian jiu-jitsu, the ground fighting that a century on would remake combat sport in the cages of the UFC.

The thing worth carrying from Kanō is the lesson he found as a small boy losing fights, that leverage beats strength once you understand balance. A man twice your size stands on the same two feet you do, and the instant you take those feet from under him he is only weight, and weight can be steered. That is the whole of the gentle way, and it is why the first thing a judo teacher shows a beginner is not a throw at all but how to fall.
