The Wealth of Nations

The book that founded modern economics begins where no one would think to look for it: ten men in a workshop, making pins.

In a small workshop somewhere in Britain, Adam Smith watched ten men make pins, and not one of them made a whole pin. One drew out the wire, a second straightened it, a third cut it, a fourth ground the point, and two or three more shaped the head and fixed it on and whitened the result, until the making of a single pin had been broken into about eighteen distinct operations carried out by distinct hands. A man working the trade alone, untrained and shifting from one task to the next, might manage one pin in a day and certainly not twenty. The ten together, each repeating his one motion, turned out upwards of forty-eight thousand. Smith had found the opening image of his book, and with it modern economics begins.

The pin is small on purpose. Smith calls pin-making a trifling manufacture, chosen because the whole of it fits under one roof and one glance, where the same division of labour runs invisibly through every great trade. It is the reason, he argued, that a well-run country grows productive enough for its plenty to reach the lowest ranks of the people. Here was the quiet revolution of the book. His mercantilist contemporaries measured a nation’s wealth by the gold heaped in its sovereign’s vault and the favourable balance of its trade. Smith measured it by the yearly flow of goods that a nation’s labour produced, and the way to swell that flow was to let labour specialize and then exchange its surplus freely.

What makes a man content to spend his life grinding pin-points, trusting that the baker will have bread to sell him when he brings his wages to the counter? Not the baker’s goodwill. Smith located the engine of the whole system in something plainer and more dependable than virtue, and he set it down in the most quoted sentence he ever wrote.

Give me that which I want, and you shall have this which you want, is the meaning of every such offer; and it is in this manner that we obtain from one another the far greater part of those which we stand in need of. It is not from the of the butcher, the brewer, or the baker that we expect our dinner, but from their regard to their own interest. We address ourselves, not to their humanity but to their , and never talk to them of our own necessities but of their advantages.

Adam Smith, An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations, Book I, Chapter 2 (1776).

From that ordinary motive Smith drew his most famous and most misread image. A merchant who keeps his capital at home for his own security and directs it toward his own gain, intending only his own profit, is led, Smith writes, as if by an invisible hand to promote an end that was no part of his intention, the prosperity of the society around him. The phrase appears exactly once in the whole of the Wealth of Nations, a passing metaphor rather than a banner. Smith was no apostle of greed. Seventeen years earlier, in The Theory of Moral Sentiments of 1759, the same professor of moral philosophy had grounded human conduct in sympathy, our capacity to feel with one another. The invisible hand is a figure for a mechanism: under competition, he observed, the pursuit of private interest can be turned to the public good without anyone setting out to serve it, and that is all he claimed for it.

· · ·

Smith made an unlikely founder for the science of commerce. Born in the port of Kirkcaldy on the Fife coast in 1723 and raised by his widowed mother, he became a professor of moral philosophy at Glasgow, famously so absent-minded that he once walked miles in a daydream, still in his dressing gown. He retired to his mother’s house and spent the better part of a decade on a single book. It appeared on 9 March 1776, a few months before a cluster of colonies across the Atlantic declared their independence, and the first edition sold out in six months. For all his trust in the open market, Smith kept a cold eye on the men who worked it: people of the same trade, he warned, seldom meet together even for merriment and diversion but the conversation ends in a conspiracy against the public, or some contrivance to raise prices. Two and a half centuries on, his profile and his pin factory rode together on the British twenty-pound note from 2007 to 2020, the first Scot the Bank of England ever printed on its currency, the workshop where the argument began set beside the man who saw what it meant.
