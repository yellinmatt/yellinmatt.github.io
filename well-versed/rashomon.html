Akira Kurosawa, Daiei Studios, Japan, 1950

Rashomon

The film that staged four contradictory truths, won the Venice Golden Lion, and gave the language a word for what happens when witnesses cannot agree.

Three men shelter from a downpour under a ruined wooden gate at the southern edge of Kyoto. A woodcutter speaks first: three days ago he found a samurai dead in the grove north of the city, and the trial he just attended has left him more unsettled than the corpse. A monk nods; he saw a man on horseback and a woman in a carriage on that road, minutes before. The commoner pulling roof planks for firewood wants to know what the court decided.

What the court decided is the film's entire subject. Four people give four accounts of the same event: the killing of a samurai named Kanazawa and the assault on his wife in a forest clearing. Tajomaru (Toshiro Mifune), a notorious bandit arrested while riding Kanazawa's horse, says he challenged the husband to an honorable duel and killed him in fair combat, sword against sword, after the wife herself implored him to. Masako (Machiko Kyo), testifying at the police court, says she stabbed her husband in a fugue of shame after the assault and he looked at her with cold contempt. Kanazawa, summoned through a medium, insists he took his own life with a dagger he found near his bound hands, while Masako negotiated with Tajomaru to kill him. The woodcutter, who kept silent at the trial, produces a fourth account at the gate: a cowardly, squalid scuffle in which Tajomaru and Kanazawa both behaved badly, the wife goaded them on, and no one ends up looking noble.

None of the four accounts is straightforwardly a lie. Each is consistent with what the person giving it needed to believe. Tajomaru wants to be remembered as dangerous and sexually magnetic, not as a cornered thief who panicked. Masako needs her conduct read as tragic rather than calculating. Kanazawa, dead, finds it more tolerable to have chosen his own death than to have been killed by a bandit at his wife’s instruction. The woodcutter withheld his version at trial because he stole a valuable dagger from the scene, which the film saves for its final minutes. The script, by Shinobu Hashimoto from two Ryunosuke Akutagawa stories, never supplies a fifth account that arbitrates between the others. The structure closes without a verdict, and the question it leaves is not who killed Kanazawa but what self-interest does to memory in the moment a person tells their story.

Watch the forest flashback sequences in particular: Kazuo Miyagawa, the cinematographer, pointed the camera directly at the sun through the forest canopy, using the dappled shadows and bursts of direct light to give the grove a hallucinatory, fever-dream quality that no courtroom scene could match. The contrast between the drenched gate and the bright, almost overexposed forest is doing structural work: neither space fully belongs to ordinary reality.

Miyagawa and Kurosawa built the forest scenes from scratch at Daiei’s Kyoto studio. The Rashomon gate itself, which appears at the film’s opening and close and gave Akutagawa’s 1915 short story its title, was reconstructed on a backlot, then soaked by hired fire engines for every shot. The original gate stood at the southern exit from Heian-period Kyoto, was built in 789 CE, and had fallen into such ruin by the end of that era that it became, according to the sources Akutagawa drew on, a dumping ground for the city’s dead and unwanted. Kurosawa later wrote that he had wanted to surround the set with black-market stalls of the kind crammed into bombed-out Tokyo during the postwar occupation. Daiei’s budget, roughly $140,000, twice the usual for a Japanese studio production, would not stretch that far.

The film was entered at Venice in 1951 without Kurosawa’s knowledge. Daiei, the studio, nominated it for the Golden Lion and did not bother to notify the director. When Rashomon won, Kurosawa was in the middle of a difficult production on The Idiot and had not expected word from Italy. The Golden Lion brought RKO to Daiei; the film opened in New York in December 1951, the first Japanese production to receive a wide release from a major American studio, and picked up an honorary Academy Award for best foreign language film in 1952. Japanese critics were not grateful. The domestic consensus held that the prize reflected Western fascination with exotic Japan rather than cinematic achievement. “These two prizes were simply reflections of Westerners’ curiosity and taste for Oriental exoticism,” Kurosawa wrote in his 1983 memoir, Something Like an Autobiography. “Which struck me then, and now, as terrible.”

· · ·

The term “the Rashomon effect” had entered academic psychology, legal scholarship, and journalism by the late twentieth century, used to describe the documented pattern of incompatible witness accounts arising not from deliberate deception but from the way self-interest and memory work together. The 2022 Sight & Sound poll, the most authoritative map of the cinema canon, places Rashomon among the films that defined what the medium could do with the gap between event and narration. Quentin Tarantino, Bryan Singer, and Zhang Yimou have all adapted the structure. The four witnesses and the ruined gate have proven to be one of the most portable ideas the cinema produced in its first century.
