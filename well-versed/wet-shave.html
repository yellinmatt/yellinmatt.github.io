The Wet Shave

A single blade, a brush, and a bowl of lather: the slow shave your grandfather knew, and why a hundred-year-old design still beats the five-blade cartridge.

Blade angle

≈30° to the skin

The method

Three light passes

Pressure

The razor’s own weight

The drugstore aisle sells the shave as a problem of more: a fourth blade, then a fifth, a lubricating strip, a battery to make the handle buzz. The traditional wet shave goes the other way. It uses one blade, drawn slowly over a face that has been softened with hot water and lathered with a brush, and it costs almost nothing each morning. Once the hand learns it, it leaves the skin smoother than any cartridge and without the rash of red bumps that several blades dragging across the same stroke tend to raise. The whole kit is three things: a razor, a brush, and a soap or cream.

The razor most people picture is the double-edged safety razor, a heavy metal handle that clamps a thin replaceable blade behind a protective guard, and its story corrects a common mistake. King Camp Gillette did not invent the safety razor. The straight razor, the open blade a barber strops on leather, had shaved men for centuries, and the idea of guarding that blade so it could not bite too deep was already old: a Frenchman, Jean-Jacques Perret, fitted a wooden guard to a straight razor around 1770, and in 1880 the Kampfe brothers of New York sold the first tool actually marketed as a “safety razor.” What Gillette and his engineer William Nickerson patented in 1904 was narrower and far more lucrative: a blade so thin and cheap it could be stamped from sheet steel, used a few times, and thrown away.

That throwaway blade carried the whole idea. Gillette could sell the handle cheaply, even at a loss, because the money lay in the blades a man would keep buying for the rest of his life, the “razor and blades” model that now names the economics of everything from printers to game consoles. When the United States entered the First World War it issued Gillette shaving kits to its soldiers, and a generation came home owning a razor and the daily habit of using it.

Most men have never met the brush, and it does more than spread cream around. Swirled in soap, a brush of badger or boar hair whips warm water and air into a dense lather, lifts each whisker up and away from the skin, and lays a film of slickness under the blade. Begin from a hot shower or a hot towel, because heat and water are what actually soften a beard; a whisker held wet for a couple of minutes cuts with something close to the ease of warm wax.

Two habits separate a clean shave from a bloody one, and both run against instinct. The first is the angle. The blade wants to sit at about thirty degrees to the skin, so you rest the flat top of the razor head on your cheek with the handle held level, then lower the handle slowly until the edge just begins to bite. The second is pressure, or the want of it. The weight of the razor alone is enough to cut, so the hand only guides and never pushes; many shavers grip the very end of the handle to break themselves of the urge to bear down. Press, and you trade whiskers for nicks.

The closeness is built up in light passes, each taking the stubble down a little. The first goes with the grain, the direction the hair lies, which you find by rubbing a day’s growth with the back of a finger and feeling which way is smooth. Re-lather, and the second pass goes across the grain. Only for the closest finish, and only once the skin is used to the routine, does a third pass go against the grain. Each pass shortens the whisker by about a third, and the result is a face shaved smooth with a fraction of the irritation one hard scrape would leave behind.

How to do it

The sequence is the same whether you shave from a vintage Gillette or a new Merkur. Five movements, in order.

01

Prep the beard

Soften the whiskers with heat and water before the blade touches them.

  • BestShave straight out of a hot shower, when the beard is fully hydrated
  • OrHold a hot, wet towel to the face for a minute or two
  • WhyWarm water, not the blade, does the softening

02

Build the lather

Whip soap and water into a thick cream with the brush.

  • LoadSwirl a wet brush over the soap puck or a dab of cream
  • WhipWork it in a mug or on the face until it turns thick and glossy
  • AimPaint up under the whiskers, not merely over the top of them

03

Set the angle

About thirty degrees of blade to skin, found the same way every time.

  • Find itHead flat on the cheek, handle level, then drop until the edge bites
  • Hold itGrip the end of the handle to fight the urge to press down
  • Trust itThe razor’s own weight is the only pressure you need

04

Make the passes

Reduce the beard in stages rather than removing it in one stroke.

  • FirstWith the grain, the way the hair lies
  • ThenRe-lather and go across the grain
  • LastAgainst the grain only for the closest shave, once the skin is used to it

05

Finish

Close the skin, soothe it, and look after the blade.

  • RinseCold water to calm and tighten the skin
  • TreatA splash of aftershave or a balm to settle any sting
  • KeepDry the blade after use; a dry edge lasts far longer than a wet one

None of this takes longer than a few minutes once the hand knows the way. Keep a razor, a brush, and a cake of soap by the sink, give the first week the patience to learn the angle, and the morning scrape you used to hurry through turns into a small daily craft, one that costs a few cents a shave and leaves the skin better than the expensive way ever did.
