The Handshake

Old enough to seal an Assyrian treaty and tender enough to mark a Greek farewell, the plainest of greetings still settles what a stranger makes of you in the space of a few seconds.

Two strangers meet, their right hands come up almost without instruction, and for two or three seconds palm presses palm. By the time the hands drop, each has formed a quiet opinion of the other. The handshake is the standard greeting across most of the Western world, and one of the very few that ask two people to touch at all. In that brief contact it does a surprising amount of work.

The gesture reaches back far past the boardroom. The oldest known image of a handshake was carved in Assyria in the ninth century BC, on the stone base of a royal throne: two kings, Shalmaneser III of Assyria and Marduk-zakir-shumi of Babylon, lock right hands beneath a fringed canopy to mark an alliance, made after the Assyrian had helped put the Babylonian back on his throne. Why the right hand became the hand of greeting is guessed at more than it is known. One old explanation is that an open right hand shows it holds no weapon; another, harder to wave away, is that two hands clasped make a promise physical, a bond you can feel being sealed.

The Greeks gave it a name and a depth of feeling. They called it dexiosis, the giving of the right hand, and by the fifth century BC it was a fixed motif in their art. After about 430 BC it became the dominant image on Athenian grave reliefs, where a seated figure and a standing one reach out and clasp hands across the boundary between the living and the dead. The marble above, carved near 340 BC for a woman named Euandria and a man named Thraseas, holds exactly that moment. The same gesture that greeted a guest and sealed a treaty also served as the last goodbye, one motion stretched across the whole arc of a relationship.

Rome kept the custom and ritualized it. The dextrarum iunctio, the joining of right hands, stood at the center of a Roman wedding, and a coin struck in AD 97 set two joined hands beside the name of Concordia, the goddess of accord. Much later the handshake picked up a democratic charge as well. When seventeenth-century Quakers refused to bow or lift their hats to anyone's rank, they offered the same plain handshake to everyone they met, turning a greeting into a small statement that no man stood above another.

· · ·

For all that history, the thing still comes down to a few seconds that are easy to botch. The Emily Post Institute, the steadiest voice in American manners since 1922, reduces the good handshake to a short drill: grip with about the pressure you would use to turn a doorknob, fit the crook of your thumb against the crook of theirs, give two or three small pumps, hold the other person's eye, and keep the hand dry. Two failures are notorious. One is the limp hand that lies in yours and does nothing; the other is the knuckle-grinder who mistakes force for warmth.

ReadEmily Post, Etiquette, 1922

“A handshake often creates a feeling of liking or of irritation between two strangers. Who does not dislike a ‘boneless’ hand extended as though it were a spray of seaweed, or a miniature boiled pudding? It is equally annoying to have one's hand clutched aloft in grotesque affectation and shaken violently sideways, as though it were being used to clean a spot out of the atmosphere.”

“The proper handshake is made briefly; but there should be a feeling of strength and warmth in the clasp, and, as in bowing, one should at the same time look into the countenance of the person whose hand one takes.”

Emily Post, Etiquette in Society, in Business, in Politics and at Home, 1922, ch. III.

Even now the gesture may be doing more than we notice. A 2015 study at Israel's Weizmann Institute found that people often raise a just-shaken hand toward the face soon after, and argued that the handshake quietly samples a stranger's chemistry, a polite survival of the way most animals greet by scent. The handshake lasts because it is efficient. In a few seconds it takes a stranger's measure, closes a bargain, or marks a parting, and it repays the small attention of being done with care.
