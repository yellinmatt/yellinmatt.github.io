Medicine · The Antibiotic Age

Penicillin

The first antibiotic began as a contaminated dish a bacteriologist nearly overlooked, and it took a war and three countries to turn the accident into a medicine.

On the third of September 1928, Alexander Fleming came back from a month away to a bench stacked with the culture plates he had left behind in his laboratory at St Mary's Hospital in London. He was a bacteriologist, and the plates were seeded with staphylococcus, the round bacterium behind boils and sore throats and lethal blood poisoning. One plate had gone wrong. A blob of blue-green mould had drifted in and taken hold, and in a clear halo all around it the staphylococcus had simply dissolved. Most people would have rinsed the dish and started over. Fleming looked closer, and saw that the mould was killing the bacteria.

He grew the intruder in pure culture, found it belonged to the genus Penicillium, and named the substance it gave off penicillin. Tested against the common pathogens, the mould juice was astonishing: diluted a thousand times over it still stopped staphylococcus cold, and it left the body's white blood cells untouched, which the harsh antiseptics of the day never managed. Fleming was honest about the luck of it. “My only merit,” he said later, “is that I did not neglect the observation.” He had trained as a bacteriologist rather than a chemist, though, and penicillin proved maddeningly fragile; he and his assistants could never concentrate the stuff into a stable, usable form. He published his findings in 1929, returned to the subject once or twice, and let it lapse. For a decade the most important drug of the century sat in a journal almost no one read.

The rescue came eleven years later, from Oxford. At the Sir William Dunn School of Pathology, Howard Florey, an Australian pathologist, and Ernst Chain, a German-Jewish biochemist who had fled Hitler's Germany, were combing the literature for substances that kill bacteria when they stopped at Fleming's forgotten paper. They had the chemistry he had lacked. Their colleague Norman Heatley rigged an ingenious extraction that coaxed the delicate penicillin out of gallons of mould broth, and the Dunn School became a strange sort of factory, the mould fermenting in baths and bedpans and milk churns, tended by a crew of women hired at two pounds a week. In May 1940 Florey ran the experiment that settled the matter. Eight mice were injected with a lethal dose of streptococcus; four of them also got penicillin. By the next morning the four treated mice were alive and the four untreated ones were dead.

In February 1941 the team found its first human patient. Albert Alexander, a forty-three-year-old Oxford policeman, was dying of a raging infection that had spread from a small facial wound into his eyes and lungs (the account handed down has him scratched while pruning roses, a detail later historians doubt). Injected with the Oxford penicillin, he began within days a startling recovery. Then the supply gave out. So little of the drug existed that the team filtered it back out of the patient's own urine and re-injected it, but the trickle could not keep pace with the disease. Alexander relapsed and died on the fifteenth of March. The drug had worked. There was simply not enough of it, and there would not be until someone could make it by the ton.

· · ·

Britain's chemical industry was swallowed whole by the war, so in the summer of 1941 Florey and Heatley flew to the United States to find anyone who could make penicillin at scale. They were sent to a government laboratory in Peoria, Illinois, whose specialty was fermentation, and the Americans broke the problem open. Swapping the sugar in the growing medium for corn-steep liquor, a cheap by-product of milling, lifted the yield tenfold. Growing the mould deep inside aerated tanks instead of across the surface of a tray lifted it again. And a worldwide hunt for a more productive strain ended, with some irony, at a Peoria fruit market, on a mouldy cantaloupe whose Penicillium outyielded every sample mailed in from around the globe. Output climbed from one-litre flasks to ten-thousand-gallon tanks; by the Normandy landings of June 1944 there was enough penicillin to treat every Allied soldier who needed it.

“The time may come when penicillin can be bought by anyone in the shops. Then there is the danger that the ignorant man may easily underdose himself and by exposing his microbes to non-lethal quantities of the drug make them resistant.”

“Moral: If you use penicillin, use enough.”

Alexander Fleming, Nobel Lecture, 11 December 1945.

In 1945 the Nobel Prize in Medicine went jointly to Fleming, Florey, and Chain. Heatley, whose hands had made the first usable drug, was left off it; Oxford gave him an honorary doctorate of medicine half a century later, the first in its history. Fleming spent part of his Nobel lecture on a warning that has only sharpened with time, that bacteria starved of a full dose of penicillin learn to shrug it off, and that careless use would breed microbes the drug could no longer touch. He had named the central danger of the antibiotic age in the same hour the world honoured him for opening it.
