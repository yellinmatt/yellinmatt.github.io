Basingstoke to the Western Front, 1879–1918

The Trench Coat

The most familiar coat in the world was cut for one war, and it still carries the kit list: the gun flap, the D-rings, the buckled storm cuffs.

Look closely at a classic trench coat and you are reading a kit list from the Western Front. Flat straps sit across the shoulders, the epaulettes, where an officer once buttoned his rank or slung a pair of gloves. A square flap hangs loose over the right breast, the gun flap, cut to pad a rifle butt and to throw rain clear of the chest. The belt rides the waist with small D-shaped rings hanging off it, meant for a map case or a sword. The cuffs cinch with their own little straps, so that water would not run down a raised forearm to a pair of binoculars. None of it was decoration. Every buckle and flap began as an answer to mud, weather, and the load an officer carried, and the coat keeps the answers a century after the questions went away.

The cloth came first. In 1879 Thomas Burberry, a draper who had opened his shop in Basingstoke in 1856 at the age of twenty-one, worked out how to make a fabric shed rain without rubberizing it. A rubber coat kept the rain out and the sweat in, and the man inside it cooked. Burberry took his idea from the lanolin-greased smocks that Hampshire shepherds wore in the field, and proofed the wool yarn before weaving it rather than coating the finished cloth. The result was gabardine, a worsted woven so tightly that rain ran off the surface while air still passed through.

He patented gabardine in 1888, and within a decade it was dressing the men who needed weatherproofing most. Roald Amundsen wore Burberry gabardine to the South Pole; Ernest Shackleton took it into the Antarctic. Closer to home, the firm sold gabardine suits to anglers, motorists, and shooting parties, advertising a cloth on which rain ran off like dew from a leaf. The garment that became a soldier's was first a sportsman's, sold to keep an Edwardian gentleman dry on a riverbank.

Burberry submitted a design for an officer's raincoat to the War Office in 1901, and in 1912 patented the Tielocken, a wrap coat closed by a single strap and buckle that was the trench coat's direct forerunner. When war came, the standard issue was a serge greatcoat that soaked through and dragged a man down with its own wet weight. Officers, who bought their own kit, wanted something lighter that stayed dry, and the gabardine raincoat answered. The men in the line gave it the name that stuck. A London rival, Aquascutum, presses a claim of its own reaching to the 1850s, and no single house truly invented the garment; the trenches simply finished a design both had been circling for years.

It was an officer's coat in the strictest sense. Trench coats were optional kit, bought privately by officers and senior warrant officers, and other ranks were not permitted to wear them at all, so the coat carried rank as plainly as a pip on the shoulder. Its famously generous cut had a reason too: it was sized to go over the British Warm, the short wool coat an officer wore in real cold, so that one waterproof layer could cover both. When the war ended, men carried their coats home, and the garment shed the army while keeping its bearing.

Hollywood did the rest. Humphrey Bogart turned up the collar of one in the closing rain of Casablanca; Marlene Dietrich wore one through occupied Berlin in A Foreign Affair; Peter Sellers buttoned Inspector Clouseau into one. The trench passed to the detective, the spy, the foreign correspondent, anyone whose work meant standing in the weather looking like they knew something. A coat built to keep an officer dry in Flanders became the most quietly authoritative thing a civilian can wear in the rain.
