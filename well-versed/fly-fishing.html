Fly Fishing

The fly weighs almost nothing, so the angler casts the line itself, and a whole contemplative art unrolls from that one fact.

Everything strange about fly fishing follows from one small problem. The lure at the end of the line, a hook dressed in a pinch of feather and thread, weighs next to nothing, and you cannot throw a thing that has no weight. So the angler throws the line instead. A fly line is thick and heavy on purpose, and the long limber rod is a spring: you sweep it back until the line straightens behind you and bends the rod under its own moving mass, then drive it forward, and the rod unloads and rolls the line out ahead in a tight travelling loop that carries the weightless fly to the water. A spin fisher does the opposite, letting a heavy lure pull thin line off a reel. Learn to feel the rod load and the whole rhythm clicks into place, which is most of why people who take it up rarely put it down.

At the business end is the fly, and the craft of it is imitation. A trout eats insects, so the fly is tied to look and behave like the insect a trout is eating right now. Flies come in three great families. A dry fly floats on the surface film and copies the winged adult riding the current, the classic and the prettiest to fish because you watch the take. A nymph sinks and copies the same insect in its underwater larval stage, which is where trout do most of their feeding, out of sight. A streamer is bigger, tied to suggest a small fish or a leech, and stripped back through the water to draw a chase. The whole game hangs on a phrase, match the hatch: when a particular insect is emerging, you tie on the fly that mimics its size and color, and the fish that ignored everything else turns and eats.

Choosing the fly is the easy half. The hard half is reading the water, and it is the skill that separates the season-one beginner from the lifelong angler. A trout wants to eat without working, so it holds where the current delivers food to its mouth at the lowest cost: in the seam where fast water runs alongside slow, along the foam line that gathers drifting insects, in the tail of a pool, in the soft pocket behind a boulder. Rivers repeat the same three units, and each one tells you something. A riffle is shallow and broken, churning in oxygen and hatching bugs. A run is the deeper, smoother channel below it where the big fish lie. A pool is the slow deep water where they rest. You can teach a decent cast in an afternoon; learning to look at moving water and know where the fish is takes years, and never quite finishes.

The sport carries a long literary pedigree, which is part of what a fly fisher is buying into. The first printed instruction in English is the Treatyse of Fysshynge with an Angle, run off by Wynkyn de Worde in 1496, already listing artificial fly dressings tied to the month; it is credited to Dame Juliana Berners, a prioress near St. Albans, though modern scholars doubt she existed at all, her name surviving in three spellings and no records. The book everyone actually names is Izaak Walton’s The Compleat Angler of 1653, a gentle dialogue about fishing and the good life that became one of the most reprinted works in English, outsold across the centuries only by the Bible, Shakespeare, and Pilgrim’s Progress. Walton himself fished mostly with bait; the fly-fishing chapters were added by his friend Charles Cotton in 1676, the first real treatise on the fly in the language.

· · ·

That reputation for stillness reached the modern American mind through one book and one film. Norman Maclean’s 1976 novella A River Runs Through It, set on the Blackfoot in Montana, opens on the line every fly fisher knows by heart, that in his family there was no clear line between religion and fly fishing, and closes on another, that he is haunted by waters. Robert Redford filmed it in 1992 with Brad Pitt as the doomed younger brother, and in the two years after its release the fly-fishing industry more than doubled in size. The sport that boom helped mainstream now runs largely on catch and release, the fish slipped back into the current so the whole thing can happen again. Which is the quiet answer to why a grown adult stands waist-deep in cold water waving a stick: the point was never really the fish.
