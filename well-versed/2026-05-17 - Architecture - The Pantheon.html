Rome, Campus Martius, c. 118–128 CE

The Pantheon

Hadrian's temple to all the gods. The largest unreinforced concrete dome ever built. Standing for nineteen centuries because a pope mistook it for a martyrs' shrine.

The Pantheon you see in Rome today is not the one Agrippa built. Marcus Vipsanius Agrippa, Augustus's son-in-law and right hand, raised the first temple here on the Campus Martius around 27 BCE. It burned in 80 CE under Titus. Domitian rebuilt it. Lightning took that one in 110. The temple that survives is Hadrian's, raised between roughly 118 and 128, and Hadrian made an unusual decision when he was done. He carved Agrippa's name across the front, not his own. M·AGRIPPA·L·F·COS·TERTIVM·FECIT: Marcus Agrippa, son of Lucius, consul for the third time, built this. The inscription is a lie about who built the building and a gesture about who built Rome.

The façade is a Greek temple. Eight monolithic gray-and-pink granite columns from Egypt, each 11.8 m tall, hold up a pediment as if the Parthenon had been shipped to Tiber-side. Walk under it and the building changes register. Behind the pediment, the architect grafts the Greek portico to a Roman rotunda, and the experience inside is the experience of a sphere. The interior is exactly as tall as it is wide: 43.3 m floor to oculus, 43.3 m wall to wall. Geometry tells you that a perfect sphere of that diameter, set inside, will touch the floor and the oculus at once. Romans understood the room as a model of the cosmos. The dome is the heavens; the eight thresholds in the wall, the planets and the great gods; the round eye of the oculus, the sun.

The engineering is more astonishing than the geometry. The dome is unreinforced concrete and has been for nineteen centuries. The Romans cast it in horizontal pours and tuned the aggregate as the wall climbed: travertine and tufa at the springing, where compression is hardest, then brick and pumice, then porous volcanic scoria near the crown so the top would weigh as little as possible. The shell is 5.9 m thick at the base and 1.5 m thick at the oculus. Five concentric rings of square coffers, 28 panels per ring, deepen the dome's interior and hide load-bearing ribs. There is no rebar. No engineer until Brunelleschi, thirteen hundred years later, dared to vault a comparable span, and Brunelleschi went to Rome to study this one before he tried.

The reason it survived is that the Eastern emperor Phocas gave it away. In the year 608 the Byzantine emperor handed the pagan temple to Pope Boniface IV, who consecrated it on 13 May 609 as Santa Maria ad Martyres, the Basilica of Mary and the Martyrs. He had cartloads of bones from the catacombs installed under the altar. From that day forward the Pantheon was a church and Christian Rome would not strip it for marble or burn it for lime. Roman temples by the hundreds were quarried; the Pantheon kept its skin. The bronze ceiling of the portico was eventually melted down by Pope Urban VIII in 1626 (Bernini turned some of it into the baldachin at St. Peter's), but the building itself survived because Rome had decided it was theirs.

The afterlife is everywhere. Brunelleschi cracked the Pantheon problem at scale in Florence in the 1420s and put a brick dome over Santa Maria del Fiore. Michelangelo lifted the idea again at St. Peter's. Soufflot ran it through Paris at the Panthéon. Jefferson built the University of Virginia Rotunda as a half-sized Pantheon and put his library inside it, secular knowledge under the sphere of the cosmos. Almost any state capitol you have ever walked into, any nineteenth-century bank or museum with a coffered dome, any rotunda that asks you to look up: it is in conversation with this room.

The oculus is the part everyone remembers. It is nine meters across, open to the sky, no glass. When it rains the marble floor floods slightly, then drains through twenty-two small holes carved into the floor that have been working since Hadrian. On clear days a circle of sunlight tracks the wall as the earth turns, and on 21 April, Rome's traditional birthday, it falls on the entrance threshold at noon. The Romans built a room where the sun and the rain are part of the architecture.
