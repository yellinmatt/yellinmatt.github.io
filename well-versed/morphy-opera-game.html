The Opera Game

Paul Morphy's seventeen-move masterpiece in a box at the Italiens, 1858.

The Duke of Brunswick kept a private box at the Théâtre-Italien in Paris because he liked to play chess through operas, and the management indulged him by letting him bring the board. On a night in the late autumn of 1858 his guest was a slight, twenty-one-year-old New Orleanian named Paul Morphy, who had crossed the Atlantic that summer and was already beating every master Europe could put in front of him. Edward Winter's chess history, the most rigorous source on the matter, places the game during a performance of either Bellini's Norma in October or Rossini's Il barbiere di Siviglia on the fourth of November. The Duke heard the whole performance with his back to the stage. He and the French nobleman Comte Isouard de Vauvenargues consulted on the black pieces. Morphy played white alone, in seventeen moves, and forgot the opera entirely.

Morphy was the strongest player anyone had yet seen. He had won the First American Chess Congress at twenty in 1857 and sailed for Europe in June 1858. The Anderssen match would come in December: he took it seven games to two, with two draws, and left the Continent without a serious opponent. He played the consultation game with the Duke and the Count offhand, as one of perhaps fifty he gave that month, and the Duke probably did not know he was on the wrong side of the most quoted game in chess history.

Read it as a short lesson in how chess actually works. The pieces start on the back rank; the side that gets them off it first, into the centre and pointed at the enemy king, almost always wins. Morphy's third move pushes a pawn to d4 to claim the middle of the board; the Duke replies with a bishop move that the modern eye reads as careless (3...Bg4), and by move five he has traded that bishop away for nothing. By move six Morphy has a bishop on c4 aimed at the f7 square in front of the Black king. By move eleven he has castled queenside, both rooks behind the centre file, while one of the Duke's bishops has not moved at all. The Duke's pieces are doing one job each. Morphy's are doing two.

The Duke's 9...b5, a pawn-push meant to chase the bishop on g5, is the losing move. Morphy refuses to retreat. He sacrifices a knight (10.Nxb5), then a bishop (11.Bxb5+) so that the Black king is pinned to its own d-file with no air. Three moves later he sacrifices a rook on d7 to peel away a defender, then a bishop, and finally on move sixteen his queen drops onto b8 with check, asking to be captured. When the lone surviving knight takes it, the second rook slides up to d8 (17.Rd8#) and there is nowhere for the king to run. The bishop on g5 quietly covers the e7 escape; the Duke's own untouched bishop on f8 blocks the other. Mate by a rook and a bishop, with six of Morphy's pawns still sitting on their starting squares.

Emanuel Lasker, three world championships later, looked back at move eight and noted that Morphy could have grabbed material there with 8.Qxb7 or 8.Bxf7+, and refused. “That would have been a butcher's method, not an artist's.” The line is the most quoted sentence in chess criticism and the cleanest summary of what made Morphy different. Eighteenth-century players had treated chess as combat with combinatorial flourishes. Morphy treated it as a single argument with one shape, in which every move develops a piece, opens a line, or makes a threat the opponent has to answer. He invented the principle chess teachers have repeated since Steinitz: develop your pieces first, get the king safe, only then think about winning material. The Opera Game is the lesson, played live, by the man who codified it.

· · ·

The mating pattern carries its own name. The opera mate, a bishop pinning a knight against the king and a rook delivering mate on the back rank, recurs in beginner manuals because the geometry is clean and because every white piece is doing exactly one essential job. Bobby Fischer filmed an analysis of it in 1964, and Kasparov gave it pages in My Great Predecessors. The editors of chess.com voted it the second-greatest game ever played. John Nunn dissents on the grounds that the opponents were duffers. He is right about the duffers. That is the wrong reason to skip the game, because the game is taught precisely because Morphy's logic, slowed down against weak resistance, becomes legible.

Morphy himself retired from competitive chess within four years. He went home to New Orleans, tried to practice law, slid into paranoia in the 1870s, and died of a stroke in his mother's house in July 1884 at forty-seven. The chess world has argued about his unhappy life ever since. The Duke of Brunswick kept his opera box, ran through his fortune, and died in Geneva in 1873 with no obituary in chess journals except a footnote citing this consultation game. The night at the Italiens was the only night that ever mattered for either of them. Morphy spent it ignoring Bellini or Rossini. The Duke spent it losing in seventeen moves.
