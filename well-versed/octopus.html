The other intelligence in the sea

The Octopus

Three hearts, blue blood, two-thirds of its neurons in its arms: the octopus is the nearest thing on Earth to an intelligence that grew up entirely apart from our own.

Watch an octopus slip through a hole the size of a coin and you have seen the fact that organizes the whole animal: the only hard part of an octopus is the parrot-like beak at the center of its arms. Everything else is boneless muscle, so a creature the size of a house cat can pour itself through any gap its beak will clear. With no fixed shape to keep, it can be a knob of coral one moment and a smear of sand the next, and it spends its waking life reading the world around it and answering.

Inside, the design is just as odd. An octopus runs on three hearts: two push blood through the gills, and a third drives it around the body, stalling whenever the animal swims, which is part of why an octopus would usually prefer to crawl. The blood is blue rather than red, because it ferries oxygen on copper instead of iron, a pigment called hemocyanin that holds up well in the cold, oxygen-poor water these animals favor.

Then there is the brain, or the brains. An octopus carries around five hundred million neurons, near the tally of a dog, but it does not pile them where we pile ours. Close to two-thirds are spread through the arms, so each arm can taste, feel, and decide on its own, working a crevice while the animal looks elsewhere. The philosopher Peter Godfrey-Smith, whose 2016 book Other Minds carried the idea to a wide readership, calls the octopus an independent experiment in the evolution of large brains: a mind that arose on a branch that split from ours roughly six hundred million years ago, when the ancestor we share was a flattened worm a few millimeters long. If we ever wonder what an alien intelligence might be like, this is the closest we have come to one.

Nowhere does the strangeness show more plainly than on the skin. An octopus changes color and texture in about a fifth of a second, quicker than you can blink, by firing nerves straight from the brain to millions of tiny pigment sacs called chromatophores, with reflective cells beneath them for sheen and white. Roger Hanlon, who has filmed this for decades, once clocked a single octopus making an average of 177 separate camouflage decisions in an hour. The deepest puzzle is that octopuses seem to be colorblind, with one kind of light receptor, yet they match the color of a reef closely enough to fool sharp-eyed predators; one clue is that their skin itself carries the light-sensing molecules usually confined to eyes.

Roger Hanlon’s footage of an octopus erupting from a patch of reef it had matched exactly. Watch the first seconds: the rock you are looking at is the animal, and it gives itself away only when it moves.

The cleverness extends past the skin. On the sand flats off Indonesia, biologists watched the veined octopus dig up discarded coconut shells, rinse them out, stack them, and stilt-walk stiffly across the seabed carrying the load to build a shelter elsewhere. Hauling a tool to use another day had never been recorded in an invertebrate. In tanks, octopuses unscrew jars, learn to tell individual keepers apart, run mazes, and squeeze out from under sealed lids at night to raid a neighbor’s supper, and aquarium keepers trade these stories the way dog owners trade theirs.

All of it runs on a life that is almost unfairly brief. Most octopuses last only a year or two and breed a single time: the female stops eating to brood her eggs and dies as they hatch, so every generation builds its world from nothing, with no elders to copy. That so quick a mind should gutter out so fast is part of what pulls people toward them, and part of why the question of how much an octopus can suffer has moved from wonder into law. A 2021 review commissioned by the British government judged octopuses to be almost certainly sentient; the United Kingdom wrote them into its animal-welfare act the next year, and Washington and California have since moved to outlaw octopus farming.
