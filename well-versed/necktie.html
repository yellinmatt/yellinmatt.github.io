The Necktie

The one garment a man wears purely to be looked at, from a Croatian soldier's knotted neckcloth to the bias-cut silk at your throat.

Of everything a man puts on in the morning, the necktie is the piece that does no work. A shoe guards the foot, a coat turns the rain, a shirt covers the skin; the tie only hangs there, a folded strip of silk knotted at the throat and tucked behind a jacket, good for not one practical thing. It is pure signal, and men have argued and preened over it for close to four hundred years. The argument starts on a battlefield.

During the Thirty Years' War, France hired regiments of Croatian light cavalry, and those horsemen wore a small cloth of linen or silk knotted at the neck, part uniform, part keepsake from home. Parisians noticed. By the 1660s the knotted neckcloth had climbed from the barracks to the court, and the French took its name from the men who brought it: cravate, from Croate, their word for a Croat, though the Croats call themselves Hrvat. Louis XIV, who could make a thing fashionable simply by wearing it, took to an elaborate lace cravat and kept a court official whose whole task was to present a fresh selection each morning. He raised a regiment and called it the Royal-Cravate. A soldier's kit had become the badge of a gentleman, and it stayed one.

For the next century and a half the cravat was a wide band of white linen wound around the neck and tied in front, and tying it well became a small art. Its master was George Bryan Brummell, the Regency dandy known as Beau, who dressed with such fanatical restraint that he set the taste of London and, through it, of Europe. Brummell starched his cravats to a precise stiffness and tied them slowly, and the story goes that a caller who found his dressing-room floor strewn with crumpled linen was told by the valet, “Those, sir, are our failures.” In 1818 a satirical pamphlet, Neckclothitania, laid out a dozen approved knots by name, the Mathematical, the Oriental, the Mail Coach, each with its own fussy character.

The elaborate cravat suited men with time and a valet. The nineteenth century had less of both. As the frock coat gave way to the business suit and the middle-class office filled with men who dressed themselves, a plainer thing appeared: a long, narrow strip of cloth looped once and pulled through in a small slip knot that a man could tie in seconds and undo at night. The knot took its name from the Four-in-Hand Club, a London coaching set whose members drove teams of four horses, and it is still the knot most men reach for, a neat asymmetric triangle that has never really gone out of style.

One knot did come away with a grander name. The thick, symmetrical triangle called the Windsor is popularly credited to Edward, Duke of Windsor, the king who abdicated in 1936 and spent the rest of his life as the most closely watched dresser alive. He denied it plainly.

What people had seen was simpler than a new knot. He ordered his ties with an unusually thick lining and tied them in an ordinary four-in-hand, so the knot came out fat, and Americans reverse-engineered the look with extra passes and hung his name on it. The knot he actually preferred was the plain one everyone already knew.

The tie you own was engineered in New York. In April 1922 a tie maker named Jesse Langsdorf filed a patent, granted the next February, for cutting the silk on the bias, at forty-five degrees to the grain, and building the tie from three pieces around a soft resilient lining. Cut along the weave, a tie twists and puckers as it is knotted and holds the distortion; cut on the bias, the same silk gives evenly, drapes in a straight line, and springs back to shape once the knot is pulled out at the end of the day. Nearly every tie made since is a Langsdorf tie, and the bias is the reason a good one falls clean and a cheap one curls like a shaving.

Knowing that tells you what to look for. Hold a tie by its narrow end and let it hang: a well-made one drops straight and refuses to spiral. Look for the bias cut, a wool or wool-blend lining of the right weight, and edges slip-stitched by hand, with a loose loop of thread at the back so the tie can flex and recover. A dependable woven-silk tie runs around fifty dollars; an English or Italian one from a house like Drake's or Turnbull & Asser, a hundred and fifty and up; a hand-rolled seven-fold, folded from a single square of silk with no lining at all, more still. One rule outlasts every swing of fashion and width: the tip should just reach the waistband and kiss the top of the belt buckle, no higher, no lower. Everything above that line is decoration, which was always the point.
