Wine

Champagne

The bubbles come from a second fermentation sealed inside the bottle, in the one French region allowed to print the name.

Region

Reims & Épernay

Grapes

Chardonnay, Pinot Noir, Meunier

In the bottle

~6 bar (90 psi)

Tip a flute toward the light and the bubbles climb in fine, even threads from a few fixed points on the glass. Each thread is the residue of a fermentation that happened after the wine was already corked, in a cold chalk cellar northeast of Paris. That second fermentation, trapped under glass, is the whole trick. Everything else about Champagne is geography, blending, and law: only wine grown in the delimited zone around Reims and Épernay, from seven authorized grapes, may carry the name. The zone was drawn in 1927 and the appellation fixed in 1936.

01

The fizz is grown, not added. Eight steps turn a still wine into a sparkling one.

  • 01Assemblage: blend still base wines across grapes, plots, often years.
  • 02Tirage: bottle with the liqueur de tirage, yeast plus about 24 g/L sugar, sealed under a crown cap.
  • 03Prise de mousse: the yeast eats the sugar, and the CO2 cannot escape, building to ~6 bar.
  • 04Sur lie: age on the dead yeast; autolysis gives the brioche and toast notes.
  • 05Remuage: riddle the bottle neck-down so the sediment slides into the neck.
  • 06Dégorgement: freeze the neck, then let the pressure fire out the plug of sediment.
  • 07Dosage: top up with the liqueur d'expédition; the sugar added sets the sweetness.
  • 08Bouchage: cork, wire cage (the muselet), and rest.

Aging is regulated from bottling: non-vintage at least 15 months (12 on the lees), vintage at least 3 years. The tank method used for Prosecco runs the second fermentation in a pressurized vat instead of the bottle, which is why it costs less and tastes fruitier and simpler.

02

The word on the label is a sugar measurement, and the dry end runs backward from common sense.

  • 0–3 g/LBrut Nature (no dosage added; also Brut Zéro)
  • 0–6 g/LExtra Brut
  • 0–12 g/LBrut (the default; nearly every major house)
  • 12–17Extra Dry (confusingly, sweeter than Brut)
  • 17–32Sec (labeled "dry," tastes off-dry)
  • 32–50Demi-Sec (dessert range)
  • 50+ g/LDoux (rare, very sweet)

Dosage is the sugar in the liqueur d'expédition added at disgorgement. Almost everything you meet is Brut. Extra Dry sitting sweeter than Brut is the classic trap on a wine list.

03

Five or six words on the front tell you what is in the bottle.

Non-Vintage
NV / sans année
A blend across years built to a steady house taste. The bulk of all Champagne, and the truest test of a house.
Vintage
millésimé
A single declared year, made only in strong harvests, aged at least three years. Meant to express the season.
Blanc de Blancs
white from whites
All Chardonnay. Lean, mineral, citric; ages for decades.
Blanc de Noirs
white from blacks
Pinot Noir and Meunier pressed to a pale juice. Rounder and fuller-bodied.
Rosé
roh-ZAY
Pink, usually from blending in a little red wine, sometimes by brief skin contact (saignée).
Prestige cuvée
tête de cuvée
A house's top bottling: Dom Pérignon (Moët), Cristal (Roederer), Krug Grande Cuvée.

04

Two small letters near the bottom of the label name who actually farmed the grapes.

NM
négociant-manipulant
The big houses. They buy grapes from many growers and blend at scale. Moët, Veuve Clicquot, Bollinger, Taittinger.
RM
récoltant-manipulant
Grower Champagne. One estate farms the grapes and makes the wine, at least 95% from its own vines. The "grower" or "farmer fizz" movement sommeliers chase.
CM · RC · MA
co-op & buyer brands
Co-op bottlings (CM), co-op wine sold under a grower's name (RC), and buyer's-own-brand (MA), which covers many supermarket labels.
Hautvillers, 1700

The monk gets the credit and deserves little of it. Pierre Pérignon, born 1638, ran the cellars at the Benedictine abbey of Hautvillers from 1668 until his death in 1715. The line everyone quotes, "Come quickly, I am tasting the stars," first appeared in print in the 1820s, more than a century after he died, in promotional copy from a later monk who wanted to lift the abbey's profile. Pérignon's real work ran the other way. In his day a sparkling wine was a fault, the cause of burst bottles and lost stock, and he spent his career trying to keep the bubbles out. What he genuinely advanced was the blending of grapes from different plots and the pressing of black grapes into a clear, pale juice.

The engineering that made clear Champagne possible came from a 27-year-old widow. Barbe-Nicole Clicquot Ponsardin took over her late husband's house in 1805 (veuve is French for widow). Early Champagne poured cloudy, fouled by the dead yeast left behind after the second fermentation, and the only way to clear it wasted half the wine.

Around 1816, working with her cellar master, she had a heavy table drilled with angled holes and stood the bottles in them neck-down. A daily turn and slight tilt walked the sediment down into the neck, where it could be frozen and ejected in a single plug. That table, the pupitre, made a routine product out of what had been a lucky one. The riddling and disgorging she standardized are steps five and six of nearly every bottle made today.
