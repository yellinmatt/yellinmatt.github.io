The Five Mother Sauces

Five sauces, one French chef, and the family tree from which roughly two hundred classical sauces derive.

Codified by

Escoffier, 1903

Working text

Le Guide Culinaire

Daughters

~200 classical

Drop a yellow ribbon of egg yolk over a bath of warm water, beat in clarified butter a thread at a time, and two liquids that have no business getting along braid into something thick, glossy, and rich. That is hollandaise. Build a pale roux of butter and flour, pour in scalded milk, and the milk thickens to a coating cream. That is béchamel. Two of the five sauces from which roughly two hundred classical French sauces derive, all of them organized by one Frenchman in one cookbook, and the lineage he drew is still how working chefs are trained today.

Auguste Escoffier opened the Carlton Hotel kitchen in London the year before the book came out. Le Guide Culinaire arrived in 1903: eight hundred and twenty-two pages, more than five thousand recipes, organized for a working brigade. The sauces chapter at the front fixed the structure. Four grandes sauces of base, with a constellation of petites sauces drawn from each. Hollandaise, the fifth most cooks know today, was a petite sauce in that first French edition on page 150. The English translation of 1907 reshuffled the deck, demoted allemande, and promoted hollandaise. The canon most chefs recite is closer to the Heinemann edition than to Escoffier's own.

01

Béchamel bay-shah-MEL

Milk thickened by a pale roux, finished with a whisper of nutmeg.

  • BaseWhole milk, scalded with an onion piqueé (onion studded with clove and bay).
  • ThickenerWhite roux: equal weights butter and flour, cooked just past raw, no color.
  • MethodWhisk milk into roux, simmer twenty minutes, strain, season with salt and grated nutmeg.
  • DaughtersMornay (Gruyère, Parmesan, yolk), Soubise (onion purée), Nantua (crayfish butter), crème.

Named for Louis de Béchameil, marquis de Nointel (1630–1703), Louis XIV's head steward. The Duke of Escars supposedly grumbled that he had cooked chicken in cream long before Béchameil was born, without ever getting a sauce of his own.

02

Velouté veh-loo-TAY

Light stock thickened by a blond roux. Velvety, as the name says.

  • BaseWhite stock: volaille (chicken), veau (veal), or poisson (fish), depending on the dish it will dress.
  • ThickenerBlond roux: butter and flour cooked one stage past white, the color of straw.
  • MethodWhisk stock into roux, simmer at least an hour, skim, strain through muslin.
  • DaughtersAllemande (egg-yolk and cream liaison), Suprême (chicken velouté reduced with cream), Bercy (shallot, white wine), Normande (cider, mushroom, cream, for fish).

The canvas. Carême's first, the one that takes whatever stock you put under it.

03

Espagnole es-pahn-YOLE

Brown stock thickened by a brown roux, with tomato paste and a long, patient simmer.

  • BaseBrown veal stock from bones roasted to mahogany before they hit the pot.
  • ThickenerBrown roux, deepened to coffee with tomato paste; mirepoix and sometimes bacon for backbone.
  • MethodHours of low simmering, skimmed religiously, then strained.
  • DaughtersDemi-glace (espagnole and brown stock, equal parts, reduced by half), Bordelaise (red wine, shallot, marrow), Robert (mustard, white wine, onion), Chasseur (mushroom, white wine, tomato).

"Spanish": one story credits the Spanish cooks who arrived with Anne of Austria for Louis XIII's wedding. The likelier story is the tomato itself, which Spain brought up from the New World and put on the French table.

04

Tomate toh-MAHT

Tomato, fat pork, mirepoix, white stock. Escoffier's upstart.

  • BaseTomato concassé with white stock; sometimes a little roux for body, often none.
  • AromaticsSalt pork or pork belly rendered first; mirepoix of carrot, onion, and thyme.
  • MethodSimmer until the pan coats a spoon, then push through a sieve.
  • DaughtersCreole, Portugaise (tomato, onion, garlic, parsley), Provençale (garlic, herbs, olive oil).

Carême had no tomato sauce among his grandes. Escoffier added it. The French canon caught up with the New World about three hundred years late.

05

Hollandaise oh-lahn-DEZ

Egg yolks and clarified butter, held in emulsion by lemon and a steady hand.

  • BaseEgg yolks beaten with a splash of cold water, or with a tablespoon of white-wine vinegar reduction for the classical version.
  • BodyClarified butter, warm, added in a thin stream while whisking over a bain-marie.
  • FinishLemon juice and salt. Cayenne if you want a thread of heat.
  • DaughtersBéarnaise (tarragon and shallot reduction), Mousseline (folded whipped cream), Maltaise (blood orange), Choron (tomato paste).

The only sauce of the five without a roux. Escoffier filed it among the petites sauces in 1903; only the 1907 English edition pulled it up into the canon.

From Carême to Escoffier

The classification is older than Escoffier. In 1833 Marie-Antoine Carême, the chef who had cooked for Talleyrand, the Prince Regent, and Tsar Alexander I, published L'art de la cuisine française au dix-neuvième siècle. He named four grandes sauces: espagnole, velouté, allemande, béchamel. The first three are stock-based; the fourth is milk. Every other sauce in his repertoire, he wrote, was a petite, a derivation of one of those four.

An 1844 issue of Revue de Paris caught the new way of thinking in a kitchen wit's summary: "Don't you know that the grand sauce espagnole is a mother sauce, of which all the other preparations, such as reductions, stocks, jus, veloutés, essences, and coulis, are, strictly speaking, only derivatives?" The metaphor stuck. The sauces had mothers and daughters.

Seventy years later Escoffier, by then the most famous chef in Europe, kept four of Carême's grandes and swapped sauce tomate in for allemande. Tomatoes had arrived in earnest; allemande was really a velouté with an egg-yolk liaison, a derivative dressed up as a parent. Hollandaise sat as a petite sauce on page 150 of the 1903 first edition. Four years later, the English translation by Cracknell and Kaufmann wrote allemande out of the basics with a single sentence ("not, strictly speaking, a basic sauce") and put hollandaise among them. That edition shipped to the Anglophone hotel kitchens Escoffier had a hand in stocking, and the five sauces we now repeat became the global default.

Knowing the five is the point. A chef who knows them carries five patterns and a rule for moving between them, and that is enough. The pattern is base plus thickener plus aromatic, with a finish that names the daughter. A Mornay is béchamel with cheese; a béarnaise is hollandaise with tarragon; a chasseur is espagnole with mushrooms. Once you see the architecture, the family tree of French cooking reads at a glance.
