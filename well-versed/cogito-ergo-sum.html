Cogito, Ergo Sum

Descartes set out to doubt everything he could, hunting the one belief that doubt could not touch, and found it in the act of doubting itself.

In the winter of 1619 a young French gentleman-soldier waited out the cold shut up alone in a stove-heated room in Germany, and used the quiet to begin taking his own mind apart. René Descartes had come to suspect that he believed a great many things he had never examined, absorbed without question from teachers and books and his own eyes, and he wanted to know whether any of it would survive a hard look. His plan was severe: treat as flatly false anything he could doubt even a little, and keep pulling down until he struck something that could not be doubted at all.

He began with the senses, since they supply most of what we think we know. The senses deceive: a square tower looks round from a distance, an oar dipped in water looks bent, and what has fooled you once you cannot fully trust. Suppose, then, the senses tell you nothing true. Push harder. You feel certain you are awake and sitting where you sit, yet you have felt that same certainty inside a dream and woken to find none of it real, with no sure mark to separate the two. Suppose, then, you are dreaming, and the whole outside world along with your own body is a fiction playing behind your eyes.

In the Meditations of 1641 Descartes turns the screw one notch further. Imagine, he writes, an all-powerful evil demon who has bent his whole cunning to deceiving you, so that the earth, the sky, and your own hands are bait laid for your belief. Under that supposition almost everything collapses. Even arithmetic wobbles, since the demon could make you go wrong each time you add two and three. The doubt is now very nearly total.

One thing holds. Suppose the demon deceives you about everything he possibly can. He still cannot make you believe you exist while you do not exist, because to be fooled at all you have to be there to be fooled. The act of doubting is itself an act of thinking, and a thought must have a thinker. The harder Descartes tries to doubt his own existence, the more plainly the trying proves it. I think, therefore I am. Here is the one belief that walks out of the fire, the fixed point he had been hunting, and he sets it down as the first stone of everything he means to build.

“But immediately upon this I observed that, whilst I thus wished to think that all was false, it was absolutely necessary that I, who thus thought, should be something; and as I observed that this truth, I think, therefore I am, was so certain and of such evidence that no ground of doubt, however extravagant, could be alleged by the sceptics capable of shaking it, I concluded that I might, without scruple, accept it as the first principle of the philosophy of which I was in search.”

Discourse on the Method, 1637, Part IV, tr. John Veitch, 1850.

The line has a tangled pedigree worth keeping straight. The famous Latin, Cogito, ergo sum, is how most people meet it, but Descartes first set it down in French, je pense, donc je suis, in the Discourse on the Method of 1637. The Latin tag itself reached print later, in the Principles of Philosophy of 1644. And in the Meditations, the book where the doubt cuts deepest, he drops the slogan entirely: there he writes only that the proposition I am, I exist is necessarily true each time he states it or turns it over in his mind. When a critic pressed him, he warned against reading it as a tidy syllogism: the cogito is grasped whole, in a single glance of the mind, rather than deduced step by step from premises.

What survives the fire is thin on purpose. The cogito proves that a thinking thing exists; it does not prove you have a body, or that the room is real, or that another soul is near you. From this single grain Descartes rebuilds the world. He finds in himself the idea of a perfect God, which an imperfect mind could not have invented on its own, so God must exist; a perfect God would stoop to no deception; and the clear and distinct ideas such a God planted in him can therefore be trusted, which lets the external world back in. Critics pounced then and pounce still on the loop: he leans on clear and distinct ideas to prove God, then on God to license those same ideas. It has a name, the Cartesian circle, and whether the argument escapes it has been argued for nearly four centuries.

The cogito also drew a line that modern thought has been worrying ever since. If the thing you cannot doubt is that you are a thing that thinks, then your essence is mind, and mind looks nothing like matter: it has no shape, no size, no place. Descartes concluded that mind and body are two different kinds of substance, a thinking thing and an extended thing, somehow harnessed together in a living person. That is Cartesian dualism, and it opens the mind-body problem in its modern form. How does a weightless mind push a physical body around? He guessed the two met in the pineal gland, deep in the brain. Almost no one followed him there, and almost everyone inherited the puzzle.

· · ·

Descartes did not die at his desk. In 1649 Queen Christina of Sweden summoned him to Stockholm and demanded her philosophy lessons at five in the morning in a cold library, a hard sentence for a man who had always done his best thinking warm and late in bed. Within months he caught pneumonia, and in February 1650 he was dead at fifty-three. He is called the father of modern philosophy because he shifted the ground beneath it. The medieval mind had started from God and authority and reasoned down to the self; Descartes started from the self, alone in a room, doubting, and reasoned out. The first move a student of philosophy still learns is his: to find what you actually know, try to doubt everything, and watch what stays standing when you do.
