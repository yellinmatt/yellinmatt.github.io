Sharpening a Knife

An edge is two planes meeting in a line, and a stone is how you rebuild that line. The angle, the grit, and the small ridge of steel that tells you when you are done.

Angle per side

≈20° Western, 15° Japanese

When it’s sharp

A burr along the edge

Grit progression

Coarse to fine

Press a tired knife into a ripe tomato and it skates, then crushes, and the skin gives way only when you saw at it. A sharp one drops straight through under the weight of the blade. The steel has not changed since the day you bought it. What has worn away is the edge, and an edge is a perishable thing: it is the line where the two faces of the blade meet, ground so fine that near the very tip it is only a few thousandths of a millimeter wide. Everyday cutting rolls that line over and wears it back into a rounded ledge that pushes food apart rather than parting it. Sharpening is the work of grinding the two faces down until they meet again in a clean line.

That is worth separating from honing, which is what the steel rod in the knife block does. Honing removes almost no metal; it only bends the rolled-over edge back to straight, and a few strokes before each use will keep a good knife keen for weeks. Sharpening cuts fresh steel away and rebuilds the edge from the bevel up. The rule of thumb is to hone often and sharpen seldom, a few times a year for a kitchen knife in regular use, and the stone is the tool that does the deeper job.

The classic tool is the whetstone, and the name says exactly what it is for: whet is an old English word meaning to sharpen, with nothing to do with wet, though most stones are soaked or splashed so that water can float away the swarf, the grey slurry of spent grit and steel. A stone is graded by grit, the fineness of its abrasive. A coarse stone around 400 grit cuts quickly and leaves a toothy edge; a fine stone of 3,000 or 6,000 polishes that edge smooth. You work from coarse to fine the way you would sand a board, each stone erasing the scratches left by the one before.

Everything then turns on a single number: the angle at which you hold the blade to the stone. Lay it too flat and you polish the side of the blade without ever reaching the edge; tip it too high and you grind the edge blunt. A Western kitchen knife wants about twenty degrees on each side; a Japanese knife, thinner and harder, wants closer to fifteen. The bevel is the angled face you cut, and the two bevels together make the edge, so a twenty-degree grind on each face adds up to a forty-degree included edge. If you cannot picture twenty degrees, there is a trick every sharpening manual teaches: color the bevel with a felt marker, take a stroke or two, and look at where the ink came off. Ink gone from the very edge means the angle is right; ink still sitting at the edge means you are riding too low.

Hold that angle and push the blade along the stone, edge first, in long strokes from heel to tip, and the steel slowly gives. You judge the work by feel rather than by eye. After enough strokes on one side, a faint ridge appears along the whole length of the opposite edge, a wire-thin curl of displaced metal called the burr. The burr is the most useful thing in sharpening, because it is proof that you have ground all the way to the apex; there is nowhere left for the metal to go but over the top. Draw a thumb gently across the edge, never along it, and you will feel it catch. When the burr runs the full length, that side is done, and you turn the knife over and raise it again on the other.

How to do it

The procedure is the same on any stone, from a cheap combination block to a stacked set of Japanese water stones. Five movements, in order.

01

Set the angle

Roughly twenty degrees per side for a Western knife, fifteen for a Japanese one.

  • Find itColor the bevel with a marker; a stroke or two shows where the edge is meeting the stone
  • Hold itLock the wrist and move from the shoulder so the angle never drifts mid-stroke
  • Wet itSoak a water stone five to ten minutes, or splash it, to float off the swarf

02

Raise a burr

Work one side until a wire of steel forms along the whole far edge.

  • StrokeEdge first, heel to tip, moderate and even pressure
  • The tellFeel for the burr with a thumb drawn across the edge, never along it
  • DoneWhen the burr runs the full length, that side has reached the apex

03

Match the other side

Flip the knife and repeat until the burr forms back on the first side.

  • RepeatSame angle, same stroke, roughly the same number of passes
  • WhyMatching both sides centers the edge instead of grinding it crooked
  • ResultThe burr has now travelled back the way it came

04

Climb the grits

Move from coarse to fine, each stone polishing out the last one’s scratches.

  • PathCoarse near 400 to set the edge, medium near 1,000, fine 3,000 to 6,000 to refine
  • PressureLighter and with fewer strokes as the grit climbs
  • SwitchMove up when a fresh, finer burr appears on the new stone

05

Finish and strop

Shed the burr and align the apex into a clean edge.

  • FeatherA few very light alternating passes, side to side, to break the burr off
  • StropDraw the edge backward over leather, or even a folded magazine, to true it
  • TestSlice a sheet of paper held in the air, then take the skin of a tomato

The test is the proof. A sharp knife slides through a sheet of paper held up in one hand, taking it in a clean line, and it bites the skin of a tomato on the weight of the blade alone. Keep a stone in the kitchen, learn the angle once with the marker, and you stop replacing knives and start keeping them. Of all the small household skills, this is one of the few that pays its keep every single day.
