How a cheap Edo print, and a new blue, became the face of Japanese art

The Great Wave off Kanagawa

A towering wave, three fishing boats, and Mount Fuji shrunk to a speck: the most reproduced image in the history of art began as a cheap New Year’s print sold on the streets of Edo.

In the last days of 1830 a print shop in Edo, the city we now call Tokyo, advertised a new set of landscapes for the New Year trade. The publisher was Nishimuraya Yohachi, whose firm went by the name Eijudō, and the series was Thirty-Six Views of Mount Fuji. Its selling point was colour: a deep, saturated blue, imported and newly cheap, that no Japanese landscape had worn before. One sheet in the set showed a wave. It would become the most famous picture Japan ever made.

The design looks simple. A single great wave rears up on the left and curls over, its crest breaking into fingers of foam that reach down toward three long boats caught in the trough below. The boats are oshiokuri-bune, the fast rowing craft that carried fresh fish into Edo’s markets, and their crews crouch low along the hulls as the water rises over them. Far off, in the still centre of the picture, sits Mount Fuji, Japan’s sacred peak, so shrunk by the play of perspective that the wave’s spray seems about to fall on it like snow. The English-speaking world often calls this a tsunami, but there is no earthquake in it; this is a rogue swell of the open sea, and the drama is the everyday one of small men against big water.

The colour was the technology. That blue is Prussian blue, a synthetic pigment made in Europe and only lately affordable in Japan, brighter and steadier than the indigo local printers had always relied on. What the Met’s conservators found under the microscope is that Eijudō’s craftsmen did not simply swap one blue for the other. They mixed indigo into Prussian blue for the dark outlines, then laid pure Prussian blue over the top in the hollow of the wave, so the water climbs from a leaden dusk-blue to a ringing, luminous one as the eye travels up the curl. A woodblock print was never one person’s work. A publisher commissioned it, a block-cutter carved Hokusai’s drawing into cherrywood, and a team of printers pulled each colour by hand. Hokusai signed it, and his is the only name we kept, but the wave you remember was made by a shop.

Fuji is the thread that binds the series, and Hokusai gives the mountain a fresh mood in every sheet. In the print now called Red Fuji it stands alone in clear morning light, its slopes flushed under a mackerel sky, calm where the wave is violent. Landscape itself was close to a new subject. For most of its history the popular print, ukiyo-e or “pictures of the floating world,” had meant kabuki actors and the courtesans of the pleasure districts; Hokusai helped turn it outward to weather and mountains and the open road. The public bought it. Thirty-Six Views sold so well that Eijudō pushed the set past its own title to forty-six prints, and the Great Wave itself ran to several thousand cheap impressions, the kind of picture a shopkeeper could carry home in his sleeve.

WatchGreat Art Explained on the making and meaning of the print

James Payne walks through the print in seventeen minutes: how a shuttered, self-isolated Japan produced a mass art for ordinary people, how the block-cutters and printers turned Hokusai’s drawing into that saturated blue, and why the tiny Fuji in the trough is the whole point of a picture that looks like it is about the wave.

The man who drew it was already old and still restless. Katsushika Hokusai was around seventy when the wave appeared, working under the name Iitsu, “one again,” one of more than thirty names he took across a life of reinventions. He called himself Gakyō Rōjin, the old man mad about painting, and meant it plainly. In a note attached to a later book he wrote that from the age of six he had been seized by the urge to draw, that nothing he made before seventy was worth counting, and that were heaven to grant him to a hundred and ten, every dot and line of his would at last be alive. He died in 1849, at eighty-eight, still certain he was getting better.

When Japan opened to trade after two centuries of near-isolation, prints like this one travelled west as wrapping and packing paper and landed among artists hungry for something new. The French called the enthusiasm japonisme. Monet hung Japanese prints throughout his house; van Gogh copied them and wrote to his brother about the claw-like reach of Hokusai’s waves; Debussy set the Great Wave on the cover of his score for La Mer in 1905 and let its shape move through the music. The picture that began as a cheap novelty on an Edo street now rides on passports, phone screens, and a keyboard emoji, the single image the world reaches for when it means Japan, or the sea, or a small boat holding on.
