The Bowline

The one knot to learn if you learn only one: a fixed loop that holds under load, unties with a finger, and can be tied one-handed in the dark.

In the catalogue

Ashley No. 1010

By reputation

The king of knots

Rope strength kept

About two-thirds

A man goes over the side of a boat, or off a cliff edge onto a ledge, and someone above him throws down a rope. What he needs at the end of it is a loop he can pass around his chest that will not draw tight and crush him as he is hauled up, that will not slip its size under his weight, and that he can tie with one cold hand while the other keeps its grip. That loop is the bowline. It makes a fixed eye at the end of a line, holds against any pull the rope itself will bear, and then loosens with a push of the thumb the moment the load comes off.

The name is older than the loop most sailors now tie with it. On a square-rigged ship a bowline, sometimes written as two words, was a rope that hauled the windward edge of a square sail forward toward the bow so the canvas would not shiver and flap back against the mast. A ship pulling those lines as taut as they would go, to point as near the wind as she could sail, was said to be sailing “on a taut bowline.” The knot that made those lines fast borrowed their name and kept it.

It is an old knot. Its first clear appearance in print comes in 1627, in John Smith’s A Sea Grammar, where he sets down the “boling knot” and vouches for its hold: the bridles, he writes, are “so firmly made and fastened… they will break, or the sail split before it will slip.” A knot much like it turned up on the rigging of the pharaoh Khufu’s funerary ship, sealed in a pit beside the Great Pyramid around 2500 BC and lifted out in 1954, which would carry the idea back more than four thousand years.

Clifford Ashley, the sailor and painter whose 1944 Ashley Book of Knots is still the great encyclopedia of the craft, listed it as number 1010 and put the whole sailor’s case for it in a single line: “so good a knot a sailor seldom uses another.” Its merits come to three. It makes a fixed loop that will neither run nor bind under strain. It refuses to jam, so it comes free easily even after bearing a heavy load, where a coarser knot welds itself shut. And it can be thrown with one hand, which is why it belongs to rescue crews and figures in the qualified-firefighter test in Britain.

One honest weakness is worth naming before a life hangs on it. The bowline sits beautifully under a steady pull, but left slack, worked back and forth, or shocked in a rope that is stiff or slick, it can shake loose or capsize into a form that slips. Sailors and climbers answer this by finishing it with a stopper, an overhand knot tied in the tail and snugged against the loop, or by tying a more secure cousin such as the Yosemite bowline. The knot also costs the rope roughly a third of its strength, the toll its tight bends charge on any line under load.

For all that, it is quick to learn, and generations have learned it by the same small story. The loop you first lay in the standing part is a rabbit hole; the standing part rising out of it is a tree; the free end in your hand is the rabbit. The rabbit comes up through the hole, passes around the back of the tree, and drops back down the same hole it came from. Draw the standing part and the loop is fixed.

How to tie it

Five movements, in order. Tie it slowly a dozen times where nothing depends on it, and the hand will keep the pattern long after the mind forgets the words.

01

Make the hole

Lay a small loop in the standing part, the free end crossing over the top.

  • DoTwist a short overhand loop a foot or so from the end
  • WatchThe end must lie over the standing part, not under it
  • WhyReverse the crossing and you tie the look-alike Eskimo bowline by mistake

02

Up through the hole

Bring the working end up through the small loop from underneath.

  • DoFeed the tail up through the loop you just made
  • Set the sizeDraw it until the big loop below is the size you want

03

Around the tree

Pass the end behind the standing part, left to right.

  • DoTake the tail around the back of the standing line
  • KeepThe big loop the size you set; do not let it shrink

04

Back down the hole

Return the end down through the same small loop it first came up.

  • DoTuck the tail back down beside where it emerged
  • CheckThe end now sits inside the loop, held by its own collar

05

Dress and set

Tighten by the standing part, leave a good tail, and back it up when it matters.

  • SetPull the standing part to snug the knot; work the parts straight
  • LeaveA tail at least as long as the rope is thick, a hand’s width and more
  • SecureFor anything load-bearing, add an overhand stopper in the tail

Learn this one knot and you can moor a boat, hang a bear bag, throw a rescue line, guy out a tarp, or tie down a light aircraft, all with the same three seconds of movement. Keep a short length of cord in a drawer and tie it until the rabbit runs its course without a thought, and you will have the knot Ashley said a sailor seldom trades for another.
