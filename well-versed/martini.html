The Martini

Gin, vermouth, and cold: three things and a hundred years of argument about the proportions.

Build

Stirred, served up

Glass

Chilled cocktail

Garnish

Olive or twist

Two clear liquids, a long stir over ice, a chilled glass, one olive. Nothing about a Martini looks difficult, and nothing in the cocktail world has been fought over more. The whole drink is gin and dry vermouth, and every choice past that is a small declaration of taste: how much vermouth, gin or vodka, olive or twist, stirred or shaken. Order one with intent and you have said something about yourself before the first sip.

The modern default is dry, which means stingy with the vermouth. The International Bartenders Association fixes the standard at six parts gin to one part vermouth, stirred until very cold, strained straight up, and finished with the oil of a lemon peel or a single olive. That word "dry" carries the whole history: the drink began sweet and shed its vermouth by degrees across the twentieth century, until some drinkers left it out altogether.

The spec

The IBA Dry Martini, the reference point every variation is measured against.

  • Gin60 ml (2 oz) London dry gin
  • Vermouth10 ml (⅓ oz) dry vermouth
  • MethodStir with ice until very cold, strain
  • GlassChilled cocktail glass, served up
  • GarnishLemon-peel oil, or a green olive

How to order one

Past the base recipe, ordering a Martini is a short series of choices. Each has a name, and knowing them is most of the fluency.

01

Dry, Wet, or Perfect

The first thing a good bartender wants to know is how much vermouth.

  • DryLittle vermouth, roughly 5:1 to 6:1; "extra dry" means a whisper or none at all
  • WetA generous pour, 2:1 or wetter, the vermouth tasted on purpose; back in fashion at serious bars
  • PerfectEqual parts dry and sweet vermouth, not a verdict on quality

02

Gin or vodka

Gin is the original and the purist's drink; vodka is the colder, quieter cousin.

  • GinJuniper-forward London dry is the classic; the botanicals are the whole point
  • VodkaNeutral, all chill and texture; rose with the 1960s and must be asked for by name
  • The caseDavid Embury, 1948: a vodka version is "raw alcohol with a faint herb flavor"

03

Stirred, not shaken

Bond ordered his shaken, and most bartenders will quietly disagree.

  • StirA clear, spirit-only drink is stirred to stay silky and bright
  • ShakeColder and faster, but it clouds the drink with air and over-waters it
  • LoreA shaken Martini has its own old name, the Bradford; "shaken, not stirred" enters in Fleming's 1956 novel

04

The garnish

Some versions are named for nothing but what sits in the glass.

  • ClassicA green olive or a twist of lemon, whose oils perfume the surface
  • DirtyA splash of olive brine, cloudy and savory
  • GibsonThe same drink finished with a cocktail onion instead

05

The Vesper

James Bond's own invention is, strictly, not a Martini at all.

  • RecipeThree measures Gordon's gin, one of vodka, half a measure of Kina Lillet, shaken, lemon peel
  • SourceFleming, Casino Royale, 1953; named for the double agent Vesper Lynd
  • TodayKina Lillet is discontinued; Lillet Blanc or Cocchi Americano stand in
Where it came from

The Martini has no birth certificate. The likeliest line of descent runs from the Manhattan to the Martinez, a sweeter mix of old tom gin, sweet vermouth, curaçao, and bitters written down in the 1880s, and from there to a "Martini Cocktail" that Harry Johnson printed in 1888, still sweet and still a long way from the cold dry drink we now picture. A rival theory simply credits the vermouth house Martini & Rossi, whose turn-of-the-century American advertising pushed a "Dry Martini." Both can be true at once: a drink and a brand drying out together.

Whoever poured the first one, the recipe kept losing vermouth. By the 1920s the recognizable form had set, London dry gin and dry vermouth at about two to one, stirred, and the ratio only fell from there: three to one in the 1930s, four to one in the 1940s, and on to the bone-dry extremes drinkers gave joking names, the fifteen-to-one Montgomery after the field marshal who liked long odds. The apocrypha followed the fashion. Churchill was said to make his by glancing at the vermouth bottle from across the room, Noël Coward by waving the gin in the direction of Italy. The vermouth had all but vanished. Lately the pendulum has swung back, and a wetter, vermouth-forward Martini is once again what the best bars want to pour you.
