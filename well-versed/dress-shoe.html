The Dress Shoe

The two things that separate a good shoe from a cheap one: how it laces, and how the sole is built so a cobbler can renew it for years.

An old rule says that to size a man up quickly you look down, because shoes are the one thing a rushed dresser forgets and the last economy a careful one makes. Two things decide whether a pair is any good, and neither is the name stamped inside. The first is how it laces. The second is how the sole is joined to the rest, which settles whether the shoe lasts a season or a lifetime of resoling.

Start with the lacing, because it sorts almost every dress shoe into two families. On an oxford, the flaps that carry the eyelets are stitched down underneath the front of the shoe, so the two sides close into a clean V and the throat lies flat and seamless. On a derby, those same flaps are sewn on top and left open at the bottom, so they spread apart as the laces loosen. The closed lacing of the oxford is trimmer and reads as more formal; the open lacing of the derby gives more room over the instep and a little more ease, in fit and in manner both. A plain black oxford is the most formal leather shoe a man owns, the one that belongs under a dark suit or a dinner jacket, and everything else steps down from it.

Within each family, the difference is decoration. A line of stitching across the toe makes a cap toe, the safe default. Punch that leather with small holes and you have a brogue, and the holes carry a history older than the dress shoe itself. In the wet uplands of Ireland and Scotland, countrymen wore a rough hide shoe, the bróg, and pierced it so that water taken on while crossing a bog could drain back out rather than pool around the foot. For centuries that kept the brogue strictly a country shoe, too rustic for town. It took figures like the Prince of Wales, the future Edward VIII and the most-watched dresser of the 1920s, to wear it into wider acceptance. The perforation is pure ornament now, and its logic runs backward: the more a shoe is punched, the more casual it reads.

A full brogue, the shoe men call a wingtip, carries a pointed toe cap cut in a W that sweeps back along both sides. Straighten that wing into an ordinary cap and you have a half brogue; take the punching down to a faint dotting along the seams and you have a quarter brogue, formal enough for most offices. The rule is the same each time. Read the amount of decoration and you can place the shoe on the ladder from country to town without being told.

The lacing and the punching are what you notice. The welt is what you pay for. Turn a good shoe over and find the strip of leather running around the edge of the sole, between the upper and the ground. That strip is the welt, and in a welted shoe it is stitched first to the upper and to a ridge on the insole, then the sole is stitched to the welt, with the hollow between them packed in cork that molds to the foot. The point of all that work is repair. Because the sole is joined to the welt and not driven straight through the upper, a cobbler can cut it away and stitch on a fresh one, over and over, without ever disturbing the part shaped to your foot. A welted shoe can be resoled until the uppers themselves give out, which for a cared-for pair means decades.

Cordwainers welted shoes by hand from about 1500. The name we hang on the method belongs to the man who mechanized it: Charles Goodyear Jr., who used the fortune of his father, the inventor of vulcanized rubber, to perfect a welting machine in the 1860s, so a “Goodyear welt” now means a welt sewn his way. The cheaper alternatives are worth knowing by their tells. A Blake-stitched shoe runs a single line of stitching straight through the sole and insole, which makes a sleeker shoe but a harder one to resole. A glued, or cemented, sole has no stitching at all, and when it wears through it is thrown away with the shoe.

For the English-speaking world, one town owns this craft. Northampton sits on the old road to London and has made shoes since the Middle Ages; it shod Cromwell's army, and in the First World War turned out most of the boots the British soldier marched in. The names that survive there are a shorthand for the proper English shoe: Church's, founded in 1873; Crockett & Jones, of 1879 and still run by the family; Edward Green, whose motto was “excellence without compromise”; and John Lobb, whose bespoke workshop will carve a last to the shape of your own foot. “Goodyear-welted, made in Northampton” is a phrase you can lean on.

None of this asks you to spend a fortune, only to spend once and keep. A welted shoe from Loake or Meermin costs a few hundred dollars and will outlast several pairs of glued ones. A Crockett & Jones or a Carmina, at five hundred to a thousand, is the honest middle, and an Edward Green or a ready-made John Lobb runs past a thousand, with true bespoke into several. Buy the fit before the label. Start with a black cap-toe oxford, because it goes anywhere, add a pair of shoe trees to draw the damp out overnight, and rotate two pairs so neither is worn wet two days running. Look down at a man's feet, and you can usually tell whether he did.
