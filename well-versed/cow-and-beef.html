Cow and Beef

English keeps a different word for the animal in the field and the meat on the plate, and the reason is a French duke and one October morning in 1066.

Raise a cow in a field and it is a cow. Kill it, cook it, and set it before a guest, and the same animal arrives at the table as beef. Tend a pig and you order pork; keep sheep and you carve mutton; mind a calf and you eat veal. English insists on two words for these creatures, one for the beast in the meadow and another for the meat on the plate, and the wall between them is one of the cleanest fossils the language carries of a single morning's history.

The morning was the fourteenth of October, 1066, on a ridge near Hastings, where the cavalry of Duke William of Normandy broke the shield-wall of the Anglo-Saxon king Harold and left him dead in the line. For the next three centuries the people who ran England spoke French. A French-speaking aristocracy held the land, the courts, the high offices of the Church, and the great dining halls; English carried on underfoot, the tongue of the people who worked the fields. The division in the vocabulary tracks the division in the society almost exactly. The Saxon who raised the animal kept the old name for it, and the Norman who ate it brought a new one to the table.

The neatest turn hides inside that very first pair. Cow and beef are, in fact, cousins, separated at birth and reunited as strangers. Both descend from one ancestor in Proto-Indo-European, a root linguists reconstruct as gʷows, the word for the animal spoken on the Eurasian steppe thousands of years before either English or French existed. One branch carried it north into the Germanic languages, where it wore down to Old English cū and then to cow. Another branch carried it south into Latin as bovem, which softened through French into boef and crossed the Channel, riding on a Norman appetite, as beef. The two words left the same home, walked around opposite shoulders of Europe for three or four thousand years, and met again on an English plate, no longer recognising each other.

The observation is old, and it owes its fame to a novelist. In Ivanhoe, published in 1819 and set in the bitter generation after the Conquest, Walter Scott handed the point to Wamba, a Saxon jester, who explains to the swineherd Gurth why their animals seem to change nationality on the road to the manor.

“And swine is good Saxon; but how call you the sow when she is , and drawn, and quartered, and hung up by the heels, like a traitor?” “Pork,” answered the swine-herd.

“There is old Ox continues to hold his Saxon epithet, while he is under the charge of serfs and such as thou, but becomes Beef, a fiery French gallant, when he arrives before the worshipful jaws that are destined to consume him. Calf, too, becomes Monsieur de Veau in the like manner: he is Saxon when he requires , and takes a Norman name when he becomes matter of enjoyment.”

Walter Scott, Ivanhoe (1819), chapter 1.

The food words are the famous case, but the Conquest reached well past the kitchen. French and Latin raised a second storey onto the plain Germanic house of English, some thousands of words across the medieval centuries, and they tend to sit a register above their native equivalents. You ask in Saxon and inquire in French. A thing is kingly in Old English, royal in French, regal in Latin, three words for one idea climbing the stairs of formality. Freedom is Saxon and liberty French; a Saxon helps where a Norman aids. The blunt, intimate, short-vowelled core of the language came up from the people in the fields, and the polished official vocabulary came in with the people who governed them.

· · ·

The pattern is a tendency rather than a law, and it helps to know where it frays. Chicken stayed Saxon all the way to the plate, lamb keeps its barnyard name at table, and venison, Old French for the spoils of a hunt, covers a deer that nobody farmed. Still the broad shape holds, and it is doing quiet work every time you open your mouth. Reach for the short word and you are speaking the serf's English; reach for the long one and you are dressing in the lord's. Nearly a thousand years after Hastings, the choice between the plain and the formal in an English sentence is still, faintly, an echo of who once held the land and who once worked it.
