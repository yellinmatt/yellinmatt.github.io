The Heartland Theory

In 1904 a British geographer argued that whoever held the unreachable core of Asia would hold the keys to the world, and a century of strategists has argued with him ever since.

On the evening of 25 January 1904, a forty-two-year-old geographer rose before the Royal Geographical Society in London to read a paper, and the map of the world has looked different ever since. Halford Mackinder was no armchair theorist. Five years earlier he had stood on the summit of Mount Kenya, the first European to reach it, after a march through plague and famine. Now he proposed something colder and larger: that the shape of the land itself sets the deep terms of history, more than the genius of any statesman. He called the paper “The Geographical Pivot of History,” and in it he located the hinge of world power deep in the interior of Asia, far from any sea or coast.

The claim cut against the reigning wisdom. The American admiral Alfred Thayer Mahan had taught a generation that sea power decided empires, and Britain, mistress of the oceans, had every reason to believe him. Mackinder answered that the age of sea power was a passing accident. For four centuries, since Columbus, ships had ruled because the great Eurasian interior was unreachable by them, walled off by ice to the north and mountain and desert to the south. But the railway was now stitching that interior together, and what the horse-nomad once roamed, the locomotive could hold and supply. “Is not the pivot region of the world’s politics,” he asked, “that vast area of Euro-Asia which is inaccessible to ships, but in antiquity lay open to the horse-riding nomads, and is to-day about to be covered with a network of railways?” The land was about to take its revenge on the sea.

Mackinder drew the globe as three rings. At the center lay the World-Island, the single connected mass of Asia, Europe, and Africa, by far the largest, richest, and most populous ground on Earth. Around it sat the offshore islands, Britain and Japan among them, and past those the outer islands, including the whole of the Americas. The strategic core of the World-Island he named the Heartland: the Eurasian interior running from the Volga to the Yangtze and from the Arctic to the Himalayas, roughly the territory of the Russian Empire. Sheltered from naval attack, stocked with men and resources, and now laced with rail, it was the one stronghold a sea power could neither reach nor blockade. Whoever held it, Mackinder argued, held the pivot on which the rest of the world turned.

Fifteen years later, with the guns of the First World War just silent and the diplomats redrawing Europe at Versailles, Mackinder compressed the whole theory into three lines that every strategist since has had to answer.

ReadMackinder, Democratic Ideals and Reality, 1919

Who rules East Europe commands the Heartland;
who rules the Heartland commands the World-Island;
who rules the World-Island commands the world.

Halford J. Mackinder, Democratic Ideals and Reality (1919), p. 106.

He meant it as a warning. The book begged the peacemakers not to leave Germany and Russia free to combine over the plains of Eastern Europe, the gateway to the Heartland; a buffer of independent states between the two, he urged, was the price of a lasting peace. The warning went unheeded, and the theory took on a life its author never intended.

· · ·

In Munich the geopolitician Karl Haushofer fed a crude version of it to the Nazi movement as a rationale for Lebensraum and the drive east. After 1945 the same map served the other side: George Kennan’s containment, the ring of American alliances from NATO to Japan, can be read as Mackinder turned into a manual for fencing the Heartland in instead of conquering it. The Yale scholar Nicholas Spykman flipped the emphasis, arguing that the real prize was the coastal “Rimland” fringing the interior, the belt of populous shores rather than the empty core: “Who controls the rimland rules Eurasia; who rules Eurasia controls the destinies of the world.” Much of Cold War strategy was an argument between those two sentences.

More than a century on, the pivot keeps reappearing. Russia’s wars along its western edge, its pipelines running east and west, China’s railways and ports reaching across Central Asia toward Europe, Washington’s announced “pivot to Asia,” all of it can be read as the old contest for the Heartland and its rim. That is also the theory’s danger. Critics called it geographical determinism from the start, the error of mistaking the board for the game; plenty of powers have ruled Eastern Europe without ruling the world, and missiles, aircraft, and cyberattacks now leap clean over the walls that made the Heartland a fortress. The honest verdict is that Mackinder gave us a lens, not a law. But an instrument that statesmen and generals keep reaching for after a hundred and twenty years is worth picking up yourself.
