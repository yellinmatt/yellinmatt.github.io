The predator that came back

The Wolf

A predator erased from Yellowstone for seventy years and carried back in a crate. How the wolf returned, why the “alpha wolf” you have heard of never existed in the wild, and what a top predator really does.

In the winter of 1995, wildlife officers carried wolves into Yellowstone in wooden crates. The last one born in the park had been shot in 1926, when killing predators was still park policy, and for seventy years the country's oldest national park held elk and bison and grizzlies but no wolves. Fourteen animals trapped in the mountains of Alberta came south that January, seventeen more the following winter, and after a few weeks penned in acclimation enclosures they were let loose into the Lamar Valley. The predator was home.

The gray wolf, Canis lupus, is the largest wild member of the dog family. A big northern male runs to fifty-five kilograms and beyond, the weight of a large German shepherd but built long and rangy, with a broad head, a heavy ruff at the throat, and legs made for trotting thirty miles between dusk and dawn. Its coat runs from near-white through the grizzled gray most people picture to solid black, and a single Yellowstone pack will often carry both gray and black wolves. Before we shot and poisoned it out of most of its range, the wolf was the most widely distributed wild land mammal on Earth, at home across nearly the whole northern hemisphere.

Almost everything the phrase “alpha wolf” suggests is wrong. It goes back to a 1947 study by the Swiss biologist Rudolph Schenkel, who watched wolves struggle for rank in a Basel zoo. The wolves were strangers penned together behind a fence, and they behaved like strangers penned together, sorting themselves into a tense hierarchy by force. David Mech carried that picture into his 1970 book The Wolf, the most-read wolf book of its generation, and the word “alpha” lodged in the language. Then Mech spent years watching wild packs on Ellesmere Island and in Minnesota and found something quieter. A wild pack is a family: a breeding pair and their pups from the last year or two, and the parents lead it the way parents do, through experience and the young's own dependence. Mech eventually asked his publisher to stop reprinting the book. The correction has never caught up with the myth.

The wolves also handed ecology its most famous parable. Within a decade of the reintroduction, researchers reported that elk, no longer free to graze the riverbanks in idle safety, had drifted off the open bottomlands; willow and aspen and cottonwood grew back along the water; beavers returned to the new willow, songbirds to the new trees, and the firmed-up banks began to reshape the very channels of the streams. George Monbiot's four-minute telling of it, laid over sweeping Yellowstone footage, has been watched more than forty million times.

It is a beautiful story, and it is almost certainly too clean. Twenty years of careful measurement have complicated it. A long study by the ecologists Tom Hobbs and David Cooper found that the willows grew taller but not more widespread, and only where beavers and groundwater already allowed it; cougars and grizzlies recovered in the same decades and leaned on the elk too; the northern range turned out to hold far more moving parts than a single chain running from wolf to river. The wolf matters to Yellowstone. It is one hand on the system among several, and the honest lesson is how hard a wild valley is to reduce to a single cause.

The last thing worth knowing about the wolf is asleep on the floor. Every dog alive, from the wolfish husky to the lapdog that would not last a hard night outdoors, descends from gray wolves, from an extinct population that split off somewhere between twenty and forty thousand years ago and cast its lot with ours. The animal we drove out of Yellowstone with traps and strychnine and the animal curled up by the fire are one animal, met at two different doors.
