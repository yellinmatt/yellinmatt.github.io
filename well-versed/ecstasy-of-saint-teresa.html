Gian Lorenzo Bernini · Cornaro Chapel, Santa Maria della Vittoria, Rome · completed 1652

The Ecstasy of Saint Teresa

An angel, a golden arrow, and a fainting nun: Bernini’s wager that the only language for the soul meeting God was the language of the body at its most overwhelmed.

An angel leans over a collapsing nun, a golden arrow loose in his hand, the faintest smile on his face. She has gone slack. Her head falls back, her lips part, one bare foot hangs into the air below the cloud that carries her, and her habit churns around her in folds that seem to move on their own. Almost none of it looks like stone. From a single white block Bernini has cut wool, skin, feathers, and vapor, then lit the whole apparition from a window you cannot see. This is the heart of the Cornaro Chapel, finished in 1652, and it sets a single sentence from a Spanish nun’s autobiography into permanent marble.

The nun was Teresa of Ávila, who lived from 1515 to 1582, reformed the Carmelite order, founded convents the length of Spain, and wrote some of the most exact descriptions of mystical experience in the Western tradition. Rome canonized her in 1622, only a generation before Bernini took up the commission. In the twenty-ninth chapter of her Life she set down a vision that returned to her over several days: a small angel, beautiful and burning, who pierced her again and again with a fire-tipped spear. She wrote it without ornament, which is exactly what gives it force.

Read Teresa of Ávila, The Life, ch. 29

“I saw in his hand a long spear of gold, and at the iron’s point there seemed to be a little fire. He appeared to me to be thrusting it at times into my heart, and to pierce my very entrails; when he drew it out, he seemed to draw them out also, and to leave me all on fire with a great love of God. The pain was so great, that it made me moan; and yet so surpassing was the sweetness of this excessive pain, that I could not wish to be rid of it.”

Teresa of Ávila, The Life of Teresa of Jesus, ch. 29, trans. David Lewis (1904).

Bernini read that passage the way a sculptor reads a brief: a body in a precise posture, in a precise light, at the instant the spear withdraws. He was the most celebrated artist in Rome, a sculptor and architect who had run the visual life of the papacy under Urban VIII and could paint or design a spectacle as readily as he could carve one. When Urban died in 1644 the new pope, Innocent X, wanted nothing from his predecessor’s favorite, and for the first time in his career Bernini’s studio fell idle. That vacancy is why the chapel exists. A Venetian cardinal, Federico Cornaro, secured the out-of-favor master for his burial chapel in an unremarkable Carmelite church and paid an enormous fee of twelve thousand scudi.

What Cornaro received was a room that behaves like a theater. Bernini raised the marble group on a shallow stage above the altar, flooded it from a window concealed behind the pediment, and ran gilded bronze rods down the wall to fix the falling light as solid rays. On the two side walls he carved the cardinal and seven male Cornaro ancestors as half-length figures leaning out of what are unmistakably opera boxes, talking, reading, watching the miracle across the room. The vault opens into a painted sky. Rudolf Wittkower called the result a deliberate hierarchy of realities: the Cornaro men share our world and our space, while Teresa’s vision is lifted into a sphere of its own by the canopy and the unaccountable light. The arts do not merely sit together here. Sculpture, architecture, painting, and daylight are fused into the single effect the period named a bel composto, a beautiful whole.

· · ·

The work has never stopped provoking the same argument. Teresa’s parted lips and limp body, the angel’s almost teasing poise, the sheer abundance of the drapery have struck many viewers as more erotic than holy. A Victorian critic wanted it destroyed; Jacques Lacan built a theory of feminine pleasure around it; Simon Schama wrote that scholars “tie themselves in knots, trying to avoid stating the obvious.” But the charge of indecency mistakes a deliberate choice for an accident. Teresa herself had written that the pain was not bodily but spiritual, “though the body has its share in it,” and that the sweetness was so great she could not wish to be rid of it. Bernini took her at her word. He had concluded that the soul’s union with God could be shown in no language but the body’s, carried past its own limits, and he set that conviction in marble so persuasively that four centuries of viewers are still arguing about what they see.
