Daedalus and Icarus

Everyone remembers the boy who flew too close to the sun. He was warned about the sea as well, and the painting that made the myth famous shows a world too busy to look up.

A farmer leans into his plough at the front of the picture. A shepherd stands beside his flock and gazes up at the wrong patch of sky. An angler hunches over the water at the shore. A merchant ship runs before the wind toward a harbor in the gold evening light. You have to hunt for the disaster. In the lower right corner, beside the ship's stern, a pair of pale legs kicks above the green water and is about to vanish. That splash is Icarus, and the whole design of the painting is that no one in it has noticed.

The man who built the wings was the most celebrated craftsman in the Greek world. Daedalus was an Athenian, an architect and inventor exiled to Crete, where King Minos put him to work. He built the Labyrinth at Knossos, the maze that held the Minotaur, and built it so cunningly that he himself could barely find the way out. When he later helped Theseus kill the creature and escape, by giving Ariadne the trick of the unspooled thread, Minos shut the inventor and his young son Icarus on the island. Daedalus reasoned that the king held the land and the sea but had no claim on the air. He gathered feathers, graded them small to large like the pipes of a panflute, lashed them with thread, and sealed them with wax.

Then he fastened a pair to the boy's shoulders and gave him one instruction. The instruction is the part the proverb forgets.

ReadOvid, trans. A. S. Kline

Let me warn you, Icarus, to take the middle way, in case the moisture weighs down your wings, if you fly too low, or if you go too high, the sun scorches them. Travel between the extremes.

. . . His nearness to the devouring sun softened the fragrant wax that held the wings: and the wax melted: he flailed with bare arms, but losing his oar-like wings, could not ride the air. Even as his mouth was crying his father's name, it vanished into the dark blue sea, the Icarian Sea, called after him. The unhappy father, now no longer a father, shouted ‘Icarus, Icarus where are you? Which way should I be looking, to see you?’ ‘Icarus’ he called again.

Metamorphoses, Book VIII, trans. A. S. Kline (2000).

“Flew too close to the sun” is half of what Daedalus actually said. The wax would melt if Icarus climbed, true, but spray off the waves would soak and weigh the feathers if he dropped too low. The danger sat at both ends. The whole counsel was moderation, the middle line between water and fire, and Ovid puts it in a father's mouth as plain practical advice. Icarus broke the rule in the more glamorous direction, which is why a culture that loves a cautionary tale kept the sun and quietly dropped the sea.

Ovid had also given the flight an audience. As the two crossed the water, he wrote, “some angler catching fish with a quivering rod, or a shepherd leaning on his crook, or a ploughman resting on the handles of his plough, saw them, perhaps, and stood there amazed, believing them to be gods able to travel the sky.” Look back at the painting. The angler, the shepherd, and the ploughman are all there, lifted straight from the Latin. The painter kept Ovid's cast and reversed the wonder. In the poem they look up and take the fliers for gods. On the canvas they keep working while a boy drowns at their feet.

The other half of the myth is tenderness, and the finest version of it is in marble. Antonio Canova carved his Daedalus and Icarus in Venice in 1779, when he was about twenty. The father kneels and binds a wing to the boy's shoulder, frowning down at his own handiwork; the boy stands at ease, looking out, with no idea what the wings are for. Canova sold the group for a hundred gold zecchins and spent the money on his first journey to Rome, where he became the sculptor who would define Neoclassicism. The marble freezes the moment before the lesson is given, before the flight, before the fall.

The Bruegel hung in Brussels, and in the winter of 1938 a young Englishman named W. H. Auden stood in front of it and wrote “Musée des Beaux Arts.” “About suffering they were never wrong, / The Old Masters,” it begins, and it closes on this canvas: the expensive ship that “had somewhere to get to and sailed calmly on” past the white legs in the water. William Carlos Williams answered with his own poem on the same painting in 1960. Between them the picture has become shorthand for two lessons at once: the recklessness of the boy, and the indifference of the ordinary world to one private catastrophe. The adjective “Icarian” still means recklessly, fatally ambitious.

Ovid darkens it once more at the grave. As Daedalus buries his son, a partridge watches from a ditch and claps its wings, delighted. The bird is Perdix, the nephew Daedalus had once thrown from the Acropolis in a jealous rage for inventing the saw; the gods caught the falling boy and made him a partridge, a bird that fears height and nests low to the ground. At the funeral of a son killed by flying too high, the murdered nephew looks on as a creature that will never leave the earth.

Daedalus flew on alone and reached Sicily. He buried the boy on a scrap of land in the Aegean. The island is still called Icaria. The water around it is still the Icarian Sea.
