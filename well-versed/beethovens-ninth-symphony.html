Beethoven's Ninth Symphony

How a stone-deaf composer wrote the first symphony to break into song, and gave the world a melody that outgrew him.

On the evening of 7 May 1824, a stocky, gray-haired man stood at the front of the stage of Vienna's Theater am Kärntnertor and beat time to music he could not hear. Ludwig van Beethoven was by then almost completely deaf. The theater's Kapellmeister, Michael Umlauf, had quietly told the players to watch his own baton and ignore the composer, who had wrecked a rehearsal of his opera Fidelio two years earlier. When the last chord died away, Beethoven was still several bars behind, turning the pages of his score with his back to the hall. One of the soloists, the twenty-year-old contralto Caroline Unger, stepped over and turned him around so he could see what he could not hear: the audience on its feet, waving hats and handkerchiefs, five times over. It was his first appearance on a Vienna stage in twelve years, and the work they were cheering was the Ninth Symphony.

The Symphony No. 9 in D minor, Opus 125, was Beethoven's last complete symphony, written mostly between 1822 and 1824, though its roots reach back further. The Philharmonic Society of London had commissioned it in 1817, and even in the earliest sketches he set the key as D minor and pencilled in a plan for voices. He had been turning Friedrich Schiller's poem An die Freude, the Ode to Joy, over in his mind since his twenties in Bonn, more than thirty years. The finished symphony runs past an hour, the longest and largest orchestral work he wrote, and it ends by doing something no major composer had done inside a symphony before. It opens its mouth and sings.

For three movements the Ninth behaves as its first listeners understood the word symphony to behave, though a stranger and stormier example than they were used to. It opens with bare open fifths, hushed and hollow, no theme yet, as if an orchestra were feeling for its notes in the dark, until the full D-minor subject crashes down out of the haze. Beethoven then breaks the usual order and puts the hurtling scherzo second, ahead of the slow movement's long song. And then the finale attempts the impossible. The cellos and basses, in wordless recitative, reject the themes of everything that has come before, searching for something worthier; they find the Ode to Joy melody and pass it up through the orchestra, quiet at first, then gathering weight.

Then a human voice arrives. The bass soloist sings words Beethoven wrote himself and set in front of Schiller's: O Freunde, nicht diese Töne, oh friends, not these sounds, let us strike up something more joyful. It is the first time in the history of the symphony that a singer is heard at all, and it comes as an interruption, a person stepping out of the machine to speak. What the chorus then proclaims is Schiller's vision of a reconciled humanity: Alle Menschen werden Brüder, all men become brothers, wherever joy's gentle wing rests. Beethoven drives the theme through everything he knows, a fugue, a Turkish march clashing with cymbals and triangle, a hymn to the millions, and lifts the whole enormous apparatus into a blazing D major. The finale alone lasts about twenty-five minutes, longer than some complete symphonies of the generation before him.

ListenBarenboim & the West-Eastern Divan, 2012

Go to the finale. Hear the low strings mutter and reject the earlier themes, then state the Ode to Joy melody alone, almost shyly, before the orchestra takes it up. Wait for the baritone's O Freunde to break in, and stay for the double fugue near the end, where the joy theme and the "be embraced, you millions" theme are sung against each other at once. This orchestra, founded by Daniel Barenboim to seat young Arab and Israeli musicians side by side, makes the "all men become brothers" text more than a slogan.

The cruelty is that Beethoven never heard a note of it. He had written the most public and communal music imaginable, a work built to gather thousands of strangers into a single feeling, from inside a silence no performance could reach. He conducted the premiere only in the sense that he stood there and moved his arms. He would die less than three years later, in March 1827, the Ninth already understood as a summit and its composer already a legend.

· · ·

The tune outgrew the man, and eventually the poem too. In 1972 the Council of Europe adopted the Ode to Joy melody, stripped of Schiller's German words, as the Anthem of Europe; the European Union took it up in turn. It now plays at ceremonies of union and, just as often, at moments of protest, hummed by crowds who have never read a bar of the score. The symphony its own composer could not hear has become one of the most performed works on earth. The reason is the same one that made a young singer turn a deaf man around to face a roaring hall. It was written to be shared.
