The Fall of Constantinople

On a Tuesday before dawn in May 1453, a wall that had held for a thousand years failed, and the Roman Empire finally ended.

For a thousand years the land walls of Constantinople had never been forced. Theodosius II threw them up across the neck of the peninsula around 413, a triple line of moat, outer rampart, and a great inner wall forty feet high and sixteen feet thick at the base. Avars, Arabs, Bulgars, and Rus had all broken against them. By the spring of 1453 the city they guarded was a hollowed-out relic, perhaps fifty thousand people rattling around inside fortifications built for half a million, the last splinter of an empire that had once ringed the Mediterranean. Behind the walls stood roughly seven thousand fighting men. Outside them lay a Turkish army of sixty to eighty thousand and the most ambitious siege train Europe had ever seen.

The man who brought it was twenty-one. Mehmed II had taken the Ottoman throne for the second time in 1451 and made the conquest of Constantinople the whole point of his reign. In 1452 he built a fortress at the narrowest bend of the Bosphorus, Rumelihisarı, which the Turks called Boğazkesen, the throat-cutter, and used it to choke the straits. Then he commissioned the guns. A Hungarian gunfounder named Orban had first offered his cannon to Constantine XI; the emperor could not pay, so Orban carried the design to the sultan, who could. The largest piece ran twenty-seven feet, threw a stone ball of more than half a ton, and needed sixty oxen and some two hundred men to haul it. The walls had never been built to answer artillery like that, because artillery like that had never existed.

The siege opened on 6 April. The bombards battered the land walls by day; the defenders, led by the Genoese soldier Giovanni Giustiniani, patched the breaches by night, and for seven weeks the patching kept pace with the pounding. The sea was supposed to be safe. A great iron chain slung across the mouth of the Golden Horn kept the Ottoman fleet out of the harbor. So Mehmed went around it. On the night of 22 April he had some seventy ships hauled out of the Bosphorus, dragged on greased wooden rollers over the ridge behind the Genoese colony of Galata, and floated down into the Horn behind the chain. Overnight the defenders found enemy warships in the one stretch of water they had counted on, and a longer wall they could no longer fully man.

The final assault came in the dark hours of 29 May. Mehmed sent his waves in turn: irregulars first to tire the defenders, then his Anatolian regiments, and last the Janissaries, his elite. Giustiniani took a shot through his armor and was carried from the wall, and his men’s nerve went with him. The Turks found a small sally-port, the Kerkoporta, left ajar, and got men onto the inner wall near the Gate of St. Romanus. Constantine XI is said to have stripped off his imperial regalia so as to die unrecognized, and went down sword in hand in the breach. The man who recorded his death was writing for the sultan.

“There the Emperor Constantine, with all who were with him, fell in gallant combat.”

Kritovoulos of Imbros, History of Mehmed the Conqueror, §235. Trans. Charles T. Riggs, Princeton, 1954. Kritovoulos served Mehmed and dedicated the work to him.

By midday the sultan rode through the captured city to Hagia Sophia, the largest church in Christendom for nine hundred years, and had it turned into a mosque on the spot. Kritovoulos says that when Mehmed saw the ruin his men had made, he wept: “What a city we have given over to plunder and destruction.” He moved his capital here from Edirne and took a title to match it, Kayser-i Rûm, Caesar of Rome. He meant it literally.

· · ·

That is the part worth carrying. The schoolbook says Rome fell in 476, but the empire only ended on this Tuesday in 1453, nearly a thousand years later, in a Greek-speaking Christian city on the Bosphorus whose people never stopped calling themselves Romans, Rhomaioi. “Byzantine” is a label a German scholar pinned on them a century after they were gone. Their imperial title outlived them too: Caesar became the German Kaiser and the Russian Tsar, and Moscow would soon style itself the Third Rome. The old story that fleeing Greek scholars carried antiquity to Italy and lit the Renaissance is too tidy, Italian humanism was already decades along, but the date still works as a hinge, the year a medieval order cracked and the Ottomans became a European power for the next four centuries. The Greeks took it hardest of all. They still call Tuesday an unlucky day.
