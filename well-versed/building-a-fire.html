Building a Fire

A fire is won before the match is struck, in how you gather and grade the wood from a spark-catching wisp to a steady log.

The fire triangle

Heat · Fuel · Air

Build in three sizes

Tinder, kindling, fuel

Clear a circle

At least one metre

Watch a beginner and the fire dies the same way every time. He lays a fat log across a heap of sticks, holds a match under it, and waits while the flame licks the bark, gutters, and goes out, leaving him with smoke and a warm thumb. The wood was never the problem. A fire needs three things at once, and the old woodsman’s triangle names them: heat, fuel, and air. Take away any one of the three and the flame dies, which is why a log pressed flat to cold ground will not catch and a fire buried under too much wood chokes for want of air. And the fuel does not burn quite the way it appears to. Heat drives a gas out of the wood, and that gas, mixing with the oxygen around it, is what actually burns. Pile on cold wood too soon and you rob the flame of the heat it needs to make more gas, and it starves.

So you build in three sizes, smallest first. Tinder is the fine, bone-dry stuff that will take a spark and give back a flame: a bird’s nest of dead grass, shaved bark, or a stick scraped into a feather of curls. Kindling is the pencil-to-finger-thick wood you lay on the young flame to drive its heat up. Fuel is the wrist-and-forearm wood that burns low and long once the fire is strong enough to hold it. The one habit that separates a fire that lights from one that does not is gathering and sorting all three before you strike anything, more of each than you think you need, laid within easy reach. A match burns for a few seconds. If you are still snapping twigs when it goes out, the fire is already lost.

Choose the ground before you build. You want a dry patch out of the wind, near enough to your shelter to feel it and clear of anything that would carry the flame away, so scrape the spot down to bare earth and open a circle at least a metre across. A low wall of logs or stones on the windward side will throw the heat back toward you and break the gusts, though the survival manuals carry one blunt warning here, that you should never ring a fire with wet or porous stones, which can trap steam as they heat and burst like a shell.

How you stack the wood is the lay, and a handful of patterns cover almost every situation. The tepee is the one to learn first. Stand the kindling in a cone around a fist of tinder, leaving a gap on the windward side for a door, and as it catches the outer sticks burn through and topple inward, feeding themselves, so the tepee takes even in damp wood. The lean-to props a single green stick into the wind at a low angle with the tinder tucked beneath it and kindling leaned along it like a roof. The cross-ditch is a shallow cross scratched into the earth, which lets air sweep under the tinder from every side. The pyramid stacks wood in a squat tower, each layer smaller and set crosswise to the one below, and lit from the top it burns quietly downward on its own, the fire you want when you would rather sleep than tend it.

Light from the upwind side, low, where the moving air will push the flame into the fuel instead of away from it. Touch the match to the tinder itself, never to the sticks above it, and once the tinder is burning give it kindling before you give it fuel, small to large, never smothering the young flame with a log it cannot yet swallow. A fire that has burned down to a bed of red coals holds more steady heat than one still throwing tall flame, and coals are what cook a meal and warm a camp through the night.

The match is the least of it. On a bright day a lens from a camera or a pair of binoculars will light dry tinder by focusing the sun to a point. A ferrocerium rod, the modern “metal match,” throws a shower of hot sparks when scraped with the spine of a knife and works even when soaked. Below those lie the old methods our ancestors lived by, which reward a great deal of practice: flint struck against carbon steel, the fire-plow, the bow and drill. Any of them will light the same small wisp of tinder. Whether it grows into a fire still depends on the wood you laid under it.

How to build it

Five steps, in order. Do them slowly on a calm afternoon a few times, where nothing depends on the outcome, and the sequence will hold when it counts.

01

Gather and sort

Make three piles before you strike anything, more of each than seems necessary.

  • TinderDead grass, shaved bark, feathered curls; bone dry, enough to fill both hands
  • KindlingPencil-to-finger-thick dry sticks, a good armful
  • FuelWrist-and-forearm wood for later, set aside off the wet ground

02

Prepare the ground

Clear a dry, sheltered spot down to bare earth.

  • ClearScrape a circle at least a metre across, out of the wind
  • WallA low ring of logs or dry stones on the windward side to reflect heat
  • AvoidWet or porous rocks, which can burst when they heat

03

Build a tepee

Stand a cone of kindling around a fist of tinder.

  • CentreA loose fist of tinder on the ground
  • ConeLean kindling around it, tips meeting, gaps for air
  • DoorLeave an opening on the windward side to reach the tinder

04

Light it low and upwind

Take the flame to the tinder, not to the wood above it.

  • WhereFrom the upwind side, low, at the tinder itself
  • ShelterCup the flame from the wind until the tinder is truly caught

05

Feed small to large

Grow it in stages, then let it settle into coals.

  • FirstKindling onto the burning tinder before any fuel
  • ThenFuel wood once the fire can hold it without choking
  • KeepBank the coals to hold the heat; drown and stir it dead before you leave

One last piece of woodcraft outlives every method. Do not grab the branches lying on the ground, which soak up damp and rot soft even when they look dry, and will crumble instead of catch. Take dead wood still standing, or snap it from the low dead limbs of a tree, and split a log to reach the dry heart inside it. Keep your tinder dry against your body and your reserve wood off the wet earth. Learn to read a fire this way and you can boil water, dry your clothes, cook a meal, signal for rescue, and hold the cold and the dark at arm’s length, all of it from a single match and a careful ten minutes with a knife and an armful of wood.
