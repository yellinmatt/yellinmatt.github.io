The Marshall Plan

A soldier stood up at a Harvard lunch, offered to pay for Europe's recovery, and drew the map of the postwar world.

At ten minutes to three on the afternoon of the fifth of June 1947, in a warm and crowded Harvard Yard, the Secretary of State began to read from a few typed pages he had asked not be treated as a major speech. The State Department had told reporters the same thing, that this was a routine commencement talk and nothing of any importance. Fifteen thousand alumni had come to see the most admired public servant in America, not to watch history turn. What George Marshall read to them in a little over ten minutes launched the largest act of peacetime generosity one nation has ever extended to others, and it did more than rebuild a continent. It fixed the shape of the world we still live in.

Two years after the guns went quiet, Europe was not recovering. The bombed cities and broken rail lines were the visible wound, but the deeper injury was invisible: the whole machinery of trade, the banks and shipping firms and stable currencies, had seized up, and a brutal winter had pushed the crisis to the edge. Marshall's economic advisor, Will Clayton, had come home from a tour of the continent and written that millions in the cities were slowly starving. The trap fed itself. A farmer would not sell his grain for money that bought nothing in the shops, so he grazed his fields and kept the food, and the towns went hungrier still, and each government drained its last dollars buying necessities abroad instead of rebuilding. Break that circle, Marshall argued, or watch free institutions give way to the desperation that follows hunger.

His remedy carried two conditions that gave it its genius. America would supply the money, but Europe would draw up the plan, together, deciding among themselves what they needed and how they would help each other. Washington would not dictate terms from across an ocean. And the aim, he insisted, was a cure and not another emergency handout when the next crisis came. He set the whole purpose down in one sentence that has outlived every other in the speech.

ReadGeorge C. Marshall, Harvard, 5 June 1947

“It is logical that the United States should do whatever it is able to do to assist in the return of normal economic health in the world, without which there can be no political stability and no assured peace. Our policy is directed not against any country or doctrine but against hunger, poverty, desperation and chaos. Its purpose should be the revival of a working economy in the world so as to permit the emergence of political and social conditions in which free institutions can exist.”

From the “Marshall Plan” speech, Harvard University. George C. Marshall Foundation.

The first move came from London. The British foreign secretary Ernest Bevin caught a BBC report of the speech and grasped at once what had been offered, and within days he was on the telephone to his French counterpart, and by July the representatives of sixteen nations were meeting in Paris to draft the joint program Marshall had asked for. The offer had been open, in principle, to everyone, the Soviet Union included. Stalin sent his foreign minister Molotov to Paris to test it, then pulled him out on the second of July, denouncing the whole scheme as American economic imperialism and forcing Poland and Czechoslovakia, which had shown interest, back into line. The Communist coup in Prague the following February erased the last doubts in Congress. An offer meant to heal Europe had instead marked the seam along which it split, and the aid became the economic front of the Cold War.

President Truman signed the act in April 1948 and handed the machinery to a businessman, Paul Hoffman of the Studebaker car company, who ran it as its own agency at arm's length from the State Department. Over the next four years roughly thirteen billion dollars flowed to the sixteen countries, a colossal sum for the time. By 1952, as the money wound down, the output of every recipient stood above its prewar level, most of them a third or more above where they had been in 1938. The following year the Nobel committee gave its Peace Prize to Marshall, the only professional soldier ever to receive it.

· · ·

Economists still argue over the arithmetic, since thirteen billion dollars was a small fraction of what Europe itself invested in those years, and some of the recovery would have come regardless. What the plan supplied that no ledger captures was confidence, a signal that the richest nation on earth would not let the continent fall, and it did so by tying its own interest to its generosity in a way that made both easier to sell. That, more than the dollars, is why the name still gets invoked whenever anyone proposes to rebuild a broken place at scale. And the name itself was a calculation: Truman put a five-star general's name on the bill rather than his own, knowing a Republican Congress would fund a soldier's plan sooner than a Democratic president's. Marshall, characteristically, let it stand and never once interfered with the men who spent the money.
