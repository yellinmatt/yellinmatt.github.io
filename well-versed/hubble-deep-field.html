Ursa Major, December 18–28, 1995

The Hubble Deep Field

For ten days the most powerful telescope ever built stared at an apparently empty patch of sky the size of a grain of sand at arm's length. It came back crowded with galaxies.

In 1995 Robert Williams ran the Space Telescope Science Institute, which gave him control of the director's discretionary time on the Hubble Space Telescope, roughly a tenth of its schedule, usually held in reserve for sudden events like a supernova. Williams proposed spending ten straight days of it on a patch of sky chosen because there was, as far as anyone could tell, nothing in it. Colleagues warned him it was a bet against his own standing: aim the world's most expensive telescope at darkness for ten days and you might get back ten days of darkness. He did it anyway.

The target sat just off the handle of the Big Dipper, in Ursa Major, a speck 2.6 arcminutes across. Hold a tennis ball at arm's length a hundred meters away and you have covered roughly that much sky, about one twenty-four-millionth of the whole. The spot was chosen for its dullness: high above the obscuring dust of the Milky Way's disc, with no bright foreground star to flare the detector. Between December 18 and 28 the telescope circled Earth about 150 times and took 342 separate exposures through four filters, from ultraviolet to near-infrared. The team shifted the aim by less than a single pixel between frames and stacked them with a new technique called drizzling, recovering detail finer than any one exposure could hold.

The picture was unveiled at the American Astronomical Society meeting in January 1996, and it was not dark. It held about three thousand galaxies. Fewer than twenty of the dots were foreground stars; nearly everything else was a galaxy, some only a few pixels wide, many of them blue, ragged, and colliding in ways the orderly nearby universe has mostly outgrown. Because light is slow, the faintest were also the oldest: their glow set out when the cosmos was a fraction of its present age, some of it traveling for around twelve billion years before reaching the camera. The deep field was a core sample of time. Look far enough into emptiness and you are looking at the young universe.

So they went deeper. In 2004, with the new Advanced Camera for Surveys, Hubble stared at a comparably blank patch in Fornax for the equivalent of eleven and a third days across 400 orbits and counted about ten thousand galaxies: the Hubble Ultra Deep Field. A 2012 reprocessing, the eXtreme Deep Field, pulled out galaxies whose light dates to the first half-billion years after the Big Bang. A twin deep field shot in the southern sky in 1998 looked, reassuringly, just like the northern one, which is the universe quietly confirming that it presents the same face in every direction.

WatchNASA · Ultra Deep Field flythrough

Astronomers measured distances to thousands of the Ultra Deep Field galaxies and placed them in three dimensions, then flew a camera through. Watch the flat photograph become a corridor: the galaxies you pass first are nearby and recent; the ones at the far end are small, red, and billions of years back in time. The image is not a wall. It is a tunnel.

In July 2022 the James Webb Space Telescope released its first image, the galaxy cluster SMACS 0723, and reached deeper than Hubble's weeks of staring in twelve and a half hours. Webb works in the infrared, where the most stretched and ancient light finally registers, and it used the cluster's own gravity as a lens, bending and magnifying galaxies behind it into smeared arcs. Some of them shine from less than a billion years after the beginning. President Biden unveiled the picture at the White House the night before the rest of the batch went public, which tells you what kind of photograph it was understood to be.

The lesson of the deep fields is the one Williams was betting on. There is no empty sky. Aim a good enough instrument at the darkest gap you can find, hold still, and the gap fills with galaxies, each holding hundreds of billions of stars, most of them older than the Sun. The reason the image turns up on posters and phone cases is not only that it is beautiful, though it is. It is that it quietly moves you off-center. The Hubble Deep Field is a photograph of almost nothing that turned out to be a photograph of nearly everything.
