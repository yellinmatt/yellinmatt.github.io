The Hubble Deep Field

For ten days the most powerful telescope ever built stared at an apparently empty patch of sky the size of a grain of sand at arm's length. It came back crowded with galaxies.

In 1995 Robert Williams decided to aim the Hubble Space Telescope at nothing for ten days. As director of the Space Telescope Science Institute he held the director's discretionary time, roughly a tenth of Hubble's schedule, normally saved for the unexpected: a fresh supernova, a passing comet. He proposed to spend a long block of it on one small patch of sky picked precisely because it looked empty. Senior colleagues warned him he was staking a fortune in observing time, and his own standing, on a frame that might come back black. The working group hedged where it could. Hubble normally locks onto a single pair of guide stars; for this they demanded a second backup pair before they would commit.

The target sat just off the handle of the Big Dipper, in Ursa Major, a speck 2.6 arcminutes across. Hold a tennis ball at arm's length a hundred meters away and you have covered roughly that much sky, about one twenty-four-millionth of the whole. The spot was chosen for its dullness: high above the obscuring dust of the Milky Way's disc, with no bright foreground star to flare the detector. Between December 18 and 28 the telescope circled Earth about 150 times and took 342 separate exposures through four filters, from ultraviolet to near-infrared. The team shifted the aim by less than a single pixel between frames and stacked them with a new technique called drizzling, recovering detail finer than any one exposure could hold.

The result was announced at the American Astronomical Society meeting in January 1996, and the empty patch was not empty. It held about three thousand galaxies. Fewer than twenty of the points of light were foreground stars; the rest were whole galaxies, some only a few pixels across, many of them blue, lopsided, and colliding in ways the settled nearby universe rarely shows. The faintest were also the oldest, because their light had traveled longest: some of it had left when the cosmos was a fraction of its present age, around twelve billion years ago. One frame of apparent emptiness had become a core sample of cosmic time.

So they went deeper. In 2004, with the sharper Advanced Camera for Surveys, Hubble fixed on a comparably blank patch in the southern constellation Fornax for the equivalent of eleven and a third days, spread across 400 orbits, and resolved about ten thousand galaxies: the Hubble Ultra Deep Field. A 2012 reprocessing, the eXtreme Deep Field, recovered galaxies whose light dates to the first half-billion years after the Big Bang. A matching deep field shot in the southern sky in 1998 had come back almost identical to the northern one, evidence that the universe shows the same face in every direction.

Astronomers measured distances to thousands of the Ultra Deep Field galaxies, placed them in three dimensions, and flew a camera through the model. The flat photograph opens into depth: the galaxies you pass first are nearby and recent, while the ones at the far end are small, red, and billions of years back. The picture has a front and a back.

In July 2022 the James Webb Space Telescope released its first image: the galaxy cluster SMACS 0723, reaching deeper in twelve and a half hours than Hubble had in weeks. Webb works in the infrared, where the most stretched and ancient light finally registers, and it used the cluster's own gravity as a lens, bending and magnifying the galaxies behind it into thin arcs. Some of them shine from less than a billion years after the beginning. President Biden presented the image at the White House the evening before the rest of Webb's first batch was released.

Williams had bet that there is no truly empty sky, and the deep fields proved him right. Aim a sensitive enough instrument at the darkest gap you can find, hold it steady, and the gap fills with galaxies, each holding hundreds of billions of stars, most of them older than the Sun. The picture sells now on posters and phone cases, but its real content is a measurement: in a piece of sky the size of a grain of sand, roughly three thousand galaxies, with no reason to expect any other grain to look different.
