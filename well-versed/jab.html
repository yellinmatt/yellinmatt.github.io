The Jab

Boxing's plainest punch is also its most important: the lead hand thrown straight, the one that measures the fight and sets up everything else.

“Establish your jab.” It is the first thing a trainer shouts from the corner and the last thing a great fighter forgets. The punch itself is the simplest in boxing: from a hands-up guard the lead fist travels straight to the target, the arm extending fully, the fist turning over at the last instant so it lands flat on the first two knuckles, then returning to the guard before the other man can answer. No wind-up, no lean, no weight thrown after it. In the gym shorthand that every boxer speaks, it is simply punch number one.

That shorthand is worth knowing, because corners and commentators both talk in it. The numbers are built on the orthodox stance, the right-hander's stance, with the left foot and left hand forward and the power held in reserve on the right. Odd numbers belong to the lead hand: one is the jab, three the lead hook, five the lead uppercut. Even numbers belong to the rear, the power hand: two is the cross, four the rear hook, six the rear uppercut. So the famous “one-two” is a jab followed by a straight right, the most basic sequence in the sport, and nearly every combination opens with the one. The jab is also the only punch a fighter can throw without opening his own guard, which is why it is thrown more than all the others combined.

Its work is mostly quiet. The jab measures the distance, finding the range at which the heavier punches will land; it breaks the other man's timing before he can set himself; it banks points with the judges round after round; and held out long and stiff, it keeps a bigger, stronger opponent at the end of an arm where he can do no harm. “Stick and move” is the whole craft of the boxer who would rather not be hit, and the stick is the jab. A fighter who cannot solve his opponent's jab tends to lose the fight, whatever else he is carrying.

The Victorians called it the straight left, and the connoisseur's argument over who threw it best is itself a piece of boxing culture. Larry Holmes is the usual answer at heavyweight, a jab so long and so heavy it broke fights open on its own. Joe Louis snapped his out so crisply and so accurately that opponents seldom saw the right hand behind it. Sonny Liston's was a club. And Muhammad Ali's was simply faster than anyone's had a right to be: once timed on a laboratory instrument, it crossed sixteen and a half inches to splinter a balsa board in nineteen-hundredths of a second. Run the line back further and it passes through men like Peter Jackson, the West Indies-born heavyweight who made his name in Australia and is drawn above demonstrating the straight left on an 1893 tobacco card. The fundamentals have barely moved in a century and a half. Put a beginner and a champion in the same gym and the tell is always the same: the beginner swings for the knockout, and the boxer, patient behind his lead hand, jabs.
