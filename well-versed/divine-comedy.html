The Divine Comedy

A Florentine exile wrote himself a guided tour of the afterlife in the language of the street, and gave Italy its literature and the West its map of Hell.

In the year 1300, at the midpoint of the biblical threescore years and ten, a man wakes to find himself lost in a dark wood, the straight path out of sight. That man is named Dante, and he is also the author holding the pen. This is the daring at the center of the Divine Comedy: the poet casts himself as the pilgrim, and sends himself down through Hell, up the mountain of Purgatory, and out through the turning heavens, all in the first person and the vivid present tense. The dead Roman poet Virgil rises out of the gloom to be his guide, and the reader walks in behind them both.

The whole journey runs to a hundred cantos in three books, called cantiche: Inferno, Purgatorio, Paradiso, thirty-three cantos each with one more up front to open the whole. Dante built it in terza rima, interlocking three-line stanzas that chain forward on a rhyme (aba, bcb, cdc), a verse form he seems to have invented for the poem and that pulls the reader down the page the way the pilgrim is pulled down through the earth. The first thing the two travelers meet at the mouth of Hell is a gate, and carved across its lintel is one of the most quoted sentences ever written.

Past the gate the ground funnels down in nine descending circles, and the logic that orders them is the poem’s great invention: contrapasso, the punishment that answers the sin in kind. The lustful, who let passion sweep away their reason, are blown forever on a black wind; Dante stops to hear Paolo and Francesca, lovers killed mid-affair, and faints from pity. The gluttons lie in cold filth. Fraudulent counselors, who hid their fire, walk sheathed in flame. At the very bottom the traitors are frozen to their necks in a lake of ice, and Satan himself is fixed there, weeping, chewing the arch-betrayers in his three mouths. Hell in Dante has the cold order of a courthouse, engineered to the last soul.

None of this was written from a comfortable study. Dante was born into a Florence tearing itself apart between factions, and when the wrong side won in 1302 he was condemned in absence, sentenced to be burned alive if he ever came home. He never did. He wrote the Comedy across nearly twenty years of exile, moving from court to court, and he used it to settle every score: he meets popes head-down in the rock of the simoniacs, plants Florentine enemies in the ice, and gives the heights of Heaven to Beatrice Portinari, the girl he had loved from a distance since childhood and who had died young in 1290. She takes over from Virgil as his guide through Paradise. The poem is at once a work of theology, a political weapon, and the longest love letter in the language.

· · ·

That last phrase, “the language,” is the reason the Comedy matters beyond its story. Serious writing in Dante’s day meant Latin; he wrote his hundred cantos in the Tuscan speech of Florence, the vernacular a mother used with her children, and defended the choice as fiercely as he composed in it. The gamble reset a civilization’s literature. Tuscan as Dante shaped it became the spine of literary Italian, which is why Italians still call him il Sommo Poeta, the supreme poet, and the father of their tongue. He titled the work simply Comedìa, by the medieval rule that a comedy begins in misery and ends in joy and speaks in the plain style; the reverent adjective came later, from Boccaccio, and reached the title page of a 1555 Venetian edition as La Divina Comedia. To read it, even a single canto, is to hear the sentence a whole language chose to become.
