The Omega Speedmaster

A chronograph built in 1957 to time motor races, the only watch NASA ever certified for spaceflight, and still hand-wound exactly as it was when it reached the Moon.

In 1957 Omega built a stopwatch into a wristwatch and aimed it at the racetrack. The Speedmaster was a chronograph, a watch with a second mechanism that times an interval at the press of a button, and its one real novelty was where the speed scale lived. Earlier chronographs printed the tachymeter, the scale that turns elapsed time over a measured distance into a reading in miles per hour, around an already crowded dial. Omega moved it out to the steel bezel ringing the crystal. That cleared the face and let a driver read his average speed at a glance. The first reference, the CK2915, had a black dial, broad arrow-shaped hands, and a manually wound movement called the caliber 321. Nothing about it suggested space.

The watch reached space on merit, and almost by accident. In the early 1960s a NASA engineer named James Ragan was handed the job of finding a wristwatch an astronaut could trust when the spacecraft's own instruments failed. Ragan wrote a specification, sent it to several Swiss and American makers, and bought the candidate watches off the shelf so that no manufacturer could prepare a special sample. Of the firms approached, four answered. Hamilton mailed a pocket watch and was disqualified for not reading the brief. Three wristwatches went onto the bench, where Ragan ran them through eleven trials that read like a catalogue of ways to kill a watch: heat to 93 degrees Celsius and cold to minus 18, a near-vacuum, ninety-five percent humidity, a pure-oxygen atmosphere, shocks of forty g from six directions, hard vibration, and acoustic noise. Two of the three failed. The Omega kept time. NASA certified it on 1 March 1965 and that June declared the Speedmaster flight-qualified for all manned space missions, the words Omega still engraves on the caseback.

It had already been to orbit unofficially, on Wally Schirra's own wrist aboard a Mercury capsule in 1962. Once the watch was qualified, the agency issued it as standard kit, and Ed White wore one outside his spacecraft on Gemini 4, America's first spacewalk, only days after it passed. Then came Apollo.

Both Apollo 11 astronauts carried the Speedmaster, reference 105.012, strapped over the sleeve of the spacesuit. Neil Armstrong left his inside the lunar module: the cabin's electronic timer had quit, and the commander's watch became the backup mission clock. So when Buzz Aldrin climbed down the ladder a few minutes after Armstrong, the watch on Aldrin's wrist became the first ever worn on the surface of the Moon. He sent it to the Smithsonian a few years later and it vanished somewhere in the mail. It has never been found, which makes it the most famous lost watch in the world.

Apollo 13, the flight that went wrong, is where the Speedmaster earned its keep. In April 1970, two days out, an oxygen tank exploded and crippled the service module. The crew shut down the command module to save its batteries for re-entry, which left them without the automated timing they needed to fire the engine for a course correction that would bend their crippled ship back toward Earth. Command module pilot Jack Swigert timed the burn by hand off his Speedmaster: fourteen seconds, started and stopped on the chronograph, beginning a hundred and five hours and eighteen minutes into the flight and ending to the tenth of a second. They splashed down on 17 April. That October NASA gave Omega its Silver Snoopy Award, the small sterling pin astronauts reserve for the people who help bring them home.

· · ·

Sixty years on, the watch NASA still certifies is mechanical. The Moonwatch is hand-wound, with no battery, the same three-register dial, and the same plexiglass crystal the agency insisted on because acrylic, unlike glass, will not shatter into splinters that float around a cabin in zero gravity. The movement has been revised three times since 1957, from the caliber 321 to today's 3861, and the case grew its asymmetric twisted lugs in 1964, yet the object is recognizably the racing chronograph it started as. That is the appeal of a tool watch, the kind made to be read at a glance and to do a job rather than to ornament a cuff. Omega never set out to build a watch for space. It made a good instrument, NASA tested it close to destruction, and the survivor is the one you can still buy today, reading Speedmaster Professional across a black dial, the same words that went to the Moon.
