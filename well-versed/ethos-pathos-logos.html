Rhetoric · The Art of Persuasion

Ethos, Pathos, Logos

Aristotle cut the whole art of persuasion down to three appeals, and every speech that has ever moved you has pulled at least one of them.

Stand a man in an Athenian law court in the fourth century before Christ and hand him nothing but his voice. No attorney will argue in his place, no report will speak for the evidence, and he has until the water clock runs dry to talk several hundred fellow citizens out of a ruinous verdict. Persuasion there decided property, exile, and now and then a life. The men who taught it, the sophists, sold it as a bag of clever tricks, and Aristotle, who had watched them work, sat down around the middle of that century to make a real discipline of it. His Rhetoric opens by cutting the whole art down to three.

The three are the ways a speaker can move an audience, and Aristotle named each for where its power sits. The first he called ethos, from the Greek word for character: we believe a speaker we take to be credible, decent, and worth trusting, and much of a speech's work is finished before a single argument lands, done by the impression the speaker makes of himself. The second is pathos, from the word for feeling: put a jury in a generous mood and it forgives, frighten or anger it and it condemns, so the practised speaker shapes the emotional weather of the room. The third is logos, from the word that means both speech and reason: the proof carried in the argument itself, the evidence and the logic of the case as the words lay it out.

He set them down in a single sentence that has organized the teaching of persuasion ever since.

ReadAristotle, Rhetoric, Book I

“Of the modes of persuasion furnished by the spoken word there are three kinds. The first kind depends on the personal character of the speaker; the second on putting the audience into a certain frame of mind; the third on the proof, or apparent proof, provided by the words of the speech itself.”

Aristotle, Rhetoric I.2 (1356a), trans. W. Rhys Roberts.

There are three because there are only three things present in any act of speech: someone speaking, someone listening, and something said. Ethos belongs to the speaker, pathos to the listener, and logos to the message that passes between them. That is why the appeals are still taught as the three corners of a triangle, each pulling on a different part of the same exchange.

You can hear all three pulling together in the most studied American speech of the last century. When Martin Luther King stood on the steps of the Lincoln Memorial on the twenty-eighth of August 1963, his ethos had arrived before he opened his mouth: a minister with the cadence of the pulpit, standing where he stood, opening in the borrowed voice of Lincoln himself. His logos was an argument built like a legal brief. The architects of the republic, he said, had signed “a promissory note to which every American was to fall heir,” a promise of “the unalienable rights of life, liberty and the pursuit of happiness,” and America had handed its Black citizens instead “a bad check, a check that has come back marked ‘insufficient funds.’” Then came the pathos, the passage the whole world kept, the dream that his four children would “one day live in a nation where they will not be judged by the color of their skin but by the content of their character.” Character, feeling, and argument, sounded at once.

Aristotle did not pretend the three were always used honestly. He complained that the teachers before him had dwelt on pathos alone, on stirring the judge, because inflaming a crowd is easier than reasoning with one, and he set logos above the rest for that reason. His own teacher, Plato, had gone further and dismissed the whole art as flattery in the dress of knowledge. Aristotle's reply was that rhetoric is a tool, no more moral or immoral than the hand that grips it, the counterpart of logic aimed at a living audience.

· · ·

The lasting gift of his three-part scheme is that it runs in both directions. Once you can name the appeals you can build a case that stands on all three, and you can also hear, when a speech or an advertisement or a candidate is working on you, which lever is being pulled: whether you are being offered a reason, courted through your feelings, or simply asked to trust a face. The educated listener is not the one who never feels the pull. He is the one who knows which of the three he is feeling.
