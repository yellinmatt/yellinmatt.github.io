Kind of Blue

The best-selling jazz album ever pressed, recorded in two short sessions with next to no rehearsal, and built on a radical act of subtraction.

On a spring afternoon in 1959, six musicians filed into Columbia Records' converted-church studio on East 30th Street in Manhattan and recorded music most of them were seeing for the first time. Miles Davis had written the pieces only hours earlier and arrived with sketches, a scale here, a sixteen-bar outline there, instead of finished scores. There was almost no rehearsal. For most of the album, the first complete performance the group ever played is the take you hear. Out of two sessions, nine hours of studio time, and four reels of tape came Kind of Blue, which has since sold more than five million copies in the United States and stands, decade after decade, as the best-selling jazz record ever made.

Davis got there by taking things away. Bebop, the fast, virtuosic jazz he had grown up playing beside Charlie Parker, had grown crowded with chords, a fresh harmony every beat or two, and the soloist's task was to get cleanly through the maze at speed. “The music has gotten thick,” Davis told The Jazz Review in 1958. “Guys give me tunes and they're full of chords. I can't play them.” His remedy was modal jazz: instead of a sprinting chord sequence, hand the player a single scale, a mode, and let it hold for bars at a time. Fewer chords, more air, the harmony standing still while the melody roams. The approach owed much to the composer George Russell, whose Lydian Chromatic Concept had been passing among musicians, and Davis had already tested it on the title track of Milestones the year before.

You can hear the whole theory in the opening track. “So What” runs thirty-two bars in an AABA shape, two matching eight-bar sections, a contrasting bridge, then the first section again, and across all thirty-two it leans on only two scales: D Dorian for the outer sections, lifting a half-step to E-flat Dorian for the bridge before it settles home. That is the entire harmonic plan. Gil Evans scored the hushed piano-and-bass introduction; then Paul Chambers's bass poses a two-note question and the horns answer, a call and response descended from the way Duke Ellington once let his bassist trade phrases with the whole orchestra. Over so little, Davis plays one of the most studied solos in jazz, more singing than running.

ListenMiles Davis, “So What,” 1959

Begin with the loose, almost out-of-time intro, then the two-note figure the bass sets down and the horns answer. When Davis enters on muted trumpet he leaves great gaps; notice how long he is willing to wait before the next phrase. Then Coltrane arrives and pours notes into the same opening. One frame, two opposite temperaments.

The musicians around him made a summit. John Coltrane on tenor saxophone, already reaching toward the dense “sheets of sound” that would define his own records; Julian “Cannonball” Adderley on alto, all blues and daylight; Bill Evans at the piano, with the blues specialist Wynton Kelly sitting in for the lone straight-ahead blues, “Freddie Freeloader”; Paul Chambers on bass and Jimmy Cobb on drums. Everyone was paid union scale. The record that now tops nearly every list of the greatest jazz albums cost Columbia a contract advance of a few thousand dollars, the studio hours, and a piano tuner's fee.

Bill Evans is the album's quiet architect. He had taken in Russell's modal ideas and the watercolor harmonies of Debussy and Ravel, and his touch sets the record's contemplative weather. He also wrote its liner notes, a brief essay called “Improvisation in Jazz” that reached for a picture to explain the method. Evans described a Japanese painter working on thin parchment with brush and black ink, where “an unnatural or interrupted stroke will destroy the line” and “erasures or changes are impossible.” That, he wrote, is the discipline of the improviser: the direct deed, no second draft. It is also how the album was cut, in single takes. His part ran deeper than the prose. Evans composed “Blue in Green” and helped shape “All Blues,” though both carried Davis's name alone for decades; the Davis estate acknowledged Evans's authorship of “Blue in Green” in 2002.

What keeps the album alive is the quality that made it new: restraint. Nothing hurries, the silences carry as much as the notes, and a first-time listener and a conservatory student can each find a door in. Davis himself kept moving, into the orchestral Sketches of Spain, the second great quintet of the 1960s, and the electric storm of Bitches Brew, and he rarely looked back. But the record he made in those nine hours outran him; it became the album that classical, rock, and pop listeners keep when they keep no other jazz at all. Jimmy Cobb, the last of the six to die, never offered much of an explanation for how it came together so cleanly in so little time. “It must have been made in heaven,” he said.
