The Shape of a Well-Rounded Mind

Plotting a self-directed general education against the whole map of what an educated person is expected to know.

Compiled by Matthew Yellin · A living coverage report

A well-rounded mind should, in principle, fill a circle. This report plots a self-directed education against the whole map of what an educated person is expected to know, field by field, as a radar of breadth: what has been covered, and which way it still has to grow.

Methodology
  1. Population. The set of topics is fixed in advance. The project's domain map enumerates every topic worth knowing across two dozen fields, split into three priority tiers: Anchor, Standard, and Light. Coverage is measured against this closed set, never against a count of lessons, which has no ceiling.
  2. Unit. The unit of coverage is the topic, not the lesson. A topic counts as covered the first time a lesson teaches it. A second lesson on the same topic deepens it but does not raise coverage.
  3. Mapping. Each published lesson is assigned to one topic and its parent field. A field's reach is the share of its topics taught at least once; a second lesson in a field extends that reach toward the rim.
  4. Projection. Each field is an axis; its radius is the fraction of its topics covered. A perfectly well-rounded mind fills the circle. The figure is drawn to be honest about how far that is, not to flatter the total.
  5. Limitations. The map is a menu, not a mandate. The tiers bias rotation toward Anchor topics, so coverage grows unevenly by design. Field boundaries are editorial. The radar rewards breadth over depth, which is the entire point of the exercise.
