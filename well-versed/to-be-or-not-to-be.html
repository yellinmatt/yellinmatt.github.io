To be, or not to be

The most quoted speech in English literature, and what almost no one notices about it.

Hamlet walks on at the start of Act 3, Scene 1, and thinks he is alone. He is not. Polonius and Claudius have placed Ophelia in his path and hidden behind an arras to overhear what he says to her. The audience sees the trap. The speaker does not. The most private soliloquy in English drama is in fact the most spied-on. That is a Hamlet joke before the speech has begun.

The line everyone quotes is the first. The argument is in the next three. Is it nobler “to suffer / The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune, / Or to take arms against a sea of troubles, / And by opposing end them?” The question turns on action, with death as one form of action and outrageous fortune as the alternative. Suicide enters around line eight (“To die, to sleep”) and stays through the middle of the speech. It is never the only thing on the table.

To be, or not to be: that is the question:
Whether ’tis nobler in the mind to suffer
The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune,
Or to take arms against a sea of troubles,
And by opposing end them? To die: to sleep;
No more; and by a sleep to say we end
The heart-ache and the thousand natural shocks
That flesh is heir to, ’tis a consummation
Devoutly to be wish’d. To die, to sleep;
To sleep: perchance to dream: ay, there’s the ;
For in that sleep of death what dreams may come
When we have shuffled off this mortal coil,
Must give us pause: there’s the respect
That makes calamity of so long life;
For who would bear the whips and scorns of time,
The oppressor’s wrong, the proud man’s ,
The pangs of despised love, the law’s delay,
The insolence of office and the spurns
That patient merit of the unworthy takes,
When he himself might his make
With a ? who would bear,
To grunt and sweat under a weary life,
But that the dread of something after death,
The undiscover’d country from whose bourn
No traveller returns, puzzles the will
And makes us rather bear those ills we have
Than fly to others that we know not of?
Thus conscience does make cowards of us all;
And thus the native hue of resolution
Is sicklied o’er with the pale cast of thought,
And enterprises of great pith and moment
With this regard their currents turn awry,
And lose the name of action.—Soft you now!
The fair Ophelia! Nymph, in thy
Be all my sins remember’d.

Hamlet, Act 3, Scene 1, in the Second Quarto (1604–5) and First Folio (1623). Text per the Academy of American Poets.

Two arguments interlock. The first is the opening: suffer or act. The second comes near the middle: why we hesitate. The hinge between them is the word rub. In lawn bowling a rub is any unevenness on the green that diverts the ball off its line. Hamlet uses it for death’s unknown, the thing we cannot see past. The line that names the actual problem comes later and quieter. “Conscience does make cowards of us all.” Conscience here is closer to consciousness than to moral compass: the part of mind that hesitates because it can imagine what comes next.

The version above is from the Second Quarto of 1604 and the First Folio of 1623, the latter compiled by John Heminges and Henry Condell seven years after Shakespeare’s death. The first printed Hamlet, the quarto of 1603, opens the same speech this way: “To be, or not to be, I there’s the point, / To Die, to sleep, is that all?” In that edition Polonius is called Corambis and Reynaldo is called Montano. The bibliographer Alfred Pollard named such texts “bad quartos” in 1909, and Henry David Gray argued in 1915 that the actor who played Marcellus had reconstructed Q1 from memory after leaving the company. Whether or not Gray was right, the gap between Q1 and the canonical text is what scholars cite when they argue about how the play actually sounded onstage in London in 1601.

Shakespeare wrote the role for Richard Burbage. The Globe had opened on Bankside in 1599, owned in part by Burbage and his brother Cuthbert; Hamlet probably premiered there in 1600 or 1601, when the building was still new. Burbage was the most admired actor in London, and the elegy printed at his death in 1619 named “young Hamlet” first among the roles the city had lost with him. Shakespeare’s only son, Hamnet, had died of unknown causes at eleven in 1596, four or five years before the play. Modern editors note the name and stop there. So should we.

WatchDavid Tennant, RSC 2009

Gregory Doran’s 2008 RSC production, filmed for BBC Two in 2009 with the original stage cast. Watch Tennant play the speech as a real argument with himself rather than a recital; he lands on rub as if he has just felt the word catch.

The speech is the most quoted thing in English after the King James Bible. “Slings and arrows of outrageous fortune” is now everyday idiom. So are “sea of troubles,” “shuffled off this mortal coil,” and “there’s the rub.” What Dreams May Come became a Robin Williams film in 1998. The Undiscovered Country became the subtitle of Star Trek VI in 1991, in which a Klingon general claims the speech has never been heard until it has been heard in the original Klingon. The phrases have outlived the dramatic situation that produced them by four centuries.

· · ·

Read the last full line aloud. “And lose the name of action.” Hamlet talks his way out of his own resolve, and the rest of the play is five more acts of him doing the same thing under fresh pressure. By the close of Act 5, Elsinore has eight bodies on the floor.
