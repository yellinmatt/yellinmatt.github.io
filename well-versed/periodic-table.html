The Periodic Table

How Dmitri Mendeleev arranged the known elements by weight, left blank squares for elements no one had yet found, and turned a chemistry teacher’s chart into a law that could foretell the unseen.

Lay the known elements in a line by their weight, lightest first, and something strange happens: their properties keep coming back around. Lithium, a soft metal that catches fire in water, is followed several places on by sodium, which does the same, and several places on again by potassium, more violent still. Fluorine, which grabs at nearly anything it touches, sits directly above chlorine and bromine, its near-twins in appetite. Read the elements this way and they settle into families, columns of cousins, and the chart stops being a list to memorize and becomes a map of how matter behaves. That recurrence is the periodic law, and the man who saw it most clearly was a Siberian chemist named Dmitri Mendeleev.

He was not the first to try. Through the 1860s, after an 1860 congress at Karlsruhe finally settled reliable atomic weights, at least half a dozen chemists across Europe drew up systems of the elements, among them the German Julius Lothar Meyer, whose table of 1864 grouped them much as Mendeleev’s soon would. What set the Russian apart was nerve.

In March 1869 Mendeleev’s paper was read to the Russian Chemical Society, though not by him: he was off in the countryside inspecting cheese-makers and left a colleague to present it. He liked to say the arrangement had come to him whole in a dream, a story too neat to trust entirely, though he had certainly spent years shuffling the elements like a hand of cards, one to a slip of paper, until the order felt right.

His table set the sixty-three elements then known in order of rising atomic weight and broke the line into rows so that elements with like properties stacked into columns. Where the weights and the families disagreed, he trusted the families and let the weight wait. And where no known element fit, he did the thing his rivals shrank from: he left the square empty and declared that an element must one day come to fill it. Three of those blanks he described in detail, naming them provisionally eka-boron, eka-aluminium, and eka-silicon, from the Sanskrit eka for one, meaning one place beyond the element above. For eka-silicon he set down a grey metal of a particular weight and density that would form an oxide of a particular formula, all of it sight unseen.

The elements came. In 1875 a Frenchman isolated gallium, and its properties answered to eka-aluminium; in 1879 a Swede found scandium, the eka-boron; and in 1886 the German chemist Clemens Winkler drew out germanium and read off figures so close to Mendeleev’s eka-silicon that the prediction looked less a guess than a measurement taken years in advance. A chart that could foretell the unknown was no longer a teacher’s convenience.

Meyer had drawn a similar table and, cautious where Mendeleev was bold, had chosen not to predict. The two argued priority for a decade, a quarrel sharpened by a translation that turned Mendeleev’s Russian word for periodic into a German one meaning merely stepwise, and in 1882 the Royal Society split the honor, awarding both men its Davy Medal. Still, it was the predictions that fixed Mendeleev’s name to the table, because foretelling elements is what raised a description into a law.

One flaw remained. A few neighboring pairs sat in an order their weights could not justify: tellurium is heavier than iodine, yet its chemistry demands the earlier seat. The answer arrived in 1913, when the young English physicist Henry Moseley fired electrons at one element after another and measured the X-rays they threw back. The frequencies rose in exact whole-number steps, and Moseley saw that what truly ranks an element is the positive charge of its nucleus, the count of protons we now call the atomic number. Order the table by that charge and every stubborn pair falls into place. Moseley was killed at Gallipoli two years later, twenty-seven years old; the atomic number outlived him as the table’s true spine.

· · ·

Mendeleev never learned why his law held. The electron and the nucleus were found after him, and he doubted both to the end. The reason is that the columns he built by hand are columns of shared electron structure, so the table now explains the very chemistry it was first drawn to summarize. And it keeps working. The noble gases, unknown in 1869, slid in as a whole new column; the synthetic elements forged in reactors take the seats their properties assign them. A century and a half on, a chemist still reads the grid the way Mendeleev taught the world to read it, across for the trend and down for the family, and knows how an element will behave before ever meeting it.
