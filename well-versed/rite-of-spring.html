The night modern music arrived in a riot

The Rite of Spring

A pagan sacrifice danced by stamping, turned-in bodies to music no one had heard before, staged in Paris on a spring night in 1913 and met with one of the loudest scandals in the history of art.

On the evening of 29 May 1913 a fashionable crowd filled the brand-new Théâtre des Champs-Élysées on the avenue Montaigne, one of the most glittering houses Paris had. The lights went down and a solo bassoon began to climb into a register so high the instrument sounds strained and thin, almost like a voice in pain. Within minutes the audience was jeering. By the end the noise in the hall had nearly swallowed the orchestra, and one of the most influential works of the century had been given for the first time to a house at war with itself.

The evening belonged to the Ballets Russes, Sergei Diaghilev’s Russian company that had spent four seasons dazzling Paris with exotic colour and the leaps of its star dancer. This was something else. Le Sacre du printemps, the Rite of Spring, staged a pagan spring rite out of prehistoric Russia: tribes gather, the earth is worshipped, and a young woman is chosen to dance herself to death so that spring will return. The painter and archaeologist Nicholas Roerich devised the scenario and drew the earth-toned costumes and backdrops from his study of Slavic folklore. The music was by a thirty-year-old Igor Stravinsky.

Nijinsky’s choreography was the immediate provocation. Vaslav Nijinsky, the company’s idolized male star, had turned the classical vocabulary inside out. Where ballet reaches upward, light and turned-out, his dancers stood pigeon-toed and hunched, feet turned in, pounding the boards flat-footed in heavy rhythmic beats and jerking their heads into profile. To an audience raised on grace it looked less like dancing than like a seizure.

Trouble started before the first scene had ended. There were catcalls and mocking laughter, then whistling and shouting, then the ballet’s defenders shouting back; accounts tell of objects thrown and near-brawls in the boxes, though how far the “riot” actually went is still argued by historians. What is certain is the chaos onstage. The dancers could no longer hear the orchestra over the din, so Nijinsky climbed onto a chair in the wings and screamed the counts to them in Russian, while the conductor Pierre Monteux, on Diaghilev’s order, drove the players straight through to the end without stopping.

WatchThe Nijinsky choreography, reconstructed by Hodson and Archer

Nijinsky’s original steps were lost within a year of the premiere and rebuilt only in the 1980s from sketches and eyewitness memory. Watch the feet: turned inward, flat, hammering the floor on the beat, the bodies clenched and heavy where a classical dancer would rise and open. This is what Paris was not ready to see in 1913.

The scandal has almost buried what made the Rite genuinely new, which was the score. Stravinsky built it on rhythm before melody, hammering a single grinding chord over and over in the “Augurs of Spring” with the accents landing in unpredictable places, so the pulse lurches and the ear can never settle. He stacked unrelated harmonies on top of one another and scored the whole thing for an enormous orchestra that could roar. Concert music had never sounded like this, and a great deal of what came after it, from film scores to the rhythmic drive of the century’s popular music, took its lesson from that night.

Le Sacre barely survived its own premiere as a ballet. It was danced only a handful of times in 1913, and Nijinsky’s choreography was dropped and lost within the year, unseen in anything close to its original form until Millicent Hodson and Kenneth Archer spent fifteen years piecing it back together for the Joffrey Ballet in 1987. The music needed no rescue. At a concert performance in Paris in April 1914, again under Monteux, the very score that had provoked the riot was received with rapture; the crowd, the story goes, carried Stravinsky out of the hall on their shoulders, “a triumph,” he later said, “such as composers rarely enjoy.”

Within a generation the Rite had become a fixture of the concert hall, and in 1940 Disney set the birth of the world to it in Fantasia. The riot made it famous. What made it last was the sound Stravinsky found that night, a way of writing music around rhythm and shock that composers have been drawing on ever since.
