The Double Helix

How two men, a pile of cardboard, and a photograph they were not meant to see revealed the shape of the molecule of heredity.

At lunchtime on 28 February 1953, Francis Crick walked into the Eagle, a pub a few steps from the Cavendish Laboratory in Cambridge, and announced to the room that he and Jim Watson had found the secret of life. Watson, who recorded the line in his memoir, was mortified. They had run no experiment that morning. They had cut shapes out of stiff cardboard, the four chemical bases of DNA, and found that two of them locked together at one width and the other two at exactly the same width, so that a strand of any composition would always call up its precise complement. The cardboard told them how heredity copies itself.

For a century DNA had sat in the cell like a locked box whose contents everyone underrated. Most biologists in 1950 still assumed genes were made of protein. Deoxyribonucleic acid, a long monotonous chain of sugar, phosphate, and four repeating bases, looked far too plain to carry the whole inheritance of a living thing. The open question of the early 1950s was the molecule's architecture, and the architecture, it turned out, was the entire answer.

The sharpest evidence was being made at King's College London, where Rosalind Franklin, a physical chemist trained in X-ray crystallography, fired X-rays through purified fibres of DNA and read the structure off the shadows they threw onto film. In May 1952 she and her student Raymond Gosling captured the cleanest image yet of the wet, “B” form of the molecule, catalogued as Photograph 51. Its bold diagonal cross of smears was, to a trained eye, the signature of a helix. That coming January, Franklin's colleague Maurice Wilkins showed the photograph to Watson, without her knowledge. Watson later wrote that the sight left him stunned and his pulse racing.

Two more clues already sat on the table. Erwin Chargaff had found that in any sample of DNA the amount of adenine always equalled the amount of thymine, and guanine always equalled cytosine, a regularity nobody could explain. And the field's most formidable chemist, Linus Pauling, had just published a model with three chains twisted together and the phosphates crowded into the core, where their like charges would have torn the molecule apart. Pauling, denied any sight of Franklin's pictures, had got it wrong in print. The race now carried a clock.

Watson and Crick built no instruments. They built models, and they thought about fit. The turn came when Watson, shuffling his cut-out bases across a desk, saw that adenine joined to thymine made a pair the same size and shape as guanine joined to cytosine. Two strands could then run in opposite directions, sugar-phosphate backbones winding round the outside, with these matched rungs holding the rails a constant distance apart. Chargaff's ratios fell out on their own: every A faced a T, every G faced a C. And because each strand named the other, you could unzip the ladder and each half would rebuild its lost partner.

They published on 25 April 1953 in Nature, a single page, backed in the same issue by two papers from the King's College teams of Wilkins and of Franklin and Gosling, which supplied the X-ray proof. The article is famous now for its restraint. It opens with a shrug and closes with one of the most quietly devastating sentences in the history of science.

ReadWatson & Crick, Nature, 1953

“We wish to suggest a structure for the salt of deoxyribose nucleic acid (D.N.A.). This structure has novel features which are of considerable biological interest.”

“It has not escaped our notice that the specific pairing we have postulated immediately suggests a possible copying mechanism for the genetic material.”

The first and last sentences of the paper. Nature 171, 737–738, 25 April 1953.

That closing line is the hinge of modern biology. Once you know the structure, the mechanism of inheritance stops being a riddle to chase and becomes a thing to read straight off the geometry. The shape is the function. Within a decade the genetic code was cracked and the copying mechanism confirmed, and the science that now gives us mRNA vaccines and gene editing all runs downstream of two men and that pile of cardboard.

· · ·

Franklin never learned how much her data had shaped the model; the 1953 paper acknowledged the King's College work in a single glancing sentence. She died of ovarian cancer in 1958, at thirty-seven. The Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine went to Watson, Crick, and Wilkins in 1962, and its rules forbid awarding it to the dead, so what she might have shared can never be settled. What can be said is that the photograph she made is the reason the model is right, and that the understatement now taught in every biology class rests on an image she never agreed to share.
