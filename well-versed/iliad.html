The Iliad

The poem at the head of Western literature covers some fifty days of a ten-year war, and it spends its last line burying the loser.

The first word of the poem is anger. Greek word order allows it, so the Iliad opens on mēnin, the wrath, before the reader learns whose wrath or why: a heavy, ritual word that epic elsewhere reserves for the anger of gods. An audience in the eighth century BCE heard the subject of the next 15,000 lines announced in one syllable-cluster before the singer had even called on the Muse. Everything that follows, the deaths of Patroclus and Hector, the grief of two armies, the burning of the funeral pyres, unspools from one man's rage at being publicly shorted of his prize.

Knowing what the poem leaves out is half of knowing the poem. Across 15,693 lines in twenty-four books of dactylic hexameter, the verse stays inside roughly fifty days of the war's tenth year. There is no judgment of Paris, no Achilles' heel, no wooden horse, no fall of Troy; all of that lives in other poems and later authors. The story runs from Agamemnon's insult to Achilles, through Achilles' withdrawal from the fighting and the carnage his absence permits, to the death of his beloved companion Patroclus, his return, his killing of Hector, and the quiet final book in which the rage finally breaks. The last line of the epic that stands at the source of Western literature reads, in Lattimore's English, “Such was their burial of Hektor, breaker of horses.” A war poem soldiers have carried into the field for more than two thousand years closes on the funeral of the defeated.

Who wrote it is the oldest open question in the discipline, and the modern answer reshaped how the poem is read. In 1928 a young American scholar named Milman Parry demonstrated that the Homeric epics are built from inherited formulas, ready-made phrases fitted to the meter, which is why Achilles is “swift-footed” and Hector “of the shining helm” even in scenes where no one is running and no helmet is in sight. The epithets are load-bearing beams of a tradition of illiterate singers who improvised in performance, the way a jazz musician improvises through changes. The Iliad is the written crystallization of centuries of that singing, fixed around the eighth century BCE, just as alphabetic writing reached Greece. Whether “Homer” names one shaping genius or the tradition itself is the Homeric Question; the poem's design, most readers feel, argues for a master.

Its staying power lives in the small human scenes between the battles. In Book 6 Hector, home from the fighting, reaches for his infant son, and the baby screams at the nodding horsehair crest of his helmet; both parents burst out laughing, and Hector sets the helmet on the ground before kissing his child. In Book 9 Achilles lays out the choice his goddess mother told him he carries: stay at Troy and die young with imperishable glory, or sail home and live long in obscurity. The whole heroic code sits in that bargain, and the poem has the nerve to let its greatest hero call the bargain into doubt. And in Book 24 old Priam, king of Troy, slips into the Greek camp at night, kneels before the man who killed his son, and asks for the body back: “I put my lips to the hands of the man who has killed my children.” Achilles thinks of his own father and weeps with his enemy. The fury announced in the poem's first word ends there, dissolved in shared grief.

· · ·

Because so few now read it in Greek, the Iliad in English is really a shelf of rival poems, and the shelf is worth knowing as conversation in its own right. George Chapman's version, completed in 1611, so electrified the young John Keats one night in 1816 that he went home and wrote “On First Looking into Chapman's Homer” before breakfast. Alexander Pope's rhyming couplets of 1715 to 1720 made him rich and prompted the classicist Richard Bentley's reported verdict: a very pretty poem, but he must not call it Homer. The modern bench runs from Lattimore (1951), the scholars' choice, long six-beat lines that track the Greek almost word for word, through Robert Fagles (1990), muscular and propulsive, the one most people own, to Emily Wilson (2023), whose plain iambic pentameter opens “Goddess, sing of the cataclysmic wrath of great Achilles.” Pick a fight over which is best and you are already inside the tradition; arguing about Homer is the second-oldest game in Western letters, right after singing him.
