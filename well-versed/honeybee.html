The Honeybee

How a hive of fifty thousand insects works as one animal, and how a patient Austrian professor proved that a bee home from a good flower can tell the others exactly where to fly.

A forager comes home to the hive in the dark, having found a stand of clover a mile off, and she has no way to lead the others to it. The comb is black, the workers are packed shoulder to shoulder, and the flowers are a mile away over ground none of them has seen. So she dances. She runs a short straight line across the vertical face of the comb, shaking her abdomen fast from side to side, then loops back and runs the same line again, and again, tracing a flattened figure eight; and within minutes bees who only watched her are flying out of the hive and going straight to the clover. That performance is the waggle dance, and what it carries is a set of directions.

The Austrian zoologist Karl von Frisch spent much of his life reading it. Working from the 1920s into the 1940s with glass-walled hives and paint-marked bees, he found the dance to be exact. A find close to home, within about fifty meters, brings a simple round dance that says only near, go search. Anything farther brings the waggle, and the straight waggle run is the whole message. Its tilt gives the direction: a bee that runs straight up the comb means fly toward the sun, and one that runs forty degrees to the right of vertical means fly forty degrees to the right of the sun. The length of that run gives the distance. The bees translate toward the sun into straight up and read a meadow off a wall in the dark, and von Frisch showed they keep the sun's bearing even under cloud by reading the polarized light of the sky. This is abstract reference, a symbol standing in for a place, and nothing else in the insect world is known to do it. The 1973 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine went to von Frisch together with Konrad Lorenz and Nikolaas Tinbergen, the three men who founded the science of animal behavior.

Watch a returning forager run the straight waggle, wagging her abdomen, then loop back to run it again. The angle of that run off vertical is the angle to the sun, and the bees crowding around her are reading the directions off her body.

The dancer is one cell of something larger. A honeybee colony runs on a single fertile queen, who may lay fifteen hundred to two thousand eggs a day, on tens of thousands of sterile female workers who do every job the hive needs, and on a few hundred male drones whose one task is to mate. No bee governs the rest. The queen rules nothing; she is an ovary the colony keeps fed, while the workers decide together, through the dances and through chemical signals, where to forage, when to raise a new queen, and whether to swarm. A worker lives about six weeks in summer and spends them climbing a career, nurse then builder then guard then forager, as her body ages. Biologists call the colony a superorganism, because the thing that feeds and defends and reproduces itself is the whole hive rather than the single bee, and a lone honeybee, like a cell scraped off a body, soon dies.

This matters well past the wonder of it. A bee working a flower for nectar carries pollen from bloom to bloom on the branched hairs of her body, and by doing so she fertilizes the plants, which is why so much of the food supply leans on her. Animal pollinators service roughly a third of the crops people eat, and the domesticated honeybee, trucked between orchards by the millions of hives, does most of that farmed work; the United States Department of Agriculture has valued honeybee pollination near fifteen billion dollars a year. That reliance turned frightening in the winter of 2006, when American keepers opened their hives to find the workers simply gone, no bodies left behind, the queen and the brood abandoned. The pattern was named Colony Collapse Disorder, and its cause is still argued over as a tangle of the parasitic Varroa mite, farm pesticides, viruses, and thin forage, a standing warning about resting so much of the harvest on one insect.

The way we keep them is younger than it looks. For most of history a hive was a fixed mass of comb inside a straw basket or a hollow log, and taking the honey meant wrecking the nest. Then in 1851 a Philadelphia clergyman named Lorenzo Langstroth noticed that his bees leave any gap of about three-eighths of an inch open as a passage, while they glue shut anything narrower and fill anything wider with comb. He built a hive of hanging wooden frames spaced at exactly that bee space, so each frame could be lifted out clean, read, and set back with the colony unharmed. Patented in 1852, it is essentially the white box standing in every apiary today, and it earned Langstroth the name father of modern beekeeping.

The thing to carry from the honeybee is the dance. In a brain the size of a grass seed, a forager just back from the field encodes the direction and the distance of a flower she found and hands that message to a sister who has never left the hive, and the sister flies straight out to it. No single bee grasps the meadow, or the colony, or the plan. The hive knows what no bee in it knows, and it says so by dancing on the wall.
