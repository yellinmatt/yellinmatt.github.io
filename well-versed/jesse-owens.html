Four golds at the Nazi Olympics

Jesse Owens

In ten days at Hitler’s Olympics, a sharecropper’s son from Alabama won four gold medals and turned a stadium built to showcase the master race into the stage for its clearest rebuttal.

In August 1936 a hundred thousand spectators filled the new Olympiastadion in Berlin, swastikas hung from every rail, and the whole spectacle was staged by the Nazi regime as a ten-day advertisement for racial supremacy. The fastest man on the field was a twenty-two-year-old Black American named Jesse Owens. Over those ten days he won four gold medals and broke or equalled Olympic records in each event, and he did it under the eyes of Adolf Hitler. No athlete has ever answered a political theory more completely with his own body.

Owens had come a long way to that track. He was born in 1913 in Oakville, Alabama, the son of a sharecropper and the grandson of people who had been enslaved. When he was nine his family joined the Great Migration north to Cleveland, where a teacher who heard the boy give his initials, J.C., in a soft Southern drawl wrote down “Jesse,” and the name held for the rest of his life. By college he was running for Ohio State and fast enough that the rest of the world was racing for second.

He had already done something close to impossible before Berlin. On 25 May 1935, at a college meet in Ann Arbor, Michigan, nursing a back so sore that he had to be coaxed into competing, Owens set three world records and tied a fourth in the space of about forty-five minutes. His long jump that afternoon, 8.13 metres, would stand as the world record for the next twenty-five years. Sportswriters still call it the greatest forty-five minutes in the history of sport, and no one has matched it since.

At the Berlin Games the events fell to him one after another. He took the 100 metres in 10.3 seconds, then the long jump at 8.06 metres, then the 200 metres around the turn in 20.7 seconds for a world record, and finally a leg of the 4×100 relay that crossed in 39.8 seconds, a mark that lasted twenty years. He was the first American track athlete to win four golds at a single Games, and no American matched him until Carl Lewis took the same four events in 1984.

Archival footage of Owens in the 100 metres, the long jump, and the relay. Watch his upright, almost gliding stride: he barely seems to strain while the field behind him is fighting the track.

The most lasting image of the Games is the long-jump podium. Owens had nearly fouled out of qualifying when the German champion Luz Long, blond and the very embodiment of the Aryan ideal the posters were selling, came over to him on the runway. The two struck up a friendship in plain view of Hitler’s box, and after Owens won, Long was the first to reach him, throwing an arm around the American as they walked off together. The often-told version, in which Long quietly coaches Owens past the foul line, is mostly embellishment that Owens himself later softened; the friendship was real. Long was killed in the war in 1943, and Owens went on writing to his family for years. “You can melt down all the medals and cups I have,” Owens said, “and they wouldn’t be a plating on the twenty-four-carat friendship I felt for Luz Long.”

The story most people half-remember, that Hitler stormed out rather than shake Owens’s hand, is largely a myth. Hitler had stopped publicly congratulating any winner after the first day, on the Olympic committee’s insistence that he greet all the champions or none. The snub Owens actually felt came from home. No telegram arrived from the White House and no invitation followed; only the white members of the team were received there. “Hitler didn’t snub me,” Owens said. “It was our president who snubbed me.” The American squad carried its own shadow too. On the morning of the relay the only two Jewish runners, Marty Glickman and Sam Stoller, were pulled from the team and replaced, a move widely read as a courtesy to the Nazi hosts.

Owens came home a hero with nowhere to be one. He rode at the front of a ticker-tape parade in New York, then was sent to a freight elevator at his own reception because the hotel would not let a Black man through its front doors. Stripped of his amateur standing for skipping a money-making European tour, he made a living for a while racing against horses and motorcycles at county fairs. “People said it was degrading,” he said. “But what was I supposed to do? I had four gold medals, but you can’t eat four gold medals.” The honors came late, a Presidential Medal of Freedom in 1976, four years before his death, and a Congressional Gold Medal long after. The ten days in Berlin never needed the medals to be understood. A regime had built a stadium to prove a doctrine, and one man had run straight through it.
