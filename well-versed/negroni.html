The Negroni

Florence, 1919. Three ingredients, equal parts, no shaker. The litmus test of a modern bar.

Origin

Florence, 1919

Method

Built, stirred

Ratio

1 : 1 : 1

The Negroni is the modern bartender's litmus test. Three ingredients in equal parts. No shaker, no flame, no obscure liqueur to track down. What you taste is the quality of the spirits and the discipline of the maker. The drink reads as elegant because the recipe refuses to hide behind anything.

01

The recipe codified by the International Bartenders Association is the recipe printed in a 1949 Barcelona bar manual.

  • 30 mlBitter Campari
  • 30 mlLondon dry gin
  • 30 mlSweet red vermouth

Pour directly into a chilled old fashioned glass filled with ice. Stir gently. Garnish with half an orange slice. Serious bars reach for Carpano Antica Formula on the vermouth; a juniper-forward London dry like Beefeater or Tanqueray on the gin.

02

Swap one ingredient and you have a new classic.

Boulevardier
boo-luh-var-dee-AY
Replace the gin with bourbon or rye. Heavier, oakier, better suited to cold weather. First printed in Harry McElhone's 1927 Barflies and Cocktails, named for a Paris expatriate magazine of the same era.
Negroni Sbagliato
neg-RO-nee zbal-YAH-toh
Replace the gin with sparkling wine, usually prosecco. Sbagliato means mistaken, after Mirko Stocchetto at Bar Basso in Milan in 1972, who reached for the wrong bottle. His son later insisted the swap was deliberate.
Florence, 1919

The popular story places the invention at Caffé Casoni on Via de' Tornabuoni in Florence. Count Camillo Negroni walked in one afternoon and asked the bartender Fosco Scarselli to fortify his usual Americano. Replace the soda, he said, with gin. Scarselli swapped the lemon garnish for orange to mark the new drink. Negroni's circle began ordering "one of Count Negroni's drinks," and the name stuck.

The history is shaky in places. A descendant, Colonel Hector Andres Negroni, has argued that no Count Camillo appears in the Negroni family genealogy. The cocktail historian David Wondrich confirmed Camillo existed and found that his grandfather Luigi was indeed a count, though the rank did not pass cleanly down. Wondrich also turned up Camillo in a 1902 New York business directory as a professor of fencing. The legend that Camillo spent years as a cowboy and riverboat gambler in the American West is unverified. The fencing master in Manhattan is real. The earliest printed 1:1:1 recipe surfaces in 1949, in Jacinto Sanfeliu Brucart's Spanish bar manual, already in the form we drink today.

Campari is the anchor. It is an Italian bitter liqueur of carmine red and quinine bite, developed in 1860 in Novara by Gaspare Campari, infused with a secret blend of herbs and citrus peel the family still does not publish. The sweet vermouth, a fortified wine aromatized with wormwood and other botanicals, rounds the bitterness with caramel and vanilla. The gin's juniper sharpens both. Italians drink bitter aperitivi before dinner because the bitterness primes the palate and stimulates appetite. The Negroni is the apex of that tradition.
