The Hippocratic Oath

The oldest promise in medicine is misquoted more than any other, and the real text is stranger and older than the maxim it gets remembered by.

Every spring, new doctors stand together in gowns and recite an oath before they are handed the power to cut, prescribe, and decide. The ceremony feels ancient because it is: the ritual descends from a short Greek text set down roughly twenty-four centuries ago, on the island of Kos in the Aegean. Ask most people what that text promises and they will answer with three words, “first, do no harm.” Those words appear nowhere in it. Almost everything the oath is famous for turns out to be something later ages read into it, and the document itself is a far odder thing than its reputation.

It comes down to us inside the Hippocratic Corpus, some sixty medical works gathered under the name of Hippocrates, the physician of Kos who lived from about 460 to 370 BC and who ancient writers already treated as the father of the craft. He almost certainly did not write the oath himself; his followers did, sometime in the fifth or fourth century BC. The classical scholar Ludwig Edelstein argued in 1943 that its unusual bans, on abortion and on giving poison, marked it as a Pythagorean document rather than a general physician’s creed, a theory later scholars have disputed for want of evidence of any Pythagorean school of medicine. What the text plainly is, on its face, is a loyalty pledge. Its longest passage binds the young doctor to the man who taught him, to hold that teacher as dear as his own parents, to support him in need, and to teach the art free of charge to the teacher’s sons. Medicine on Kos was a family trade, guarded by the clan of the Asclepiads, and the oath reads like the moment that trade began admitting outsiders, on the condition that they swear themselves into the family first.

The doctor swears by the healing gods, and the choice of gods is a small map of what medicine was. Apollo the physician, god of light and truth; Asclepius, Apollo’s son and the father-figure of the art; and Asclepius’s two daughters, Hygieia, whose name gives us hygiene and stands for keeping well, and Panacea, whose name means all-heal and stands for the cure. Having called those witnesses, the physician stakes his own fortune on the bargain: keep the oath and prosper in life and art, break it and let the reverse be his fate.

What the oath forbids is more specific, and stranger, than the vague benevolence people imagine. It bars the doctor from giving a deadly drug to anyone who asks, or even suggesting one, which rules out assisting a suicide. It bars him from giving a woman a pessary, a medicated plug, to cause an abortion. It forbids him to use the knife, even on a patient suffering from bladder stones, and to leave that work to the men trained in it, a clause that fixed a division of labor in which the physician kept his hands clean of surgery. It tells him to enter a sick person’s house for their benefit alone, to keep away from the beds of the household, free or enslaved, and to hold his tongue about anything he sees or hears there. Confidentiality, the one clause a modern patient would recognize instantly, sits at the very end.

ReadThe Hippocratic Oath, classical version

“I swear by Apollo the physician, and Asclepius, and Hygieia and Panacea and all the gods and goddesses as my witnesses, that, according to my ability and judgement, I will keep this Oath and this contract…

I will use those dietary regimens which will benefit my patients according to my greatest ability and judgement, and I will do no harm or injustice to them. I will not give a lethal drug to anyone if I am asked, nor will I advise such a plan; and similarly I will not give a woman a pessary to cause an abortion…

Whatever I see or hear in the lives of my patients, whether in connection with my professional practice or not, which ought not to be spoken of outside, I will keep secret, as considering all such things to be private.”

Translated by Michael North, National Library of Medicine, 2002.

Now the famous phrase. “First, do no harm” is Latin at heart, primum non nocere, and Hippocrates wrote in Greek, which is the first sign the attribution is off. The oath’s own wording is milder and broader: the doctor will do “no harm or injustice.” The crisp Latin maxim cannot be traced back to antiquity at all. Historians who went hunting for it find it surfacing in the nineteenth century, credited to a Paris clinician named Chomel and carried into English-language medicine by writers of the 1840s through 1860s. The closest the ancient corpus comes sits in the Epidemics, a separate work, where the physician is told to help, or at least to do no harm. Even that puts helping first. The proverb everyone quotes quietly inverts the priority the Greeks actually set.

That the oath survived at all is a story of steady editing. Christian Byzantium could not have its physicians swearing by Apollo, so scribes swapped the pagan invocation for a Christian preamble and, in one twelfth-century manuscript, wrote the whole text out in the shape of a cross. The same pressure works today. A physician bound to the letter of the classical oath could perform no abortions, prescribe nothing lethal to the dying, take no fee for teaching, and never pick up a scalpel, so modern schools rarely use it. Most administer some oath at graduation, and the most popular is a rewrite composed in 1964 by Louis Lasagna, then dean at Tufts, which keeps the spirit and drops the gods and the bans. Its warmest line is the one that has lasted: the reminder that “warmth, sympathy, and understanding may outweigh the surgeon’s knife or the chemist’s drug,” and that a doctor treats “a sick human being” rather than a fever chart.

· · ·

Strip away the pagan gods, the abortion clause, the ban on surgery, and the guild loyalties, and something durable is left standing. The oath was the moment a person promised out loud, before witnesses and at the risk of their own good fortune, to hold their craft to a standard higher than their private judgment or their profit. Twenty-four centuries later the gowns and the words have changed almost beyond recognition, and the gesture underneath them has not. That is why the ceremony still feels like it means something, even to people who could not quote a line of it correctly.
