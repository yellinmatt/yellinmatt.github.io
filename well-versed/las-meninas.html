Madrid, the Royal Alcázar, 1656

Las Meninas

A Spanish princess and her retinue, a painter at his easel, a mirror on the far wall: one of the most studied pictures in Western art, and the one that seems to watch you back.

A five-year-old in a stiff white gown stands in the middle of a dim, high-ceilinged room, and everyone is attending to her, or rather, looking past her, toward us. One maid kneels with a tray. A second dips into a curtsy. A mastiff dozes across the front. Off to the left, half in shadow, a painter steps back from a canvas taller than he is and meets your eye. This is Las Meninas, finished by Diego Velázquez in 1656, and four centuries of viewers have stood where you are standing and felt the same small jolt: the picture seems to be watching them back. It hangs in the Museo del Prado in Madrid, ten and a half feet tall, and it is one of the most analyzed paintings in all of Western art.

Velázquez was the leading painter at the court of Philip IV, and the room is his own studio inside the royal Alcázar palace. The child is the Infanta Margarita, the king's only living child at the time and the plain center of the picture: the light finds her face, her pale hair, the bell of her skirt. Around her stands the apparatus of a princess. The two young women attending her are the meninas, the maids of honour who gave the painting the name it has carried only since 1843; for two centuries before that the palace inventories called it simply The Family of Philip IV. To the right are two of the court's dwarfs, Mari Bárbola and the boy Nicolás, who nudges the drowsing dog with his foot. A chaperone in mourning murmurs to a guard behind. Every figure was named, one by one, by the painter Antonio Palomino in 1724, which is how we still know who they all are.

Then you notice the small bright rectangle on the back wall. It is a mirror, and in it, faint and ghostly, hang two more faces: King Philip IV and Queen Mariana. They appear nowhere else in the painting, which means they are standing outside it, in the very spot you occupy as you look. The picture has quietly slipped you into the monarch's shoes. Either the royal couple are posing for the portrait Velázquez works on, their reflection thrown back off the hidden face of his canvas, or they have just entered and the whole room has turned to greet them. Velázquez never settles it, and that withheld answer is the engine of the painting.

Look back at the painter. On his black doublet sits a red cross, the badge of the Order of Santiago, a knighthood Velázquez did not receive until 1659, three years after he laid down his brush on this canvas. Palomino reports that the king ordered the cross added afterward, and that some said Philip painted it with his own hand. The detail carries weight, because painting in seventeenth-century Spain was held to be a manual trade rather than a liberal art, and the Order turned away anyone who worked with his hands. By setting himself at the easel, brush raised, inside the royal family's own room, Velázquez pressed a claim about his rank: the man who makes the image stands among the people he paints.

Deeper in, a figure pauses on a lit staircase, one foot raised, holding back a curtain. He is Don José Nieto, the queen's chamberlain, caught between coming and going. He stands at the exact vanishing point of the room's perspective, a doorway of daylight at the dark heart of the picture, and Velázquez built the whole space to draw the eye there and then release it back outward. The Prado's conservators have matched the dim paintings crowding the rear wall to the works the palace inventory recorded hanging in that very room, so the setting is no invention.

The picture has never stopped generating talk. The Italian painter Luca Giordano, allowed in to see it, called it “the theology of painting.” Michel Foucault opened his 1966 book The Order of Things with a long reading of it, treating the empty space in front of the canvas as the place where the modern idea of the spectator is born. And in 1957 Picasso shut himself away and reworked it fifty-eight times over, version after version, the whole suite now kept together at the Museu Picasso in Barcelona. Few paintings repay looking the way this one does, and almost none have been looked at so hard.

“We are looking at a picture in which the painter is in turn looking out at us. A mere confrontation, eyes catching one another's glance, direct looks superimposing themselves upon one another as they cross. And yet this slender line of reciprocal visibility embraces a whole complex network of uncertainties, exchanges, and feints.”

Michel Foucault, opening of The Order of Things (1966)
