Beef Bourguignon

America learned to cook French from this one Burgundian braise, and its real lesson is what a long, low fire and a bottle of red wine do to a cheap cut of beef.

Set a heavy pot of tough, cheap beef, a bottle of red wine, and a few carrots and onions over the lowest flame the stove will hold, and leave it for the better part of an afternoon. What you lift the lid on is not the gray, seized meat you would get from boiling. The beef pulls apart under a fork, the wine has thickened to something dark and glossy that coats a spoon, and the kitchen smells of a French restaurant. The method is braising, slow moist cooking in a little liquid, and beef bourguignon is its most famous single expression, the dish that put the technique on kitchen tables from Dijon to Des Moines.

Its bones are humble. You take chuck, the collagen-rich shoulder that is too tough to grill, brown it hard in a hot pan, then simmer it for hours in red wine and stock with carrots, an onion, garlic, and a bouquet garni of thyme and bay. What lifts it from stew to something with a name is the finish, the garniture bourguignonne: glazed pearl onions, sauteed mushrooms, and lardons, little batons of smoky bacon, folded in near the end. When a French menu says a dish is done à la bourguignonne, that trio and the red wine are exactly what it is promising.

WatchThe French Chef · WGBH Boston, 11 February 1963

The debut episode of Julia Child's series, once feared lost and later recovered. Watch how she browns the beef in batches, drying and coloring each piece before it ever meets the wine: the crust on the meat is where the flavor of the finished sauce is built.

Its fame in America is the work of one woman. In 1961 Julia Child, with her French collaborators Simone Beck and Louisette Bertholle, published Mastering the Art of French Cooking, a book that treated a home cook as capable of the real thing rather than a shortcut. Of this dish she wrote that, carefully done and perfectly flavored, it is “certainly one of the most delicious beef dishes concocted by man.” Two years later, on 11 February 1963, her show The French Chef went on the air at WGBH in Boston, and the first thing she cooked in front of a camera was bœuf bourguignon. A generation that had cooked from cans watched a tall, warbling woman debone the fear out of French food, and the country followed her into it.

The one thing beef bourguignon is not is an ancient heirloom. For all the talk of Burgundian grandmothers and medieval hunters, the dish shows up in print surprisingly late: first in Pierre Larousse's great dictionary of 1867, then codified by Auguste Escoffier in his Guide Culinaire of 1903, the book that fixed French restaurant cooking into a system and pinned down that exact garnish of onions, mushrooms, and salt pork. Some food historians think it began as a Parisian bistro stew before it was claimed by Burgundy at all. The truer story is the one hiding in the pot: a peasant trick for making a hard cut edible, dressed over time in good wine and a chef's precision until it could sit at any table.

· · ·

The reason to learn this one dish is that it teaches you all the others like it. Master the braise and you have quietly unlocked coq au vin, osso buco, short ribs, and half of what a bistro serves in winter, because they are the same three moves. You brown the meat hard first, for the deep savor the Maillard reaction builds on a dry seared surface. Then you give it hours of gentle, wet heat, during which the collagen that makes chuck tough slowly melts into gelatin, turning the meat tender and the liquid silky at the same time. The wine should be a dry red you would actually drink, not the priciest bottle in the shop and never the jug labeled “cooking wine.” Do that once and you will understand every braise you ever meet, which is a decent return on an afternoon by the stove.
