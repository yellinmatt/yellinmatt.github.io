Chiasmus & Antimetabole

The oldest crossing trick in oratory, and the test that tells the line that earns it from the one that only sounds like it.

The cold was the first thing anyone mentioned. Eight inches of snow had fallen on Washington overnight, the new president stood bareheaded at the East Front of the Capitol on 20 January 1961, and fourteen minutes later he had given a speech that schoolchildren would still be reciting sixty years on. One sentence carried most of the weight: “Ask not what your country can do for you; ask what you can do for your country.” Strip away the occasion and a small machine is doing the work. Four key words appear, then the same four appear again in flipped order, and the flip is the whole point. The Greeks had a name for that machine. They called it chiasmus.

The name comes from the letter chi, written X, and means something like “a diagonal arrangement.” Chiasmus is the repetition of ideas or grammar in inverted order: a structure that runs A, B, then B, A, so that the second half mirrors the first across an unseen center. When the inverted terms are the very same words, as in Kennedy's line, the figure has a sharper name of its own, antimetabole, from the Greek for “turning about.” The distinction is worth carrying. Chiasmus can cross with synonyms or with parallel grammar alone; antimetabole crosses with identical words. Every antimetabole is a chiasmus; not every chiasmus repeats its words. The reference handbooks often fold the two together and treat the labels as interchangeable, which is fine for reading and useless for showing off. The person who knows that “ask not” is specifically antimetabole is the person worth having at the table.

The trick is ancient and was catalogued early. The Rhetorica ad Herennium, a Latin handbook from around the 80s BCE once attributed to Cicero, lists it under the name commutatio among the figures a young advocate was expected to drill until they came without thinking. The shape is older than any of its names. Hebrew scripture is built on it: Isaiah warns against those who “call evil good, and good evil; that put darkness for light, and light for darkness,” and the Gospel of Matthew turns the social order over in six words, “the first shall be last, and the last shall be first.” The form keeps reappearing because it does something no flat sentence can. It performs its own meaning. A sentence about reversal physically reverses, and the ear registers the symmetry as a kind of proof before the mind has checked the claim.

ReadKennedy, Inaugural Address, 1961

“So let us begin anew, remembering on both sides that civility is not a sign of weakness, and sincerity is always subject to proof. Let us never negotiate out of fear. But let us never fear to negotiate.”

“And so, my fellow Americans: ask not what your country can do for you—ask what you can do for your country.”

Inaugural Address, 20 January 1961. Transcript, U.S. National Archives.

Kennedy gave the audience two antimetaboles minutes apart, and the difference between them is the lesson. “Never negotiate out of fear; never fear to negotiate” flips negotiate and fear and lands a genuine second thought: the first clause guards against weakness, the second against paralysis, and only the crossing holds both at once. The closing line does the same heavier work, turning the citizen's relationship to the state inside out so that obligation runs the other way. Both pass a test that not every glittering reversal survives.

That test is the only thing worth memorizing here, because the figure is so satisfying that it will counterfeit depth on demand. Reversing a string of words is easy; reversing a thought is hard, and the two feel identical for about a second. So cover the second half of any chiasmus you meet and ask whether the first half had already told you something. “When the going gets tough, the tough get going” mostly enjoys its own symmetry; the swap adds little the locker room did not already have. Samuel Johnson's “integrity without knowledge is weak and useless, and knowledge without integrity is dangerous and dreadful” passes cleanly, because the two halves carry opposite warnings. The figure multiplies force. It does not generate it, and it flatters an empty line and a full one with exactly the same music.

· · ·

So keep the move in your pocket and keep the test beside it. When a speaker's sentence lands a little too neatly, run the cover-the-second-half check, and you will know at once whether you have caught a real turn of thought or just a pleasing noise. Then, if the moment allows, you can note that the device has a name, that the strict version with the repeated words is antimetabole, and that the shape on the page, the two clauses crossing at the center, is the letter the Greeks named it for.
