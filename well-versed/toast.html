The Toast

How a piece of spiced bread floating in Elizabethan wine became the social ritual everyone is eventually drafted to perform, and the rules that still make one land.

Sooner or later someone taps a glass and the table turns to you. The toast is the one piece of public speaking almost everyone is eventually drafted into, at a wedding, a retirement, a milestone birthday, and it is the easiest thing in the world to do badly: too long, too vague, too much about the speaker. The reassuring part is that the form is old, generous, and forgiving, and the handful of rules that make a toast land have barely moved in three hundred years.

Start with the word, which is stranger than it looks. In the alehouses of Elizabethan England a piece of spiced, charred bread was dropped into the cup of wine or ale, partly to soften a harsh drink and partly to carry the spice through it. Shakespeare has Falstaff bellow for exactly this in The Merry Wives of Windsor: “Go fetch me a quart of sack; put a toast in’t.” The sodden bread bobbing in the cup was the toast.

Around 1700 the word leapt from the cup to a person. In the men’s clubs of the day it became the fashion to drink to the health of some celebrated lady, and the conceit took hold that her name flavored the wine the way the old spiced bread once had. She was the toast. From there it spread to anyone whose health was drunk, so that the most admired figure in a city became its toast of the town, a phrase that has long outlived any memory of the soggy bread behind it.

The custom runs far deeper than the English word for it. The Greeks at a symposium poured the first of the wine to the gods and drank in turn to one another’s health, and the Romans made the libation, a few drops tipped out for the divine or the dead, a fixed part of any serious drinking. To lift a cup to someone was to wish them well aloud and to call the others present as witnesses to the wish. The gesture was social long before it was merely polite.

One piece of toasting lore is worth retiring. You will hear that we clink glasses to scare off the devil with the bell-like ring, or that wary medieval drinkers knocked their cups hard enough to slosh wine from one to another and so prove neither had been poisoned. Both are fine stories and almost certainly invented after the fact: there is no real evidence for the poison version, and people were clinking long before anyone wrote down a reason. The likelier explanation is plainer. A drink already engages taste, smell, sight, and touch, and the clink supplies the missing fifth sense, sound, while binding the whole table into a single gesture at a single moment.

Which leaves the part that matters once the table has turned to you. Stand, and wait; you do not need to bang on a glass, because standing with a raised glass will gather the room within a few seconds. Then keep it short, a minute or two at the very most, since the best toasts are brief and the bad ones never are. Say who you are to the person being honored, tell one specific story instead of a string of adjectives, name the quality that the story quietly proves, and close on a clear line that tells everyone to lift their glasses. Emily Post’s rule for the honoree is the one people forget: you never drink to yourself, so when the glasses rise for you, stay seated, then stand afterward to say thank you.

A toast is a small machine for paying attention in public, and that is the whole of both its difficulty and its charm. The spiced bread is long gone and the gods no longer get the first pour, yet the gesture survives because it still does the one thing it always did, which is to stop a room, name something good, and ask everyone there to agree to it out loud.
