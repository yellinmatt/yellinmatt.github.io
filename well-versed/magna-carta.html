Magna Carta

A failed peace treaty between a cornered king and his rebel barons, dead within nine weeks, that later ages rewrote into the founding charter of liberty under law.

On a damp meadow at Runnymede, on the bank of the Thames between Windsor and Staines, on the fifteenth of June 1215, a defeated King John let his clerks press the great seal of England into wax at the foot of a charter his barons had written for him. He had run out of room to refuse. London was in rebel hands, his treasury was empty, and the men kneeling around him in the grass had renounced their oaths of allegiance and called themselves an army. The document reads today like a founding act of constitutional government. It began as something closer to a ransom note, the price a beaten king paid to call off a revolt.

The king was John, known in his own time as John Lackland, who had taken the throne in 1199 and spent the next fifteen years losing things. He lost Normandy to the French crown in 1204 and his last hope of winning it back at the battle of Bouvines in 1214. He lost his standing with the Church in a long quarrel with Pope Innocent III that left England under a papal interdict and John himself excommunicated, until he submitted in 1213 by handing his kingdom to the pope and receiving it back as a fief. Every failed campaign had to be paid for, and John paid for them by squeezing the barons who owed him service: punishing fees to inherit an estate, the sale of wardships and marriages, scutage levied again and again on men who would not follow him to France.

By the spring of 1215 the northern barons had stopped paying and started marching. They took London in May, drew up their grievances, and rallied behind Robert Fitzwalter under the grand title Marshal of the Army of God and Holy Church. Stephen Langton, the Archbishop of Canterbury whose appointment John had fought so bitterly, worked the ground between the two sides and helped shape the demands into something a king might seal. What he sealed at Runnymede ran to sixty-three clauses.

Most of them are the small print of a feudal quarrel, and they make strange reading for anyone expecting a hymn to freedom. Clauses fix the king's debts, return hostages, standardize the measures of wine and cloth, pull fish-weirs out of the Thames, and promise that a widow will not be forced to remarry. The barons were defending their own pockets and privileges; the common people barely entered their thinking, and the charter's protections reach only the “free man,” perhaps a quarter of England in an age when most men were unfree villeins bound to a lord's land. Buried in that thicket of feudal housekeeping sit two short clauses, the thirty-ninth and the fortieth, that say something larger than their authors quite intended.

The charter failed almost at once. John had sealed it under threat and never meant to keep it, and within weeks he had written to Rome asking to be released from his oath. On the twenty-fourth of August 1215 Pope Innocent III obliged, annulling the whole document in a bull that called it shameful, unlawful, and “null and void of all validity for ever.” England fell into civil war. The rebel barons offered the crown to Prince Louis of France, who landed and held half the country, and the fighting was still going when John died of dysentery in October 1216, the charter a dead letter barely a season old.

It was rescued by the people who had the most to gain from a tamed monarchy. The regents of John's nine-year-old son, Henry III, reissued the charter in 1216 and again in 1217, pairing it that year with a smaller Charter of the Forest, and it is from that pairing that it took its name: the larger document was the magna carta, the great charter, great in size before it was great in meaning. A definitive version followed in 1225, and in 1297 Edward I confirmed it onto the statute roll, where it became part of the law of England. Four centuries later, lawyers led by Sir Edward Coke went back to clause 39 and read into it trial by jury, habeas corpus, and the principle that the crown is bound by law, weapons they turned against the Stuart kings in the Petition of Right of 1628. The American colonists inherited the same reading; its “law of the land” became the “due process of law” of the Fifth Amendment, and in 1948 Eleanor Roosevelt called the new Universal Declaration of Human Rights “a Magna Carta for all mankind.”

· · ·

Of the sixty-three clauses, three still stand in English law today; the rest were repealed or quietly went obsolete. The honest account of Magna Carta is that it created no democracy, freed no serfs, and settled nothing for more than a few weeks. Its power came from being read forward. Each generation that needed a check on power found it in those lines and quietly widened the meaning of “free man” until it covered everyone, so the charter came to matter less for what its barons wrote in 1215 than for what later centuries decided they had written. When someone reaches for Magna Carta now, the thing they are reaching for is the idea that came of it: that the king, too, stands under the law.
