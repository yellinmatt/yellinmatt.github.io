The scandal that became the world’s favourite opera

Carmen

The opera that scandalized Paris in 1875, broke its composer three months before it conquered the world, and hid two of the most familiar tunes ever written inside a story nobody wanted to watch.

On the evening of 3 March 1875 the audience at the Opéra-Comique in Paris sat through something it had not paid to see. The house was the home of light, well-mannered entertainment, the sort of theatre a respectable family attended together, and its operas ended with order restored and lovers reconciled. That night the family watched a soldier knife a woman to death on the steps of a bullring while the crowd inside roared for the matador. The woman was Carmen, a cigarette-factory worker who took her lovers as she pleased and answered to no one, and Paris did not know what to do with her. Her composer, Georges Bizet, went home certain he had written a catastrophe.

The story came from a hard little novella that Prosper Mérimée had published in 1845, which the librettists Henri Meilhac and Ludovic Halévy reshaped for the stage. The setting is Seville. Carmen is a Romani woman working the tobacco factory; Don José is the dutiful corporal who throws away his rank, his gentle fiancée Micaëla, and at last his freedom to follow her; Escamillo is the swaggering toreador who supplants him in her affections. An opéra-comique in this period was not necessarily comic. The term meant a French opera that mixed sung numbers with spoken dialogue, and the genre carried an unspoken promise of charm. Bizet broke the promise.

The murder was only part of the trouble. What truly unsettled the first audiences was the heroine. Carmen wants what she wants, sleeps with whom she likes, and goes to her death without a flicker of repentance, and the opera declines either to punish her with a moral or to soften the evening with a happy ending. The first run played to half-empty houses even after the management handed out free tickets, and for a stretch it shared the season with Verdi’s far more popular Requiem. The critics called it obscene. The box office agreed.

Yet Bizet had buried two melodies in the score that the whole world now carries by heart. Carmen introduces herself with the Habanera, “L’amour est un oiseau rebelle” (love is a rebellious bird), a slow, coiling dance over a bass line that slides downward step by step like something circling. A habanera is a Cuban dance rhythm that had drifted back across the Atlantic into Spanish popular song, and Bizet lifted this particular tune from a number called “El Arreglito,” by the Spanish composer Sebastián Iradier, in the belief that it was folk music. Told that it had an author who had died a decade earlier, he added a small credit to the printed score. He rewrote the aria something like a dozen times to fit the voice of his Carmen, the mezzo-soprano Célestine Galli-Marié. The Toreador’s Song from the second act is the other melody nearly everyone can hum without knowing where it comes from.

Carmen’s entrance aria, the Habanera. Listen for the bass walking slowly downward under her while the melody keeps slipping off the beat, never quite landing where you expect. She is describing love as a wild bird that no one can cage, and the music refuses to be pinned down in exactly the same way. The men onstage gather around her without meaning to, which is the whole point.

Bizet never learned how wrong he had been. On the night of 3 June 1875, during the opera’s thirty-third performance, he died of a heart attack at thirty-six, three months to the day after the premiere. He was a Paris prodigy who had won the Prix de Rome at nineteen and spent his short career searching for the success that arrived only after he was in the ground. He went believing that Carmen had failed him.

The turn came almost at once. A production in Vienna that October carried the opera out of Paris and across Europe, and within a decade Carmen was among the most performed works in the repertory, where it has stayed ever since. Tchaikovsky heard it in 1876 and predicted that in ten years it would be the most popular opera in the world. Friedrich Nietzsche seized on it as the cure for everything he had come to hate in Richard Wagner, all that German fog and grandeur. “Yesterday—would you believe it?—I heard Bizet’s masterpiece for the twentieth time,” he wrote, calling for a “Mediterraneanization of music,” for sunlight and health and a heroine who loves like a force of nature and pays for it without complaint.

So the factory girl who emptied a Paris theatre now opens more seasons than almost any work ever written, and the tune she walks on to is, for a great many people, the very first thing they recognize as opera. The audience that recoiled in 1875 was right about one thing. They had never seen a woman like her on that stage. They simply mistook that for a reason to stay away.
