The Transistor

The twentieth century's most consequential invention debuted in New Jersey two days before Christmas, 1947, and the press shrugged.

On the afternoon of December 16, 1947, in a laboratory at Bell Telephone Laboratories in Murray Hill, New Jersey, Walter Brattain pressed a plastic triangle wrapped in gold foil against a slab of germanium the size of a fingernail. The foil was slit at the triangle's point, so the crystal felt two gold contacts a hair's width apart, held down by a makeshift spring. When Brattain fed a small signal into one contact, a much larger copy of it flowed out of the other: the crystal amplified, up to a hundredfold, with no glowing filament, no glass bulb, no warm-up, and almost no power. A week later, on December 23, he and the theorist John Bardeen demonstrated the device to the laboratory brass, passing spoken words through the crystal and out a set of headphones. Their group leader, William Shockley, called it “a magnificent Christmas present.”

The thing it replaced explains the excitement. Electronics had run for forty years on the vacuum tube, which could amplify and switch but did so as a small hot lightbulb: fragile, power-hungry, and short-lived, a chronic headache for a phone company pushing calls across a continent. Bell's research chief Mervin Kelly had built Shockley's solid-state group in 1945 precisely to find a replacement in the wartime semiconductor materials, germanium and silicon. The new device needed a name, and the engineer John Pierce won the office ballot with transistor, transfer crossed with resistor, beating out “crystal triode” and “Iotatron.” Bell Labs unveiled it at a New York press conference on June 30, 1948, and the next morning The New York Times gave the founding device of the digital age three paragraphs at the bottom of “The News of Radio” on page 46, below word that the program Waltz Time was coming to NBC.

The three inventors were already pulling apart. Bell's patent lawyers wrote Shockley out of the application, since the working device rested on Bardeen's theory and Brattain's hands, and Shockley, stung, shut himself in a Chicago hotel room over New Year's and worked out something better on his own: the junction transistor, a layered sandwich of treated semiconductor with no delicate metal points, the rugged design factories could actually mass-produce. The famous publicity photograph tells the credit story in one frame. Shockley sits at Brattain's microscope as if at the controls, the two men who built the device standing behind him, and Brattain hated the staging ever after. The Nobel committee split the 1956 physics prize three ways and got it right. Bardeen, by then at the University of Illinois, won a second physics Nobel in 1972 for the theory of superconductivity; he remains the only person to win two.

Shockley cashed in his fame in 1956, leaving Bell to found Shockley Semiconductor Laboratory in Mountain View, California, in the apricot-orchard country south of San Francisco, chosen partly so he could be near his aging mother in Palo Alto. Engineers who knew him declined to follow, so he recruited brilliant young ones, then drove them out: he second-guessed everything, redirected research away from the silicon transistor they had been promised, and demanded lie-detector tests after a secretary cut her hand and he suspected sabotage. In September 1957 eight of them resigned together: Robert Noyce, Gordon Moore, Jean Hoerni, Eugene Kleiner, Jay Last, Sheldon Roberts, Victor Grinich, and Julius Blank. Shockley branded them the traitorous eight. With $1.38 million from the camera-and-instrument maker Sherman Fairchild, a deal brokered by a young banker named Arthur Rock who would go on to more or less invent venture capital, they founded Fairchild Semiconductor a few miles up the road.

Fairchild built the industry. Hoerni's planar process of 1959 made transistors flat, stable, and printable, and within months Noyce used it to lay several of them on one sliver of silicon: the integrated circuit, conceived independently of Jack Kilby at Texas Instruments, whose cruder version had worked the September before. Kilby collected the Nobel for it in 2000, a decade after Noyce's death. Fairchild's people then did to Fairchild what the eight had done to Shockley, spinning out the dozens of firms the trade press called the Fairchildren: Noyce and Moore founded Intel in 1968, and Kleiner co-founded the venture firm Kleiner Perkins in 1972. Moore had already written the region's business plan in a 1965 magazine article observing that the number of components on a chip was doubling every year, a pace he revised in 1975 to every two; everyone else named it Moore's law. The place got its name last. In January 1971 the trade journalist Don Hoefler titled a column series on the chip business “Silicon Valley U.S.A.,” and the orchards were renamed for good.

· · ·

The arithmetic since then has no precedent in the history of manufacture. The first transistor filled a palm; the processor in a current iPhone carries on the order of twenty billion, each switching billions of times a second, each costing a vanishingly small fraction of a cent. By unit count the transistor is the most manufactured artifact humans have ever produced, and every digital thing you own, phone and laptop and car and watch, is Brattain's gold-wrapped wedge shrunk past seeing and repeated without end. At the 1948 unveiling, a Bell spokesman ventured that the device “may have far-reaching significance in electronics and electrical communication.” It stands among the great understatements on record.
