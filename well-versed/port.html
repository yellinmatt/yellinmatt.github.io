Port

Portugal's great fortified wine: how it is made, the styles worth knowing, and the one ritual that tells a guest from a gentleman.

Home

Douro Valley, Portugal

Strength

19–22% ABV

Aged at

Vila Nova de Gaia

Pour a glass of Port after dinner and you are drinking a wine that was deliberately interrupted. Partway through fermentation, while the yeast is still turning grape sugar into alcohol, the winemaker adds a clear grape spirit called aguardente and stops the whole process at once. The spirit kills the yeast, so the sugar it had not yet eaten stays behind, and the wine finishes sweet and strong, somewhere between nineteen and twenty-two percent alcohol. That single act, fortification, is the entire idea: a red wine caught halfway and held there.

All of it begins in the Douro, a gorge of slate terraces climbing above a river in northern Portugal, where the summers are punishing and many of the vines are old. In 1756 the Marquês de Pombal drew a boundary around these hills and graded the vineyards inside it, making the Douro the first wine region in the world to be formally demarcated, its edges fixed by stone pillars that still stand. The British had a hand in the trade well before that. The Methuen Treaty of 1703 gave Portuguese wine a tariff advantage in Britain, and merchant families from England and Scotland settled in to ship it north.

Port has always led a double life across two towns. The wine is made up in the Douro, then traditionally carried downriver to age in the cool riverside lodges of Vila Nova de Gaia, on the bank opposite the city of Porto that gives the drink its name. The barrels once made that trip by rabelo, the flat-bottomed sailing boat built to run the river's rapids. The names over the great lodges read like a roll call of a London club, Taylor's, Graham's, Warre's, Dow's, Cockburn's, Sandeman, because for two centuries the British shippers ran the business.

If you remember one thing about Port, make it this: how it ages splits in two. A wine sealed in glass matures slowly and without air, staying deep red and fruity, and in the case of a great Vintage it can improve for half a century or more. A wine left in small oak casks ages the opposite way, breathing air year upon year until its colour fades from ruby to amber and its flavour turns toward walnut, dried fig, and caramel. That wood-aged style is Tawny. Almost everything on a Port list is a variation on those two paths.

The styles

01

Ruby and Reserve

The base of the pyramid: young, fruity, and made to be poured without ceremony.

  • MadeA blend of several years, aged briefly in bulk and bottled young
  • TasteDeep purple-red, sweet, simple, fiery fruit
  • ReserveA better-quality blend of the same idea, richer and rounder

02

Late Bottled Vintage

The sensible everyday route to a vintage character, invented by Taylor's in the 1960s.

  • MadeWine of a single year, aged four to six years in vat, then bottled
  • ServingMost are filtered, so no decanting and no crust
  • NoteUnfiltered "traditional" LBV is fuller and can age further in bottle

03

Vintage

The summit, declared only in exceptional years, usually three or four times a decade.

  • MadeThe best year's best lots, bottled after about two years in wood
  • AgesThen for decades in the bottle; the finest last a lifetime
  • ServingThrows a heavy crust, so it must be decanted off its sediment

04

Aged Tawny

Vintage's counterpart, and to many its equal: mellow, nutty, and gently oxidative.

  • MadeBlended and aged for years in small casks, called pipes
  • LabelAn age indication of the blend's average: 10, 20, 30, or 40 years
  • ServingLovely slightly chilled; ready to drink, no decanting

05

Colheita and White

The connoisseur's tawny and the summer surprise most people never meet.

  • ColheitaA tawny from one single harvest, aged at least seven years in wood; two dates on the label, harvest and bottling
  • WhiteMade from white grapes; served cold, and with tonic it is a genuine aperitif
At the table

The custom

Passing the Port, and the Bishop of Norwich.

A Vintage or other unfiltered Port throws a sediment, or crust, so it is decanted off it before serving; a tawny or a filtered LBV needs no decanting, and a tawny is often served a touch chilled. Then there is the ritual of the table. The decanter starts to the host's right and moves only to the left, clockwise, and is never passed back across the table. If it stalls in front of a distracted guest, the gentle prompt is to ask, "Do you know the Bishop of Norwich?", a nudge named for a nineteenth-century bishop who kept dozing off and hoarding the bottle at his elbow.
