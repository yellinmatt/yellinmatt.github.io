Financial statements as a system

One transaction, three statements, one story.

Predict · before you read on

A company records $10 of depreciation on equipment it already owns. No cash leaves the building. What happens to its cash this period?

Reveal

Commit to a guess first — then read on to find out. (The answer is more surprising than it looks, and holding your guess against it is what makes it stick.)

A lawyer who cannot read the three statements is reading half a deal. The purchase price, the covenants, the earn-out, the indemnity caps: every number that matters in an agreement is priced off the same financial system, and that system has exactly three moving parts that are welded together. The single most useful thing you can know about them is that they are not three documents. They are one.

The income statement asks a single question over a period: did the business make money? Revenue at the top, expenses beneath, net income at the bottom. The balance sheet asks a different one, frozen at an instant: what does the business own, what does it owe, and what is left for the owners? Assets on one side; liabilities and equity on the other; the two sides equal by construction. The cash flow statement asks the most honest question of the three: forget the accounting, how much actual cash moved, and where did it go?

The seam

Here is the joint that holds the machine together. Net incomeDefinitionThe bottom line of the income statement: revenue minus every expense, including tax. An accounting result, not a cash balance. does not go into anyone's pocket. It leaves the bottom of the income statement and lands in exactly two places. It increases retained earnings on the balance sheet, and it is the starting line of the cash flow statement, from which we back out the non-cash items to find real cash. Change one statement and the other two must move, or the books do not balance and the model is wrong.

Trace one transaction through all three and keep the balance sheet balanced, and you can read any deal's numbers.

Check yourself
Net income lands in which two places — and why must the balance sheet still balance afterward?

Retained earnings (inside equity) and the top of the cash flow statement. The balance sheet stays balanced because the same net income that raised equity also drove the matching change in assets and cash on the other side. One number, two landing spots, both sides move together.

Watch it move

The cash-flow line is the surprising one. Read it as a bridge: net income starts below zero, the non-cash charge is added back, and cash lands in positive territory.

Read what just happened, and check it against the guess you committed at the top. A pure paper charge with no cash cost ended up increasing cash by $2, because it lowered the tax bill. Check yourself · run the mechanic

Same $10 charge, but now the tax rate is 35%. How much cash does the shield produce?

The trap most people fall for

The assumption: "Depreciation is an expense, so more of it always means less money." It feels obviously true — and it is how a purchase-price or earn-out fight gets misread.

Why it's wrong: depreciation is a non-cash charge. It lowers reported profit, but no cash leaves the business — and because it is tax-deductible, it actually increases cash by the tax it shields. The rule to carry: separate the accounting result from the cash result. Every clause that defines a number picks which lines of this system count and which get added back; you cannot draft or challenge that clause if you cannot see the flow.

Practice · your hands

Pull the seam from a real filing

Open EDGAR, pick any 10-K you like, and find three numbers: net income (income statement), the change in retained earnings (balance sheet or statement of equity), and the top line of the cash flow statement. Confirm that net income is the starting number on the cash flow statement, and that it moved retained earnings. Write the three figures and the company in one line.

Right result: the net income figure appears verbatim as the first line of cash from operations, and the year-over-year change in retained earnings equals net income minus any dividends paid.
