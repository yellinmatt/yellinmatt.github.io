The income statement

Revenue to net income, and what each line hides.

8 min read · predict · worked example · your turn · practice ≈ 10 min on a real 10-K

Already read income statements line by line? Skip to the worked waterfall ↓

Predict · before you read on

A company posts record net income this year, up 38% from last year. Yet its operating income — profit from actually running the business — fell. Possible? And if so, how?

Reveal

Commit to a guess first — then read on to find out. The staircase below shows exactly where the two numbers part ways, and holding your guess against it is what makes it stick.

The income statement looks like a list. It is really a staircase. You read it from the top down, and at each step it strips in a different kind of cost, so every subtotal answers a different question about the business. Miss the staircase and you will quote the wrong number in a negotiation: the whole art of reading one is knowing which line measures the business and which lines have been contaminated by everything else.

Start at the top and walk down. Revenue is everything the company sold in the period; it says nothing yet about whether any of it was profitable. Subtract the direct cost of making the thing — cost of goods soldDefinitionThe direct cost of producing what was sold: materials, direct labor, and the like. Excludes the overhead of running the company, which lives in operating expenses. — and you reach gross profit, the first read on pricing power. Subtract the cost of running the business (sales, general and administrative, R&D) and you reach operating income, also called EBITTerm of artEarnings before interest and taxes. Operating profit: what the business earned from operations, before we ask how it is financed or taxed. — profit from the business itself. Subtract interest, and the company's debt load enters the picture. Subtract tax, and you finally land on net income, the bottom line.

The organizing rule

Here is the one idea to carry out of this lesson. The higher the line, the closer it is to the business; the lower the line, the more it is contaminated by things that have nothing to do with how well the company operates. Operating income is the business. Net income is the business plus its balance sheet (interest), plus its tax situation, plus anything that happened only once this year. Two companies can run identical operations and report wildly different net income — because everything below operating income is about financing, jurisdiction, and luck, not about the business.

Operating income is the business; net income is the business plus its debts, its tax bill, and its luck this year.

Watch it move

Read the three subtotals as three different questions. Gross margin (40%) asks whether the product itself earns its keep. Operating margin (15%) asks whether the whole business, run end to end, is profitable. Net margin (8.7%) is what is left for the owners after the lenders and the tax authority take their cut — useful, but no longer a clean read on the business, because two of its three ingredients (interest and tax) are not about operations at all.

What each line hides

Now the payoff, and the answer to the prediction. Everything below operating income is quarantined noise: interest is a financing choice, tax is jurisdiction and structure, and — the big one — non-recurring items land here too. A gain on selling a warehouse, a restructuring charge, an impairment write-down, a one-time legal settlement, a tax windfall: each swings net income without touching the business. So a company can grow its bottom line while its operating income shrinks, simply by booking a one-time gain below the operating line. Record net income, falling operating income, no contradiction.

This is why net income is the most-quoted and least-comparable number on the page, and why analysts spend so much effort climbing back up the staircase. “Adjusted” earnings and EBITDADefinitionEarnings before interest, taxes, depreciation, and amortization. A climb back up toward operating cash generation, stripping financing, tax, and major non-cash charges. Why a partner cares. In a merger agreement, an earn-out or a purchase-price formula is almost always defined off EBITDA or operating income — never off net income — precisely to strip out the buyer's post-closing financing and the seller's one-time items. Draft that clause off “net income” and you have quietly imported the tax rate and every asset sale into the deal price. You cannot challenge, or write, that clause if you cannot see which rung of the staircase it stands on.

Operating income $300 (700 − 400 = 300, a 15% operating margin). Then pretax = 300 − 60 interest = 240; tax at 25% = 60; net income $180 (9% net margin).

The twist: now book a $50 one-time litigation charge below the operating line. Operating income is still $300 — the business did not change — but pretax falls to 190, tax to about 48, and net income drops to roughly $142. The staircase quarantines the one-off; the bottom line does not.

The trap most people fall for

The assumption: “The bottom line is the bottom line — net income tells you how the company did.” It is the number the headlines quote, so it feels like the summary.

Why it's wrong: net income blends four different stories — operations, financing, tax, and one-time luck. Two identical businesses can post very different net income based on leverage, tax jurisdiction, and whether one of them sold a building this year. Read up the staircase to operating income to see the business; treat net income as the end of a long sentence, not its summary.

Practice · your hands

Walk a real income statement

Open EDGAR, pick any 10-K, and find its income statement. Walk it top to bottom: revenue → gross profit → operating income → net income. Compute the three margins (gross, operating, net). Then find one line below operating income — interest, tax, or a non-recurring gain or charge — that helps explain the gap between the operating margin and the net margin. Write the company, the three margins, and that one line in three lines.

Right result: your operating margin and net margin differ, and you can name at least one financing, tax, or one-time item that accounts for the gap.

Before you scroll on — in one sentence, what is the single thing to remember?

Reveal the line

Read the income statement top-down: each subtotal strips in a different cost, so the higher the line the truer it is to the business — and net income is the most-quoted, least-comparable number on the page.

Keep this
  • The staircase: Revenue → Gross profit → Operating income (EBIT) → Pretax → Net income; each step strips in a different kind of cost.
  • Operating income measures the business; net income adds financing, tax, and one-time items on top.
  • Non-recurring gains and charges land below the operating line — that is why net income is the least comparable number, and why deals price off EBITDA or operating income.
