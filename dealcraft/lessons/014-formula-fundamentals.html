Formula fundamentals

Anchoring, precedence, and reading a formula aloud — how one corner formula fills a whole grid, and why =-3^2 returns 9.

6 min read · predict · anchor grid · your turn · practice ≈ 10 min in a scratch workbook

Already fluent with $ and F4? Skip to precedence ↓

Predict · before you read on

In cell C4 you type =A4*B1. You copy that cell down one row, into C5. What does the formula in C5 now compute?

Reveal

Commit to a guess first, then read on. Whether both references moved, one moved, or neither moved is the entire idea behind anchoring — and it is why the dollar sign exists.

A cell reference in Excel is a set of directions, not an address. When you write =A4*B1 in C4, Excel does not really store “multiply A4 by B1”; it stores something closer to “multiply the cell three to my left by the cell two up and one left.” Copy that formula somewhere else and it faithfully follows the same directions from the new spot — which is why copying C4 down into C5 gives you =A5*B2, not =A4*B1. Both references shifted down one row, because both were relativeTerm of artA reference with no dollar sign (A1). Excel stores it as a direction relative to the formula’s own cell, so it shifts when the formula is copied — the default, and usually what you want.. That is the answer to the prediction, and it is not a nuisance to work around. It is the feature that lets one formula do a thousand cells’ work.

The trouble starts the moment a reference should not move. Say a single tax rate lives in B1 and you want to apply it to a whole column of pre-tax amounts. Write =A4*B1 and copy it down, and the amount reference walks correctly to A5, A6, A7, but the rate reference walks too, into B2, B3, B4 — empty cells. Every row below the first silently multiplies by zero. No error, no red flag, just a column of wrong numbers. You fix it by anchoring the reference that must stay put: put a dollar sign in front of the column letter and the row number, and B1 becomes =$B$1, an absoluteTerm of artA fully locked reference ($B$1). The dollar sign freezes what follows it; with both column and row frozen, the reference never moves, no matter where the formula is copied. reference that never moves. The dollar sign is a lock, and it locks whatever comes right after it.

A reference isn’t an address. It’s a set of directions — and the dollar sign is how you nail one of them to the floor.

That gives you four states for any reference, and one key to cycle them. With the reference selected in the formula bar, press F4 repeatedly: B1 (nothing locked) → $B$1 (both locked) → B$1 (row locked, column free) → $B1 (column locked, row free) → back to B1. The two half-locked states are mixed referencesTerm of artA reference with exactly one dollar sign (B$1 or $B1). It freezes one dimension — the row or the column — and lets the other travel. The workhorse of any formula meant to fill a two-way grid., and they are the ones that separate people who retype formulas from people who build a grid with one keystroke. Here is the payoff: a table whose every cell is the product of a row header and a column header, filled from a single corner formula.

The corner formula in B2 is =$A2*B$1. Read what each lock is doing: $A2 freezes the column (always column A, the units) but lets the row travel; B$1 freezes the row (always row 1, the prices) but lets the column travel. Now fill B2 across and down — and watch where those half-locks send each reference.

Check yourself · pick the anchoring

Same grid: units down column A, prices across row 1. You’ll type one formula and copy it across and down to fill the block. Which reference makes the units always read from column A and the price always from row 1?

The second half: what Excel does first

Anchoring decides which cells a formula reads. Precedence decides in what order it combines them — and Excel’s order is not quite the schoolbook one. Most of it is familiar: parentheses win, then exponents, then multiplication and division, then addition and subtraction, left to right within a tier. That is why =5+2*3 is 11, not 21 — the multiply happens before the add. But there is one rung that trips nearly everyone, and it is worth burning in. In Excel’s precedence table, negation — the minus sign that means “make this negative” — sits above exponentiation. So =-3^2 is not “three squared, then negate”; Excel negates first, giving (-3)^2, which is positive 9. To get the −9 you probably meant, you write =-(3^2) and force the exponent to go first.

This is not trivia. The day you write a returns or growth formula with a leading minus and an exponent — a discount factor, a CAGR, a squared deviation — a sign error of this kind flows straight into the answer and looks perfectly reasonable on the way. The defense is cheap and it is the third skill of this lesson: read the formula aloud. Say =-3^2 as Excel reads it — “negative three, squared” — and the 9 stops being a surprise. Say a copied model formula aloud — “amount times rate” — and if “rate” now names an empty cell, your ear catches what your eye skims past. Reading a formula in words is the same reviewer’s move as tracing a precedent: it forces you to process what is actually there, not what you meant to type.

Your turn · predict the number

No Excel — from the precedence order (negation, then ^, then × ÷, then + −), predict what each formula returns:   =-3^2  ·  =-(3^2)  ·  =10-2^2
I have my three answers

=-3^2 → 9. Negation outranks exponentiation, so Excel makes 3 negative first, then squares: (-3)^2 = 9.   =-(3^2) → −9. The parentheses force the exponent first (3² = 9), then the minus negates it.   =10-2^2 → 6. Here the minus is subtraction, not negation — and subtraction sits below exponentiation, so 2^2 = 4 happens first, then 10 − 4 = 6. The lesson in the contrast: a leading minus (negation) beats ^; a minus between two numbers (subtraction) loses to it.

The trap most people fall for

The assumption: “Copying a formula copies the calculation — so dragging =A4*B1 down the column just repeats the same math on each row.”

Why it’s wrong: it copies the directions, not the calculation. Every relative reference shifts, so B1 (your one rate, up top) marches down into B2, B3, empty cells — and the whole column below row one multiplies by nothing. There is no error to catch your eye; the sheet just carries a wrong number forward. Two habits close the gap for good: anchor before you fill — the instant a reference should hold still (a rate, a header, a single input), lock it with F4 — and read one copied cell aloud before trusting the fill. “Amount times rate” is fine; “amount times blank” is the bug announcing itself in words.

Practice · your hands

Fill a grid from one cell, then break it on purpose

Open a scratch workbook. Type units 100, 200, 300 down A2:A4 and prices 10, 12, 15 across B1:D1. In B2 type exactly one formula: =$A2*B$1 (use F4 to place the locks). Fill it right to D2 and down to D4, and confirm the far corner D4 reads 4,500 (300 × 15). Now break it: delete both dollar signs so B2 reads =A2*B1, fill again, and watch the grid fill with wrong numbers and blanks — the anchoring failure, made visible. Fix it back. Finally, in any empty cell type =-3^2, say it aloud as Excel reads it, and confirm the 9.

Right result: before you press fill, you can name which cell each copy will point to; and you can say out loud why =-3^2 is 9. That predictive certainty — not speed — is the skill.

Before you scroll on — in one sentence, what is the single thing to remember?

Reveal the line

A reference without a dollar sign moves when you copy it, so anchoring with F4 is you deciding — dimension by dimension — what holds still; and reading the formula aloud is how you hear the reference that shouldn’t have moved.

Keep this
  • Four states, one key: B1 moves both ways on copy; $B$1 locks both; B$1 locks the row; $B1 locks the column. F4 cycles all four.
  • Anchor the part that must hold still — a rate, a header row, a single input. With the right mixed refs, one corner formula (=$A2*B$1) fills a whole two-way grid.
  • Excel’s real order: parentheses, then negation, %, ^, then × ÷, then + −. So =-3^2 is 9; write =-(3^2) to mean −9.
  • Audit by ear: read every formula aloud left to right. A copied formula that names a blank cell is the error announcing itself.
