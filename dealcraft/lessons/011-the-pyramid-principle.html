The pyramid principle

Lead with the answer, then support it with groups that don't overlap and don't leave a gap. It is how a board proxy argues, and how you should.

7 min read · predict · a proxy autopsy · two checks · practice ≈ 12 min

Predict · before you read on

A board must tell shareholders why it recommends selling the company. It has months of analysis and a fairness opinion. Where does the recommendation itself go — at the end, after walking through the deal history, or at the very top, before the reasons?

Reveal

Commit first. School trains the opposite instinct — introduction, body, build to the conclusion — but real proxies, memos, and partner emails run the other way. The recommendation goes first, in one sentence, and everything below it exists to support that one sentence. That inversion is the pyramid principle, and the rest of the lesson is why the busiest, most skeptical reader you have makes it the only structure that survives.

The pyramid principle is one rule with two halves: lead with the single governing thought, then support it with a group of ideas that is MECE. Barbara Minto named it at McKinsey in the 1960s after noticing she kept reorganizing every report she edited into the same shape — one point on top, derived from the points beneath it. The governing thoughtDefined termThe single sentence that answers the reader's question and that every idea below exists to support. If you cannot state it in one sentence, you do not yet have an argument — you have a topic. is the answer to the question the reader actually has: Should we do this? Which option? What happened? It is an assertion, not a heading. "The proposed acquisition" is a topic. "The board should approve the acquisition because it delivers a full-value, all-cash exit with high closing certainty" is a governing thought.

The second half is the discipline that makes the support trustworthy: the group beneath the governing thought must be MECEDefined term · "mee-see"Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive. Minto coined it. The reasons in a group must not overlap (mutual exclusivity) and must together cover the whole question with no gap (collective exhaustiveness). She pronounces it to rhyme with "niece." — Mutually Exclusive, Collectively Exhaustive. Mutually exclusive means the reasons do not overlap: "regulatory risk" and "antitrust risk" cannot be two peers in the same list, because antitrust is a regulatory risk, and a reader who sees both wonders what you have double-counted. Collectively exhaustive means the group leaves no gap: if your three reasons to approve a deal say nothing about price, the skeptic's first question ("but is it a fair price?") has no home, and your argument has a hole where its most important support should be. MECE is not tidiness. It is the test that catches an argument that overlaps itself or quietly omits its weakest flank.

Minto reduced the whole structure to three rules a group must obey, and they are worth memorizing exactly. As she puts it: "The point above has to be a summary of those below, because it is derived from them. You can't derive an idea from a grouping unless the ideas in the grouping are logically the same, and in logical order." Read that as a checklist: the top summarizes the bottom (not a new idea), the items in a group are the same kind of thing (all reasons, or all steps, never a mix), and they sit in a deliberate order (by importance, by time, by structure). Three tests, and most weak memos fail at least one.

One more piece sets the pyramid up. Before the governing thought lands, the reader needs to know why the question is even being asked, and the standard opener is SCQADefined termSituation → Complication → Question → Answer. State the stable context, the change that disturbs it, the question that change raises, and then the answer — which is your governing thought. It is the on-ramp that makes leading with the answer feel earned, not abrupt.: Situation (the stable context both of you accept), Complication (the change that disturbs it), Question (what that change forces you to decide), Answer (your governing thought). "The company has grown steadily [S]. An unsolicited all-cash offer arrived at a 40% premium [C]. Should the board sell now or stay independent [Q]? The board should sell [A, the governing thought]." The answer to the question is the top of the pyramid. Now the shape is complete: SCQA on-ramp, one governing thought, a MECE group of support beneath it.

The autopsy

This is not a consulting abstraction. Open almost any merger proxy on SEC EDGAR and the pyramid is sitting there in black and white, because a board is legally arguing a recommendation to skeptical owners and cannot afford a loose one. Take Denny's Corporation's 2025 merger proxy (a DEFM14ADefined termThe definitive merger proxy statement a public company files under SEC rules to solicit shareholder votes on a merger. It must lay out the board's recommendation and its reasons — which is exactly why it is built as a pyramid., the definitive merger proxy) filed after the board agreed to be taken private in November 2025. Its section titled "Recommendations of the Denny's Corporation Board and Reasons for the Merger" is a textbook pyramid, and it is organized top-down on purpose.

The recommendation on top, the reasons in one clean group beneath it: that is the pyramid, and it is why you can trust the argument.

Why the partner reads top-down

The reason to invert the school-essay order is not aesthetic; it is a fact about your reader. The partner, the board, the client is busy and skeptical, and reads the way such people read: they want the answer in the first sentence so they can decide whether to keep going, and if they keep going they drill straight to the one reason they doubt. A pyramid serves exactly that reader. The governing thought gives the answer in five seconds; the MECE group lets them jump to "certainty of closing" without wading through "price" and "process" first, because the reasons are peers, not a chain. A bottom-up narrative — history, then analysis, then at last the recommendation — forces that reader to hold everything in suspense and trust that a point is coming. Busy readers do not extend that trust. They skip to the end, find your conclusion unsupported by anything they have read in order, and lose confidence in the whole document.

There is a second payoff, and it is the one that improves your actual thinking. Forcing yourself to write the governing thought first, in one sentence, is a test you can fail before anyone else sees the memo. If you cannot compress your position into a single assertion, you do not yet have a position — you have a pile of observations. If your supporting reasons will not go MECE, the argument itself has an overlap you were hiding or a gap you were avoiding. The pyramid is usually taught as a formatting habit for decks; it is really a thinking tool that happens to also format the deck. Build the pyramid and the argument's weaknesses surface while they are still cheap to fix. That is why the same shape runs through a partner's one-line email, a two-page decision memo, a board proxy, and a twelve-slide deck: it is not a template, it is what a sound argument looks like.

The trap most people fall for

The assumption: "The pyramid principle is a slide-formatting trick for management consultants. Lawyers write in prose and build to a conclusion; leading with the answer is for PowerPoint people." Structure gets filed under presentation.

Why it's wrong: the pyramid is a thinking discipline that formats itself. Its two demands — state one governing thought, support it MECE — are exactly the tests that expose a weak argument: a position you cannot compress to a sentence, support that overlaps itself, a gap where the strongest objection should be answered. A merger proxy is the least PowerPoint document there is, filed under securities law by lawyers, and it is built as a pyramid precisely because a board is arguing to skeptical owners and cannot afford an unfollowable case. Prose or slides, memo or email, the shape is the same. Lead with the answer, group the support so it neither overlaps nor leaks, and a busy reader trusts you in thirty seconds. Bury the answer and make them dig, and they stop reading before they reach it.

Practice · your hands

Reverse-engineer a real proxy's pyramid onto one page

On SEC EDGAR (full-text search), open any recent DEFM14A and jump to its "Reasons for the Merger" section. On a single page, rebuild its pyramid: write the board's governing thought in one sentence, list its reasons as a group, then run the MECE test on that group — flag any two reasons that overlap (not mutually exclusive) and name one question a shareholder could ask that the reasons do not answer (a gap in collective exhaustiveness). If the section keeps its negative factors in a separate group, note that as the drafters keeping the two groups mutually exclusive.

Right result: one page with a one-sentence apex, a labeled reasons group beneath it, and at least one honest MECE flag — the same audit you will run on your own memos before a partner does.

Reveal the line

Lead with one governing thought, then support it with a group that neither overlaps nor leaves a gap — and if you cannot, the argument, not the format, is what is broken.

Keep this
  • Governing thought first. One sentence that answers the reader's question, stated at the top. An assertion, not a topic label — if you can't write it, you don't have an argument yet.
  • MECE support (mee-see): the group under it must be Mutually Exclusive (no overlap) and Collectively Exhaustive (no gap). It's a test for double-counting and blind spots, not a tidiness rule.
  • SCQA on-ramp: Situation → Complication → Question → Answer, where the answer is your governing thought — the setup that makes leading with the answer feel earned.
  • The busy, skeptical reader reads top-down: answer in five seconds, then drill to the one reason they doubt. It's how proxies, memos, and partner emails are all built — not a slide trick.
