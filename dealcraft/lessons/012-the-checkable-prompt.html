The checkable prompt

A good legal prompt is not the one that gets a cleverer answer. It is the one whose answer you can verify in seconds. Three moves get you there: give it context, set constraints, and demand output you can check.

Which one saves you more time overall?

Reveal

Guess first. B wins, and the reason is the whole lesson. Both answers might be equally correct — but from Lesson 006 you cannot sign either one until you have verified it, and that verification is where the time actually goes. Prompt A hands you a bare list, so to trust it you re-read the agreement looking for each condition. Prompt B hands you a list plus the exact sentence and section for each, so verifying is a 30-second jump to five sections to confirm the quotes are really there. The drafting was never the expensive part. The check was. B is engineered to make the check cheap.

Lesson 006 left you with a rule and a bill. The rule: draft with AI freely, verify without it always, sign only what you confirmed yourself. The bill: that verification pass costs real time, and a sloppy prompt makes it cost more. So the craft of prompting for legal work is not chasing a magic phrase that unlocks a better answer. It is writing the prompt so two things happen at once — the model stays on the grounded, reliable side of the line, and the answer comes back in a shape you can audit fast. Three moves do the work: context, constraints, and checkable output. The first two keep the model honest; the third keeps you fast.

Context: put the source in the room

The most important move in a legal prompt is the one Lesson 006 already earned. Groundedness is reliability, so a good prompt grounds the task: it pastes the actual document and tells the model to work only from it, rather than asking the model to recall. “Summarize the indemnification section” with the section pasted below is a condensation the model can do well; “how does indemnification usually work” with nothing pasted is an invitation to generate. Two more pieces of context sharpen it. Give the model a roleTechnique · role promptingTelling the model who to be — “You are a transactional associate reviewing a merger agreement.” Anthropic's guidance notes role prompting can meaningfully improve performance on complex work and names legal analysis as an example. It sets the register and the standard the answer is held to. — “you are a transactional associate reviewing this agreement” — which measurably sharpens performance on complex work and is the cheapest quality gain available. And separate your instruction from the document so the model never confuses the two: wrap the pasted text in tagsTechnique · XML tagsDelimiters like <document>…</document> and <instruction>…</instruction> that mark where the source ends and your command begins. Claude is trained to read this structure, so tagging stops a long pasted agreement from being misread as part of the request. such as <document>…</document>. Context is you handing the model the right raw material and telling it what chair it is sitting in.

Constraints: draw the fences

Context supplies the material; constraints govern what the model may do with it. Be clear and direct — state the jurisdiction, the format, the audience, and the exact scope, because a vague instruction is filled with the model's guesses. But the constraint that matters most for legal work is a negative one, and it is the direct antidote to the fabrication mode from Lesson 006: tell the model it may use only the pasted text, and that if the answer is not there it must say “Not stated” rather than supply one. Left to its defaults, a model asked for a governing-law clause in a contract that has none will helpfully invent the most likely one; that is the gap-fill that gets juniors in trouble. A one-line constraint — “answer only from the document; if something is absent, write Not stated” — converts a confident fabrication into an honest blank you can act on. Constraints are how you tell the model where the fences are, and the most valuable fence keeps it from wandering off the page you gave it.

Context and constraints keep the model honest. The third move keeps you fast.

Checkable output: engineer the answer for the audit

Here is the move that separates a legal prompt from a generic one, and it follows straight from “you sign the work.” Because you must verify everything the model asserts, the smartest thing a prompt can do is shape the output so verifying is cheap. Do not ask only for the answer; ask for the answer plus the evidence you would need to check it. Three habits carry most of the value. First, demand a claim-to-source tableThe core habitA structured answer where every assertion sits next to the exact quote that supports it and the section or page it came from. It turns “re-read the document to trust the list” into “jump to five cited spots and confirm the quotes.”: for every point, the exact quoted sentence and its section or page number. Second, make the model separate what is grounded in the text from what it inferred — label each row Stated or Inferred — so the risky judgments are flagged rather than blended in. Third, require an explicit uncertainty marker: “Not stated” where the text is silent, never a smoothed-over guess. An answer built this way is not just more trustworthy; it is faster to trust, because the model has pre-sorted its output into the exact piles your verification pass needs. The prompt did half the checking for you.

The trap most people fall for

The assumption: “Prompting is about finding the clever wording — the magic phrase that makes the model give a smarter answer.” So people collect tricks and judge a prompt by how impressive the reply reads.

Why it's wrong: for legal work the goal of a prompt is not a cleverer answer, it is a checkable one. The best legal prompt is usually longer and more boring than a clever one — it names a role, pastes the source, fences the scope, and demands quotes and section numbers — precisely because it is engineered to make your verification pass fast, not to charm the model. A dazzling answer you still have to re-read the whole document to trust has saved you nothing; a plain answer with every claim tied to a quoted section has saved you the afternoon. Judge a prompt by the check it enables, not the reply it produces.

Practice · your hands

Write one checkable prompt and time the check

Pull a short real agreement from EDGAR (a merger agreement, an NDA, or an employment agreement) or use one you already have. Write a single prompt that does all three moves: give the model a role and paste the text in <document> tags (context); tell it to use only that text and write “Not stated” for anything absent (constraint); and require a table of claim, exact quote, and section number, each marked Stated or Inferred (checkable output). Ask it for one real thing — the closing conditions, the indemnification cap, or the termination triggers. Run it, then verify: jump to each cited section and confirm the quote is really there and really says that. Note how long the check took.

Right result: an answer you audited in a couple of minutes by jumping to cited sections, plus at least one caught defect — a misquote, a wrong section, or a claim the model marked Inferred that it had smuggled in as fact. That timed check is the Toolkit T1 workflow taking shape: the prompt that makes signing safe and fast.

Before you scroll on — in one sentence, what is a legal prompt actually for?

Reveal the line

A legal prompt's job is not a better answer but a checkable one: give it the source and a role, fence it to the text, and demand the quote and the section — so verifying takes seconds, not a re-read.

Keep this
  • Context — ground the task: paste the actual document and say “use only this,” give the model a role (it sharpens complex/legal work), and wrap the source in tags so it isn't misread as your instruction.
  • Constraints — be clear and direct on jurisdiction, format, scope; the highest-value constraint is the negative one: “answer only from the text; if it's absent, write Not stated” — the antidote to gap-fill fabrication.
  • Checkable output — ask for the answer plus the evidence: a claim-to-source table (exact quote + section), rows marked Stated vs Inferred, and explicit “Not stated” flags. Verifying becomes a jump, not a re-read.
  • The through-line: a checkable prompt makes Lesson 006's verification contract cheap. You still sign the work — the prompt just makes confirming it fast.
