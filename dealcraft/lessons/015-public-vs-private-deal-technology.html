From Lessons 003 & 009: the seller’s representations are true at signing and tested again at closing — the “bring-down.” Here is today’s question: a public-company merger closes, and six months later the buyer finds a rep was slightly false. Who does it sue?

No one — and that is the whole lesson. The former public shareholders took cash and scattered; there is no identifiable seller left, no escrow, and the reps did not survive the closing. The one moment the buyer could act on that false rep was before closing, by refusing to close. That single fact — who is left to sue after the money changes hands — is what makes a public deal and a private deal two different machines.

Public vs. private deal technology

The same word — “acquisition” — names two machines built from opposite parts. One variable decides which parts you get.

Predict · before you read on

A buyer pays about $500 million for each of two targets. Target A trades on the NYSE; Target B is a venture-backed private startup. In which deal does the buyer fight hardest for an 18-month survival period, a large escrow, and an earn-out?

Reveal

Commit to a guess first. The answer does not turn on the size of the check — a $500 million public deal and a $500 million private deal are built from different parts — but on one question you may not have weighed: who is standing there to answer for the reps after the money changes hands. The ledger below sorts every difference under that one variable.

You will hear both called “acquisitions,” and a first-year associate reasonably assumes they are the same contract at different sizes. They are not. A public-company merger and a private-company acquisition are engineered from opposite parts, and the reason is a single structural fact: who is left to sue after closing. Get that one variable, and every other difference — survival, escrow, earn-outs, no-shops, fiduciary outs — falls into place as a consequence.

In a private deal, the sellers are a handful of identifiable people or funds. They sign the agreement, they receive the money, and they remain reachable — often with a slice of the price held back in escrow precisely so the buyer can reach them. Because the seller is a known, solvent counterparty, the contract can promise recourse: if a representation turns out false, the buyer claws money back. In a public deal, the “seller” is thousands of dispersed shareholders who tender their shares for cash and vanish. There is no one to indemnify the buyer, no escrow to draw on, no party to sue. So the public-deal contract cannot be built around post-closing recourse. It is built around something else entirely: getting the deal to close at all.

The same deal, two machines

Every row is downstream of the first one. Decide who is left to sue after closing, and the rest of the contract writes itself in one of two directions.

Read the two columns as one logic

Run your eye down the public column and notice what is missing: no survival, no indemnity, no escrow, no earn-out. A junior sees a stripped-down contract and assumes the buyer got worse terms. The opposite is true — those parts are missing because there is no one to enforce them against. Aquantia’s shareholders received $13.25 per share in cash and were gone; a survival clause promising recourse against them would be a promise to sue a crowd that no longer exists. So the public contract spends its energy where energy can still buy something: the no-shop, the fiduciary out, the match right, the termination fee — the machinery of closing certainty, of holding a signed deal together against a shareholder vote and a rival who might swoop in with a higher bid.

Now the private column. Riverbed bought Mazu, a private company, for $25 million in cash plus up to $22 million more if Mazu’s sales cleared $35 million in the first year — an earn-outDefined termPart of the price paid later and only if the business hits agreed targets — a bet that bridges a disagreement over what the company is worth. It requires a known seller to pay, or not pay, so it lives in private deals.. An earn-out is unthinkable in the Aquantia deal: who would you pay the extra $22 million to, and who would you hold to the sales target? The private deal can carry an earn-out, a survival period, and an escrow for exactly the reason the public deal cannot — the seller is a named, reachable party. Same word, opposite machine.

Check yourself · where the recourse went

Aquantia’s shareholders took $13.25 per share in cash and the company folded into Marvell. Six months later the buyer finds a rep in the merger agreement was slightly false. What is the buyer’s recourse against the former shareholders?

The trap most people fall for

The assumption: “Reps and warranties are the seller’s promises — if one turns out false, the buyer gets paid. That’s true in any deal.”

Why it’s wrong: the same clause does two different jobs. In a private deal, that reading is roughly right — the reps survive, and indemnity and escrow turn a false rep into dollars back. In a public deal, the identical words are only a closing condition. They give the buyer a right to walk away before closing (the bring-down), and nothing after. There is no damages claim, because there is no seller left to pay it. A junior who walks into a public deal asking “where’s the escrow?” or “what’s the survival period?” has just announced they are reading the wrong machine. The rule: recourse only exists where a solvent, identifiable seller is still standing after closing — the reps follow that fact, not the other way around.

Why this matters to a partner

Because it tells a junior where the negotiation lives before they read a page. Hand an associate a private deal and the center of gravity is the indemnification package — basket, cap, escrow, survival, the sandbagging clause, and any earn-out or working-capital true-up; that is where days of markup and argument go, because that is where the money moves after signing. Hand the same associate a public deal and none of that exists to negotiate. The energy is entirely on deal certainty and process: how tight the no-shop is, how generous the fiduciary out, how large the termination fee, how the conditions and regulatory covenants allocate the risk of the gap between signing and closing. Knowing which machine you are in tells you which clauses to open first, which questions are live, and which are category errors. That is the difference between an associate who needs the map drawn for them and one who already carries it.

Practice · your hands

Find the public-deal fingerprints in a real agreement

On EDGAR, pull the Aquantia merger agreement (search the company, CIK 0001316016; the agreement is an exhibit to a 2019 8-K, and the deal is described in the DEFM14A proxy). Hunt for three fingerprints of a public deal: (1) a survival section stating the representations do not survive the closing; (2) a no-shop / non-solicitation covenant with a fiduciary-out exception and a termination fee; and (3) the absence of any indemnification or escrow article. For each, write one line on why it looks that way — tracing it back to the fact that the shareholders take cash and disappear at closing.

Right result: you can point to the three tells and explain each as a consequence of “no seller left to sue” — and you would now instantly notice if a private deal you were handed was missing its escrow.

Before you scroll on — in one sentence, what sorts a public deal from a private one?

Reveal the line

Whether an identifiable seller is still standing after closing: if yes, the contract is built for recourse (survival, indemnity, escrow, earn-outs); if no, it is built for certainty of closing (no-shop, fiduciary out, termination fee).

Keep this
  • One variable drives the whole architecture: who you can sue after closing. Private target = identifiable sellers who stay reachable; public target = thousands of holders who take cash and vanish.
  • Private parts: reps that survive, indemnity, escrow/holdback, working-capital true-up, earn-out (Riverbed/Mazu: $25M + up to $22M contingent). Public parts: none of that — instead no-shop, fiduciary out, match right, termination fee, and a shareholder vote (Marvell/Aquantia: $13.25/share, adopted July 10, 2019).
  • The reps are the tell: in a private deal they are recourse (false rep → money back); in a public deal they are only a closing condition (false rep → a right to walk before closing). Same words, opposite job.
