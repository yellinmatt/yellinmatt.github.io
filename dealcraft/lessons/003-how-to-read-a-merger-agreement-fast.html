How to read a merger agreement fast

The architecture map every deal lawyer carries in their head.

8 min read · predict · document autopsy + timeline · check yourself · practice ≈ 12 min on EDGAR

Already map agreements by article in your sleep? Skip to the floor plan ↓

Predict · before you read on

You open a 120-page merger agreement you have never seen, and the clock is running. Which article do you read first — and why?

Reveal

Commit to a guess first, then read on. A professional does not start at page one — they start where the money is, because everything else in the document allocates risk around that number. Which article holds the money is exactly what the floor plan below will show you.

A merger agreement looks like 120 pages of dense text, but a deal lawyer does not read it front to back. They read it as a building: eight rooms, always in the same order, each doing one job. Once you carry the floor plan in your head, you can open any acquisition agreement — a $50 million private carve-out or a $50 billion public merger — and land on the clause you need in seconds. This lesson hands you that floor plan.

Almost every U.S. acquisition agreement, private or public, follows the same skeleton. The labels vary and the schedules balloon, but the articles march in a fixed order because each one answers the next question a deal raises: what are we agreeing about, what happens at closing, what is the price, what am I promising, how do we behave until closing, what has to be true to close, when can we walk, and the boilerplate that governs it all.

The floor plan

You do not read a merger agreement in page order; you read it in risk order — price, then promises, then the gates and exits.

Notice the reading order a professional actually uses, marked above: Article III first (what is the price and how is my client paid), then Article I (because a single defined term like Material Adverse EffectDefined termThe standard that lets a buyer refuse to close if something catastrophic happens to the target between signing and closing. Its carve-outs are among the most negotiated words in any deal. can swing millions), then the reps, covenants, conditions, and termination as a linked system. The document is not a story; it is a machine for allocating risk around the number in Article III.

Two rooms everyone confuses

Articles V and VI look similar and do opposite jobs, and the confusion is expensive. Article V holds covenants — promises to do things between signing and closing: run the business in the ordinary course, do not shop the deal, use efforts to win approvals. Article VI holds conditions — things that must be true before a party is forced to close: the reps are still accurate, the approvals are in hand, no court has enjoined the deal. The difference is the remedy. Break a covenant and the other side has a claim for damages, but the deal may still have to close. Fail a condition and the counterparty is simply excused from closing at all. A buyer who wants out needs a failed condition, not merely a broken promise — which is exactly why the accuracy of the reps is wired into Article VI as a closing condition.

Check yourself · condition or covenant

A buyer has cold feet. The target has technically breached a minor interim-operating covenant, but every condition in Article VI is still satisfied at the closing date. What can the buyer do?

The trap most people fall for

The assumption: "If the target has bad news between signing and closing, the buyer invokes the Material Adverse Effect clause and walks." Every junior reaches for the MAE.

Why it's wrong: a MAE is one of the hardest outs to actually win. The definition is riddled with negotiated carve-outs (economy-wide, industry, pandemic, the deal's own announcement), and courts demand a decline that is durationally significant, not one bad quarter. In Delaware, buyers argued MAEs for two decades before a court first upheld one — Akorn v. Fresenius (2018), and even there the target had also breached its regulatory reps and its ordinary-course covenant. The rule: the MAE is a backstop, not a trapdoor — read its carve-outs, not just its headline.

Before you scroll on — in one sentence, how does a pro read a merger agreement fast?

Reveal the line

You do not read a merger agreement in page order; you read it in risk order — price, then promises, then the gates and exits.

Keep this
  • Eight articles, always the same order; read in risk order, not page order.
  • Article III is the money; Article I is where one defined term (MAE) can swing millions.
  • A condition (Art. VI) excuses closing; a covenant (Art. V) breach only pays damages — and the MAE is a backstop, not a trapdoor.
