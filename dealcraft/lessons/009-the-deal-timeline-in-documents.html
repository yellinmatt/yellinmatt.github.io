The deal timeline in documents

What gets signed when, from the NDA to the closing set — and the moment the binding force flips.

8 min read · predict · document ledger · check yourself · practice ≈ 12 min on EDGAR

Already know the NDA → LOI → signing → closing sequence cold? Skip to the ledger ↓

Predict · before you read on

Two CEOs sign a formal document headed “Letter of Intent to Acquire.” Diligence goes fine. The buyer then walks over price. Is the buyer legally bound to complete the purchase?

Reveal

Commit to a guess first, then read on. The surprise is that a signed, formal LOI can bind the buyer to almost nothing about the purchase itself — while a few of its clauses bind hard. Which clauses, and where the real commitment finally lands, is exactly what the ledger below maps.

A deal is not one contract; it is a sequence of documents, and each one is signed at a different moment to do a different job. The merger agreement you learned to read in Lesson 003 does not appear out of nowhere. It is the fourth or fifth document in a chain, and a seasoned deal lawyer tracks not just what each document says but when it is signed and how much it binds. That second axis, the binding force, is where the most expensive rookie errors live.

The reason the sequence exists is that the parties want to explore a deal without committing to it. Buyer and seller need to share secrets, run diligence, and negotiate for weeks or months before either is willing to be locked in. So the early documents are engineered to keep almost everything optional — on purpose — while a handful of clauses are engineered to bind from the first handshake. Read the chain wrong and you either treat a non-commitment as a commitment, or miss the one clause that already has teeth.

The five documents

Before the merger agreement, the deal is deliberately non-binding — except for the two clauses that quietly are: confidentiality and exclusivity.

Where the binding force flips

Run your eye down the right-hand column and you see the whole logic of the sequence. The NDA binds, but only on a narrow point: keep the secrets, do not raid the employees, stand still. The LOI is the interesting one. Its commercial terms — price, structure, the deal itself — are written to be non-binding, so either side can still walk during diligence. But a few provisions are deliberately carved out as binding: exclusivity (the seller will not shop the deal for, say, 45 days), confidentiality (often folding in the NDA), and usually expense allocation and governing law. Then comes the flip. The merger agreement is the first document that says, in enforceable terms, “we will close, if the conditions are met.” Everything upstream was scaffolding built not to commit either party to the purchase; the merger agreement is the commitment. The signing 8-K that follows is not a new promise at all — it is a disclosure that makes the promise public.

Check yourself · what actually binds

A signed LOI states that its terms are “non-binding.” During the 45-day exclusivity period, the seller receives a higher unsolicited offer and starts negotiating with the new bidder. Has the seller breached anything?

The trap most people fall for

The assumption: "A non-binding LOI is just a formality — nothing in it can hurt me, so sign it and keep talking to everyone." The label says non-binding, so the reader treats the whole page as air.

Why it's wrong: the “non-binding” label is surgical, not total — it disclaims the deal terms while leaving exclusivity, confidentiality, and expenses fully enforceable. Courts read intent and language, not the headline: a document waving “non-binding” over its price can still hold a seller to a no-shop, or even bind a party that used commitment language elsewhere. The rule: never read the label — read the binding-effect clause and find out which provisions were carved out to survive. In a pre-signing document, that carve-out list is the contract.

Why the clock matters to a partner

Two reasons the timeline, not just the text, is a working skill. First, binding force is leverage. Knowing that a signed LOI does not commit the buyer to the price but does lock the seller out of the market tells you exactly who holds the cards at each stage — and a junior who cannot read that gets the negotiation posture wrong. Second, the gap between signing and closing is where deals break. The merger agreement is signed and filed on an 8-K within four business days, but the deal is not done: it must still clear the Article VI conditions — regulatory approvals, the bring-down of the reps — before the closing set. That gap can run many months, and it is precisely where the fights happen: the buyer with cold feet, the Material Adverse Effect argument, the regulatory hold-up.

The cleanest cautionary tale is LVMH’s acquisition of Tiffany. The merger agreement was signed on November 24, 2019, at $135 per share — roughly a $16.2 billion deal. Then the pandemic hit the signing-to-closing gap, LVMH signaled it wanted out, and Tiffany sued in Delaware to enforce the agreement. The document that decided the outcome was not the price both sides had already signed; it was the machinery governing the gap — the conditions, the ordinary-course covenant, the walk-away rights. The parties renegotiated to $131.50 per share and closed on January 7, 2021. The lesson sits exactly on this timeline: a deal is signed long before it is done, and the interval in between has its own law.

Practice · your hands

Trace the timeline in a real deal

On EDGAR, open a recent public-company merger proxy (a DEFM14A) and find its “Background of the Merger” section — the narrative of how the deal came together. Read it only to build the timeline: note the date the parties first signed a confidentiality agreement, the point an exclusivity or letter of intent appears, and the date the merger agreement was signed. Then find the signing 8-K on the same company’s filing list and confirm it landed within four business days.

Right result: you can state, in five lines, which document was signed on which date and what each one bound — proof you now read a deal along the clock, not just the page.

Reveal the line

A deal is a sequence of documents, and the binding force flips at the merger agreement — everything before it is non-binding by design, except the exclusivity and confidentiality clauses written to bind.

Keep this
  • Five documents, in order: NDA → LOI/term sheet → merger agreement + disclosure schedules → signing 8-K → closing set.
  • Binding flips at the merger agreement. The LOI’s deal terms are non-binding; its exclusivity and confidentiality clauses are not — read the carve-out list, never the label.
  • Signing is not closing. The 8-K makes it public within four business days; the Article VI conditions and the bring-down still stand between signing and the closing set — and that gap is where deals break.
