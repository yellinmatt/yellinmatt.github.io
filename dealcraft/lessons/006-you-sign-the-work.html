You sign the work

Generative AI drafts fast and fabricates confidently. Because your name goes on the work, three disciplines follow: know what it is good at, guard what goes in, and verify everything that comes out.

7 min read · predict · a capability ledger · two checks · practice ≈ 12 min

Already run AI behind a hard verification gate? Skip to the capability ledger ↓

Predict · before you read on

A lawyer's brief cited six cases. Uneasy, he went back to the same chatbot and asked, “Are these real cases?” It answered yes — they can be found on Westlaw and LexisNexis. Every one was invented. What was his deeper mistake?

Reveal

Commit to a guess first. Citing unverified cases is the obvious error. The deeper, more instructive one is the second question: he “confirmed” the fake cases by asking the very system that fabricated them, and it obligingly agreed. A model cannot audit itself — its confidence is generated the same way its citations are, and it has no internal sense of true versus invented. Real verification is always external: you pull the case from an actual source. That single distinction is the spine of this lesson. (This is Mata v. Avianca, and we return to it below.)

Start with what the tool actually is, because most misuse traces back to getting this wrong. A generative AI model is a fluent-language engineHow to think of itA system trained to predict the next most plausible span of text given what came before. It is optimized for fluency and plausibility, not truth — it has no database it looks facts up in and no internal flag for “I am not sure.”: it predicts the most plausible next stretch of text. It is not a database you query and it is not a reasoning oracle. That single fact sets both its power and its defining danger. The power is that it produces fluent, well-structured language faster than you can type. The danger is the flip side of the same coin: when it is wrong, the wrong output is fluent and well-structured too. A fabricated case citation arrives in the same crisp Bluebook form as a real one; an invented statutory rule reads with the same calm authority as the real rule. A hallucinationDefined termModel output that is confident and well-formed but fabricated — invented cases, quotes, facts, or numbers presented in exactly the same authoritative form as true ones. The word is standard shorthand; the operative point is that nothing in the output signals which parts are made up. wears the same suit as a fact.

So the first discipline is capability: knowing, concretely, where the tool is strong and where it silently fails. The rule of thumb that organizes everything else is groundedness. When the model works on text you put in front of it — summarize this section, restructure this argument, tighten this clause — it is reorganizing a source it can see, and it is genuinely reliable. When you ask it to supply something from its own training — name the leading cases, state the rule, recall a filing's terms — it is generating plausible text with no source in the room, and it will invent. Same tool, opposite trustworthiness. Hold the two apart.

Its output is fluent, confident, and unsourced by default. None of those three is truth.

Guard what goes in

The second discipline runs in the opposite direction from the first. Capability is about what you let out of the tool; confidentiality is about what you put in. Under Model Rule 1.6 a lawyer must protect all information relating to the representation, whatever its source — and pasting a client's draft agreement into a tool is a disclosure. A free, consumer chatbot may retain your input, use it to train the next version of the model, or expose it in ways you do not control. So the client's named parties, price, and terms can walk out of your control the instant you hit enter, before the tool has produced a single word of output.

This is exactly the ground ABA Formal Opinion 512 (July 2024), the profession's first ethics guidance on generative AI, was written to cover. It reads Rule 1.6 onto these tools directly: you must understand a tool's data-handling well enough to use it competently (Rule 1.1), and for a self-learning toolWhy it mattersA tool that trains on the inputs users give it folds your text into its future outputs, potentially surfacing it to others. ABA 512 flags this as the case where general caution is not enough and specific, informed client consent is likely required. that trains on what users submit, you likely need the client's informed consent before feeding in their confidential information. The practical form of the rule fits on an index card: before you paste, ask where this text is going and whether it could identify the client or the deal. An enterprise tool with a no-training, walled-off contract changes the answer — but it changes the calculus, never the duty. The duty is always yours.

The verification contract

The third discipline is the one the whole lane is named for, and it is the one the prediction set up. Call it a contract because that is how it behaves: AI may draft, but only your hands may verify, and nothing the model asserts crosses into filed work until you have confirmed it against the real source. Every fact, every citation, every quotation, every number is unverified by default — not because the tool is usually wrong, but because you cannot tell from the output which parts are the times it is. And the corollary the prediction turned on: asking the model to check its own work is not verification. It is the same unreliable source, queried twice. Steven Schwartz did exactly that in Mata v. Avianca — the cases were fake, he asked the chatbot if they were real, it said yes, and Judge Castel sanctioned him $5,000 under Rule 11 for the brief that resulted.

The reason the contract is non-negotiable is the title of this lesson. The signature block is yours; the certification to the court under Rule 11 is yours; the candor duty under Rule 3.3 is yours. The model signs nothing and answers to no one. When a partner's name goes on the filing above yours, you have not delegated the work to the AI — you have borrowed its speed and kept every ounce of the responsibility. That is why the verification pass is not a nicety you do when there is time; it is the price of using the tool at all. Draft with it freely. Verify without it, always. Sign only what you confirmed yourself.

The trap most people fall for

The assumption: “AI either can be trusted or it can't — and since it makes things up, the careful, professional move is to just not use it for real work.” Caution gets equated with abstinence.

Why it's wrong: the false binary is itself the mistake. The tool is neither a source of truth nor useless — it is a fast, unreliable drafter. The competent associate of 2028 uses it heavily where it is strong (first drafts, summarizing supplied text, restructuring, issue-spotting), behind a hard verification gate, and never as the origin of a fact, a citation, or a number. Abstinence forfeits the speed your peers are using; blind trust forfeits your license. The discipline is neither extreme: draft with it, verify without it, and sign only what you confirmed. The lawyers who lose to AI are the ones who trusted it; the lawyers who lose because of AI are the ones who refused to touch it.

Practice · your hands

Run one real task through the full gate

Pick one document — pull a short real agreement from EDGAR, or use a memo you already have. Ask an AI to do two things: (1) summarize it, and (2) name the three most important cases or rules on its main issue. Then run the gate. Check the summary against the actual text. Try to pull every case it cited on a free source (Google Scholar or CourtListener) and mark which ones actually exist. Finally, write one sentence: could this document have gone into this tool, and why or why not (Rule 1.6)?

Right result: a short verification log — summary confirmed against the source, each citation pulled or flagged as fabricated, and one confidentiality call. That log is the Toolkit T1 proof in miniature: the AI workflow a partner will actually sign.

Before you scroll on — in one sentence, what does AI actually change about your work?

Reveal the line

AI changes what you can produce, never who is responsible for it: it drafts, you verify, and you sign.

Keep this
  • Generative AI is a fluent-text engine, not a database of truth. Its defining risk: wrong output looks exactly as confident and polished as right output — a hallucination wears the same suit as a fact.
  • Capability: strong at first drafts, summarizing text you supply, restructuring, brainstorming. It fabricates when asked to recall case law, state a rule, or do math with no source in the room — verify those externally, always.
  • Confidentiality (Rule 1.6 / ABA 512): guard what goes in. Before you paste, ask where the text goes and whether it could identify the client or the deal; public tools that retain or train on input are the danger.
  • The verification contract (Mata v. Avianca / Rule 11): you sign it, so you confirm every fact, cite, and number against the real source. Asking the model to check itself is not verification.
