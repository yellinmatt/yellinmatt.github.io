The balance sheet

What a company owns, owes, and is worth on paper.

8 min read · predict · worked example · your turn · practice ≈ 10 min on a real 10-K

Already read balance sheets fluently? Skip to the worked snapshot ↓

Predict · before you read on

A company's balance sheet reports total equity — its book value — of $2 billion. The same afternoon, the stock market values that same equity at $18 billion. One of those numbers must be wrong. Which one, and why?

Reveal

Commit to a guess first — then read on. The whole lesson turns on why this gap is normal, not an error, and the snapshot below shows exactly what the balance sheet is measuring when it says “equity.”

The income statement is a movie of one year; the balance sheet is a photograph taken the instant that year ends. It answers three questions at a single frozen moment: what does the company own, what does it owe, and what is left over for the owners. Those three blocks are locked together by one equation that can never break, and the trap is believing the last number tells you what the company is worth.

The equation is the whole architecture. Assets = Liabilities + Equity. Everything the company controls sits on the left; every claim against it sits on the right; the two sides are equal by construction, because equity is defined as whatever is left after the lenders and suppliers are subtracted out. AssetsDefinitionResources the company owns or controls that are expected to produce future economic benefit: cash, receivables, inventory, property, plant and equipment, and the like. are split into current (cash and things becoming cash within a year: receivables, inventory) and non-current (the long-lived stuff: property, plant and equipment). LiabilitiesDefinitionClaims of outsiders against the company: what it owes. Current (payable within a year, like accounts payable) and long-term (like bonds and term loans). split the same way. And equity — also called book valueTerm of artTotal assets minus total liabilities, as carried on the books. The owners' residual claim — and, crucially, an accounting figure, not a market valuation. — is the residual: assets minus liabilities, the owners' leftover claim.

The organizing rule

Here is the one idea to carry out of this lesson. Assets are recorded at what the company paid for them, not what they are worth today. Accountants start from historical cost and, absent a strong reason otherwise, leave it there. A headquarters bought for $5 million in 1985 and worth $60 million now still sits on the books near $5 million, less depreciation. So the equity line — assets minus liabilities — inherits that same backward-looking measuring stick. Book value is what the company is worth on paper, assembled from old purchase prices, and it is almost never what the company is actually worth.

Equity on the balance sheet is a residual measured at historical cost, not a verdict on what the company is worth.

Why book value and worth diverge

Now the payoff, and the answer to the prediction: neither number was wrong. The $2 billion book value and the $18 billion market value are two different measurements of the same equity. Book value looks backward — it sums historical purchase prices net of what is owed. Market value looks forward — it prices the cash the business will throw off for years to come. And the biggest value-drivers a company has, its brand, its customer relationships, its assembled workforce, its growth runway, were never purchased in a transaction, so accounting has no cost to record and they appear on the balance sheet at exactly zero. The gap between $2B and $18B is the market pricing everything the books cannot see.

Watch what happens when someone actually buys that gap. A buyer pays $1,800 for the equity of a company whose books show $400. The $1,400 paid above book value does not vanish and is not a loss — it lands on the buyer's balance sheet as goodwillTerm of artThe excess of purchase price over the fair value of the identifiable net assets acquired. It is where the value the seller's own books never captured finally gets recorded — on the buyer's books, at the moment of purchase., a new asset:

Why a partner cares. Acquisition agreements are stitched to these balance-sheet numbers: a net working capital target (current assets minus current liabilities) that trues up the price at closing, a net-debt adjustment, indemnity baskets sized off book figures, and the goodwill that lands on the buyer's books the day the deal closes. Almost every purchase price sits far above book equity, so a covenant or condition drafted against “book value” imports historical cost, while one drafted against “fair market value” imports an appraisal — two different numbers, often billions apart. Confuse them in the drafting and you have either mispriced the deal or written a threshold that never bites. You cannot mark up that clause if you cannot see which number the balance sheet is actually reporting.

I have my answer

Book value of equity $700 (1,500 − 800 — the residual, and the amount that makes both sides of the sheet read $1,500). Then market minus book = $1,700 (2,400 − 700).

What that $1,700 is: the value the market assigns to everything the balance sheet never recorded — the brand, the customer base, the growth ahead — because none of it was ever bought in a transaction. Pay $2,400 for this equity and roughly $1,700 lands on your books as goodwill. Book value gave you the floor; it did not give you the company.

The trap most people fall for

The assumption: “Equity on the balance sheet is what the company is worth — assets minus debts, that's the net worth.” The arithmetic is right, so the conclusion feels right.

Why it's wrong: the arithmetic is measuring the wrong things. Assets sit at historical cost, appreciated real estate and brands and growth are either understated or entirely absent, so book equity is an accounting residual, not a valuation. That is why nearly every company trades above book value and nearly every acquisition price exceeds it, with the excess booked as goodwill. Read book value as the floor the accountants can defend, then look to the market and the models — the later lessons in this lane — for what the business is actually worth.

Practice · your hands

Balance a real one, then find the gap

Open EDGAR, pick any 10-K, and find the balance sheet (“Consolidated Balance Sheets”). First, confirm the identity to the dollar: total assets = total liabilities + total stockholders' equity. Then read off total stockholders' equity — that is book value — and compare it to the company's market cap (shares outstanding × a recent share price, both on the cover or in the filing). Write the company, its book equity, its market cap, and one sentence naming what the gap between them represents.

Right result: your two sides balance exactly, and you can state whether the market values the equity above or below book — and name at least one thing (brand, growth, appreciated assets) the balance sheet leaves out that explains the gap.

Before you scroll on — in one sentence, what is the single thing to remember?

Reveal the line

The balance sheet is a snapshot that always balances because equity is the plug — assets minus liabilities, carried at historical cost — which is why book value is what a company is worth on paper and almost never what it sells for.

Keep this
  • Snapshot at one instant; the identity Assets = Liabilities + Equity always holds because equity is the residual, the plug that makes both sides match.
  • Assets are carried at historical cost, not current market value; unpurchased value (brand, growth, appreciated property) is understated or absent.
  • Book value ≠ market value ≠ deal price. The excess a buyer pays over book value is recorded as goodwill on the buyer's balance sheet.
