Alan Greenspan, the "Maestro" who governed two decades of American money, dies at 100

Alan Greenspan, who led the Federal ReserveInstitutionThe United States central bank, which sets interest rates and oversees the banking system; Greenspan chaired it longer than all but one of his predecessors. for more than eighteen years across four presidencies and became the most recognizable central banker of his era, died Monday at his home in Washington at 100, of complications from Parkinson's disease. Critics tie the crisis in part to the light-touch supervision and low rates of his later years; admirers credit him with taming inflation and steering markets through the 1987 crash, the 1998 hedge-fund rescue, and the dot-com boom.

He leaves a vocabulary as much as a record. Why it mattersGreenspan is the figure against whom every later Fed chair has been measured, and his career is a referendum on an entire idea, market self-regulation, that he himself recanted.
