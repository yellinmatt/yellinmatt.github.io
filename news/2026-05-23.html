Artificial Intelligence · New York / San Francisco

Anthropic nears a $30 billion raise that would make it the world's most valuable AI startup

Anthropic is set to close a funding round of more than $30 billion at a valuation above $900 billion as soon as next week, Bloomberg reported Friday, vaulting the Claude maker past OpenAI to become the most valuable AI startup in private markets. Sequoia Capital, Dragoneer Investment Group, Altimeter Capital and Greenoaks Capital Partners are each expected to put in roughly $2 billion to co-lead the financing. OpenAI's most recent round, completed in March, valued it at $852 billion, so the new mark would seat Anthropic, narrowly, at the top of the table.

The number tracks revenue, not just narrative. Anthropic's annualized revenue ran near $9 billion at the end of 2025 and crossed $30 billion by April 2026, with the company guiding toward $45 billion shortly and projecting its first operating profit this quarter. A $900-billion private valuation sets an aggressive anchor for the public listing investors increasingly expect, and it escalates the capital arms race with OpenAI just as both labs chase the same enterprise budgets, the same chips, and the same scarce engineers. Whoever raises the most, fastest, gets to keep training the largest models.

"The world's most valuable AI startup, ahead of OpenAI."
— Bloomberg, May 22

Why it mattersFor a capital-markets lawyer in training, this is the private-market comparable that will frame every AI listing to come; for the novelist tracking AI's reach into the professions, it is the balance sheet underneath the disruption.

Corporate · New York

Wall Street closes its eighth straight winning week, Dow at a record

The Dow Jones Industrial Average rose 294 points, or 0.58%, to a record 50,579.70 on Friday, while the S&P 500 added 0.37% to 7,473.47 and the Nasdaq Composite gained 0.19% to 26,343.97. The S&P logged its eighth consecutive weekly advance, its longest winning streak since 2023, heading into the Memorial Day weekend. Every sector finished higher, led by health care (+1.19%) and technology (+1.02%), and the small-cap Russell 2000 outperformed the majors on the day.

The grind higher rode easing 10-year Treasury yields and softer oil, with stock futures firmer after Washington and Tehran both signaled progress toward ending the Middle East war. Single names did the lifting: Dell and HP jumped double digits ahead of next week's earnings, Texas Instruments rose on an analyst upgrade tied to data-center power demand, Qualcomm climbed on a partnership, and Workday gained on a well-received quarter. A building IPO pipeline and steady M&A kept the bank stocks bid.

Why it mattersRecords compress risk premia and pull issuance forward, exactly the window in which the SpaceX and OpenAI listings are being timed; a capital-markets desk lives and dies by how long this stays open.

Culture · Cannes, France

Cannes hands out the Palme d'Or tonight, with no runaway favorite and an honorary nod to Streisand

The 79th Festival de Cannes closes Saturday with a jury chaired by Park Chan-wook awarding the Palme d'Or among the 22 films in competition; Tilda Swinton presents the top prize, and Isabelle Huppert delivers an honorary Palme to Barbra Streisand, at Streisand's request. Forecasters have converged loosely on "Fatherland" and "All of a Sudden," with Andrey Zvyagintsev's "Minotaur" tipped as a spoiler and Javier Calvo and Javier Ambrossi's "The Black Ball" drawing one of the festival's most passionate receptions.

What makes the night hard to call is the absence of a single front-runner, unusual for a festival whose laurels often telegraph the awards season to come. The specialty distributor Neon has carried the last six Palme winners, including last year's "It Was Just an Accident" and 2024's "Anora," so its competition slate is worth watching as buyers weigh acquisitions in an unusually thin sales market on the Croisette.

Why it mattersFor an entertainment-law track, the Palme is the year's most valuable piece of festival IP, the moment distribution rights, awards campaigns and talent deals get repriced overnight.

Corporate · New York

Debevoise cracks the lockstep model, adding a discretionary partner bonus pool

Debevoise & Plimpton, long a standard-bearer for pure lockstep partner pay, is creating a new bonus pool that gives firm leadership room to reward top performers, Above the Law reported Friday, the latest elite firm to bend its compensation system toward an overheated lateral market. The firm and its peers are dressing the shift in the language of "collaboration" and "rewarding excellence," but the driver is retention: keeping rainmakers from being poached as competitors dangle uncapped, individualized packages.

The move caps a spring of structural churn in BigLaw, layoffs at newly merged firms, the spread of nonequity partner tiers, and private-equity interest in the Am Law second hundred. Lockstep, which pays partners by seniority rather than by book of business, has been eroding for years; a holdout as prestigious as Debevoise blinking signals how hard pure seniority pay is to defend once rivals compete openly on individual compensation.

Why it mattersThis is the economics of the firms recruiting Matthew's class; how a target firm pays its partners shapes culture, lateral risk, and where a capital-markets associate's career compounds.

Bolivia's capital is paralyzed as protests push President Paz toward the brink

La Paz is under siege after two weeks of road blockades by the COB labor federation, miners and peasant unions emptied its markets, drained hospital oxygen reserves and, by business estimates, are bleeding the economy more than $50 million a day, with roughly 5,000 vehicles stranded on highways. At least three people have died after blockades kept emergency vehicles from medical centers; miners wielding dynamite have clashed with police as marches converge on government offices. President Rodrigo Paz, six months into office, faces open demands to resign.

The unrest sits atop Bolivia's worst economic crisis in 40 years, with inflation near 14% in April, chronic fuel shortages and a "junk gasoline" scandal that damaged thousands of vehicles and forced out two executives at the state oil company. Former president Evo Morales has mobilized supporters on a 190-kilometer march toward the capital, demanding Paz's exit and a halt to judicial proceedings against himself; more than a dozen countries across the Americas have called for calm.

Why it mattersA commodity-dependent economy unraveling in real time is the kind of sovereign-risk story that moves emerging-market debt and reminds anyone heading into capital markets how fast political risk reprices.

Framing noteLeft outlets (Al Jazeera, NPR) foreground inequality and fuel scarcity, while the conservative WORLD frames the unrest as a failure of socialist governance and a test of Bolivian democracy.

News · Washington / Tehran · cross-spectrum LLCR

US reports "slight progress" with Iran as it emerges Washington spent half its THAAD interceptors defending Israel

Secretary of State Marco Rubio said Friday there was "slight progress" in talks with Iran but no agreement, keeping alive the question of whether the spring war resumes, even as President Trump said the conflict "will be over with soon." A source close to Iran's negotiating team told the semi-official Tasnim agency that a deal will be signed only once all issues are resolved, beginning with an end to the fighting. Iran's lead negotiator warned of "crushing, bitter" consequences if the US "foolishly" restarts the war, and said Tehran rebuilt its forces during the ceasefire.

The diplomacy unfolds against a striking disclosure: the US expended more than half its THAAD anti-missile interceptor inventory shielding Israel from Iranian ballistic strikes during the war, a drawdown that constrains American air defense worldwide and raises the cost of any renewed round. Washington separately sanctioned nine Lebanese and Iranian officials it accused of sabotaging Hezbollah's disarmament, even as it signaled it wants the broader conflict closed.

Why it mattersOil and equity futures track every signal out of these talks; the THAAD depletion is the kind of hard-capacity fact that quietly shapes defense budgets, energy prices and the risk backdrop for markets.

Framing noteThe Washington Post stresses diplomatic progress and the strain on US arsenals, while Fox News leads with "no deal yet" and Trump keeping the military option on the table.
