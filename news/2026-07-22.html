A second chokepoint closes: the Houthis blockade Saudi Arabia as tankers turn back from Hormuz

Yemen’s HouthiInstitutionThe Iran-aligned movement that controls northern Yemen and the Red Sea coast. Its missile and drone reach over the Bab el-Mandeb strait lets it threaten shipping far from the Gulf fighting. movement declared a full maritime blockade of Saudi Arabia on Monday, warning shipowners by email that any vessel calling at a Saudi port would lose safe passage through the Bab el-MandebPlaceThe strait at the mouth of the Red Sea between Yemen and the Horn of Africa. With Hormuz shut, it is the alternate artery for Saudi crude bound west toward the Suez Canal. strait. The first confirmed casualty was the Xin Long Yang, a Chinese very large crude carrier that aborted its passage and executed a U-turn in the Red Sea; by Tuesday several more tankers had paused or diverted north toward Suez. Houthi spokesman Yahya Sarea framed it as retaliation for Saudi strikes on Sanaa, calling it “an eye for an eye.”

The blockade opens a second front on global energy just as the first tightens. The United States completed a fresh round of strikes on Iran on Monday evening, and Iran struck a tanker inside the Strait of HormuzPlaceThe passage between Iran and Oman carrying about 20% of seaborne oil. Iran’s interdiction there is the central lever of a war now in its fifth month. early Tuesday, its effective closure of that passage now compounded by the threat to Bab el-Mandeb. Analysts cited by NPR warned that shutting the second strait could halt Saudi oil exports to Asia and cut global supply by as much as 7%. Regional mediators are still circulating a ten-day ceasefire proposal that would pair a truce with a “middle corridor” through Hormuz, but Washington has pressed for a longer halt and no text is signed.

The compounding is the danger. A single contested strait is a premium on the oil price; two contested straits, one at each end of the Arabian Peninsula, is a structural squeeze on the physical flow, and it lands with Brent already pushed past $90 on the war. President Trump publicly downplayed the Houthi threat, telling reporters the group had given “no problem,” even as the ships themselves turned around.

“An eye for an eye.”

Why it mattersA two-strait squeeze reprices the largest geopolitical premium in the oil market and hands the inflation problem straight to a Fed under Kevin Warsh, while a Chinese supertanker reversing course is the clearest signal that the war has stopped being a Gulf story and started being a world-trade one.

Framing noteCenter and financial outlets (Bloomberg) lead with the shipping and price mechanics; the left (Foreign Policy) leads with a widening, open-ended war and the human toll of a fifth month of fighting.

Nvidia reveals a $5 billion stake in Nebius, buying into the cloud it sells its chips to

A regulatory filing disclosed Monday showed NvidiaInstitutionThe dominant designer of AI accelerators, whose chips are the scarce input every model builder and cloud competes for. It has begun taking equity in the customers that buy them. now holds roughly 22.3 million shares of Nebius GroupInstitutionA Nasdaq-listed “neocloud” that rents out AI computing capacity. It is the former parent of Russia’s Yandex, restructured and relisted after that business was sold., a 9.3% stake worth close to $5 billion. The position, which includes shares tied to a warrant from Nvidia’s earlier $2 billion investment, sent the neocloudConceptA new class of provider that rents GPU compute at scale, competing with the hyperscalers. CoreWeave and Nebius are the archetypes, often financed and supplied by Nvidia itself. up nearly 19% Tuesday, to just under $217.

The move deepens a pattern that has drawn scrutiny all year: Nvidia investing in the very companies that buy its hardware, from CoreWeave to OpenAI-adjacent infrastructure, a circularity critics call vendor financing dressed as strategy and Nvidia calls backing the buildout of demand. The warrant cannot be exercised until after September 11, and the disclosure lands as the chip complex tries to steady itself after a 9% weekly slide, with the sector’s $725 billion capex wave the question hanging over every AI print this week.

Why it mattersWhen the supplier takes an equity stake in its customer, the line between selling chips and financing the demand for them blurs, which is exactly the kind of related-party structure a securities lawyer parses for disclosure and a skeptic watches for the first sign the AI buildout is leaning on its own vendor to keep growing.
