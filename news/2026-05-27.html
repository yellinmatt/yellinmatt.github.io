Stratechery reads Nvidia’s quiet reporting break: hyperscalers in one column, everyone else in another

Inside the hyperscaler bucket, Nvidia is in a margin fight against custom in-house chips and against the buyers’ own commoditization pressure; outside it, Nvidia is the whole stack. Hogan Lovells and Cadwalader enter the final 40 days; the largest law-firm merger in history confronts the integration crunch

With a July 1 close 40 days out, and , Wickersham & Taft are sorting through the live operational questions of the largest BigLaw merger in history: leadership titles, business-team alignment, software stacks, office consolidation. Above the Law reported Tuesday that the firms are clearing the final pre-close hurdles after a roughly 95% partner vote of approval in mid-April. Hogan Lovells CEO will run the combined firm, with Cadwalader’s Wes Misson taking global managing partner of the finance practice and Pat Quinn taking client and practice integration.

The combined platform will field roughly 3,100 lawyers across the Americas, EMEA, and APAC, with about $3.9 billion of fee income, putting it inside the top tier of firms by revenue. The internal model is finance-led: Cadwalader brings its structured-finance and bank-regulatory bench, Hogan Lovells brings public-company and regulatory scale. Bloomberg Law has separately flagged a quieter problem: Hogan Lovells inherits Cadwalader’s book of Trump-administration-adjacent deal work, and a handful of senior litigation partners departed earlier this year in the merger’s aftermath. The integration crunch in May is operational; the strategic question (whether a finance-first marriage holds together once a downturn tests the lockstep) is the one that will define the firm’s second half of 2026.

Why it mattersFor a JD/MBA aimed at capital-markets practice, the league table for deal counsel is being rewritten in real time. The clients Matthew will sit across from in 2027 are choosing their counsel now, and a $3.9 billion finance-led firm with global regulatory depth is a new option on every pitch deck.

Trump convenes Cabinet on Iran as Rubio says talks need “several more days” and Senate Republicans question the terms

President Trump is meeting his Cabinet on Wednesday to push through the closing stages of an agreement intended to end the US-Iran war, with the negotiation visibly less settled than the administration claimed on Sunday. Secretary of State told reporters Tuesday that talks on extending the ceasefire and on reopening the would take “several more days,” framing the choice as “a good deal or no deal,” while CNN reported that Iranian state media circulated a draft memo that the White House called “a fabrication.” The current text would have Tehran give up its stockpile of in exchange for sanctions relief, with whether the ceasefire also covers Israel’s operations against in Lebanon among the load-bearing open questions.

The political problem now is at home as much as it is in Tehran. Senators of South Carolina, Roger Wicker of Mississippi, and Ted Cruz of Texas have said the terms appear too favorable to Tehran, opening a flank of Republican criticism that the administration had not faced when the deal looked like a fait accompli over the weekend. Trump’s Sunday call demanding that eight Arab and Muslim countries sign onto the Abraham Accords as a condition of the Iran deal added a regional precondition that Pakistan rejected outright, and that Saudi and Qatari leaders met with what an attendee described to Axios as “stunned silence.” The Wednesday Cabinet meeting is the moment where the White House either lands the text or formally moves the timeline.

Why it mattersThe single largest market and foreign-policy variable of the spring is sitting on this Cabinet meeting and the seventy-two hours that follow it. The shape of the deal — whether bilateral, regional, or stalled — sets the run rate for both Brent crude and the IPO calendar.

AI · Chicago

University of Chicago Law makes generative-AI literacy a 1L requirement; the Bigelow legal-writing program walls AI out of Autumn quarter

The will require every member of its incoming first-year class to complete a set of self-directed AI literacy modules during their first quarter, the school confirmed in a curriculum update circulated to admitted students. The modules are designed to bring everyone to a working floor on generative AI in legal work and to steer students toward tools that respect and client confidentiality, rather than letting students rely on consumer chatbots whose terms of service do not. The pitch from Dean Thomas Miles, distributed to the AALS this spring, was that minimum AI literacy is now part of professional competence rather than an elective.

The interesting move is the carve-out. The school’s — the required 1L legal research and writing course — will explicitly bar AI tools during the Autumn quarter, on the theory that students need to be able to write a clean memo and shepardize a citation under their own steam before they layer a model on top. Several new upper-level electives (Advanced Legal Writing in the Age of AI; Regulation of AI; Digital Lawyering; Generative AI and Legal Practice) sit alongside a freestanding AI Lab that will support clinical work. Above the Law surfaced the rollout Tuesday in its Morning Docket under the framing that law schools are now struggling to teach AI given how fast the tools change.

Why it mattersThe hiring profile for a 2L summer associate is already adjusting to assume baseline AI literacy. A top-six law school treating it as a structural prerequisite, rather than an enrichment, sets the curve other schools are now under pressure to match.
