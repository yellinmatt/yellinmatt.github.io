DOJ staff signal they will clear Paramount’s $110 billion takeover of Warner Bros. Discovery

Justice Department antitrust staff emerged from a two-hour meeting Tuesday persuaded that Paramount Skydance’s $110 billion cash-and-stock acquisition of should be allowed to close, according to Semafor, which broke the story Tuesday evening. , Paramount’s chairman and chief executive, used the session to commit the merged studio to roughly thirty theatrical releases a year, an answer to the open letter from nearly 3,500 Hollywood figures, including Jane Fonda, J.J. Abrams and Mark Ruffalo, who argued the deal would cut jobs and consolidate distribution.

Staff have not formally signed off and the analysis can still shift, people familiar with the meeting told Reuters, but the trajectory now leans toward clearance with behavioral, not structural, remedies. The decision sits with ’s antitrust division, which had subpoenaed the parties in March on streaming competition, content-rights bottlenecks and theatrical output. have already approved the deal; bondholders signed off on a $110 billion debt-and-cash package; the antitrust gate has been the last live constraint.

If staff carry the day, the largest American media combination since AT&T-Time Warner closes into a market where Disney, Netflix and a merged Paramount-Warner anchor a three-studio center of gravity. The capital-markets read is simpler: a $110 billion take-private clears, the bondholder consent is paid, and the bankers who priced the debt and structured the consideration mix close one of the year’s defining deal books.

U.S. strikes drone-control site near Bandar Abbas as IRGC claims retaliatory hit on American airbase

American forces shot down four Iranian one-way attack drones near the Wednesday night and then hit a ground-control station outside Bandar Abbas around 1:30 a.m. Thursday local time, the second American strike on the Iranian port in 48 hours. U.S. officials called the action defensive, aimed at preserving a fragile ceasefire that Iran’s said it would defend with a “decisive reciprocal response.”

The IRGC announced Thursday morning that it had hit an unspecified American airbase at roughly 4:50 a.m. local time in retaliation. President Trump, who spent Wednesday at a Camp David cabinet meeting telling reporters he would not “rush” an Iran deal, warned that Oman would “behave just like everybody else, or we’ll have to blow ’em up” if it joined Iran in administering Hormuz shipping, a line he repeated after Iranian state media floated a joint-management proposal he immediately disowned.

Why it mattersEach tit-for-tat strike sets a fresh marker on what counts as “defensive” under a ceasefire neither side has signed, and the Oman threat is the first time the administration has named a Gulf partner as a possible target.

Framing noteCNN leads on Trump’s “won’t rush” line as evidence the deal is slipping; Fox News Digital frames the strikes as proof Trump is negotiating from strength while talks “proceed nicely.”

BigLaw hires more lateral associates than entry-level for the first time in years

reported Wednesday that the AmLaw 200 hired more lateral associates than entry-level associates in 2025, 3,296 versus 3,039, the first reversal in years. The piece, by recruiter , frames the shift as structural: firms are recruiting laterals at the third-to-fifth-year band, mostly into white-collar defense, securities litigation and antitrust, and pulling more aggressively from former federal clerks and DOJ alumni.

The data sits on top of an associate market in which the share planning to leave doubles from one in ten first-years to one in five by year two. Geographic share is moving with it: New York’s slice of T14 hiring is down 11 points over five years, with Chicago, Washington and the Sun Belt (Atlanta, Austin, Dallas, Miami) absorbing the difference. remains the door for summer-associate placement, but the back-door pipeline is now the bigger one.

Why it mattersIf lateral capacity is the marginal hire, the firm a 2L joins as a summer associate is no longer the same firm two years later, and capital markets is one of the practice areas where the lateral premium has stayed sticky.

Companies fold tariff impacts into 2026 proxy pay disclosures

A Wednesday post on the by Joyce Chen of reports that a meaningful share of S&P 500 issuers cited Trump tariff impacts in their 2026 proxy compensation disclosures, mostly when explaining missed performance targets or compensation-committee adjustments to incentive payouts.

Issuers split with investors on what to do about it. Investors mostly recommend no board action, treating tariff drag as a macro factor executives should bear. A minority of issuers, including The Children’s Place, declined to set first-half fiscal-year performance metrics at all, citing macro unpredictability, and shifted compensation committees toward evaluating how management responded rather than what numbers it hit. The post advises companies to assess whether tariffs warrant tailored for impacted geographies and inputs.

Why it mattersThe proxy season is now where tariff policy gets priced into compensation, and the resulting say-on-pay vote is the closest thing to a public investor referendum on whether boards bent the curve fairly.
