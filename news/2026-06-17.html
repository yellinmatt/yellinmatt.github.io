After weeks of secrecy, the US publishes its 14-point Iran deal, with bombs promised if it fails

A senior US official read out the official 14-point memorandum of understandingConceptA framework recording mutual intent, not a binding treaty. This one is an interim deal with a 60-day window to negotiate the final agreement. with Iran on June 17, the first authoritative release of terms after days in which the administration declined to disclose them. CNN, Bloomberg, CBS and Time published the text in full. It calls for an immediate and permanent end to military operations on all fronts, “including in Lebanon”; the United States to lift its naval blockadeConceptThe US Navy cordon imposed during the war to choke Iranian shipping. The MOU unwinds it within 30 days, restoring prewar commercial traffic. within 30 days; and immediate US waivers for Iranian crude and petroleum exports, banking and insurance included.

On the nuclear question the text says Iran “will maintain the current status quo of its nuclear program,” while the US imposes no new sanctions and deploys no additional forces. A final agreement is to be negotiated within a maximum of 60 days, extendable by mutual consent, and the document references a roughly $300 billion reconstruction fund for Iran to be built with regional partners. The deal was signed digitally on June 15; an in-person ceremony in Switzerland remains slated for Friday, June 19, with Pakistan and Qatar mediating.

At his closing news conference at the G7InstitutionThe Group of Seven advanced economies, whose 2026 summit Macron hosted at Évian-les-Bains, France, June 15 to 17., President Trump supplied the enforcement teeth himself: “It’s a memorandum of understanding, and if I don’t like it, we’ll go back to shooting at them, dropping bombs right smack in the middle of their head.” He said the United States would resume bombing if Tehran fails to comply. Israel, which calls itself “not a party” to the deal, says it will not be bound by it.

“If I don’t like it, we’ll go back to dropping bombs.”
— President Trump, June 17

Framing noteThe right (Fox opinion, National Review) reads “maintain the status quo” as a near-capitulation that leaves Iran’s enrichment intact; the left and center (CNN, Time) lead with the war ending and Hormuz reopening, treating the bombing threat as the enforcement mechanism.

Why it mattersThe year’s largest geopolitical premium on the oil price turns on whether “maintain the status quo” is a durable freeze or a face-saving pause, and for a reader who watches how deals actually hold, the distance between a digital signature and a Friday ceremony is exactly where frameworks come apart.
