Comcast's Sky buys ITV's screens for up to £1.6bn, folding a British institution into an American giant

agreed on Monday to sell its media and entertainment division to for up to £1.6bn, about $2.1bn, handing a cornerstone of British broadcasting to the American cable giant Comcast. Sky pays roughly £1.2bn for the ITVX streaming service and ITV's free-to-air channels; the balance runs through a reciprocal swap in which Sky sells the Great British Bake Off producer Love Productions to ITV Studios, plus a payment in 2028 tied to advertising revenue. The deal, first floated in November, now goes to the UK competition regulator.

ITV keeps the crown jewel of its business, , the production engine behind I'm A Celebrity and Mr Bates vs The Post Office. To keep the shows flowing the other way, Sky signed a £2.1bn multi-year output deal committing ITV Studios to produce Love Island, Coronation Street and I'm a Celebrity through 2032, so a content-supply agreement does double duty as part of the price. Both sides pitched the combination as a British "commercial streaming champion" built to compete with Netflix, Disney+ and Amazon.

For a dealmaker the structure is the lesson: a that keeps production with the seller, an keyed to ad revenue, a reciprocal asset swap, and a locked output deal all folded into one price, now facing a review. And it cuts against the grain of Comcast's own strategy: even as the parent spins NBCUniversal's cable networks into a separate company, its Sky arm is consolidating British broadcast.

"This is a defining moment for British media."
— Dana Strong, Sky Group CEO, July 6

Why it mattersThis is the intersection a capital-markets-and-entertainment lawyer lives at: a cross-border media consolidation whose carve-out, earnout and content-supply mechanics, and its competition review, are the whole game.

The quarterly-report era nears its end as the SEC's comment window closes

The public comment period closes Monday on a Securities and Exchange Commission proposal that would let public companies file a semiannual report, a new , in place of the quarterly that has anchored corporate disclosure since the 1970s. The proposal would also amend to fit the new cadence.

If adopted as soon as 2027, calendar-year companies could elect semiannual reporting by 2028. Chair frames it as relief from quarterly short-termism and part of a wider capital-formation push; critics such as Better Markets call it the biggest rollback of investor disclosure in more than 50 years. It closes the window on a rulemaking that slots into Atkins's broader deregulatory reset.

Why it mattersThe reporting rhythm every securities lawyer, IPO issuer and capital-markets desk lives by is on the table, and the record on whether to end the quarter closes today.

Giannis Antetokounmpo is a Miami Heat

The Milwaukee Bucks agreed on Monday to trade Giannis Antetokounmpo, a two-time MVP, and Bobby Portis to the Miami Heat, a pre-draft blockbuster that reshapes the Eastern Conference. Miami sends back Tyler Herro, Jaime Jaquez Jr., Kel'el Ware and Kasparas Jakucionis, three first-round picks including the in Tuesday's draft, a 2030 pick swap and a 2033 second-rounder; the 2031 and 2033 firsts head to Milwaukee .

Antetokounmpo had signaled since May 2025 that he wanted out; the Bucks' finalists were Miami and Boston, which offered a package built around Jaylen Brown. He becomes extension-eligible October 1 but is expected to wait until January for a four-year worth about $275m.

Why it mattersA franchise-altering trade is also a masterclass in collective-bargaining mechanics, the extension window, the max math, unprotected-pick risk, and a jolt to Heat franchise value, local media and sponsorship.
