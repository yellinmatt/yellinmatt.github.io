The Splash · AI · Washington, D.C.

U.S. forces Anthropic to take its most powerful models offline

On Friday evening, Anthropic disabled its two most capable models, , for every customer worldwide after the Commerce Department ordered it to cut off all foreign nationals. The , which the company says it received at 5:21 p.m. Eastern, bars access by any foreign person inside or outside the United States, including Anthropic's own foreign-national employees; unable to wall those users off cleanly, the company pulled the models entirely. Access to its other models was unaffected. It is the first time a leading American AI company has taken a publicly deployed model offline at the federal government's command.

Commerce Secretary 's letter to chief executive cited national security but, by Anthropic's account, offered no specifics beyond a belief that someone had found a way to Fable 5. Anthropic said the technique it was shown was narrow, amounting to asking the model to read a codebase and flag software flaws, and that the same capability is "widely available" from rival systems, including OpenAI's GPT-5.5. The company is complying while disputing the premise, warning that recalling a model used by hundreds of millions of people over a narrow jailbreak would, if the standard held industry-wide, "essentially halt all new model deployments."

The order lands on a company already crosswise with the administration. The friction began when Anthropic refused to let the military use its models in fully autonomous weapons, which left it on a Pentagon blacklist as too dangerous for government use; the Commerce action now brands it too dangerous for foreign use as well. The timing cuts another way: Anthropic filed confidentially for an initial public offering this month, and a regulator's power to switch off its flagship product overnight is precisely the kind of risk a prospectus must now price.

"We believe this is a misunderstanding and are working to restore access."
— Anthropic statement, June 12

Why it mattersFor a lawyer or a markets desk, this is the moment AI governance stopped being theoretical: a Cabinet department reached into the model layer and switched off a commercial product by letter, not by statute or court order, and the company complied within hours.

Trump says an Iran deal will be signed Sunday; Tehran is cautious on timing

President Trump said Saturday that a to end the war with Iran would be signed Sunday, reopening the "immediately" and without tolls, with prewar shipping restored within roughly 30 days. The framework, mediated by Pakistan, pairs a 60-day ceasefire that folds in Lebanon with an Iranian commitment not to for 15 to 20 years and to dismantle nuclear sites, in exchange for the staggered release of about $24 billion in frozen Iranian assets sequenced to compliance. Pakistani Prime Minister said negotiators had reached a "final, agreed-upon text."

Tehran was more guarded. Iranian state media said the country remained cautious on timing and had not closed on its red lines, and Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu has said Israel is "not party" to the understanding. The war began in late February, when the United States and Israel struck Iran; Iran's retaliatory grip on the Strait, through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil moved before the fighting, has whipsawed crude markets for weeks. A signing would be the first formal off-ramp, if it holds.

Why it mattersA reopened Hormuz would pull the risk premium out of oil and steady the rate math heading into next week's Fed meeting; a collapse at the signing table would send both the wrong way.

Framing noteFox cast the deal as effectively done, leading with Trump's promise it "will be signed tomorrow," while Al Jazeera and Iranian state outlets stressed unresolved red lines and the concessions critics say Tehran has made.

SpaceX completes the largest IPO in history, and the market wants more

SpaceX priced the largest initial public offering ever this week, selling about 556 million shares at $135 to raise $75 billion, eclipsing 's $29.4 billion record, and debuted on the Nasdaq under the ticker SPCX at a valuation near $1.77 trillion. The deal was about four times , on roughly $250 billion of demand, with an unusual 30 percent set aside for retail buyers. Shares closed their first full day up 19 percent at $160.95, after opening at $150 and touching $176.52, pushing the implied value past $2 trillion.

The mechanics were as telling as the size. Gibson Dunn advised the company and Davis Polk the underwriters, on about $25.5 million in legal fees, and the folding-in of Elon Musk's made this, functionally, an AI listing. It is the first of three megacaps lining up: OpenAI and Anthropic have both behind it, which means the year's three largest listings could print within months of one another.

Why it mattersThree of the decade's biggest listings forming at once resets what "large" means in the capital markets, and each one is, at bottom, a bet on AI compute.

"No Kings" plans a 2,000-site protest day, and deliberately skips Washington

The coalition is staging what could be one of the largest single-day protests of Trump's second term on Sunday, with demonstrations planned at more than 1,800 locations, organizers say, and counts circulating near 2,000. The day is timed to the President's 80th birthday and built around a 90-minute "Rise Up, Sing Out" concert at in New York, featuring Bette Midler, Patti Smith, Jane Fonda, and Joy Reid, with watch parties feeding the local rallies. Co-presented with the , the night is cast by organizers as a defense of free speech against what they call "strongman politics."

The strategy is conspicuous. The coalition is staging no event in Washington, where Trump marks the day with a , choosing instead to "make action everywhere else the story of America that day." Turnout is the open question. Organizers reported some eight million participants on March 28; conservatives are unmoved, with a Fox News columnist dismissing the rallies as "performative" and their participants as "puppets."

Why it mattersWhether eight million turn out again or far fewer, the number will be read on both sides as a verdict on the second term, and the choice to skip Washington is a wager that a thousand small protests register louder than one march.

Framing noteThe left frames the day as grassroots defense of "First Amendment freedoms"; Fox opinion called the premise that Trump "rules as a king" divorced from reality and the protests "performative."

Culture · Box office

Spielberg's "Disclosure Day" posts his biggest original opening in decades

Steven Spielberg's "Disclosure Day," an alien-contact thriller from , opened to about $44 million domestically and $94 million worldwide, the best start of his career for a film not based on existing material. It drew $6.5 million in and $19 million on opening day, behind strong reviews and an 89 percent Rotten Tomatoes score. The figure tops the $41 million bow of his 2018 hit "Ready Player One" and marks his best opening since "Indiana Jones and the Kingdom of the Crystal Skull" in 2008.

The number matters less as a record than as a test. Analysts had set $50 million as the bar to justify the film's cost and roughly $300 million worldwide as the line for profit, so a $44 million domestic start puts "Disclosure Day" in solid but not spectacular territory for an expensive swing. In a summer crowded with sequels, the result reads as a real-time referendum on whether a marquee director and a fresh premise can still open a , the kind of bet studios have grown reluctant to make.

Why it mattersStudios have all but stopped greenlighting expensive originals; a healthy opening for a non-franchise Spielberg picture is the rare data point the people arguing to keep making them can point to.

Knicks-Spurs delivers the NBA's biggest Finals audience in years

The Knicks-Spurs Finals has turned into a ratings event. Game 3 on ABC averaged 23.79 million viewers, according to , the largest Game 3 crowd since Michael Jordan's Bulls, and Game 4, in which New York erased a 29-point deficit to win 107-106, drew 20.9 million. The series pairs the Knicks' first Finals run since 1999 with the arrival of San Antonio's , and the demographic story is the one networks prize most: viewership among teens is up 138 percent, the 18-to-24 group up 147 percent, and women up 121 percent.

For the league and , the timing is ideal. The surge arrives in the first year of the NBA's new 11-year , struck on the bet that live sports remains the last reliable mass audience. A blockbuster Finals built on a big-market team and a generational star is the proof of concept the league and its partners paid for.

Why it mattersThe entire media-rights bet rests on the premise that live sports still gathers a mass audience nothing else can; a Finals pulling north of 20 million and surging with young viewers is the evidence that thesis needed.
