OpenAI files confidentially for what could be the largest IPO ever

OpenAI confidentially filed its S-1 with the Securities and Exchange Commission on Friday, setting up a public listing targeted for the fourth quarter at a valuation between $852 billion and $1 trillion. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley are leading the offering, and a debut could come as early as September. A confidential filing keeps the prospectus private until roughly fifteen days before the roadshow, so the public will not see the full numbers for months.

The figures underneath cut in both directions. OpenAI is generating about $2 billion a month, hit a $25 billion annualized run rate by March, and counts 50 million consumer subscribers and 9 million business users. It is also burning cash at a historic rate: a negative 122% non-GAAP operating margin in the first quarter means it lost an additional $1.22 for every dollar of revenue, and it needs an estimated $207 billion in fresh capital through 2030 just to honor its existing compute commitments.

The filing arrives days after SpaceX accelerated its own record listing, and as an IPO window that stayed shut through much of 2022 to 2024 swings wide open for artificial-intelligence and frontier-tech names. With private rounds no longer large enough to cover the bill, public markets are the only pool deep enough to bridge the gap.

"For every dollar of revenue, the company lost an additional $1.22."
— Fortune, May 22

Why it mattersThis is the capital-markets event of the cycle on Matthew's exact career path. The disclosure questions it raises, governance, the for-profit conversion, and the scale of compute liabilities, will set the template for how AI companies reach public capital, and the deal teams running it sit at the BigLaw and bank seats he is aiming at.

Mungiu's 'Fjord' takes the Palme d'Or, NEON's seventh straight

Romanian director Cristian Mungiu won the Palme d'Or at the 79th Cannes Film Festival on Saturday for "Fjord," his English-language debut, starring Renate Reinsve and Sebastian Stan as devout Romanian parents who move to a small Norwegian village and find themselves accused of child abuse. It is Mungiu's second Palme, after "4 Months, 3 Weeks and 2 Days" in 2007, putting him in the festival's rare two-time club. Park Chan-wook's jury gave the Grand Prix to Andrei Zvyagintsev's "Minotaur."

The larger industry story is distribution. "Fjord" is released by NEON, whose seventh consecutive Palme extends a streak that began with "Parasite" in 2019 and runs through "Anora" last cycle, an unmatched record of acquiring eventual winners. NEON locked North American and several English-language territories for the film back in May 2025, a bet placed a full year before the jury voted that just paid off in the most valuable currency in art-house film.

Why it mattersThis is entertainment law in miniature: the value sits in the rights deal NEON struck a year early, not the trophy itself. The ability to scout, acquire, and shepherd a Palme winner is exactly the pipeline-and-rights strategy Matthew's EASL track is built around.

Trump says Iran deal 'largely negotiated,' with Hormuz reopening; key terms still disputed

President Trump said Saturday that a framework to end the US-Israel war with Iran had been "largely negotiated," and would include reopening the Strait of Hormuz, after a call with leaders from eight regional states and a separate call with Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu. Iran's foreign ministry said the two sides were in the "final stage" of a memorandum of understanding but "not to the point of an agreement." Pakistan said talks between army chief Asim Munir and Iranian leaders in Tehran produced progress toward a "final understanding."

The open questions are substantive. An outlet tied to Iran's Revolutionary Guard disputed the Hormuz claim, reporting that the strait would remain under Iranian management in the latest draft, and Reuters quoted a senior Iranian source saying Tehran has not agreed to surrender its enriched-uranium stockpile, which is not part of the preliminary deal. Trump told Axios the odds of a "good" deal versus resuming the war were "50/50," and said he would likely decide by Sunday.

Why it mattersA genuine Hormuz reopening would unwind the oil-price risk premium that has shadowed markets for weeks; a collapse would put it back. The distance between "largely negotiated" and signed is where both the market and the war now sit.

Framing noteThe split is less left-versus-right than optimism-versus-doubt: Fox and CBS foregrounded Trump's near-done framing, while NPR and Al Jazeera led with Iran's pushback, and even Sen. Ted Cruz warned the framework could be a "disastrous mistake."

New rule forces most green-card applicants to leave the US and apply abroad

The Trump administration, in a USCIS memo dated May 21 and reported Friday, will require most foreign nationals seeking permanent residence to leave the United States and apply through consulates in their home countries, ending a half-century practice that let many adjust status from within the country. Green cards will be issued domestically only in "extraordinary circumstances," with officers making case-by-case determinations. About 600,000 people apply from inside the US each year, among them spouses of citizens, work- and student-visa holders, and asylees.

USCIS framed the change as following the statute and freeing resources for naturalizations and visas for crime and trafficking victims. Immigration lawyers and aid groups warned it will force applicants to abandon jobs and families for weeks or months at their own expense, push cases into backlogged or nonexistent consulates, and route decisions through consular officers whose calls are "virtually unchallengeable." World Relief warned the policy would create "indefinite separation of families."

Why it mattersConsular processing sits largely beyond judicial review, so the change shifts enormous discretion to the State Department and away from the courts. It is a structural immigration-law shift with direct stakes for employers, universities, and the hundreds of thousands mid-application.

Framing noteFox and the Washington Times led on the "follow the law and free up USCIS" rationale, while NPR, CNN and PBS led on the family-separation "Catch-22," the same memo read as enforcement versus disruption.

Corporate · Starbase, Texas

Starship's first V3 flight reaches an orbit-class path, loses the booster

SpaceX launched the first next-generation Starship V3 on Friday from Starbase, Texas, a mixed but mostly successful debut for the scaled-up vehicle. The Super Heavy booster failed to ignite properly for its boostback burn and crash-landed in the ocean, an outcome SpaceX had flagged as acceptable for a first V3 flight with no tower catch planned. The upper stage lost one Raptor Vacuum engine on ascent but flew on.

Ship 39 reached a trajectory the company called "within bounds," deployed 22 mock satellites (two carrying cameras to inspect the heat shield), and splashed down in the Indian Ocean about an hour later. The flight is the last major test before SpaceX's IPO roadshow; the company is targeting a roughly $1.75 trillion listing, with pricing as soon as June 11 and a Nasdaq debut planned for June 12.

Why it mattersThe flight de-risks the technical story underpinning the largest IPO ever to price. For capital markets, Starship's reliability is now a prospectus input, not just an engineering milestone, and Friday's result gives bankers a cleaner narrative to sell.
