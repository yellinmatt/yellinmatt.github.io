AI · San Francisco / Washington

Anthropic's Mythos finds tens of thousands of zero-days, and bank regulators hit pause

Anthropic's new Mythos model, previewed last month, has been turned loose on the world's software and the results are staggering: it has identified thousands of previously unknown "zero-day" vulnerabilities across every major operating system and web browser, with the running total now in the tens of thousands. Where an earlier Anthropic model found roughly 20 flaws in the Firefox browser, Mythos found nearly 300. Bloomberg reported on May 19 that the Federal Reserve and the Office of the Comptroller of the Currency are pausing some cyber examinations of the largest U.S. banks, giving lenders room to harden systems against threats the model surfaced. Anthropic has confined Mythos to a small set of partners under "Project Glasswing," extended access to more than 40 critical-infrastructure organizations, and committed up to $100 million in usage credits plus $4 million in donations to open-source security groups.

On May 20, Reuters reported that fears of "unfettered hacking" were overstated: the same capability that uncovers flaws is being routed to defenders before anyone else, and the EU is still negotiating preview access. The two readings sit uneasily together. One holds that a model patching decades-old holes faster than any human team is the best thing to happen to software security in years. The other notes it is one jailbreak away from arming a hostile state. That a private company's model can move the Federal Reserve's examination calendar is the part that should not be lost: AI capability has crossed into systemic-risk territory.

"AI has reached a cyber 'moment of danger.'"
— CNBC, on Anthropic's Dario Amodei, May 5

Why it mattersFor a capital-markets lawyer the story is the regulatory mechanics: when one private model forces the Fed and the OCC to reschedule bank exams, AI has become a supervisory input, not a productivity tool. And it puts Anthropic, the firm at the center of your novel's premise, in the room where financial regulation is written.

Putin follows Trump to Beijing, and he and Xi sign a 47-page pact aimed at Washington

Days after hosting President Trump for a state visit, Xi Jinping welcomed Vladimir Putin to Beijing on May 20 with a red carpet and a military band playing both anthems. The two leaders signed roughly 40 documents and a 47-page joint statement pledging a "new type of international relations" and a multipolar order, and jointly criticized Trump's proposed $175 billion "Golden Dome" missile-defense system. On the long-stalled Power of Siberia 2 pipeline, which would carry 50 billion cubic meters of gas a year from Russia's Yamal fields to China via Mongolia, the Kremlin said the sides reached a "basic understanding" on the route but set no timeline.

The framing split along familiar lines. The New York Times cast a "weakened" Putin trailing Trump and seeking an opening from the U.S.-Iran war; Russian and Chinese state media presented deepening energy ties and a united front against American power. The economics underwrite the optics: Russia's oil-and-gas revenue is projected up 39% year-on-year in May, lifted by the war's effect on prices, even as Ukrainian drone strikes keep hammering Russian refineries.

Framing noteThe New York Times led with a "weakened" Putin trailing Trump and looking for leverage; the Washington Times led with Putin and Xi "hailing" high-level ties and growing energy trade.

Why it mattersA China-Russia bloc hardening against a Trump-led Washington reshapes the cost of capital for energy, defense, and cross-border deals: the macro weather every capital-markets desk reads before it prices risk.

Corporate · New York

SpaceX speeds up the largest IPO ever: ~$1.75 trillion, prospectus as soon as today

SpaceX is accelerating toward a listing that would dwarf every prior IPO. Reuters reports the company aims to make its prospectus public as soon as Wednesday, launch a roadshow on June 4, price as early as June 11, and debut on Nasdaq on June 12 under the ticker SPCX. The target is roughly $75 billion raised at about a $1.75 trillion valuation, a step up from the $1.25 trillion mark set when SpaceX merged with Musk's AI startup xAI in February. Morgan Stanley, Bank of America, Citigroup, JPMorgan, and Goldman Sachs are the lead bookrunners, with 16 more banks in supporting roles spanning institutional, retail, and international channels.

A faster-than-expected SEC review pulled the timeline forward, dovetailing with Chair Paul Atkins's deregulatory push: the commission proposed "transformative" reforms to public-company offerings on May 19. At Harvard's corporate-governance forum the same day, Lucian Bebchuk and Kobi Kastiel flagged the governance stakes of a founder-controlled colossus going public at a two-trillion-dollar scale, where Musk's voting control would dwarf ordinary public shareholders.

Why it mattersThis is the equity-capital-markets event of the cycle and exactly the kind of deal a Greenberg Traurig capital-markets associate lives inside. The SEC's speed signals a deregulatory window that could pull more mega-issuers off the sidelines.

Barney Frank, architect of Dodd-Frank, dies at 86

Barney Frank, who as chair of the House Financial Services Committee co-wrote the most sweeping overhaul of U.S. financial regulation since the Depression, died Tuesday evening at his home in Ogunquit, Maine, of complications from congestive heart failure. He was 86. The 2010 Dodd-Frank Act, signed by President Obama with Frank and Senator Chris Dodd at his side, created the Consumer Financial Protection Bureau, the Volcker Rule, and the architecture of post-2008 bank supervision. Frank represented southern Massachusetts for 32 years and was among the first members of Congress to come out as gay.

He had entered hospice last month and gave a characteristically caustic CNN interview on May 3. His death lands at a pointed moment: the SEC under Chair Atkins is unwinding parts of the post-crisis regime, which makes the durability of Dodd-Frank a live question rather than settled law. The obituaries crossed the spectrum, a rare consensus on a figure who spent a career provoking one.

Framing noteLeft-leaning obituaries (Boston Globe, NBC) crowned him "architect of Wall Street reform"; the Daily Caller led with the "liberal icon who called out the left on his deathbed."

Why it mattersDodd-Frank is foundational reading for any capital-markets lawyer. Frank's death is a marker as the Atkins SEC tests how much of his framework survives the deregulatory turn.

Corporate · Mountain View · AI x labor

Intuit cuts 17% of staff, about 3,000 jobs, as it bets the company on AI

Intuit, the maker of TurboTax, QuickBooks, and Credit Karma, will lay off about 17% of its global workforce, roughly 3,000 people across seven countries, according to an internal memo from CEO Sasan Goodarzi reported on May 20. The company is closing offices in Reno, Nevada, and Woodland Hills, California; affected U.S. staff receive 16 weeks' severance plus two weeks per year of service, with a final employment date of July 31. Goodarzi framed the cuts as streamlining to "sharpen focus on key priorities," chief among them artificial intelligence.

The tell is what Intuit is buying while it cuts. It has signed multi-year deals with Anthropic and OpenAI to embed their models in its software and to push Intuit's tax, accounting, and finance tools into Claude and ChatGPT. A tax-and-accounting software firm shedding 3,000 jobs while wiring itself to frontier models is the clearest corporate signal yet that AI is coming for white-collar professional services, the same week HSBC's chief executive told staff to "not fight AI" as the bank began its own cuts.

Why it mattersThis is the live case study under your novel's premise, automation hollowing a profession from the inside, and a margin-and-headcount template other professional-services firms, law included, will study closely.

Culture · New York · EASL

James Murdoch clinches New York Magazine and Vox's podcast network for $300M-plus

James Murdoch's Lupa Systems has clinched a deal to buy New York Magazine, the Vox Media Podcast Network, and Vox.com from Vox Media for more than $300 million, the companies confirmed on May 20, weeks after his offer first surfaced. The package includes New York's verticals, The Cut, Vulture, Intelligencer, The Strategist, Curbed, and Grub Street, plus marquee podcasts including Kara Swisher and Scott Galloway's "Pivot," "Criminal," and Esther Perel's "Where Should We Begin?" Vox Media expects the sale to close in four to six weeks, with the properties staying under current CEO Jim Bankoff.

The buyer is the story. The estranged son of Rupert Murdoch, who walked away from the family's News Corp empire, is assembling a prestige-media portfolio of his own. It is a bet that intellectual-brand journalism and premium podcasts retain value even as digital-media valuations sag, and a counterweight to the conservative media machine his father built.

Why it mattersA clean media-M&A comp at the intersection of content, IP, and audience: the deal logic an entertainment-and-media lawyer parses, and a window into where prestige-media assets actually trade.
