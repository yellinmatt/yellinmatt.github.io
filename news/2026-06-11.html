AI · Wall Street

Oracle books a record $638 billion backlog, and the stock falls anyway

Oracle reported the strongest quarter in its history on Wednesday and lost roughly a tenth of its market value in extended trading for the trouble. Fiscal fourth-quarter revenue hit a record $19.2 billion, up 21 percent, with cloud revenue up 47 percent to $9.9 billion and cloud infrastructure up 93 percent to $5.8 billion. Co-CEO said the company signed $67 billion in AI infrastructure contracts in the quarter alone, driving from $553 billion to $638 billion.

The selloff was about how Oracle pays for all that. CFO told analysts the company expects roughly $70 billion in net capital outlays in fiscal 2027, and Oracle has already raised about $30 billion of a planned $50 billion this year through bonds and . Investors also flagged how much of the backlog is concentrated in a handful of frontier-lab customers, OpenAI chief among them.

The report landed five days after the chip rout that wiped more than $1 trillion off semiconductor names, and it sharpens the same question that selloff asked: not whether AI demand exists, but whether the companies building for it can finance the buildout faster than the market loses patience. Oracle, which spent decades as a software cash machine, is now effectively a leveraged construction company whose biggest tenants are pre-revenue model labs.

Iran declares the Strait of Hormuz closed to all shipping and strikes two tankers

Iran's declared the Strait of Hormuz closed to all vessels early Thursday, including oil tankers and commercial ships, and said any vessel attempting passage "will be targeted." Its navy said it struck two tankers that tried to transit anyway. The declaration came after launched a second consecutive night of strikes Wednesday evening against Iranian surveillance, communications, and air-defense sites; the US military disputed that the strait, which carries roughly a fifth of globally traded oil, is actually closed.

President Trump told Fox News the bombing will get "bigger, more powerful" Thursday night and said the US plans to take and "total control" of Iran's oil industry, while claiming Washington has covertly moved "millions of barrels every night" through the strait. He also said Iranian officials he spoke with asked for the bombing to stop. Tehran's line, carried by Iranian and regional outlets, is the mirror image: the US is seeking neither ceasefire nor dialogue.

Framing noteFox News leads with Trump's plan to "take Kharg Island" and Iranian officials asking for the bombing to stop; Al Jazeera leads with Tehran's charge that the US "seeks no ceasefire or dialogue."

Why it mattersA declared total closure, enforced with strikes on tankers, is the sharpest economic escalation of the war so far. Whether or not it holds, it puts an oil-shock premium under Friday's SpaceX listing and next week's Fed meeting.

Federal judge permanently blocks Alabama's nitrogen-gas executions as unconstitutionally cruel

US District Judge permanently barred Alabama from executing death-row inmate Jeffrey Lee by , holding that the method violates the Eighth Amendment's ban on cruel and unusual punishment. The ruling came hours after an panel reversed her earlier finding for the state, calling the roughly three minutes an inmate could take to lose awareness "intolerable" given the suffering likely under Alabama's protocol.

Attorney General Steve Marshall's office filed its appeal Wednesday. The injunction formally covers only Lee, and Alabama retains lethal injection and the electric chair, but the reasoning reaches the method itself, which Alabama introduced in 2024 and other states have since adopted. A method-level Eighth Amendment holding, sustained on appeal, would be the most significant death-penalty ruling in years and a likely Supreme Court vehicle.

Why it mattersMethod-of-execution litigation is where Eighth Amendment doctrine actually moves, and a court has now struck the method states adopted as the fix for lethal injection. The en banc and cert fight that follows is worth tracking in real time.

College athletes sue to break the $20.5 million pay cap built on the House settlement

Division I football and basketball players filed a federal antitrust class action Wednesday against NCAA president Charlie Baker, CEO Bryan Seeley, and the four power-conference commissioners. The named plaintiffs, USC's Talanoa Ili and Stanford's Charlie Mirer, represented by Berger Montague and Freedman Normand Friedland, argue that the commission's clearinghouse and the $20.5 million annual cap on school payments to athletes violate name, image, and likeness statutes in California and 16 other states, along with the Sherman Act.

The suit's design is the interesting part: it does not attack the itself, which a federal court approved in 2025, but the machinery built on top of it, including rules barring payments from "associated entities or individuals" such as boosters and collectives. Sportico reports the NCAA is readying defenses grounded in the settlement's court approval.

Why it mattersWhether a court-approved settlement can immunize a wage cap from state NIL laws and fresh Sherman Act attack is the live exam question of sports law, and this is the cleanest vehicle yet. Every athlete-pay deal an EASL practice touches prices off the answer.

Belfast burns through a second night of anti-immigrant rioting after a stabbing

Police in fired water cannon at rioters who set fires and hurled bricks, rocks, and bottles through a second consecutive night of violence Wednesday, after masked men tore bricks from walls and broke up sidewalks with sledgehammers for ammunition. The unrest began Tuesday night, when houses, a Middle Eastern supermarket, and a bus were set ablaze, following the June 8 stabbing of Stephen Ogilvie, a man in his 40s left with serious eye and facial injuries; a 30-year-old Sudanese man was arrested.

The put 200 additional officers on the streets and called in support from other forces as a list of addresses where immigrants supposedly live circulated on social media. Ogilvie's own family pushed against the violence, saying the attack should not be used to incite it and that Northern Ireland depends on migrants in healthcare and beyond.

Why it mattersThe same pattern as Southport 2024 and Ballymena 2025: one crime, metabolized by social media into communal violence inside 48 hours, with the victim's family pleading against it. It is becoming the standing stress test of UK policing and platform governance.
