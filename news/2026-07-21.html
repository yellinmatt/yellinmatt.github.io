A judge approves Anthropic's $1.5 billion book settlement, the first price ever put on AI training data

A federal judge in San Francisco granted final approval on Monday to AnthropicInstitutionThe San Francisco AI lab behind the Claude models, backed by Amazon and Alphabet. It is preparing a public listing that would make it the first frontier lab priced by the market.'s roughly $1.5 billion settlement with authors who said the company pirated their books to train its Claude chatbot, the largest copyright settlement in United States history. Judge Araceli Martínez-OlguínPersonThe Biden-appointed U.S. District Judge for the Northern District of California who inherited the case after Judge Alsup's retirement and signed its final approval. overruled objectors who called the sum too small, writing that their complaint was "not grounded in a realistic assessment of the overall risks and rewards of a trial." The payout works out to about $3,000 per work across an estimated 500,000 titles, with claims already filed on more than 92% of the roughly 480,000 works covered.

The deal traces to now-retired Judge William AlsupPersonThe veteran N.D. Cal. judge, of Oracle v. Google fame, whose June 2025 ruling drew the line the settlement rewards: training was fair use, but storing pirated books was not., who ruled in June 2025 that training Claude was fair useConceptThe copyright doctrine (17 U.S.C. §107) allowing limited unlicensed use. Alsup held that AI training itself qualified, but that hoarding pirated books did not. but that Anthropic violated authors' rights by saving more than seven million pirated books to a "central library." The settlement retires that piracy exposure, and the court also trimmed the plaintiffs' lawyers to about $101 million from the $187.5 million they requested, an $86 million cut.

It is the first major U.S. AI-training copyright case to settle, and it draws the line the whole industry is fighting over: how a model gets its data, not merely what it does with it. Dozens of parallel suits against OpenAI, Google, Meta, and others remain live, and the record labels are pressing the same theory against AI-music firms. For a JD/MBA heading into entertainment law, a concrete number attached to training-data liability is the template the rest of the AI-content wars will be priced against.
