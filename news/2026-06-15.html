Trump declares the Iran war over and orders the blockade lifted; the signing slips to Friday in Geneva

President Trump declared over the weekend that the United States and Iran had finalized a memorandum of understandingConceptA non-binding written framework recording mutual intent. Here it commits both sides to halt operations and open sixty days of nuclear and sanctions talks before any final, binding accord. to end their roughly four-month war, saying he had authorized “the immediate removal of the United States Naval blockade” and the toll-free reopening of the Strait of HormuzPlaceThe narrow waterway between Iran and Oman through which about a fifth of the world’s seaborne oil passes. Threatening to close it was Iran’s chief leverage over global energy prices.. The reported terms have Iran reopening Hormuz without transit fees, the United States releasing roughly $25 billion in frozen Iranian assets staggered to compliance, and Tehran freezing its nuclear posture across a sixty-day negotiating window. The timing, though, is not what Trump described: Iran’s Deputy Foreign Minister Kazem GharibabadiPersonIran’s deputy foreign minister and a senior nuclear negotiator, who confirmed the finalized text while disputing that anything would be signed Sunday. confirmed the finalized text but said it would not be signed Sunday, pointing instead to a ceremony slated for Friday, June 19, in Switzerland, to be executed in person or electronically. An Israeli strike on Beirut’s southern suburbs hours earlier briefly threatened to capsize the whole framework before Washington intervened.

For markets the distinction barely mattered: the prospect of a reopened strait was enough to send Asian equities surging and crude tumbling at Monday’s open. The reach of the moment is the point. After four months in which Iran’s threat to Hormuz functioned as a hand on the global oil valve, a framework that reopens it removes the single largest geopolitical premium from the energy price, even as three competing draft texts kept circulating, diverging most on the money.

“Declared complete in Washington, unsigned in Tehran, and set for Geneva on Friday.”

Framing noteThe right (National Review, and the Wall Street Journal board) casts the $25 billion asset release as an “economic bailout” that squanders wartime leverage; the center (CNBC) frames the same deal as stabilization, leading with world leaders welcoming a reopened Hormuz.

Why it mattersA reopened Hormuz pulls the largest risk premium out of the oil price, and $25 billion in unfrozen assets plus a sixty-day nuclear clock are the terms that will move energy, sanctions desks, and rates. The gap between “declared over” and “signed Friday,” with an Israeli strike already testing it, is exactly where a framework becomes a treaty or unravels.

The first prizefight in White House history fills the South Lawn, and Paramount+ the screen

UFC staged a numbered card on the South Lawn of the White House on Sunday, June 14, believed to be the first professional sporting event ever held there, timed to Flag Day, President Trump’s 80th birthday, and the unofficial kickoff of the country’s 250th-anniversary year. The legal machinery underneath the spectacle is the EASL story. The event proceeded after a federal judge denied a bid to block it on Friday, and it lands as UFC posts $401 million in first-quarter revenue, up 12% year over year, the new economics that made a South Lawn octagon worth building.

Why it mattersThe card is a live exam in entertainment-and-sports law: who sanctions a bout on federal ground, who carries the cost and liability of a temporary arena at the White House, and how a streamer monetizes a one-off spectacle inside an exclusive-rights deal. It is the Paramount+ era’s clearest statement that combat sports now live behind a subscription.
