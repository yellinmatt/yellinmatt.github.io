A fourth night of strikes and a reimposed blockade push oil to a one-month high as Trump drops his Hormuz toll

American forces struck Iran for a fourth consecutive night on Tuesday, July 14, a seven-hour wave that CENTCOMInstitutionU.S. Central Command, which runs American military operations across the Middle East and is directing the current strike campaign against Iran. said hit dozens of missile and drone sites, naval assets, and coastal defenses near the Strait of HormuzPlaceThe roughly twenty-mile waterway between Iran and Oman through which about a fifth of the world’s seaborne oil passes. Iran’s army said at least seven of its personnel were killed at a base in Bampur, in the country’s southeast, and the Revolutionary GuardInstitutionThe IRGC, Iran’s elite force, which controls the country’s missile arsenal and its posture in the Strait of Hormuz. said it had disabled two tankers in Omani waters and fired missiles and drones at American assets in Kuwait, Bahrain, and, it claimed, Jordan.

The economics of the war shifted overnight. Trump quietly abandoned the 20 percent Hormuz toll he had announced only a day earlier, and instead let the naval blockadeConceptA naval cordon barring ships from a country’s ports. The retreat from the toll matters: it swaps a novel and widely rejected levy on the freedom of the seas for an older, blunter instrument that shuts Iran’s own exports rather than taxing everyone else’s.

The market read it as a chokepoint tightening. Brent crude settled at $86.47 a barrel on Tuesday, up 3.81 percent and its strongest close since June 12, with U.S. crude at $80.29; prices held near a one-month high in choppy Wednesday trading, Brent around $85 and West Texas Intermediate near $80. Ship-tracking showed 57 transits of Hormuz from Friday through Sunday, a drop of more than half from the prior week. The month-old ceasefire that was meant to hold open a nuclear negotiating window is dead, and the fight has narrowed to a single disputed fact: whether the strait is open at all, with Washington insisting traffic moves and Tehran declaring it shut.

“We’re going to keep the Strait, and we’ll probably run it. We’ll become the guardian of the Strait.”
— President Trump, July 14

Framing noteThe right (Fox) frames control and resolve, foregrounding Iranian attacks on commercial ships and Trump vowing to run the waterway; the center-left (NPR, CNN) frames a scrapped ceasefire sliding back toward open war, foregrounding Iranian casualties and the spillover strikes on Gulf neighbors.

Why it mattersEnergy is the transmission belt from the Gulf to the Fed: every barrel that cannot move reprices growth, inflation, and the room to cut rates, on the same week the Fed chair testifies and the June inflation data land. Trading a 20 percent toll for a blockade also swaps one legal theory of the seas for another, and shippers, insurers, and governments now have to price a cordon rather than a tax.
