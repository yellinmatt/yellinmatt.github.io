The Supreme Court frees the president to fire agency heads, ending a 90-year rule, then spares the Fed

In a pair of rulings handed down Monday, June 29, the Supreme Court remade the president's power to fire. In Trump v. Slaughter, a 6-3 majority overruled Humphrey's ExecutorConceptThe 1935 Supreme Court precedent that let Congress shield independent-agency commissioners from at-will presidential removal. Hours earlier, in Trump v. Cook, the same Court drew the line that matters most to markets. "Humphrey's has for decades been a result in search of a rationale."

Why it mattersThis rewires the governance of every independent agency a securities lawyer touches, the SEC and CFTC most of all, and it makes a president's election a live variable in who runs them, while the Fed carve-out keeps monetary policy, and the rates that price every deal, off the table.

Framing noteThe right (Reuters wire, conservative commentators) cast Slaughter as restoring accountable, constitutional control over an unelected bureaucracy; the left (NPR, the Sotomayor dissent) framed it as a historic seizure of power that politicizes regulators, with both sides reading the Fed carve-out as the tell that the majority knew how far it was reaching.
