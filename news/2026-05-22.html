Trump pulls his AI executive order days before signing, citing overregulation concerns

The White House postponed signing an executive order establishing a voluntary review process under which AI companies would share frontier models with federal agencies up to 90 days before public release. A signing ceremony had been scheduled Thursday afternoon and had already been postponed multiple times. Trump told reporters he "didn't like certain aspects" of the order, and Axios reporting confirmed that concerns about overregulation and the competitive race with China were driving the delay. The ceremony had been slated to include top executives from the major AI labs, who had been actively negotiating on terms.

"I didn't like certain aspects of it."
— Trump, May 21

The order carried two core components. The first was the 90-day voluntary pre-release review framework, which would have given federal agencies a structured window to assess frontier models for national security and cybersecurity risks before public launch. The second was a cybersecurity clearinghouse pairing Treasury, other agencies, and the AI companies to identify and patch vulnerabilities in unreleased models. Both OpenAI and Anthropic were involved in the negotiations. The postponement comes as all three largest AI labs are preparing public market listings and as the White House's own AI Action Plan remains the administration's stated framework for AI governance.

Why it mattersThe 90-day voluntary pre-release review would have been the first structural US government checkpoint on frontier AI models. Its repeated postponements reveal a genuine tension between the administration's stated AI leadership goal and the labs' resistance to any preview obligation, voluntary or not. For a JD/MBA heading into capital markets with clients who are AI companies, the regulatory environment just got one round of uncertainty more complex.

Framing noteCNN framed the delay as a win for deregulation advocates inside the White House. Breitbart reported it as pressure from tech executives attending the signing ceremony. The Washington Times ran a May 22 explainer on what the order would have done, treating the postponement as policy-in-flux rather than a final kill.

Anthropic tells investors it expects first-ever quarterly profit in Q2, two years ahead of its own schedule

Anthropic shared internal projections with investors showing Q2 2026 revenue of $10.9 billion, a 130% increase from $4.8 billion in Q1, and a first operating profit of $559 million, according to a Wall Street Journal report published May 20. The milestone arrives roughly two years ahead of the company's own prior guidance: Anthropic told investors as recently as last summer that full-year profitability was unlikely before 2028. Enterprise adoption is driving the acceleration, particularly in coding assistance and cybersecurity applications, with the KPMG alliance (276,000 employees, May 19) and Gates Foundation partnership ($200 million, May 14) both visible on the revenue line.

The numbers land as Anthropic prepares a potential October IPO at an estimated $900 billion valuation. The SpaceX prospectus, filed publicly May 20, revealed that Anthropic is paying SpaceX $1.25 billion per month through May 2029 for GPU compute. That compute obligation represents roughly $15 billion annually, the structural tension in the margin equation. Q2 profitability is real, but its durability depends on whether training spend scales faster than enterprise revenue. Anthropic revised its trajectory from "not profitable until 2028" to "first profit Q2 2026" in under a year.

Why it mattersThe first AI frontier lab to turn consistently profitable rewrites the investment thesis for the sector. Anthropic's shift confirms that enterprise demand, not consumer subscriptions, is where frontier AI monetization lands at scale. Every BigLaw AI licensing deal and every capital markets AI investment is now priced against a profitability floor that just moved dramatically earlier.

SpaceX's redesigned Starship V3 gets a second launch window tonight, its last before the IPO roadshow

SpaceX is targeting a second launch attempt for its Starship Version 3 rocket tonight at 6:15 PM ET, after a hydraulic pin failure on the launch tower arm scrubbed Thursday's attempt. Starship V3 stands 408 feet tall when fully stacked and produces 18 million pounds of thrust from its redesigned Raptor engines, making it the most capable launch vehicle ever attempted. The 12th flight test is the program's first with the V3 architecture, and CNBC described tonight as "the company's last chance to dazzle investors with a Starship launch before shares hit the public market."

SpaceX filed its public S-1 prospectus on May 20, disclosing among other details that Anthropic is paying the company $1.25 billion per month through May 2029 for GPU compute. The prospectus targets a June 11 IPO pricing and June 12 Nasdaq debut under the ticker SPCX at approximately a $1.75 trillion valuation. A scrub tonight would push the flight past the roadshow window. A success would validate the full-stack reusability case that underpins the valuation premium over any competing launch provider.

Why it mattersThe Starship V3 flight is not just a test; it is the public signal investors are reading to price the largest IPO in history. For capital markets, the question tonight is whether SpaceX can deliver the proof point that makes the $1.75 trillion number defensible before the roadshow begins.

Russia concludes three-day nuclear exercise in Belarus, moving warheads to forward field sites

Russia and Belarus concluded a three-day joint nuclear exercise Friday involving 64,000 military personnel, more than 200 missile launch platforms, 140 aircraft, 73 surface ships, and 13 submarines including 8 strategic submarines. Russian state footage showed troops delivering nuclear warheads to mobile Iskander-M systems, loading them, and dispersing to unprepared launch sites on Belarusian territory. The Iskander-M carries conventional or nuclear payloads to ranges of 500 kilometers; Russia has also stationed its Oreshnik intermediate-range ballistic missile in Belarus since 2025. The Defense Ministry reported all targets accurately struck across land, sea, and air platforms.

Putin has stated that Russia retains command and control of nuclear weapons deployed in Belarus, but would allow Minsk to select targets in a conflict. Ukraine's Foreign Ministry condemned the exercises as "an unprecedented challenge to the global security architecture." Al Jazeera, citing defense analysts, noted that public footage of actual warhead handling is rare in Russian nuclear doctrine and is explicitly calibrated as a message to NATO. The exercises ran May 19-21 and concluded on the same day the US and Iran were negotiating a potential ceasefire framework.

Why it mattersNATO's eastern flank now faces an adversary that has publicly rehearsed nuclear warhead delivery to forward positions in Belarus, 200 miles from Warsaw. Any Ukraine settlement framework and any Article 5 commitment must now account for a deployed, exercised tactical nuclear posture at Europe's border.

US and Iran drafting a 'letter of intent' to formally end the war; Trump says final stages, Tehran hedges

The United States and Iran are negotiating a "letter of intent" that would formally end the current conflict and launch a 30-day diplomatic period to address the harder issues: Iran's nuclear program, reopening the Strait of Hormuz, and the release of frozen Iranian funds, according to Axios reporting from May 20. Trump held a lengthy and described-as-difficult call with Netanyahu that week, informing the Israeli prime minister that mediators were working on the framework. Trump said Friday the war was in its "final stages." Pakistan is among the regional intermediaries involved.

Iran's response is measured. Foreign Minister Araghchi said he was unsure whether a deal was imminent. An advisor to Khamenei dismissed US demands for control over Iran's nuclear program as a "fantasy." Oil, which climbed above $111 earlier this month on Strait of Hormuz closure fears, moved on Friday's news. The negotiations follow a May 18 sequence: Israel killed Hamas military commander al-Haddad, reopening hostilities, and Trump announced a planned Iran strike was called off at Gulf states' request. The two events, and their reversal, have defined the rhythm of this negotiation.

Why it mattersA formal US-Iran letter of intent would be the most concrete diplomatic development since the current conflict began. Strait of Hormuz reopening alone would materially reverse oil's climb. The crux remains: Trump's "final stages" and Khamenei's "fantasy" on nuclear control can both be true simultaneously as a function of domestic political framing on each side, which is why the letter of intent structure matters. It commits to the format, not the substance.

Lukas Dhont's WWI queer romance 'Coward' gets a 13-minute Cannes ovation, emerges as early Palme frontrunner

"Coward," the competition entry from Belgian director Lukas Dhont, received a 13-minute standing ovation at its Cannes world premiere Thursday night, making it one of the most rapturously received films in this year's competition. Set on the Belgian front during World War I, the film follows two young soldiers, played by Emmanuel Macchia and Valentin Campagne, in a queer romance conducted under fire. Dhont, who won the Jury Prize at Cannes 2022 for "Close," delivered an emotional post-screening speech and dedicated the film to actress Emilie Dequenne, his collaborator on earlier work.

The ovation places "Coward" alongside "La Bola Negra," which received a 22-minute ovation for Spanish directors Javier Calvo and Javier Ambrossi earlier this week, as the competition's emotional centerpieces. This year's lineup includes Asghar Farhadi, Cristian Mungiu, Hirokazu Kore-eda, and Pawel Pawlikowski, making the Palme race unusually deep. The market is thin by recent standards, with fewer $50 million-plus action packages and a leaner emphasis on prestige theatrical titles, but deal chatter for "Coward" started quickly after Thursday night's reception.

Why it mattersA queer WWI film winning the Palme would be a landmark: no LGBTQ-centered film has taken the top prize. The combination of Dhont's pedigree, the subject matter, and a 13-minute ovation puts "Coward" at the front of the conversation with still more competition screenings ahead. For anyone tracking EASL and the entertainment rights market, the deal momentum starting off a Cannes ovation is where the business begins.
