SpaceX starts trading this morning as the largest IPO in history

SpaceX begins trading on the Nasdaq today under the ticker SPCX, after pricing 555,555,555 shares at $135 each on Thursday night, a raise of $75 billion that values the company at roughly $1.77 trillion, larger than Tesla on day one. The listing blows past , whose 2019 listing netted $29.4 billion and stood as the record until last night. Demand ran close to four times the shares on offer, with roughly $250 billion in capital chasing the deal, and the company set a near 30 percent, far above the 5 to 10 percent typical of large offerings.

There is no set time for the first trade. Nasdaq's market makers will run the until buy and sell orders balance, a process that for listings this size can stretch well past the bell. The debut lands on a fragile tape: the Nasdaq clawed back ground Thursday after shedding more than 7 percent since its June 1 all-time high, with the AI trade wobbling on return-on-investment doubts and the index still digesting Friday's chip rout.

The deal is also the leading edge of a pipeline. Fortune reported Wall Street split between analysts calling the company a Musk "holy grail" and skeptics who see a valuation that asks investors to pay for decades of Mars optionality up front.

Trump cancels strikes and announces a 60-day Iran ceasefire, with a signing possible this weekend

President Trump said Thursday evening he had canceled planned strikes on Iran, hours after a second consecutive night of and fighter-jet attacks, saying negotiations had reached Iran's most senior leadership and that the parties had approved the final contours of an agreement. The framework would extend the ceasefire 60 days, including in Lebanon, with an immediate halt to hostilities, the reopening of the , and a 15 to 20 day negotiating window on the contentious issues, including Iran's enriched-uranium stockpile. Trump said the deal had been approved by the US, Israel, Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar and others, and that Vice President Vance could attend a signing in Europe as soon as this weekend.

Supreme Court rules 6-3 that investors cannot sue under the Investment Company Act

The Supreme Court held Thursday in FS Credit Opportunities Corp. v. Saba Capital that Section 47(b) of the Investment Company Act of 1940 gives private parties no to rescind contracts that allegedly violate the Act. Justice Barrett wrote for a 6-3 majority, reversing the Second Circuit: Congress made the SEC the Act's primary enforcer and expressly authorized private suits under only two provisions, a structure the Court read as excluding the rest. Justice Kagan dissented, as did Justice Jackson, joined in full by Sotomayor.

The loser is , which had sued to void bylaws restricting the voting power of activist shareholders in , the discounted vehicles whose arbitrage is Saba's signature trade. The welcomed the decision within hours; the activist bar lost its cleanest judicial lever against fund boards, leaving the SEC as the only meaningful enforcer of the 1940 Act.

Why it mattersThis is the funds-litigation decision of the term: rescission-by-lawsuit under the ICA is gone, every closed-end-fund defense memo gets rewritten this week, and the opinion extends the Court's project of closing implied rights of action across the securities laws.

Supreme Court refuses to let Alabama carry out a nitrogen execution

The Supreme Court on Thursday declined Alabama's to proceed with the execution of Jeffery Lee by , leaving in place the lower-court rulings that found the method unconstitutional. Justices Thomas, Alito and Gorsuch dissented. The denial follows a week of whiplash in the lower courts: an Eleventh Circuit panel found the roughly three minutes a prisoner may take to lose awareness an "intolerable" window of "severe pain over and above death itself," and Judge Emily Marks then permanently barred the state from using the method on Lee, a ruling Alabama raced to Washington to undo.

The Court has never held a state's execution method unconstitutional, and it did not do so Thursday; it simply refused to disturb the courts that did, under the . The order leaves Alabama free to seek execution by firing squad, a method Lee himself has requested, and it leaves the nitrogen protocol that several states copied from Alabama legally radioactive while the merits litigation runs.

Why it mattersA method-level brake from a Court historically hostile to method challenges is a genuine doctrinal event: the first nitrogen-hypoxia prohibition to survive the full appellate ladder, and a template every capital-defense office will now run.
