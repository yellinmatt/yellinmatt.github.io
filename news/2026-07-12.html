US strikes Iran a third time as Tehran declares Hormuz closed

United States forces struck about 140 Iranian military targets on Saturday, the third round of American strikes in a single week, after Iran's Islamic Revolutionary Guard CorpsInstitutionIran's elite parallel military, which controls its ballistic-missile force, navy in the Gulf, and proxy network. It has led the attacks on shipping in the strait. fired on the Cyprus-flagged container ship GFS Galaxy as it transited the Strait of HormuzPlaceThe 21-mile-wide channel between Iran and Oman through which roughly a fifth of the world's oil passes. Iran has periodically threatened to close it since February.. One crew member is missing, and an engine-room fire left the vessel unable to continue. US Central Command said the strikes hit air defenses, command nodes, and coastal anti-ship batteries; the IRGC said it fired only a warning shot at a ship using an unapproved route and then declared the strait closed to traffic.

The exchange marks the collapse of a month-old, Pakistan-brokered truce that had partially reopened the waterway. President Trump declared the ceasefire "effectively over," and Iran's new supreme leader, Mojtaba KhameneiPersonSon of the late Ayatollah Ali Khamenei and Iran's new supreme leader after the state funeral earlier this month. He has vowed "inevitable" retaliation., vowed "inevitable" revenge. Iran said it launched retaliatory strikes on the Prince Hassan Air Base in Jordan, and Gulf states reported intercepting incoming fire. Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi met his Omani counterpart in Muscat on Saturday to discuss freedom of navigation, and Turkey's foreign minister predicted "a solution this weekend," but Washington had set a Saturday deadline for Iran to publicly declare the strait open, warning of "unspecified consequences."

Markets are pricing the risk more calmly than the rhetoric suggests. Brent crudeConceptThe global benchmark oil price. It spiked near $120 a barrel when the strait first closed in March, roughly 64% above pre-crisis levels. slipped to about $75.50 on Friday but still logged a weekly gain near 4.7%, well below the March peak near $120 that followed the first closure. Shipping through the strait, which carries close to a fifth of the world's seaborne oil, has been largely throttled since late February; each rupture of the truce reprices the war premium without yet triggering a full supply shock. The question the week leaves open is whether a declared closure, backed by force, finally does.

"The authority over the Strait of Hormuz rests exclusively with Iran."
— Iran's UN mission, July 11

Why it mattersA shooting war over the Gulf's chokepoint is the single largest tail risk to energy, inflation, and the rate path a capital-markets desk prices all year; a genuine closure would move oil, credit spreads, and the Fed at once.

Framing noteThe Right (Fox News) foregrounds Iran's breach of the Islamabad memorandum and its aggression against civilian shipping; the Left and center (CNN, Washington Post) lead with the civilian toll and the diplomatic push to de-escalate.

Apple sues OpenAI, alleging a trade-secret theft "at every level"

Apple sued OpenAI and its hardware unit, io Products, in the Northern District of CaliforniaInstitutionThe federal trial court covering Silicon Valley. Its docket handles most of the technology industry's trade-secret and patent litigation. on Friday, accusing the AI lab of systematically stealing confidential hardware designs to build its own consumer devices. The complaint names two former Apple engineers now at OpenAI: chief hardware officer Tang TanPersonA former Apple vice president who led iPhone and Apple Watch product design, now OpenAI's chief hardware officer. Apple accuses him of directing the recruitment scheme. and technical-staff member Chang Liu. Apple alleges Liu downloaded dozens of files on unreleased products and kept a company laptop, and that Tan told job candidates to bring "actual parts" to interviews and to share Apple secrets. "At every level, OpenAI has been stealing Apple's trade secrets," the filing reads.

The suit is a startling reversal for two firms that partnered in 2024 to fold ChatGPT into the iPhone. Apple's revamped Siri, due this fall, now runs on Google's Gemini rather than OpenAI, and io is the design shop OpenAI acquired from former Apple designer Jony Ive. OpenAI said it has "no interest in other companies' trade secrets" and called the claims meritless. Trade-secret cases turn on whether Apple can show the information was genuinely secret, reasonably guarded, and actually used, a fact-heavy bar that discovery will test.

Why it mattersThis is the marquee IP fight of the AI hardware race, and it puts trade-secret doctrine, employee mobility, and the Ive-designed OpenAI device on a collision course that any EASL or capital-markets lawyer near the sector will have to track.

The Front · Markets · Washington

Warsh's first Fed report leads with inflation, not the labor scare

The Federal Reserve released its semiannual Monetary Policy ReportInstitutionThe Fed's twice-yearly report to Congress on the economy and policy, delivered alongside the chair's testimony to the House and Senate banking committees. on Thursday, the first under new chair Kevin WarshPersonThe Federal Reserve chair sworn in this spring, a former Fed governor known for a hawkish, markets-focused, independence-stressing posture. He is holding the policy rate at 3.50 to 3.75%.. It called growth "solid" and productivity "strong" and saw "little sign of risk in the banking system," but warned that inflation "stepped up further this spring" on tariffs, Middle East energy, and the AI buildout, with core PCE running near double the 2% target as of May.

The report's inflation-first framing sharpens the bind exposed a week earlier, when June payrolls came in at just 57,000 against expectations near 115,000. A cooling labor market that still will not force a cut is the stagflation-lite setup markets have been dreading. Warsh, who "vowed to deliver price stability," faces House and Senate testimony next, where his independence-stressing tone will be tested against political pressure to ease.

Why it mattersThe rate path is the single variable that governs every issuance window a capital-markets desk prices, and a hawkish new chair anchoring on inflation over a weak jobs print signals cuts stay on hold longer than the market wants.

At Sun Valley, the tech titans finish taking over the moguls' summer camp

Allen & Company's annual Sun Valley conferenceInstitutionThe invitation-only Idaho gathering hosted by boutique bank Allen & Co. since 1983, historically the birthplace of media megamergers. No public sessions, no livestreams. ran through the week in Idaho, the invitation-only "billionaires' summer camp" that once birthed the Disney-ABC and Comcast-NBCUniversal deals. This year the guest list tilted decisively to AI and platform power: Sam Altman, Tim Cook, Alex Karp, Jeff Bezos, and Mark Zuckerberg drew more attention than the media chiefs who built the event, a shift Variety framed as the tech industry inheriting a franchise it helped hollow out.

The dealmaking backdrop was unmistakable. MLB's Rob Manfred and the NBA's Adam Silver arrived fresh off multibillion-dollar media-rights packages with Disney, NBCUniversal, Amazon, and Netflix; Sony's Ravi Ahuja came off a $100 million stake in Cosm; and the recently unwound $1 billion OpenAI-Disney licensing tie-up, scrapped after OpenAI shut its Sora app, hung over the AI-and-Hollywood conversation.

Why it mattersSun Valley is where the next round of media M&A and sports-rights deals gets seeded, and the tech-over-media power shift is the structural story for anyone building an entertainment-law or capital-markets practice adjacent to content.

America runs on Chinese models. US developers' share of tokens processed on Chinese open-weight models has topped 30% every week since February and spiked to 46%, per OpenRouter data, as models 60-90% cheaper than closed frontier ones (roughly $0.18 versus $4 per million tokens) undercut the labs; Coinbase halved its AI bill by defaulting to GLM-5.2 and Kimi K2.7, and on July 8 two House committees opened a joint probe into the security risk. · CNBC

Bernanke joins Anthropic's trust. Former Fed chair and 2022 economics Nobel laureate Ben Bernanke joined Anthropic's Long-Term Benefit Trust on July 9, an IPO-adjacent governance-credibility signal for a lab valued near $1.09 trillion, alongside the trust's existing members. · Anthropic

The Times says OpenAI hid the evidence. The New York Times and New York Daily News moved on July 9 to sanction OpenAI in the SDNY copyright case, alleging it deleted billions of ChatGPT logs after the preservation order and internally amassed a 78-million-conversation database gauging its own infringement, a discovery fight that could reshape the AI-copyright docket. · TechCrunch

Markets & Deals

Sky carves out ITV's media business. Comcast's Sky agreed on July 6 to buy ITV's media and entertainment arm (ITVX plus the free-to-air channels) for up to £1.6 billion, £1.2 billion cash plus a Love Productions swap and a 2028 ad-revenue earnout, leaving ITV Studios independent; the deal cuts against Comcast's own plan to spin off NBCUniversal's cable networks. · ITV

Private credit buys a whole airline. EasyJet's board backed a £5.5 billion ($7.3 billion) take-private by Minneapolis private-credit firm Castlelake at £6.90 a share, a 73% premium, an unusual case of a private-credit house acquiring an entire low-cost carrier after five rounds of bidding. · CNBC

Cohen wants to run eBay. GameStop's July 7 annual meeting authorized 2.5 billion new shares for "strategic transactions, including the proposed eBay acquisition," where it holds a roughly 9.8% toehold and a standing $125-a-share bid eBay called "neither credible nor attractive"; Matt Levine reads it as Ryan Cohen angling for a stock-heavy reverse merger. · Money Stuff

Law & the Courts

The salary war grinds on. Norton Rose Fulbright matched Milbank's $235,000 first-year, $455,000 eighth-year scale on July 3, following Sullivan & Cromwell, the sixth straight raise Milbank has led; the historic pace-setter Cravath had still not moved, unusual against a backdrop of selective associate layoffs and the AI hire-fewer debate. · Above the Law

The Fifth Circuit lets the Texas Dream Act die. A divided Fifth Circuit panel on July 10 refused to let colleges and students intervene to defend the 2001 Texas Dream Act (in-state tuition for undocumented students) after Texas and the DOJ collusively settled to kill it, with Judge Jerry Smith calling the bid "futile"; a rare test of who can defend a law the state won't. · KXAN

The prediction-market fight reaches the Court. Justice Alito gave New Jersey until August 4 to file a cert petition in Flaherty v. KalshiEX, after the Third Circuit shielded event contracts from state gaming regulation; the CFTC-versus-state clash is shaping up as a Murphy v. NCAA for the derivatives era, with real stakes for how sports-betting-adjacent products are regulated. · Bloomberg Law

The World

NATO's 5% summit. The 36th NATO summit in Ankara (July 7-8) saw Trump press allies for 5% of GDP now against the 2035 Hague target, with roughly $80 billion in 2026 Ukraine aid confirmed and a Trump-Zelensky bilateral on Patriot interceptors after the July 6 Kyiv barrage; Europe and Canada are nearing 4% of GDP. · Al Jazeera

Hamas dissolves its Gaza government. Hamas dissolved the government it had run for nearly 20 years on July 6, with civilian rule set to pass to a Cairo-backed technocratic committee under UN and US oversight per the October 2025 peace plan; Israel dismissed it as "spin with no significance." · Al Jazeera

Ukraine strikes deep into Russian oil. Ukrainian drones hit Russian oil depots more than 500 km deep (Tver, Bashkortostan, Rostov) and set a dozen Azov tankers ablaze overnight July 9, a day after Trump cleared a Patriot license; Zelensky called it "long-range sanctions" as Russia downed 73 drones. · NBC News
