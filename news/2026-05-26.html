AI · Vatican City

Pope Leo XIV releases first encyclical on AI, calls autonomous weapons impermissible and just-war theory outdated

Pope Leo XIV released his first encyclical Monday, a 42,300-word document titled "Magnifica humanitas: On safeguarding the human person in the time of artificial intelligence," declaring lethal autonomous weapons systems morally impermissible, demanding democratic oversight of AI, and calling for international coordination on AI governance comparable to nuclear arms control. The pope, an American who took the throne in May, presented the text himself at the Vatican's Synod Hall, an unusual personal launch that the National Catholic Reporter read as a deliberate move to position the Holy See as a leading moral authority on AI's development.

The doctrinal break sits in two lines. "It is important to reaffirm that the 'just war' theory, which has all too often been used to justify any kind of war, is now outdated," Leo writes; "no algorithm can make war morally acceptable." He couples that with a call to "disarm" AI, which he defines as freeing the technology from "the mentality of 'armed' competition" rather than rejecting it. At his side on the dais was Christopher Olah, a 33-year-old self-identified atheist and co-founder of Anthropic, who told the room that every frontier lab "operates inside a set of incentives and constraints that can sometimes conflict with doing the right thing," and that outside voices insisting on safety matter. The pairing of a Catholic encyclical with a working frontier-AI researcher is the structural news: the Vatican is now treating a sitting AI lab as a counterparty in the moral architecture of the technology.

"No algorithm can make war morally acceptable."
— Magnifica humanitas, May 25

Why it mattersThe encyclical is the most weighty non-state intervention to date on AI in armed conflict, and lands while the Pentagon, the EU, and major labs are still negotiating where the autonomy line sits. A papal text moves a fight that has been engineering and procurement onto explicitly moral ground that capital-markets and defense counsel will now have to address.

Corporate · Tokyo

Nikkei breaks 65,000 for the first time as SoftBank books $60B+ run on OpenAI IPO conviction

The Nikkei 225 closed above 65,000 on Monday for the first time in its history, ending at 65,158 after a 1,819-point gain, with SoftBank Group doing most of the lifting. Shares of the Masayoshi Son–led investment company rose 4.6% in Tokyo, taking the gains from May 20 to roughly 40% and pushing the company's market value above ¥40 trillion, or about $252 billion. SoftBank has added more than $60 billion of market capitalization in the past fortnight, almost all of it tracking a single thesis.

That thesis is OpenAI. SoftBank, already the largest single backer in OpenAI's $122 billion March round, committed an additional $20 billion to take its total exposure close to $65 billion; Tokyo is treating its shares as the most liquid public proxy for the coming float. OpenAI is targeting a confidential S-1 filing within days, Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley as joint global coordinators, and a fall debut at a valuation north of $1 trillion, which would make it the largest IPO ever priced. Investors also pulled forward AI and semiconductor names after weekend signaling that an Iran deal could clear the Strait of Hormuz.

Why it mattersThe capital-markets calendar Matthew is preparing to underwrite in 2027 starts forming now. A trillion-dollar listing on a confidential filing path remakes both the league table and the institutional plumbing for the next eighteen months of AI follow-ons.

Corporate · San Francisco

Quinn Emanuel hit with $3M sanction; Judge Chen finds “deeply disturbing” culture of lawyering

U.S. District Judge Edward Chen of the Northern District of California ordered Quinn Emanuel Urquhart & Sullivan to pay nearly $3 million in compensatory sanctions plus a $100,000 punitive fine for the firm's conduct defending Natera against Guardant Health's false-advertising claims, and wrote that the firm's behavior "implicates a culture of lawyering that is deeply disturbing." Two Silicon Valley partners face up to $58,000 in personal sanctions each, an associate faces $28,000, and a special master will decide whether the three must contribute personally to the firm-level sanction. Chen ordered the firm to design an eight-hour legal ethics course for the same three lawyers to complete.

The mechanics matter. Chen found that the team relied on expert testimony it had reason to know was misleading, that lawyers "deliberately failed to exercise diligence" and "violated their duties of candor," and that the misconduct ran through the entire litigation team rather than tracing to one bad actor. Above the Law noted that asking the firm to write its own ethics curriculum is itself the rare sanction: it forces a public artifact of the misconduct that will sit in the firm's training file. Quinn Emanuel has indicated it will challenge the sanctions structure; the order, in its plain text, will travel widely through BigLaw ethics committees this week.

Why it mattersThe "deeply disturbing" sentence becomes the most quoted judicial line in BigLaw for the coming month, and the sanction's design (firm money plus personal exposure plus an internally authored ethics course) is a template courts can reuse. For capital-markets practitioners, the deterrent on expert-witness handling tightens audibly.

U.S. strikes Iranian missile launch sites and mine-laying boats; IRGC says ceasefire violated

U.S. Central Command said its forces conducted "self-defense" strikes Monday near the Iranian port city of Bandar Abbas, hitting Iranian missile launch sites and small boats that, the command said, were attempting to lay mines in shipping lanes near the Strait of Hormuz. CENTCOM spokesman Navy Captain Tim Hawkins said the action was "designed to protect our troops from threats posed by Iranian forces" and that the command "continues to defend our forces while using restraint during the ongoing ceasefire." Several Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps personnel were killed, according to Iranian state media.

Iran called the strikes "a clear violation of the ceasefire" and the IRGC vowed to "respond decisively." Tehran also claimed to have downed an MQ-9 drone and fired on a second U.S. drone and an F-35; CENTCOM did not confirm. Secretary of State Marco Rubio told reporters Monday that negotiators were "down to a word, a sentence" on a deal text and that an agreement could be "a couple of days" away. Brent crude briefly spiked then reversed as traders priced the Strait of Hormuz reopening into the same session in which they priced fresh kinetic action against it.

Why it mattersThe strike puts the ceasefire's actual contours back on the table just as the parties claim they are paragraphs from a deal. Capital-markets desks watching Hormuz now have a clean two-way risk on a single news cycle.

Framing noteThe CNN live thread led on "IRGC threatens to retaliate" and the U.S. legal framing of "self-defense"; Fox News led with Trump's line that talks are "proceeding nicely" and treated the strikes as routine defensive posture rather than an escalation.

News · Washington · cross-spectrum LLCR

Trump conditions Iran deal on broader Abraham Accords; Pakistan rejects, Saudi and Qatari leaders go silent on call

President Trump used a Sunday call with the leaders of Saudi Arabia, the United Arab Emirates, Qatar, Pakistan, Turkey, Egypt, Jordan, and Bahrain to argue that any agreement closing the Iran war should "mandatorily" require those countries to sign onto the Abraham Accords, the 2020 normalization framework between Israel and several Arab states. According to a U.S. official briefed on the call and cited by Axios, the leaders of Saudi Arabia, Qatar, and Pakistan "appeared stunned" by the demand; Trump joked into the silence and asked whether anyone was still on the line. Pakistan rejected the proposal explicitly.

Trump confirmed the position publicly Monday on his social platform, writing that "After all the work done by the United States to try and pull this very complex puzzle together, it should be mandatory that all of these Countries, at a minimum, simultaneously, sign onto the Abraham Accords." Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu was not on the call. The linkage moves the Iran negotiation from a bilateral ceasefire architecture to a region-wide normalization architecture in the closing hours of drafting and gives several governments a hard veto on text they thought was nearly final.

Why it mattersA bilateral war-ending deal becomes a regional realignment in one phone call. The structure of the deal, and who gets to sign it, now resets at the same moment the parties claim they are days from a signature.

Disney clears $2 billion at the 2026 global box office, first studio of the year

Disney became the first studio to cross $2 billion in global box office for 2026 after "The Devil Wears Prada 2" added $118.8 million in its second weekend to reach $433.2 million worldwide, with the milestone landing on Memorial Day weekend. The slate that got the studio there is unfashionably broad: the original animation "Hoppers" at $371.6 million, the horror release "Send Help" at $94 million, and meaningful 2025 carryover from "Zootopia 2" and "Avatar: Fire and Ash," which is still drafting into wide release in legacy territories.

The milestone matters as a calibration. Disney's fiscal Q2 earnings, reported earlier in May, showed studio revenue up 7% on a 30%-down EPS quarter, which Wall Street read as a content-bet problem rather than a streaming problem. A $2 billion box-office year by late May validates the slate logic at the moment Netflix is publishing $45.18 billion in 2025 revenue and Universal is positioning its summer tentpole calendar. The first studio across the $2 billion line in 2026 was not pre-ordained, and the order of finish reshapes the negotiating posture for talent and IP deals through fall.

Why it mattersFor an EASL-track student, the studio that hits $2 billion first sets the comparable for talent-side packaging, profit-participation math, and the next round of streaming-output deal sheets.
