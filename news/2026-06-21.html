Iran's peace deal nearly broke over the weekend, then both sides sat back down

The interim deal that ended the Iran war on paper this week was nearly undone by the weekend. On Saturday, June 20, Iran's top joint military command declared the Strait of HormuzPlaceThe narrow chokepoint between Iran and Oman through which roughly a fifth of the world's seaborne oil passes. Reopening it toll-free was a core term of the interim deal. closed, calling it the "first step" in response to what it called continued Israeli ceasefire violations in southern Lebanon. U.S. Central CommandInstitutionCENTCOM, the U.S. military command responsible for the Middle East. It disputed Iran's closure claim with shipping data. said the waterway stayed open, reporting 55 merchant ships moved more than 17 million barrels of oil through it that day, even as the Revolutionary Guard navy warned vessels away.

On Sunday, June 21, American and Iranian delegations resumed talks at the Burgenstock resort above Lake Lucerne to finalize the memorandum of understandingConceptThe framework signed electronically on June 17 that halts the war, lifts the naval blockade within 30 days, reopens Hormuz, and opens a 60-day window to a final agreement. signed electronically on June 17. Vice President JD Vance led the U.S. side alongside Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner; Iran's parliament speaker Mohammad Bagher Ghalibaf led Tehran's, with Pakistani and Qatari mediators present at BurgenstockPlaceA mountainside resort overlooking Lake Lucerne in Switzerland, a recurring venue for high-stakes diplomacy, chosen after an in-person Geneva ceremony was scrapped.. Vance said the opening of Hormuz and the end of Iran's nuclear program "have already been accomplished," and claimed "great progress" within hours.

The talks had been postponed on Friday after Israel kept striking Lebanon hours into a separate Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire, and Iran tied any final deal to a Lebanon halt. Even as negotiators met, President Trump threatened to "hit Iran very hard again" over Hezbollah, while Israel's defense minister signaled a withdrawal from Lebanon. Analysts read the framework as a temporary stabilization whose durability rides on verification mechanisms and the next 60 days, not a settled peace.

“We've already made great progress over just the last few hours.”
— JD Vance, at Burgenstock, June 21

Why it mattersA war's end is being priced in real time across oil, defense, and shipping, and the question a capital-markets reader is weighing is whether a 60-day framework with a contested chokepoint is a floor under risk or a feint. The gap between Iran's closure declaration and CENTCOM's traffic count is exactly the kind of ambiguity that moves Brent crude before it resolves.

Framing noteAl Jazeera centers Israel's continued Lebanon strikes as the violations that triggered Iran's move; Fox News and The Washington Times lead on Iran's closure declaration as the aggression and note the U.S. denial that any ships were turned back.

Demis Hassabis thanked him publicly and framed the departure as natural career movement.

The Front · Markets & deals · Washington

On his first meeting as chair, Warsh turned the Fed hawkish and ended the cut era

The Federal Open Market CommitteeInstitutionThe FOMC, the Federal Reserve panel that sets U.S. interest rates. June 17 to 18 was Kevin Warsh's first meeting chairing it. held its benchmark rate at 3.50 to 3.75 percent on June 18, but the projections did the talking: the median 2026 dotConceptA point on the Fed's "dot plot," each official's anonymous forecast for where rates should sit. The median dot is the market's read on the committee's center of gravity. rose to 3.8 percent from 3.4, implying at least one hike, with 9 of 18 officials now penciling one in. Kevin WarshPersonFormer Fed governor and Trump's new Fed chair, long a hawk. He declined to submit his own dot and signaled he may scrap the dot-plot framework. declined to submit his own dot and signaled he may scrap the framework entirely.

Markets read the message and sold. The Dow fell 507 points to surrender its record, the S&P 500 dropped 1.21 percent and the Nasdaq 1.34 percent, while the two-year Treasury yield jumped 16 basis points. Projected inflation was marked up to 3.6 percent. The pivot reverses an April baseline that had priced essentially no hikes for the year.

Why it mattersA Fed chair who erases the last cut on his first day resets the discount rate against which every equity and every pending IPO is valued, and a hawkish turn lands directly on the OpenAI, Anthropic, and SpaceX-adjacent listings the market has been pricing for a friendly tape.

Fox is buying Roku for $22 billion, six years after selling its stake at a third the price

Fox Corporation agreed to acquire RokuInstitutionThe streaming-platform and connected-TV operating system. Its ad-supported home screen reaches tens of millions of U.S. households, the asset Fox is paying for. for about $22 billion on June 16, at $160 a share, $96 in cash plus 0.9693 of a Fox Class A share. The deal, the third-largest in U.S. television, gives Fox a connected-TVConceptThe fastest-growing slice of the ad market: streaming delivered through smart-TV operating systems like Roku's, where the home screen itself is inventory advertisers pay to reach. distribution spine; the company will hold roughly 73 percent, Roku holders 27, with a close targeted for the first half of 2027.

The price is the punchline. Fox sold its earlier Roku stake at about $58 a share in 2020 to fund TubiConceptFox's free, ad-supported streaming service, acquired in 2020 and now a centerpiece of its streaming strategy. The Roku sale helped fund it., its free ad-supported service, and is now buying the whole company back at nearly triple that. It is the clearest sign yet that legacy broadcasters see their future in the ad-supported streaming layer they once rented.

Why it mattersThis is the EASL and capital-markets story in one: a marquee media M&A at the intersection of content, distribution, and a round-trip valuation, the kind of deal a future entertainment-law associate will be asked to diligence.

A 9-0 Court told the government it cannot automatically disarm marijuana users

A unanimous Supreme Court held in U.S. v. HemaniConceptThe June 18 ruling that the government cannot categorically disarm someone solely for marijuana use, finding the prosecution inconsistent with the Second Amendment. on June 18, in an opinion by Justice Gorsuch, that the government may not categorically strip gun rights from a person solely because they use marijuana. The decision is narrow: it leaves Section 922(g)(3)ConceptThe federal statute barring "unlawful users" of controlled substances from possessing firearms. The same provision was used to convict Hunter Biden in 2024. on the books and preserves bans on the intoxicated or dangerous, but it narrows the very statute used to convict Hunter Biden in 2024.

The same day, an 8-1 Court ruled in Hunter v. United States, per Justice Kagan, that plea-deal appeal waiversConceptClauses in plea agreements by which a defendant gives up the right to appeal. The Court held them generally enforceable, but not where enforcement works a "miscarriage of justice." are generally enforceable but cannot be enforced where doing so produces a "miscarriage of justice," vacating and remanding to the Fifth Circuit with Justice Thomas alone in dissent.

Why it mattersTwo end-of-term criminal-procedure rulings that any law student should be able to state cleanly, and a reminder that the Second Amendment line the Court drew in Bruen and Rahimi keeps forcing categorical federal gun bans back to first principles.

Toy Story 5 opened to $160 million, a franchise record and the year's biggest debut

Disney and PixarInstitutionDisney's animation studio behind the Toy Story franchise. Toy Story 5 is its biggest series opening and second-best domestic debut ever, behind Incredibles 2.'s Toy Story 5 opened to $160 million domestically from 4,425 theaters, the biggest debut of 2026 and the largest in the franchise's history, supplanting Toy Story 4's $120 million in 2019. It is the second-best opening ever for an animated film in the U.S., behind Pixar's own Incredibles 2 at $182.6 million, and added $152 million abroad for a $312 million global start, the second-best in Pixar history, with roughly half the domestic gross coming from premium large-formatConceptPLF: higher-priced IMAX and large-screen auditoriums that lift average ticket prices and now drive a disproportionate share of a blockbuster's opening gross. screens.

The picture rode a Juneteenth and Father's Day tailwind, $17.5 million in previews (the best of 2026), and an A CinemaScoreConceptAn opening-night audience grade from polled moviegoers, A to F. A straight "A" signals strong word of mouth and durable legs in the weeks ahead., the strongest audience signal a sequel can get, pointing to durable legs into July.

Why it mattersA franchise-record opening is the clearest read on a theatrical business still betting that established IP can fill a summer, and the box-office number is the metric every studio deal, talent package, and EASL negotiation downstream is keyed to.

Its Deployment Simulation (June 16) replays roughly 1.3 million past conversations through a candidate model to predict misbehavior before launch; LifeSciBench (June 17) found the top model clears just 36 percent of expert life-science tasks. Markets & deals

A take-private wave crests with EQT-Intertek. Sweden's EQT made a recommended 9.5-billion-pound cash offer for testing group Intertek on June 18, Britain's third-largest take-private ever, capping a deal-dense week. Investegate

PE keeps eating consumer and payments. Yum Brands sold Pizza Hut for about $2.7 billion on June 16, and Nuvei agreed to buy Payoneer for roughly $2.75 billion, two more carve-outs into private hands. CNBC

Law & the courts

The 2026 salary war is on. Milbank reset the scale to $235,000 for first-years and $455,000 for eighth-years, and within two weeks McDermott, Quinn Emanuel and more than a dozen firms matched, led oddly by litigation boutiques. Above the Law

The world

Lebanon tests the truce. An Israel-Hezbollah ceasefire took effect June 19 after the deadliest day in months, but Israel kept striking, and Iran cited the violations to justify its Hormuz threat. NBC News

The G7 hardens on Ukraine. Leaders at Evian pledged on June 17 to surge air defenses and, for the first time, to extend production licenses for Ukraine's own weapons, including anti-ballistic interceptors. Elysee

Cuba breaks with its own model. The National Assembly unanimously passed 176 free-market reforms on June 18, scrapping the joint-venture-with-state rule and opening tourism, banking, and energy, the deepest break since 1959. PBS NewsHour

Culture & EASL

Penske absorbs the rest of Vox. Penske Media folded the remaining Vox brands, The Verge, Eater, and SB Nation among them, into a new PMX unit on June 18, pitched as the world's largest digital publisher. Billboard

Xbox's reset claims a studio. Microsoft closed Hellblade developer Ninja Theory on June 16, the first confirmed first-party casualty of the "Xbox Reset," ahead of larger gaming cuts after the fiscal year ends June 30. Bloomberg

College sports becomes an asset class. Utah became the first college athletic department to take direct private equity, at least $100 million from Otro Capital into a new for-profit commercial arm, a post-House-settlement turn. Sportico

AI music heads to court. An independent country artist filed a proposed class action against Udio on June 16, paralleling the Suno suit, alleging training on unlicensed sound recordings and arguing unsigned artists were left without a seat at the table. Billboard
