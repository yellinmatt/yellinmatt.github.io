America marks 250 years, and a president makes the party his own

The United States turned 250 on Saturday, the of the Declaration of Independence, and Washington staged the largest civic spectacle in a generation. President Trump opened the weekend Friday with a 28-minute address at , calling the country "the most exceptional nation ever to exist" and vowing it would "never be a Communist country," then turned to a National Mall celebration the White House said would draw more than a million people for "the largest pyrotechnics display in the history of the world," a 40-minute show of more than 850,000 effects pitched to set a world record.

The observance is national in scope: events anchored in Boston, Philadelphia, New York and Charleston, a Great American State Fair on the Mall, commemorative coinage, a Sail250 tall-ships gathering, and a first-ever INDYCAR street race routed around the monuments in August. But the day arrived fractured. A White House task force chaired by Trump since 2025 has steered federal money and agencies behind a celebration critics call more rally than remembrance, a reading sharpened when the administration moved a UFC card onto the South Lawn on Trump's own 80th birthday three weeks ago.

Presidential historian said Trump had "effectively hijacked" the anniversary by making it about himself; the has sued over the use of the White House grounds. The counter-case, made loudly on the right, is that a 250th birthday warrants exactly this scale and that the exceptionalism on display is the point. Security was the quiet subtext, with agencies boosting protection across the Mall for a crowd measured in seven figures.

"The most exceptional nation ever to exist."
— President Trump, at Mount Rushmore, July 3

Why it mattersThe semiquincentennial is the civic backdrop to an election-year argument over who owns American patriotism; for anyone reading the room this year, the fight over the party is the story.

Framing noteLeft outlets cast the day as a partisan takeover (CNN's "tricorn hats or Trump rally," MSNBC on the "hijacked" birthday); right coverage frames it as overdue national pride (Fox's "most exceptional nation").

A Chinese open-weight model is pricing out the American frontier

's GLM-5.2, an model released under an unrestricted MIT license, spent the week climbing developer leaderboards on the strength of coding and agent benchmarks that land within striking distance of the American frontier at a fraction of the price. Equipped with tools it scored 54.7 on Humanity's Last Exam, ahead of GPT-5.5 at 52.2 and just behind Claude Opus 4.8 at 57.9; on Pro it beat GPT-5.5 outright. Its API runs near $1.40 per million input tokens against roughly $5 for the leading closed models, and the weights are free to download and self-host.

The significance is less any single score than the shape of the market it implies. Enterprises that spent 2025 "" have pivoted to cost per unit of work, and a capable MIT-licensed model that can run on a customer's own hardware puts direct downward pressure on the pricing power OpenAI and Anthropic are carrying into their IPO filings. It also complicates the export-control logic in Washington: the frontier the government is trying to fence is being approximated by weights anyone can already pull off the internet.

Why it mattersFor labs about to ask public markets to underwrite trillion-dollar valuations, an open-weight Chinese model at one-sixth the cost is the margin question investors will press hardest.

BigLaw's summer of raises rolls on as the Milbank scale spreads

The associate pay ladder that had been frozen since 2023 broke open this summer, and the matches kept coming through the holiday. set the new scale on June 2, first-year salaries to $235,000 and eighth-years to $455,000, effective July 1; Sullivan & Cromwell and, on Thursday, Norton Rose Fulbright have since matched, joining a lengthening list. The pattern is by now familiar: one firm moves, and the rest fall in line rather than risk losing associates in a market where the top partners are clearing eight figures.

The tell this cycle is who is not on the board. , the firm whose name is synonymous with the it historically set, has not yet matched the Milbank numbers, an unusual position for the traditional pace-setter. The raises land against a backdrop of selective associate layoffs and a running argument, made by managing partners on both sides, over whether AI means hiring fewer junior lawyers or, as Baker Botts's chair put it, more.

Why it mattersFor a rising 2L headed into BigLaw, the going rate and who sets it are the clearest read on where associate leverage sits, and this summer says it is still high.

Iran opens a six-day funeral for the leader it lost to an airstrike

Iran began a state funeral on Saturday for Ayatollah , the of 37 years who was killed in the opening wave of US-Israeli airstrikes that started this year's war. Crowds packed Tehran's , chanting "revenge" and "Death to America." Officials said they expect 15 to 20 million mourners across six days of processions running to a July 9 burial in , which would make it the largest state funeral in the country's history.

The mourning doubles as a message. The United States agreed to pause its negotiations with Tehran for the week of the funeral, a rare concession that underscores how tentative the June remains, and Iran's parliament speaker has said IAEA inspectors will not be allowed back into the sites the US and Israel bombed. Whether the grief hardens into the "blood feud" mourners invoked or buys the talks time is the question hanging over the summer.

Why it mattersA slain supreme leader's funeral is where Iran's next posture gets set; the revenge chants and the paused talks are the two possible futures, side by side.

Framing noteRight coverage (Fox) leads on the "Death to America" chants and the security threat; left and center outlets (CNN, NPR) foreground the scale of the mourning and the war that killed him.

A holiday built for family films delivers a muted Fourth

's Minions & Monsters led the July Fourth weekend but did it quietly, posting about $63.5 million over five days and a $38.5 million three-day, the lowest opening in the Despicable Me franchise despite a production cost north of $170 million. It still ran first at more than 4,200 theaters; Pixar's Toy Story 5 held second in its third weekend, near $30 million for a $365-million domestic cume, and ' Young Washington opened third, counter-programmed straight at the 250th.

The soft number says less about the movie than the calendar. With July 4 falling on a Saturday, the single biggest moviegoing day of the week was spent on barbecues, fireworks and the country's 250th birthday, and the of the home-soil World Cup pulled the same audience with Argentina, France and Brazil in play. Exhibitors treated the weekend as a wash to be made up later in the month.

Why it mattersA franchise low on the year's most family-friendly weekend is the kind of soft print that feeds the running argument over whether theatrical demand is recovering or just front-loading its few big weekends.
