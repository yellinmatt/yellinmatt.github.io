SEC moves to rewrite the offerings rulebook, its biggest overhaul in two decades

The Securities and Exchange Commission voted Monday to propose what it calls the most significant modernization of the registered-offering framework in more than 20 years. The Registered Offering Reform would expand shelf registration by scrapping the seasoning and public-float eligibility tests, a change the agency estimates could increase by more than 60% the number of issuers eligible to sell an unlimited amount of securities on Form S-3.

A companion proposal raises the large-accelerated-filer threshold from $700 million to $2 billion and shields newly public companies from that status for 60 months after an IPO, extending scaled disclosure to roughly 81% of all public companies. Both proposals enter a formal notice-and-comment period before any adoption vote.

"The most significant modernization of the registered-offering framework in more than 20 years."
— Securities and Exchange Commission, May 19

Why it mattersCapital markets is the exact practice Matthew is heading into. If adopted, the rules shift structuring leverage toward issuers and reshape the diligence-versus-structuring mix that junior capital-markets associates bill against.

Standard Chartered to cut 7,000 roles, replacing "lower-value human capital" with AI

Standard Chartered said it will eliminate about 7,000 corporate-function roles by 2030, roughly 15% of that workforce, as it leans into automation. CEO Bill Winters told reporters the bank is "replacing, in some cases, lower-value human capital with the financial capital and the investment capital we are putting in." The cuts concentrate in human resources, corporate affairs, and supply-chain management across a total workforce of about 82,000, and the bank paired the news with a target to lift return on tangible equity to 18% by 2030.

Why it mattersAI displacing white-collar labor is the spine of Matthew's novel and a live capital-markets story. A bank naming a headcount number and a blunt rationale is the clearest signal yet of how the cost case gets argued in the C-suite.

Trump cancels a planned Iran strike, sets an "early next week" deadline

President Trump said he called off strikes on Iran that had been planned for Tuesday, telling reporters he was about an hour from giving final authorization before pausing for diplomacy. His administration spoke separately with the leaders of Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the United Arab Emirates, who pressed for more time after Iran routed a new peace proposal through Pakistan. From the White House South Lawn, Trump set a fresh timeline: "two or three days, maybe Friday, Saturday, Sunday, something, maybe early next week." Nuclear enrichment and Iran's near-weapons-grade stockpile remain the central impasse.

Why it mattersOil and the equity risk premium swing on the on-again, off-again posture. A deferred strike caps the tail risk that has been pricing into energy and broad markets.

McDermott Will & Schulte cuts associates a year after its mega-merger

McDermott Will & Schulte let go of more than a dozen associates across multiple practice areas, less than a year after McDermott Will & Emery combined with Schulte Roth & Zabel to form a top-20 firm. Legacy Schulte associates were hit hardest, per reports; affected lawyers received three months of severance, and the firm cited low hours and "shifting client needs." Recruiters told the trade press that another wave of BigLaw trimming is "teed up."

Why it mattersThis is the BigLaw economy Matthew enters in 2027. Post-merger associate trims show how integration risk lands on the junior ranks, the cohort he joins.

Cannes day six: Refn's lurid "Her Private Hell," Zvyagintsev's icy "Minotaur"

Nicolas Winding Refn premiered "Her Private Hell" out of competition, a grotesque fashion-slasher scored by Pino Donaggio that critics called pretentious "in the way that has been missing from cinema for far too long." In competition, Andrey Zvyagintsev returned with "Minotaur," a transposition of Claude Chabrol's 1969 thriller to present-day Russia that reviewers called a return to form, if short of the moral sweep of his 2014 "Leviathan."
