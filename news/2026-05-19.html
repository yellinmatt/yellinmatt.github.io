SEC moves to rewrite the offerings rulebook, its biggest overhaul in two decades

The Securities and Exchange Commission voted Tuesday to propose what Chairman Paul Atkins called the most significant modernization of the registered-offering framework in more than two decades. The centerpiece, the Registered Offering Reform, rewrites who may use the shelf-registration system that lets established public companies raise capital on short notice. It would scrap the 12-month seasoning requirement and the $75 million public-float test that currently gate unlimited primary offerings on Form S-3, opening rapid, on-demand market access to a far wider set of issuers.

A companion proposal redraws the line between large and smaller filers. It would lift the public-float threshold for large-accelerated-filer status from $700 million to $2 billion and exempt newly public companies from that status for 60 months after an IPO, an on-ramp that defers the heaviest disclosure and internal-control-audit costs. By the agency's own math, the higher line would leave only about 19% of reporting companies as large accelerated filers, though that group still accounts for roughly 93.5% of total U.S. market value, concentrating the strictest rules on the biggest names.

Both releases now enter a 60-day public comment period before any adoption vote. Commissioner Hester Peirce, in a statement she titled "Headache Medicine," backed the package as overdue relief, while Atkins and Commissioner Mark Uyeda framed it as capital-formation reform meant to reverse the long decline in the number of U.S. public companies.

"The most significant modernization of the registered-offering framework in more than 20 years."
— Securities and Exchange Commission, May 19

Why it mattersCapital markets is the exact practice Matthew is heading into. If adopted, the rules tilt structuring leverage toward issuers, widen the pool of companies that can run rapid shelf takedowns, and reshape the diligence-versus-structuring mix that junior capital-markets associates bill against.

Standard Chartered to cut 7,000 roles, replacing "lower-value human capital" with AI

Standard Chartered said it will eliminate more than 7,000 corporate-function roles by 2030, over 15% of that part of the bank, as it pushes automation and AI deeper into back-office work. CEO Bill Winters told reporters the bank is "replacing, in some cases, lower-value human capital" with the technology and investment it is putting in, while insisting the move "is not cost-cutting" and that affected staff would be offered reskilling. The reductions fall hardest on human resources, corporate affairs, and supply-chain management, concentrated in the bank's processing hubs in Chennai, Bengaluru, Kuala Lumpur, and Warsaw.

The cuts arrived bundled with sharply higher profitability targets: a return on tangible equity of 15% by 2028, up more than three points from 2025, rising to 18% by 2030. The framing is what makes the announcement notable. Rather than present the layoffs as retrenchment, Standard Chartered cast them as capital reallocation, swapping headcount for technology spend to lift returns. It is one of the first times a global bank has attached a specific number to the jobs it expects AI to absorb.

Why it mattersAI displacing white-collar labor is the spine of Matthew's novel and a live capital-markets story. A global bank naming a headcount, a rationale, and a return target is the clearest signal yet of how the AI cost case gets argued, and sold to investors, in the C-suite.

Trump cancels a planned Iran strike, sets an "early next week" deadline

President Trump said he called off strikes on Iran that had been set for Tuesday, telling reporters he was about an hour from final authorization before pausing for diplomacy. The reprieve followed direct appeals from three Gulf leaders, Qatari Emir Tamim bin Hamad Al Thani, Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman, and UAE President Mohammed bin Zayed, who asked for a short window after Iran routed a revised 14-point proposal through Pakistani mediators on Monday. Trump said he had put the strike off "hopefully, maybe forever, but possibly for a little while," and floated a loose new timeline of a few days.

The pause leaves the core dispute intact. Iran's uranium enrichment and its near-weapons-grade stockpile remain the central impasse, and at least one analyst warned the conflict could still run months, if not years. Trump also brushed aside questions about whether he needs congressional authorization to resume, claiming hostilities have effectively terminated. Markets read the delay as a lid on the energy-shock scenario that has been shadowing oil and equities.

Why it mattersOil and the equity risk premium swing on the on-again, off-again posture. A deferred strike caps the tail risk pricing into energy and broad markets, but an unresolved enrichment standoff means that risk is postponed, not priced out.

McDermott Will & Schulte cuts associates a year after its mega-merger

McDermott Will & Schulte cut more than a dozen associates across multiple practice areas, less than a year after McDermott Will & Emery combined with Schulte Roth & Zabel in July 2025 to form a top-20 Am Law firm. The cuts ran out of New York but reached well beyond it, and by multiple accounts legacy Schulte associates were hit hardest. Affected lawyers were told to leave effectively immediately, with three months of severance, and were given low hours and thin work in their practice groups as the reason. The firm said publicly it was separating from a small number of associates to "align with shifting client needs."

The sharper detail: during merger talks, Schulte management had repeatedly assured associates that the combination agreement carried layoff protections, and that no cuts would come before 2027 without sign-off from a panel of legacy Schulte partners. The firm spent its first year projecting growth, a large new partner class last fall and a debut in the Am Law top 20, which makes a quiet associate culling ten months in a louder signal. Recruiters told the trade press another round of BigLaw trimming is already lined up.

Why it mattersThis is the BigLaw economy Matthew enters in 2027. Post-merger integration risk lands on the junior ranks first, the cohort he joins, and assurances made during a deal do not always survive the next slow quarter.

Cannes day six: Refn's lurid "Her Private Hell," Zvyagintsev's icy "Minotaur"

Cannes spent its sixth day split between provocation and control. Out of competition, Nicolas Winding Refn premiered "Her Private Hell," his first feature in roughly a decade, a fashion-horror fever dream in which a soldier hunts for his daughter through a neon, Barbarella-tinged underworld stalked by a killer called the Leatherman. Sophie Thatcher leads. Critics split down the middle between style-drunk reverie and empty pastiche, but even skeptics singled out Pino Donaggio's score, a deliberate echo of 1960s thrillers, as the strongest thing in it. The screening drew a 12-minute standing ovation.

In competition, Andrey Zvyagintsev returned with "Minotaur," a remake of Claude Chabrol's 1969 "The Unfaithful Wife" that follows a complacent husband down a spiral after he learns of his wife's affair. Reviewers called it the Russian director's strongest work since "Leviathan" and "Loveless," a cold, exacting domestic thriller, even if it trades his usual indictment of the state for something more intimate. It lands squarely in the Palme d'Or conversation.

Why it mattersEASL is Matthew's concentration. Cannes is where specialty-film financing, distribution, and talent deals set the tone for the year's market, and where the festival titles that drive arthouse acquisitions first get priced.
