OpenAI lines up the largest tech IPO ever, with a confidential filing due as soon as Friday

OpenAI is preparing to file a confidential draft IPO prospectus with the SEC as soon as Friday, May 22, working with Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley as lead underwriters and Cooley as legal counsel, per reporting from Bloomberg, CNBC, and Axios on May 20. The ChatGPT maker was valued at $730 billion in its most recent private round, with secondary trades implying closer to $850 billion, and a public debut is targeted for the fall. An OpenAI spokesperson said only that the company "regularly evaluates a range of strategic options," declining to confirm any timeline.

A confidential filing lets OpenAI advance the offering privately before disclosing financials to the public, buying time to set terms while the company keeps burning cash at a historic clip to fund chips, data centers, and talent. It caps an extraordinary stretch for the new-issue market: SpaceX filed last week toward a roughly $1.75 trillion listing, putting the two most closely watched private companies in the world on a collision course for the same fall IPO window.

The offering will also test how public markets price a company that is at once the defining AI platform and one of the most cash-hungry enterprises ever to approach an exchange, with no clean comparable on the tape.

"We regularly evaluate a range of strategic options."
— OpenAI spokesperson, May 20

Why it mattersThis is the capital-markets event of the decade for anyone heading into the field, the AI platform at the center of the AI-and-law debate walking straight onto an exchange, with Cooley rather than a Wall Street firm running the legal side.

Google bets on agents at I/O, making Gemini 3.5 Flash its default model

At Google I/O 2026, Sundar Pichai unveiled the Gemini 3.5 series and made Gemini 3.5 Flash generally available the same day across the Gemini API, AI Studio, Vertex AI, the Gemini app, and Search's AI Mode. Google pitched it as the first model "combining frontier intelligence with action," priced at $1.50 and $9 per million input and output tokens, with a one-million-token context window and 76.2% on Terminal-Bench 2.1, beating the larger Gemini 3.1 Pro on coding and agent tasks.

The framing was the story. Google explicitly shifted from AI that assists to agents that "independently navigate complex tasks across your entire workflow," backed by an upgraded Antigravity development platform, managed agents callable in a single API line, and WebMCP, a proposed open web standard for exposing structured tools to agents. Gemini 3.5 Flash went generally available inside GitHub Copilot on day one, a sign Google wants the model embedded in developer pipelines before rivals respond.

Why it mattersAgentic models that "do" rather than "answer" are the exact mechanism behind fears of hollowed-out junior legal work; a frontier model this cheap and this fast pulls that timeline forward.

The 30-year Treasury yield touches its highest since 2007, then reverses as oil slides

The 30-year Treasury yield briefly hit 5.197% on May 19, its highest level since July 2007, before retreating more than six basis points to 5.116% on May 20; the 10-, 20-, and 30-year yields all set 52-week highs, with the long end at levels unseen in nearly two decades. The S&P 500 closed down 0.67% at 7,353.61, its third straight losing session, while the Nasdaq fell 0.84% and the Dow shed 322 points.

The reversal tracked oil. Brent crude dropped 5.63% to $105.02 after President Trump told reporters the administration was in the "final stages" of negotiations with Iran, easing the geopolitical risk premium that had been pushing inflation expectations and yields higher. The backdrop is a fractured Fed, which held the funds rate at 3.5% to 3.75% in a decision that drew an 8-4 split, the sharpest FOMC dissent in more than 30 years.

Why it mattersLong-end yields at 2007 levels reprice every leveraged buyout, debt issuance, and M&A model on the Street; the IPO surge is arriving into a punishing rate backdrop.

A24 wins a $17M Cannes bidding war as buyers stay scarce on the Croisette

A24 acquired worldwide rights to Jordan Firstman's directorial debut "Club Kid" for a reported $17 million, beating Focus Features, Searchlight, Netflix, and Mubi in the festival's marquee bidding war. Amazon took most international rights to "Pumping Black," a Mimi Cave psychological thriller starring Natalie Portman and Jonathan Bailey, in the biggest package sale of the market, while Netflix landed the animated feature "In Waves" in a mid-seven-figure deal.

The eight-figure deals were bright spots in an otherwise thin market. As Cannes enters its 79th year, the major studios largely sat it out, leaving fewer stars and no Hollywood blockbusters on the Croisette. The trades framed the open question bluntly: whether a box-office comeback can lure studios and streamers back to a festival whose dealmaking center of gravity is visibly shifting.

Why it mattersAcquisition economics and rights structuring at Cannes are the entertainment-law practice in miniature; A24 outbidding the streamers signals where leverage sits in the indie-film market right now.

For the first time since 2023, Russia is losing ground in Ukraine

Russia is posting a sustained net loss of occupied Ukrainian territory for the first time since October 2023, according to Institute for the Study of War data: a net loss of 29 square miles in the week of May 12 to 19, and 69 square miles over the four weeks through May 19. The Economist estimated Ukraine clawed back 79 square miles in the past 30 days, even as Russia launched a record 8,000-plus drones last month.

Analysts are split on what it means. The Economist cautioned that Russian forces "may be catching their breath before a major summer offensive, or this could be a turning point in the war," while a member of Russia's parliament publicly warned the economy may not sustain a prolonged fight amid soaring defense spending. The shifting battlefield hands Trump fresh leverage as he presses a settlement that would likely trade some land for security guarantees.

Why it mattersA genuine inflection in the war moves oil, European defense budgets, and the diplomatic calendar, the cross-currents that feed straight into every macro and markets read.

Corporate · New York

Lincoln International pops 13% in its NYSE debut, raising $421M

Lincoln International, the Chicago-based middle-market investment bank, priced its IPO at $20 a share, the high end of its range, raising $421 million, then opened at $22.51 on the NYSE under ticker LCLN, a 12.6% premium that briefly touched $23 intraday and valued the advisory firm near $2.3 billion. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley were joint lead bookrunners, the same pairing now steering OpenAI's filing.

The debut is a rare public listing for an M&A and capital-advisory boutique, the kind of independent shop that has multiplied as senior bankers leave bulge-bracket firms. Its warm reception, alongside SpaceX's filing and OpenAI's, reinforces the read that the IPO window is cracking open after a long drought. The offering closed May 21.

Why it mattersA pure-play M&A advisory firm going public, and popping, is a direct read on how the market values the dealmaking business itself, not just the companies it advises.
